13 Applications in classical mechanics

So far we have limited ourselves to treating stochastic processes in the context of ideal mathematical systems. Below we will see how the tools learned in this regard can also be applied in real physical systems, in which quantities measurable in the laboratory are involved.

13.1 Particle in an extended medium

The first system we are going to study is that of a particle of mass \(m\) moving in an extended medium. This medium is composed of smaller particles than the one considered, but large enough that the effect due to collisions cannot be neglected, and they constitute a thermostatic bath, homogeneous and isotropic, in thermal equilibrium at a constant temperature \(T\).

The particle, which we will refer to as a mesoscopic particle, will be subject to two types of forces: an external force \(\vec{F}_{ext}\) and the combination of the forces that each individual particle of the medium exerts on it.

Combining the two terms, the dynamics of the motion will be described by the following formula:

\[\begin{equation*} m\frac{d^2\vec{x}}{dt^2}=\vec{F}_{ext}\left(\vec{x}(t)\right)+\sum_{i=1}^N\vec{F}\left(\vec{x}(t)-\vec{x}_i(t)\right) \end{equation*}\]

assuming that \(N\) is the total number of particles in the medium.

In realistic physical systems, the number \(N\) is of the order of Avogadro’s number, therefore it is not possible to integrate the differential equation to obtain the equation of motion, but it is necessary to use an effective approach that simplifies the description of the overall dynamics. We divide the contribution exerted by the individual particles into two components: the first is an average contribution \(\vec{F}_m\), which has the effect of slowing down the particle due to collisions, and is what we commonly call viscous friction, proportional to the particle’s velocity; the second is instead a contribution of a statistical nature \(\vec{F}_n\), which we can identify as noise. Let’s focus on the latter.

In order for \(\vec{F}_n\) to be treated as a stochastic process, we need to make explicit three conditions that we must assume to be valid for the system in question:

If all this is valid, the term \(\vec{F}_n\) is nothing more than the Gaussian white noise \(\zeta(t)\), since the symmetry of the system guarantees that there is no preferential direction (\(\langle\zeta(t)\rangle=0\)) and there is also no correlation at different times (\(\langle\zeta(t)\zeta(t')\rangle=\delta(t-t')\)).

To summarize, the total forces acting are three: the external force \(\vec{F}_{ext}\), the average force \(\vec{F}_m\propto-\frac{d\vec{x}}{dt}\) and the noise \(\vec{F}_n\propto\zeta(t)\). Assuming for simplicity that there is no external force acting on the system, and considering a one-dimensional system, the equation of dynamics will be written as follows:

\[\begin{equation} \boxed{m\frac{d^2x}{dt^2}=-\gamma\frac{dx}{dt}+\sigma\zeta(t)} \end{equation}\]

13.1.1 Connection with the diffusion process

We immediately notice a parallelism between the system just described and the Brownian motion described starting from the Markov process. In fact, as we will see below, the two phenomena are not at all disconnected.

To study how the variance relative to the position of the mesoscopic particle changes, it is necessary to find the expression for the derivative of \(\langle x(t)^2\rangle\).

Let’s make the second temporal derivative of \(x^2\) explicit:

\[\begin{align*} \frac{d^2\left(x^2\right)}{dt^2}&=\frac{d}{dt}\left(\frac{d\left(x^2\right)}{dt}\right)=\frac{d}{dt}\left(\frac{d\left(x^2\right)}{dx}\frac{dx}{dt}\right)=\frac{d}{dt}\left(2x\frac{dx}{dt}\right)=\\ &=2\left(\frac{dx}{dt}\right)^2+2x\frac{d^2x}{dt^2} \end{align*}\]

Isolating the last term:

\[\begin{equation*} x\frac{d^2x}{dt^2}=\frac{1}{2}\frac{d^2\left(x^2\right)}{dt^2}-\left(\frac{dx}{dt}\right)^2 \end{equation*}\]

We will therefore have to multiply both members of (eq:meccanica-classica-mezzo-ampio-equazione-differenziale) by \(x\) and substitute what we have just found:

\[\begin{equation*} mx\frac{d^2x}{dt^2}=-\gamma x\frac{dx}{dt}+\sigma x\zeta(t) \ \Longrightarrow \ \frac{m}{2}\frac{d^2\left(x^2\right)}{dt^2}-m\left(\frac{dx}{dt}\right)^2=-\gamma x\frac{dx}{dt}+\sigma x\zeta(t) \end{equation*}\]

We have already seen above that \(\frac{d\left(x^2\right)}{dt}=2x\frac{dx}{dt}\), so we can substitute in the first term of the second member:

\[\begin{equation} \frac{m}{2}\frac{d^2\left(x^2\right)}{dt^2}-m\left(\frac{dx}{dt}\right)^2=-\frac{\gamma}{2}\frac{d\left(x^2\right)}{dt}+\sigma x\zeta(t) \end{equation}\]

At this point we can apply the average value to the entire equation:

\[\begin{equation*} \frac{m}{2}\frac{d^2\left\langle x^2\right\rangle}{dt^2}-m\left\langle\left(\frac{dx}{dt}\right)^2\right\rangle=-\frac{\gamma}{2}\frac{d\left\langle x^2\right\rangle}{dt}+\sigma\langle x\zeta(t)\rangle \end{equation*}\]

Using the Itô convention:

\[\begin{equation*} \langle x\zeta(t)\rangle=\langle x\rangle\cancel{\langle\zeta(t)\rangle}=0 \end{equation*}\]

Ordering the remaining terms with respect to the order of the derivative, we obtain:

\[\begin{equation} \frac{m}{2}\frac{d^2\left\langle x^2\right\rangle}{dt^2}+\frac{\gamma}{2}\frac{d\left\langle x^2\right\rangle}{dt}-m\left\langle\left(\frac{dx}{dt}\right)^2\right\rangle=0 \end{equation}\]

At this point we can substitute the equation given by the equipartition theorem of energy, which tells us that, in a thermodynamic system at equilibrium, each degree of freedom contributes to the average value of the kinetic energy of the system with a quantity equal to \(\frac{1}{2}k_BT\) for each mole of substance.

Assuming we are considering only one mole of fluid, it follows that

\[\begin{equation*} \frac{m}{\cancel{2}}\left\langle\left(\frac{dx}{dt}\right)^2\right\rangle=\frac{1}{\cancel{2}}k_BT \end{equation*}\]

being only one degree of freedom in a one-dimensional system.

Let’s go back to (eq:meccanica-classica-mezzo-ampio-calcolo-varianza-1):

\[\begin{equation*} \frac{m}{2}\frac{d^2\left\langle x^2\right\rangle}{dt^2}+\frac{\gamma}{2}\frac{d\left\langle x^2\right\rangle}{dt}-k_BT=0 \end{equation*}\]

To solve this differential equation we define

\[\begin{equation*} y(t)=\frac{d\left\langle x^2\right\rangle}{dt} \end{equation*}\]

In this way we obtain a first-order differential equation:

\[\begin{equation} \frac{m}{2}\frac{dy}{dt}+\frac{\gamma}{2}y-k_BT=0 \end{equation}\]

Let’s consider the associated homogeneous equation, which has as its solution

\[\begin{equation*} \frac{m}{\cancel{2}}\frac{dy}{dt}+\frac{\gamma}{\cancel{2}}y=0 \ \Longrightarrow \ \int\frac{dy}{y}=-\frac{\gamma}{m}\int dt \ \Longrightarrow \ y(t)=ke^{-\frac{\gamma}{m}t} \end{equation*}\]

with \(k\) constant.

To calculate the overall solution we need to add a constant contribution \(c\) such that

\[\begin{equation*} \frac{m}{2}\cancel{\frac{dc}{dt}}+\frac{\gamma}{2}c-k_BT=0 \ \iff \ c=\frac{2k_BT}{\gamma} \end{equation*}\]

The solution will therefore be

\[\begin{equation} y(t)=ke^{-\frac{\gamma}{m}t}+\frac{2k_BT}{\gamma} \end{equation}\]

For a system with suspended particles, it is typically found that \(\frac{m}{\gamma}\sim10^{-8} \ s\), which is a very small time interval; considering times \(t\gg10^{-8} \ s\), in fact, it is possible to neglect the exponential contribution, allowing us to write:

\[\begin{equation*} y(t)\simeq\frac{2k_BT}{\gamma} \end{equation*}\]

which, as a function of \(x(t)\), becomes

\[\begin{equation} \frac{d\left\langle x^2\right\rangle}{dt}=\frac{2k_BT}{\gamma} \end{equation}\]

We have thus reached the initial objective. The variance, in fact, will be given by the integration of this equation:

\[\begin{equation} \boxed{\left\langle x(t)^2\right\rangle=\left\langle x_0^2\right\rangle+\frac{2k_BT}{\gamma}t} \end{equation}\]

We thus find a quantity linearly proportional to time \(t\), exactly as written in (eq:diffusione-varianza) derived precisely in the context of the study of Brownian motion.

13.1.2 Einstein relation

From the link between the diffusion process and the stochastic Wiener process, one of the four results obtained by Albert Einstein in his Annus Mirabilis (1905) also emerges.

To be able to derive it, however, it is first necessary to redefine the diffusion coefficient \(D\) that we have used so far by multiplying by a factor of \(2\), thus passing from \(D\) to \(2D\). That said, it is sufficient to start from the stochastic differential equation that defines Brownian motion, written in (eq:processi-stocastici-prima-equazione-differenziale-stocastica):

\[\begin{equation} dx=\sqrt{2D}dB \end{equation}\]

having set \(\mu=0\) since \(\vec{F}_{ext}\), and there is therefore no preferential direction.

Let’s integrate both members:

\[\begin{equation*} x(t)=x_0+\sqrt{2D}B(t) \end{equation*}\]

Let’s square and take the average value of both members:

\[\begin{align*} \left\langle x(t)^2\right\rangle&=\left\langle x_0^2+2\sqrt{2D}x_0B(t)+2DB(t)^2\right\rangle=\\ &=\left\langle x_0^2\right\rangle+2\sqrt{2D}\left\langle x_0\right\rangle\cancel{\left\langle B(t)\right\rangle}+2D\left\langle B(t)^2\right\rangle \end{align*}\]

We thus obtain:

\[\begin{equation} \left\langle x(t)^2\right\rangle=\left\langle x_0^2\right\rangle+2Dt \end{equation}\]

We can now equate the second members of (eq:meccanica-classica-mezzo-esteso-varianza-risultato-finale) and (eq:meccanica-classica-varianza-moto-browniano-aggiustata), finding the Einstein relation:

\[\begin{equation} \cancel{\left\langle x_0^2\right\rangle}+\frac{2k_BT}{\gamma}t=\cancel{\left\langle x_0^2\right\rangle}+2Dt \ \Longrightarrow \ \boxed{D=\frac{k_BT}{\gamma}} \end{equation}\]

The reason why this equation is so important is because it reveals an intrinsic relationship between the microscopic properties of the system (the temperature \(T\) and the friction coefficient \(\gamma\)) and the macroscopic behavior of the particle (the diffusion coefficient \(D\)). It is also one of the first results of the fluctuation-dissipation theorem, according to which, in a thermodynamic system at equilibrium, the dissipative forces and the thermal fluctuations must balance each other: we note in fact that \(D\), which is constant, depends neither on the initial conditions nor on the nature of the interaction between the particles of the medium and the mesoscopic particle, and the ratio between \(T\) and \(\gamma\) therefore depends exclusively on the thermal equilibrium in which the system is located.

Furthermore, since \(D\) can be measured from (eq:meccanica-classica-varianza-moto-browniano-aggiustata), \(T\) is known and \(\gamma\) can be obtained from the relation \(\gamma=6\pi\eta r\), the Einstein relation made it possible to measure for the first time the Boltzmann constant \(k_B\), which in turn was useful for finding an estimate of Avogadro’s number, since the two quantities are linked by the relation \(k_B=\frac{R}{N_A}\) with \(R\) being the gas constant known since the previous century.

13.2 Boltzmann distribution

Let’s continue to consider the system described by the differential equation (eq:meccanica-classica-mezzo-ampio-equazione-differenziale) but let’s add a conservative contribution due to an external potential \(U(x)\):

\[\begin{equation} m\frac{d^2x}{dt^2}=-\gamma\frac{dx}{dt}\red{-\frac{dU}{dx}}+\sigma\zeta(t) \end{equation}\]

Let’s also study this time the overdamped limit in which \(\frac{m}{\gamma}\to0\):

\[\begin{equation*} \frac{m}{\gamma}\frac{d^2x}{dt^2}=-\frac{dx}{dt}-\frac{1}{\gamma}\frac{dU}{dx}+\frac{\sigma}{\gamma}\zeta(t) \ \xrightarrow{\frac{m}{\gamma}\to0} \ -\frac{dx}{dt}-\frac{1}{\gamma}\frac{dU}{dx}+\frac{\sigma}{\gamma}\zeta(t)=0 \end{equation*}\]

Being in equilibrium conditions, we can make the coefficient of \(\zeta(t)\) explicit in terms of physical constants; it is, in fact, given by (eq:relazione-di-einstein-equazione-diffenziale-di-partenza) and therefore equal to \(\sqrt{2D}\), with \(D\) being given by the Einstein relation (equation (eq:relazione-di-einstein)):

\[\begin{equation} \frac{\sigma}{\gamma}=\sqrt{2\cdot\frac{k_BT}{\gamma}}=\sqrt{\frac{2k_BT}{\gamma}} \ \Longrightarrow \ \frac{dx}{dt}+\frac{1}{\gamma}\frac{dU}{dx}-\sqrt{\frac{2k_BT}{\gamma}}\zeta(t)=0 \end{equation}\]

We thus find the following stochastic differential equation:

\[\begin{equation} \boxed{dx=-\frac{1}{\gamma}\frac{dU}{dx}dt+\sqrt{\frac{2k_BT}{\gamma}}dB} \end{equation}\]

We can identify the following two terms:

\[\begin{equation} \begin{cases} \mu(x)=-\frac{1}{\gamma}\frac{dU}{dx}\\ \sigma=\sqrt{\frac{2k_BT}{\gamma}} \end{cases} \end{equation}\]

We can thus identify the equilibrium distribution from (eq:fokker-planck-soluzione-all-equilibrio-forma-finale):

\[\begin{equation*} p^{st}(x)\propto e^{\cancel{2}\int_0^x\left(-\frac{1}{\cancel{\gamma}}\frac{dU}{dx'}\right)\cdot\frac{\cancel{\gamma}}{\cancel{2}k_BT}dx'}=e^{-\frac{1}{k_BT}\int_0^x\frac{dU}{dx'}dx'} \end{equation*}\]

Ultimately, we will have

\[\begin{equation} p^{st}(x)\propto e^{-\frac{1}{k_BT}U(x)} \end{equation}\]

having assumed for simplicity that \(U(0)=0\).

We note that what we have found is none other than the Boltzmann distribution, widely studied in statistical mechanics as the distribution associated with the Maxwell-Boltzmann statistics, in which the temperature of the system is sufficiently high and the density low enough to be able to ignore quantum effects. The (eq:meccanica-classica-distribuzione-di-boltzmann-equilibrio-finale) is actually a simplified version in which only the position \(x\) is considered and the kinetic term \(K\) to which the momentum contributes is ignored.

From a purely physical point of view, we note that the distribution was derived starting from the Einstein relation, which is independent of the potential \(U\) useful for generating the external force: this further strengthens the idea that the coefficient \(D\) is an intrinsic characteristic of the system, not being modified in the presence of a potential that interferes from the outside.

It is also curious to note that the choice of a harmonic potential \(U(x)=\frac{1}{2}kx^2\) implies the following differential equation:

\[\begin{equation*} dx=-\frac{k}{\gamma}xdt+\sqrt{\frac{2k_BT}{\gamma}} \end{equation*}\]

which is the one useful for defining the Ornstein-Uhlenbeck process by substituting the appropriate coefficients.

13.2.1 Damped harmonic Brownian motion

We will now try to solve equation (eq:meccanica-classica-distribuzione-di-boltzmann-equazione-di-partenza) without going to the overdamped limit, but also including the second-order derivative.

For simplicity, we will consider the potential of the harmonic oscillator, which gives rise to the so-called damped harmonic Brownian motion:

\[\begin{equation} \boxed{\frac{d^2x}{dt^2}+\frac{\gamma}{m}\frac{dx}{dt}+\frac{k}{m}x=\frac{\sqrt{2\gamma k_BT}}{m}\zeta(t)} \end{equation}\]

being \(\frac{dU}{dx}=kx\) and \(\sigma=\sqrt{2\gamma k_BT}\) (from (eq:meccanica-classica-distribuzione-di-boltzmann-calcolo-sigma-su-gamma)).

This is a non-homogeneous second-order differential equation. To solve it, we must first find the solution of the associated homogeneous equation:

\[\begin{equation} \frac{d^2x}{dt^2}+\frac{\gamma}{m}\frac{dx}{dt}+kx=0 \end{equation}\]

It has as its solution an exponential of the type \(e^{\lambda t}\), from which the equation follows

\[\begin{equation*} e^{\lambda t}\left(\lambda^2+\frac{\gamma}{m}\lambda+\frac{k}{m}\right)=0 \end{equation*}\]

which gives rise to the characteristic equation

\[\begin{equation*} \lambda^2+\frac{\gamma}{m}\lambda+\frac{k}{m}=0 \end{equation*}\]

The solutions are

\[\begin{equation*} \lambda_\pm=\frac{-\frac{\gamma}{m}\pm\sqrt{\frac{\gamma^2}{m^2}-\frac{4k}{m}}}{2}=-\frac{\gamma}{2m}\pm\sqrt{\frac{\gamma^2}{4m^2}-\frac{k}{m}} \end{equation*}\]

# Applications in classical mechanics

So far we have limited ourselves to treating stochastic processes in the context of ideal mathematical systems. Below we will see how the tools learned in this regard can also be applied in real physical systems, in which quantities measurable in the laboratory are involved.

## Particle in an extended medium

\begin{wrapfigure}{R}{0.5\textwidth}

\end{wrapfigure}

The first system we are going to study is that of a particle of mass $m$ moving in an extended medium.\footnote{By *extended* we mean that there are no barriers or obstacles, and that the particle can move in any direction in complete freedom.} This medium is composed of smaller particles than the one considered, but large enough that the effect due to collisions cannot be neglected, and they constitute a *thermostatic bath*, homogeneous and isotropic, in thermal equilibrium at a constant temperature $T$.

The particle, which we will refer to as a *mesoscopic particle*, will be subject to two types of forces: an *external force* $\vec{F}_{ext}$ and the combination of the forces that each individual particle of the medium exerts on it.

Combining the two terms, the dynamics of the motion will be described by the following formula:

\begin{equation*}
    m\frac{d^2\vec{x}}{dt^2}=\vec{F}_{ext}\left(\vec{x}(t)\right)+\sum_{i=1}^N\vec{F}\left(\vec{x}(t)-\vec{x}_i(t)\right)
\end{equation*}

assuming that $N$ is the total number of particles in the medium.

In realistic physical systems, the number $N$ is of the order of Avogadro's number, therefore it is not possible to integrate the differential equation to obtain the equation of motion, but it is necessary to use an *effective* approach that simplifies the description of the overall dynamics. We divide the contribution exerted by the individual particles into two components: the first is an *average* contribution $\vec{F}_m$, which has the effect of *slowing down* the particle due to collisions, and is what we commonly call **viscous friction**, proportional to the particle's velocity; the second is instead a contribution of a *statistical* nature $\vec{F}_n$, which we can identify as **noise**. Let's focus on the latter.

In order for $\vec{F}_n$ to be treated as a stochastic process, we need to make explicit three conditions that we must assume to be valid for the system in question:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item All particles in the medium must have motions that are *independent of each other*
    \item Considering not excessively short time intervals, the movement of the same particle in the medium must be *independent at different instants of time*
    \item There must exist a time interval $\Delta t$ such that it is much smaller than the time interval $\Delta T_O$ between one observation and another, but which at the same time is large enough to be able to consider the motions of the mesoscopic particle as *independent events* in successive $\Delta t$
\end{enumerate}

If all this is valid, the term $\vec{F}_n$ is nothing more than the **Gaussian white noise** $\zeta(t)$, since the symmetry of the system guarantees that there is no preferential direction ($\langle\zeta(t)\rangle=0$) and there is also no correlation at different times ($\langle\zeta(t)\zeta(t')\rangle=\delta(t-t')$).

To summarize, the total forces acting are three: the external force $\vec{F}_{ext}$, the average force $\vec{F}_m\propto-\frac{d\vec{x}}{dt}$ and the noise $\vec{F}_n\propto\zeta(t)$. Assuming for simplicity that there is no external force acting on the system, and considering a one-dimensional system, the equation of dynamics will be written as follows:

\begin{equation}
    \boxed{m\frac{d^2x}{dt^2}=-\gamma\frac{dx}{dt}+\sigma\zeta(t)}
\end{equation}

### Connection with the diffusion process

We immediately notice a parallelism between the system just described and the Brownian motion described starting from the Markov process. In fact, as we will see below, the two phenomena are not at all disconnected.

To study how the variance relative to the position of the mesoscopic particle changes, it is necessary to find the expression for the derivative of $\langle x(t)^2\rangle$.

Let's make the second temporal derivative of $x^2$ explicit:

\begin{align*}
    \frac{d^2\left(x^2\right)}{dt^2}&=\frac{d}{dt}\left(\frac{d\left(x^2\right)}{dt}\right)=\frac{d}{dt}\left(\frac{d\left(x^2\right)}{dx}\frac{dx}{dt}\right)=\frac{d}{dt}\left(2x\frac{dx}{dt}\right)=\\
    &=2\left(\frac{dx}{dt}\right)^2+2x\frac{d^2x}{dt^2}
\end{align*}

Isolating the last term:

\begin{equation*}
    x\frac{d^2x}{dt^2}=\frac{1}{2}\frac{d^2\left(x^2\right)}{dt^2}-\left(\frac{dx}{dt}\right)^2
\end{equation*}

We will therefore have to multiply both members of (eq:meccanica-classica-mezzo-ampio-equazione-differenziale) by $x$ and substitute what we have just found:

\begin{equation*}
    mx\frac{d^2x}{dt^2}=-\gamma x\frac{dx}{dt}+\sigma x\zeta(t) \ \Longrightarrow \ \frac{m}{2}\frac{d^2\left(x^2\right)}{dt^2}-m\left(\frac{dx}{dt}\right)^2=-\gamma x\frac{dx}{dt}+\sigma x\zeta(t)
\end{equation*}

We have already seen above that $\frac{d\left(x^2\right)}{dt}=2x\frac{dx}{dt}$, so we can substitute in the first term of the second member:

\begin{equation}
    \frac{m}{2}\frac{d^2\left(x^2\right)}{dt^2}-m\left(\frac{dx}{dt}\right)^2=-\frac{\gamma}{2}\frac{d\left(x^2\right)}{dt}+\sigma x\zeta(t)
\end{equation}

At this point we can apply the average value to the entire equation:

\begin{equation*}
    \frac{m}{2}\frac{d^2\left\langle x^2\right\rangle}{dt^2}-m\left\langle\left(\frac{dx}{dt}\right)^2\right\rangle=-\frac{\gamma}{2}\frac{d\left\langle x^2\right\rangle}{dt}+\sigma\langle x\zeta(t)\rangle
\end{equation*}

Using the Itô convention:

\begin{equation*}
    \langle x\zeta(t)\rangle=\langle x\rangle\cancel{\langle\zeta(t)\rangle}=0
\end{equation*}

Ordering the remaining terms with respect to the order of the derivative, we obtain:

\begin{equation}
    \frac{m}{2}\frac{d^2\left\langle x^2\right\rangle}{dt^2}+\frac{\gamma}{2}\frac{d\left\langle x^2\right\rangle}{dt}-m\left\langle\left(\frac{dx}{dt}\right)^2\right\rangle=0
\end{equation}

At this point we can substitute the equation given by the *equipartition theorem of energy*, which tells us that, in a thermodynamic system at equilibrium, each degree of freedom contributes to the average value of the kinetic energy of the system with a quantity equal to $\frac{1}{2}k_BT$ for each mole of substance.

Assuming we are considering only one mole of fluid, it follows that

\begin{equation*}
    \frac{m}{\cancel{2}}\left\langle\left(\frac{dx}{dt}\right)^2\right\rangle=\frac{1}{\cancel{2}}k_BT
\end{equation*}

being only one degree of freedom in a one-dimensional system.

Let's go back to (eq:meccanica-classica-mezzo-ampio-calcolo-varianza-1):

\begin{equation*}
    \frac{m}{2}\frac{d^2\left\langle x^2\right\rangle}{dt^2}+\frac{\gamma}{2}\frac{d\left\langle x^2\right\rangle}{dt}-k_BT=0
\end{equation*}

To solve this differential equation we define

\begin{equation*}
    y(t)=\frac{d\left\langle x^2\right\rangle}{dt}
\end{equation*}

In this way we obtain a first-order differential equation:

\begin{equation}
    \frac{m}{2}\frac{dy}{dt}+\frac{\gamma}{2}y-k_BT=0
\end{equation}

Let's consider the associated homogeneous equation, which has as its solution

\begin{equation*}
    \frac{m}{\cancel{2}}\frac{dy}{dt}+\frac{\gamma}{\cancel{2}}y=0 \ \Longrightarrow \ \int\frac{dy}{y}=-\frac{\gamma}{m}\int dt \ \Longrightarrow \ y(t)=ke^{-\frac{\gamma}{m}t}
\end{equation*}

with $k$ constant.

To calculate the overall solution we need to add a constant contribution $c$ such that

\begin{equation*}
    \frac{m}{2}\cancel{\frac{dc}{dt}}+\frac{\gamma}{2}c-k_BT=0 \ \iff \ c=\frac{2k_BT}{\gamma}
\end{equation*}

The solution will therefore be

\begin{equation}
    y(t)=ke^{-\frac{\gamma}{m}t}+\frac{2k_BT}{\gamma}
\end{equation}

For a system with suspended particles, it is typically found that $\frac{m}{\gamma}\sim10^{-8} \ s$, which is a very small time interval; considering times $t\gg10^{-8} \ s$, in fact, it is possible to neglect the exponential contribution,\footnote{Formally we speak of an *overdamped limit* when $\frac{m}{\gamma}\to0$, as in this case.} allowing us to write:

\begin{equation*}
    y(t)\simeq\frac{2k_BT}{\gamma}
\end{equation*}

which, as a function of $x(t)$, becomes

\begin{equation}
    \frac{d\left\langle x^2\right\rangle}{dt}=\frac{2k_BT}{\gamma}
\end{equation}

We have thus reached the initial objective. The variance, in fact, will be given by the integration of this equation:

\begin{equation}
    \boxed{\left\langle x(t)^2\right\rangle=\left\langle x_0^2\right\rangle+\frac{2k_BT}{\gamma}t}
\end{equation}

We thus find a quantity linearly proportional to time $t$, exactly as written in (eq:diffusione-varianza) derived precisely in the context of the study of Brownian motion.

### Einstein relation

From the link between the diffusion process and the stochastic Wiener process, one of the four results obtained by Albert Einstein in his *Annus Mirabilis* (1905) also emerges.

To be able to derive it, however, it is first necessary to redefine the diffusion coefficient $D$ that we have used so far by multiplying by a factor of $2$, thus passing from $D$ to $2D$.\footnote{This is yet another unjustified inconsistency on the part of the professor during the course. It is actually a pure matter of definition: the coefficient $D$ was defined in (eq:diffusione-definizioe-v-e-D) without considering the factor $\frac{1}{2}$ in the expansion (eq:diffusione-limite-continuo-calcolo-prima-della-definizione), but usually in physics it is included, as we will see in the derivation of the Einstein relation, which is one of the most important relations in modern physics.} That said, it is sufficient to start from the stochastic differential equation that defines Brownian motion, written in (eq:processi-stocastici-prima-equazione-differenziale-stocastica):

\begin{equation}
    dx=\sqrt{2D}dB
\end{equation}

having set $\mu=0$ since $\vec{F}_{ext}$, and there is therefore no preferential direction.

Let's integrate both members:

\begin{equation*}
    x(t)=x_0+\sqrt{2D}B(t)
\end{equation*}

Let's square and take the average value of both members:

\begin{align*}
    \left\langle x(t)^2\right\rangle&=\left\langle x_0^2+2\sqrt{2D}x_0B(t)+2DB(t)^2\right\rangle=\\
    &=\left\langle x_0^2\right\rangle+2\sqrt{2D}\left\langle x_0\right\rangle\cancel{\left\langle B(t)\right\rangle}+2D\left\langle B(t)^2\right\rangle
\end{align*}

We thus obtain:

\begin{equation}
    \left\langle x(t)^2\right\rangle=\left\langle x_0^2\right\rangle+2Dt
\end{equation}

We can now equate the second members of (eq:meccanica-classica-mezzo-esteso-varianza-risultato-finale) and (eq:meccanica-classica-varianza-moto-browniano-aggiustata), finding the **Einstein relation**:

\begin{equation}
    \cancel{\left\langle x_0^2\right\rangle}+\frac{2k_BT}{\gamma}t=\cancel{\left\langle x_0^2\right\rangle}+2Dt \ \Longrightarrow \ \boxed{D=\frac{k_BT}{\gamma}}
\end{equation}

The reason why this equation is so important is because it reveals an intrinsic relationship between the microscopic properties of the system (the temperature $T$ and the friction coefficient $\gamma$) and the macroscopic behavior of the particle (the diffusion coefficient $D$). It is also one of the first results of the **fluctuation-dissipation theorem**, according to which, in a thermodynamic system at equilibrium, the dissipative forces and the thermal fluctuations must balance each other: we note in fact that $D$, which is constant, depends neither on the initial conditions nor on the nature of the interaction between the particles of the medium and the mesoscopic particle, and the ratio between $T$ and $\gamma$ therefore depends exclusively on the thermal equilibrium in which the system is located.

Furthermore, since $D$ can be measured from (eq:meccanica-classica-varianza-moto-browniano-aggiustata), $T$ is known and $\gamma$ can be obtained from the relation $\gamma=6\pi\eta r$, the Einstein relation made it possible to measure for the first time the Boltzmann constant $k_B$, which in turn was useful for finding an estimate of Avogadro's number, since the two quantities are linked by the relation $k_B=\frac{R}{N_A}$ with $R$ being the gas constant known since the previous century.

## Boltzmann distribution

Let's continue to consider the system described by the differential equation (eq:meccanica-classica-mezzo-ampio-equazione-differenziale) but let's add a *conservative* contribution due to an external potential $U(x)$:

\begin{equation}
    m\frac{d^2x}{dt^2}=-\gamma\frac{dx}{dt}\red{-\frac{dU}{dx}}+\sigma\zeta(t)
\end{equation}

Let's also study this time the overdamped limit in which $\frac{m}{\gamma}\to0$:

\begin{equation*}
    \frac{m}{\gamma}\frac{d^2x}{dt^2}=-\frac{dx}{dt}-\frac{1}{\gamma}\frac{dU}{dx}+\frac{\sigma}{\gamma}\zeta(t) \ \xrightarrow{\frac{m}{\gamma}\to0} \ -\frac{dx}{dt}-\frac{1}{\gamma}\frac{dU}{dx}+\frac{\sigma}{\gamma}\zeta(t)=0
\end{equation*}

Being in equilibrium conditions, we can make the coefficient of $\zeta(t)$ explicit in terms of physical constants; it is, in fact, given by (eq:relazione-di-einstein-equazione-diffenziale-di-partenza) and therefore equal to $\sqrt{2D}$, with $D$ being given by the Einstein relation (equation (eq:relazione-di-einstein)):

\begin{equation}
    \frac{\sigma}{\gamma}=\sqrt{2\cdot\frac{k_BT}{\gamma}}=\sqrt{\frac{2k_BT}{\gamma}} \ \Longrightarrow \ \frac{dx}{dt}+\frac{1}{\gamma}\frac{dU}{dx}-\sqrt{\frac{2k_BT}{\gamma}}\zeta(t)=0
\end{equation}

We thus find the following stochastic differential equation:

\begin{equation}
    \boxed{dx=-\frac{1}{\gamma}\frac{dU}{dx}dt+\sqrt{\frac{2k_BT}{\gamma}}dB}
\end{equation}

We can identify the following two terms:\footnote{With yet another abuse of notation, here I am defining a new $\sigma$ *different* from the one written before, since it would actually be the old $\sigma$ \q{reduced} $\frac{\sigma}{\gamma}$. I am redefining $\sigma$ in this way only for consistency with the reference formula (eq:fokker-planck-equazione-di-langevin-con-delta).}

\begin{equation}
    \begin{cases}
        \mu(x)=-\frac{1}{\gamma}\frac{dU}{dx}\\
        \sigma=\sqrt{\frac{2k_BT}{\gamma}}
    \end{cases}
\end{equation}

We can thus identify the equilibrium distribution from (eq:fokker-planck-soluzione-all-equilibrio-forma-finale):

\begin{equation*}
    p^{st}(x)\propto e^{\cancel{2}\int_0^x\left(-\frac{1}{\cancel{\gamma}}\frac{dU}{dx'}\right)\cdot\frac{\cancel{\gamma}}{\cancel{2}k_BT}dx'}=e^{-\frac{1}{k_BT}\int_0^x\frac{dU}{dx'}dx'}
\end{equation*}

Ultimately, we will have

\begin{equation}
    p^{st}(x)\propto e^{-\frac{1}{k_BT}U(x)}
\end{equation}

having assumed for simplicity that $U(0)=0$.

We note that what we have found is none other than the **Boltzmann distribution**, widely studied in statistical mechanics as the distribution associated with the Maxwell-Boltzmann statistics, in which the temperature of the system is sufficiently high and the density low enough to be able to ignore quantum effects. The (eq:meccanica-classica-distribuzione-di-boltzmann-equilibrio-finale) is actually a simplified version in which only the position $x$ is considered and the kinetic term $K$ to which the momentum contributes is ignored.\footnote{The distribution we found is trivially obtained from the general one integrated with respect to momentum, which is a legitimate operation since position and momentum are independent in a classical system.}

From a purely physical point of view, we note that the distribution was derived starting from the Einstein relation, which is *independent* of the potential $U$ useful for generating the external force: this further strengthens the idea that the coefficient $D$ is an *intrinsic* characteristic of the system, not being modified in the presence of a potential that interferes from the outside.

It is also curious to note that the choice of a harmonic potential $U(x)=\frac{1}{2}kx^2$ implies the following differential equation:

\begin{equation*}
    dx=-\frac{k}{\gamma}xdt+\sqrt{\frac{2k_BT}{\gamma}}
\end{equation*}

which is the one useful for defining the **Ornstein-Uhlenbeck process** by substituting the appropriate coefficients.

### Damped harmonic Brownian motion

We will now try to solve equation (eq:meccanica-classica-distribuzione-di-boltzmann-equazione-di-partenza) without going to the overdamped limit, but also including the second-order derivative.

For simplicity, we will consider the potential of the harmonic oscillator, which gives rise to the so-called **damped harmonic Brownian motion**:

\begin{equation}
    \boxed{\frac{d^2x}{dt^2}+\frac{\gamma}{m}\frac{dx}{dt}+\frac{k}{m}x=\frac{\sqrt{2\gamma k_BT}}{m}\zeta(t)}
\end{equation}

being $\frac{dU}{dx}=kx$ and $\sigma=\sqrt{2\gamma k_BT}$ (from (eq:meccanica-classica-distribuzione-di-boltzmann-calcolo-sigma-su-gamma)).

This is a non-homogeneous second-order differential equation. To solve it, we must first find the solution of the associated homogeneous equation:

\begin{equation}
    \frac{d^2x}{dt^2}+\frac{\gamma}{m}\frac{dx}{dt}+kx=0
\end{equation}

It has as its solution an exponential of the type $e^{\lambda t}$, from which the equation follows

\begin{equation*}
    e^{\lambda t}\left(\lambda^2+\frac{\gamma}{m}\lambda+\frac{k}{m}\right)=0
\end{equation*}

which gives rise to the characteristic equation

\begin{equation*}
    \lambda^2+\frac{\gamma}{m}\lambda+\frac{k}{m}=0
\end{equation*}

The solutions are

\begin{equation*}
    \lambda_\pm=\frac{-\frac{\gamma}{m}\pm\sqrt{\frac{\gamma^2}{m^2}-\frac{4k}{m}}}{2}=-\frac{\gamma}{2m}\pm\sqrt{\frac{\gamma^2}{4m^2}-\frac{k}{m}}
\end{equation*}

Let's denote by $\gamma_r=\frac{\gamma}{2m}$ the *reduced friction coefficient* and by $\omega_0=\sqrt{\frac{k}{m}}$ the *characteristic frequency* of the system:

\begin{equation}
    \lambda_\pm=-\gamma_r\pm\sqrt{\gamma_r^2-\omega_0^2}
\end{equation}

Finally, let's denote the discriminant by $\Omega=\gamma_r^2-\omega_0^2$:

\begin{equation*}
    \lambda_\pm=-\gamma_r\pm\sqrt{\Omega}
\end{equation*}

We have three possible solutions depending on the sign of the determinant:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item **Overdamped case**: $\Omega>0 \ \iff \ \gamma_r^2>\omega_0^2$

    The roots are real and different, so the solution will be given by a linear combination of the exponentials:

    \begin{equation*}
        x^{om}(t)=Ae^{\left(-\gamma_r-\sqrt{\Omega}\right)t}+Be^{\left(-\gamma_r+\sqrt{\Omega}\right)t}=\left(Ae^{-\sqrt{\Omega}t}+Be^{\sqrt{\Omega}t}\right)e^{-\gamma_rt}
    \end{equation*}

    \item **Critical damping**: $\Omega=0 \ \iff \ \gamma_r^2=\omega_0^2$

    The roots are real and coincident, from which the solution follows

    \begin{equation*}
        x^{om}(t)=(A+Bt)e^{-\gamma_rt}
    \end{equation*}

    \item **Underdamped case**: $\Omega<0 \ \iff \ \gamma_r^2<\omega_0^2$

    The roots are complex conjugates of each other, therefore

    \begin{equation*}
        \lambda_\pm=-\gamma_r\pm\sqrt{-\abs{\Omega}}=-\gamma_r\pm i\sqrt{\abs{\Omega}}
    \end{equation*}

    The solution follows

    \begin{equation*}
        x^{om}(t)=\left[A\cos\left(\sqrt{\abs{\Omega}}t\right)+B\sin\left(\sqrt{\abs{\Omega}}t\right)\right]e^{-\gamma_rt}
    \end{equation*}
\end{enumerate}

To solve the complete equation we also need to consider the second member, in which there is not a constant term but one dependent on time according to the Gaussian noise $\zeta(t)$. To solve this type of equation it is necessary to use the **Green's function** method, according to which the initial equation is rewritten in the form

\begin{equation*}
    \mathcal{L}[x(t)]=\frac{\sqrt{{2\gamma k_BT}}}{m}\zeta(t)
\end{equation*}

where $\mathcal{L}$ is the differential operator $\frac{d^2}{dt^2}+\frac{\gamma}{m}\frac{d}{dt}+\frac{k}{m}$.

Let's rewrite the second member as a function of the Dirac delta:

\begin{equation*}
    \mathcal{L}[x(t)]=\frac{\sqrt{{2\gamma k_BT}}}{m}\int_{-\infty}^{+\infty}\zeta(t')\delta(t-t')dt'
\end{equation*}

Restricting ourselves to the interval $[0,t]$:

\begin{equation*}
    \mathcal{L}[x(t)]=\frac{\sqrt{{2\gamma k_BT}}}{m}\int_0^t\zeta(t')\delta(t-t')dt'
\end{equation*}

Let's define the *Green's function* $G(t,t')$ as that function such that, once the operator $\mathcal{L}$ is applied, the Dirac delta $\delta(t-t')$ is returned:

\begin{equation*}
    \mathcal{L}[x(t)]=\frac{\sqrt{{2\gamma k_BT}}}{m}\int_0^t\zeta(t')\mathcal{L}[G(t,t')]dt'
\end{equation*}

Due to the linearity of the operator, we can take $\mathcal{L}$ out of the integral, also including the multiplying coefficient:

\begin{equation*}
    \mathcal{L}[x(t)]=\mathcal{L}\left[\frac{\sqrt{{2\gamma k_BT}}}{m}\int_0^t\zeta(t')G(t,t')dt'\right]
\end{equation*}

The general solution thus follows

\begin{equation*}
    x(t)=x^{om}(t)+\frac{\sqrt{{2\gamma k_BT}}}{m}\int_0^t\zeta(t')G(t,t')dt'
\end{equation*}

being $\mathcal{L}\left[x^{om}(t)\right]=0$ by how we defined it.

Finally, knowing that $\zeta(t)dt=dB(t)$, we find the final formula

\begin{equation}
    \boxed{x(t)=x^{om}(t)+x^s(t)=x^{om}(t)+\frac{\sqrt{{2\gamma k_BT}}}{m}\int_0^tG(t,t')dB(t')}
\end{equation}

Green's functions are known and tabulated,\footnote{Reference table: \url{https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Green\

\begin{enumerate}
    \item **Overdamped case**: $G(t,t')=\Theta(t-t')e^{-\gamma_r(t-t')}\frac{\sinh\left[\sqrt{\Omega}(t-t')\right]}{\sqrt{\Omega}}$

    Since $t>t'$ then $\Theta(t-t')=1$, from which it follows that

    \begin{equation}
        \begin{cases}
            x^{om}(t)=\left(Ae^{-\sqrt{\Omega}t}+Be^{\sqrt{\Omega}t}\right)e^{-\gamma_rt}\\
            x^s(t)=\frac{1}{m}\sqrt{\frac{2\gamma k_BT}{\Omega}}\int_0^te^{-\gamma_r(t-t')}\sinh\left[\sqrt{\Omega}(t-t')\right]dB(t')
        \end{cases}
    \end{equation}

    The system tends to equilibrium with an exponential decay expressed by two components: one that decays faster ($e^{-\sqrt{\Omega}t}$) and one that decays more slowly ($e^{\sqrt{\Omega}t}$). The stochastic term also decays exponentially, but the decrease is modulated by the hyperbolic sine, which for large $t$ tends to have the opposite behavior ($\sim e^{\sqrt{\Omega}}$), thus balancing in the other direction.

    \item **Critical damping**: $G(t,t')=\Theta(t-t')e^{-\gamma_r(t-t')}(t-t')$

    Since $t>t'$ then $\Theta(t-t')=1$, from which it follows that

    \begin{equation}
        \begin{cases}
            x^{om}(t)=(A+Bt)e^{-\gamma_rt}\\
            x^s(t)=\frac{\sqrt{2\gamma k_BT}}{m}\int_0^te^{-\gamma_r(t-t')}(t-t')dB(t')
        \end{cases}
    \end{equation}

    This is the case in which the system approaches equilibrium faster, due to the single negative exponential term in the homogeneous contribution and the replacement of the hyperbolic sine with the linear term in the stochastic contribution, which further favors damping.

    \item **Underdamped case**: $G(t,t')=\Theta(t-t')e^{-\gamma_r(t-t')}\frac{\sin\left[\sqrt{\abs{\Omega}}(t-t')\right]}{\sqrt{\abs{\Omega}}}$

    Since $t>t'$ then $\Theta(t-t')=1$, from which it follows that

    \begin{equation}
        \begin{cases}
            x^{om}(t)=\left[A\cos\left(\sqrt{\abs{\Omega}}t\right)+B\sin\left(\sqrt{\abs{\Omega}}t\right)\right]e^{-\gamma_rt}\\
            x^{s}(t)=\frac{1}{m}\sqrt{\frac{2\gamma k_BT}{\abs{\Omega}}}\int_0^te^{-\gamma_r(t-t')}\sin\left[\sqrt{\abs{\Omega}}(t-t')\right]dB(t')
        \end{cases}
    \end{equation}

    The system tends to the equilibrium position following an oscillatory motion due to the combination of sine and cosine, to which is added the stochastic contribution that follows a similar trend.
\end{enumerate}

Finally, as we have done for all the stochastic processes studied, it is useful to derive the two-point correlation function $\left\langle x\left(t_1\right)x\left(t_2\right)\right\rangle$, which tells us how the coordinate of the system is correlated with itself at different times. Let's make the solution explicit in terms of the homogeneous and stochastic contributions as done before:

\begin{equation*}
    \left\langle x\left(t_1\right)x\left(t_2\right)\right\rangle=\left\langle\left[x^{om}\left(t_1\right)+x^s\left(t_1\right)\right]\left[x^{om}\left(t_2\right)+x^s\left(t_2\right)\right]\right\rangle
\end{equation*}

From which we obtain the following sum of average values:

\begin{equation*}
    \left\langle x^{om}\left(t_1\right)x^{om}\left(t_2\right)\right\rangle+\left\langle x^{om}\left(t_1\right)x^s\left(t_2\right)\right\rangle+\left\langle x^s\left(t_1\right)x^{om}\left(t_2\right)\right\rangle+\left\langle x^s\left(t_1\right)x^s\left(t_2\right)\right\rangle
\end{equation*}

The \q{mixed products} are null due to the independence of the increments of Brownian motion:

\begin{equation*}
    \left\langle x^{om}\left(t_1\right)x^s\left(t_2\right)\right\rangle=\frac{\sqrt{2\gamma k_BT}}{m}\left\langle x^{om}\left(t_1\right)\right\rangle\int_0^{t_2}\left\langle G\left(t_2,t'\right)\right\rangle\cancel{\langle dB(t')\rangle}=0
\end{equation*}

and the same applies to $\left\langle x^{om}\left(t_2\right)x^s\left(t_1\right)\right\rangle$.

For the product of the stochastic terms, it is convenient to rewrite $dB(t)$ as $\zeta(t)dt$:

\begin{align*}
    \left\langle x^s\left(t_1\right)x^s\left(t_2\right)\right\rangle&=\frac{2\gamma k_BT}{m^2}\int_0^{t_1}\int_0^{t_2}\left\langle G\left(t_1,t'\right)G\left(t_2,t''\right)\right\rangle\langle\zeta(t')\zeta(t'')\rangle dt''dt'=\\
    &=\frac{2\gamma k_BT}{m^2}\int_0^{t_1}\int_0^{t_2}\left\langle G\left(t_1,t'\right)G\left(t_2,t''\right)\right\rangle\delta(t'-t'')dt''dt'\\
    &=\frac{2\gamma k_BT}{m^2}\int_0^{\min\left(t_1,t_2\right)}\left\langle G\left(t_1,t'\right)G\left(t_2,t'\right)\right\rangle dt'
\end{align*}

Let's now study the three cases.

\begin{enumerate}
    \item **Overdamped case**

    Let's calculate $\left\langle x^{om}\left(t_1\right)x^{om}\left(t_2\right)\right\rangle$:

        &\left(Ae^{-\sqrt{\Omega}t_1}+Be^{\sqrt{\Omega}t_1}\right)\left(Ae^{-\sqrt{\Omega}t_2}+Be^{\sqrt{\Omega}t_2}\right)e^{-\gamma_r\left(t_1+t_2\right)}=\\
        &=\left[A^2e^{-\sqrt{\Omega}\left(t_1+t_2\right)}+AB\left(e^{-\sqrt{\Omega}\left(t_1-t_2\right)}+e^{\sqrt{\Omega}\left(t_1+t_2\right)}\right)+B^2e^{\sqrt{\Omega}\left(t_1+t_2\right)}\right]e^{-\gamma_r\left(t_1+t_2\right)}=\\
        &=\left[A^2e^{-\sqrt{\Omega}\left(t_1+t_2\right)}+2AB\cosh\left(\sqrt{\Omega}\left(t_1-t_2\right)\right)+B^2e^{\sqrt{\Omega}\left(t_1+t_2\right)}\right]e^{-\gamma_r\left(t_1+t_2\right)}
    \end{align*}

    \normalsize

    Let's calculate $\left\langle x^s\left(t_1\right)x^s\left(t_2\right)\right\rangle$:

    \small

    \begin{align*}
        &\frac{2\gamma k_BT}{m^2\Omega}\int_0^{\min\left(t_1,t_2\right)}e^{-\gamma_r\left(t_1-t'+t_2-t'\right)}\sinh\left(\sqrt{\Omega}\left(t_1-t'\right)\right)\sinh\left(\sqrt{\Omega}\left(t_2-t'\right)\right)dt'=\\
        &=\frac{2\gamma k_BT}{m^2\Omega}\int_0^{\min\left(t_1,t_2\right)}e^{-\gamma_r\left(t_1+t_2-2t'\right)}\sinh\left(\sqrt{\Omega}\left(t_1-t'\right)\right)\sinh\left(\sqrt{\Omega}\left(t_2-t'\right)\right)dt'
    \end{align*}

    \normalsize

    We can calculate the variance by setting $t_1=t_2=t$:

    \footnotesize

    \begin{equation*}
        \left\langle x(t)^2\right\rangle=\left(Ae^{-\sqrt{\Omega}t}+Be^{\sqrt{\Omega}t}\right)^2e^{-2\gamma_rt}+\frac{2\gamma k_BT}{m^2\Omega}\int_0^te^{-2\gamma_r(t-t')}\sinh^2\left(\sqrt{\Omega}(t-t')\right)dt'
    \end{equation*}

    \normalsize

    In the limit for $t\to\infty$ the first term will give $e^{-2\left(\gamma_r-\sqrt{\Omega}\right)t}$, but since $\sqrt{\Omega}=\sqrt{\gamma_r^2-\omega_0^2}$ then $\gamma_r>\sqrt{\Omega}$, so the exponent is negative and the whole thing goes to zero. As for the second term, instead, we will have to explicitly solve the integral and then find the value at the limit; setting $\tau=t-t'$, we find:

    \begin{align*}
        &-\int_t^0e^{-2\gamma_r\tau}\sinh^2\left(\sqrt{\Omega}\tau\right)d\tau=\int_0^te^{-2\gamma_r\tau}\sinh^2\left(\sqrt{\Omega}\tau\right)d\tau=\\
        &=\int_0^te^{-2\gamma_r\tau}\cdot\frac{e^{2\sqrt{\Omega}\tau}-2e^{\sqrt{\Omega}\tau-\sqrt{\Omega}\tau}+e^{-2\sqrt{\Omega}\tau}}{4}d\tau=\\
        &=\frac{1}{4}\int_0^te^{-2\left(\gamma_r-\sqrt{\Omega}\right)\tau}d\tau-\frac{2}{4}\int_0^te^{-2\gamma_r\tau}d\tau+\frac{1}{4}\int_0^te^{-2\left(\gamma_r+\sqrt{\Omega}\right)\tau}d\tau=\\
        &=-\frac{e^{-2\left(\gamma_r-\sqrt{\Omega}\right)t}-1}{8\left(\gamma_r-\sqrt{\Omega}\right)}+\frac{e^{-2\gamma_rt}-1}{4\gamma_r}-\frac{e^{-2\left(\gamma_r+\sqrt{\Omega}\right)t}-1}{8\left(\gamma_r+\sqrt{\Omega}\right)}
    \end{align*}

    At the limit all the exponents go to zero, so what remains is

    \begin{align*}
        &\frac{1}{8\left(\gamma_r-\sqrt{\Omega}\right)}-\frac{1}{4\gamma_r}+\frac{1}{8\left(\gamma_r+\sqrt{\Omega}\right)}=\\
        &=\frac{\gamma_r\left(\gamma_r+\sqrt{\Omega}\right)-2\left(\gamma_r-\sqrt{\Omega}\right)\left(\gamma_r+\sqrt{\Omega}\right)+\gamma_r\left(\gamma_r-\sqrt{\Omega}\right)}{8\gamma_r\left(\gamma_r-\sqrt{\Omega}\right)\left(\gamma_r+\sqrt{\Omega}\right)}=\\
        &=\frac{\cancel{\gamma_r^2}\cancel{+\gamma_r\sqrt{\Omega}}\cancel{-2\gamma_r^2}+2\Omega\cancel{+\gamma_r^2}\cancel{-\gamma_r\sqrt{\Omega}}}{8\gamma_r\left(\gamma_r^2-\Omega\right)}=\frac{\Omega}{4\gamma_r\left(\gamma_r^2-\Omega\right)}
    \end{align*}

    Substituting $\omega_0^2=\gamma_r^2-\Omega$ the limit will be:

    \begin{equation*}
        \left\langle x(t)^2\right\rangle \ \xrightarrow{t\to\infty} \ \frac{2\gamma k_BT}{m^2\cancel{\Omega}}\cdot\frac{\cancel{\Omega}}{4\gamma_r\omega_0^2}=\frac{\gamma k_BT}{2m^2\gamma_r\omega_0^2}
    \end{equation*}

    Finally, being $\frac{\gamma}{2m}=\gamma_r$:

    \begin{equation}
        \left\langle x(t)^2\right\rangle \ \xrightarrow{t\to\infty} \ \frac{\cancel{\gamma_r}k_BT}{m\cancel{\gamma_r}\omega_0^2}=\frac{k_BT}{m\omega_0^2}=\frac{k_BT}{k}
    \end{equation}

    We thus understand that the variance is directly proportional to the temperature of the system, which feeds the stochasticity, and inversely proportional to the constant $k$ that modulates the harmonic oscillation due to the external potential.

    \item **Critical damping**

    Let's calculate $\left\langle x^{om}\left(t_1\right)x^{om}\left(t_2\right)\right\rangle$:

    \begin{equation*}
        \left(A+Bt_1\right)\left(A+Bt_2\right)e^{-\gamma_r\left(t_1+t_2\right)}=\left[A^2+AB\left(t_1+t_2\right)+B^2t_1t_2\right]e^{-\gamma_r\left(t_1+t_2\right)}
    \end{equation*}

    Let's calculate $\left\langle x^s\left(t_1\right)x^s\left(t_2\right)\right\rangle$:

    \begin{align*}
        &\frac{2\gamma k_BT}{m^2}\int_0^{\min\left(t_1,t_2\right)}e^{-\gamma_r\left(t_1-t'+t_2-t'\right)}\left(t_1-t'\right)\left(t_2-t'\right)dt'=\\
        &=\frac{2\gamma k_BT}{m^2}\int_0^{\min\left(t_1,t_2\right)}e^{-\gamma_r\left(t_1+t_2-2t'\right)}\left[t_1t_2-\left(t_1+t_2\right)t'+t'^2\right]dt'
    \end{align*}

    We can calculate the variance by setting $t_1=t_2=t$:

    \begin{equation*}
        \left\langle x(t)^2\right\rangle=\left(A+Bt\right)^2e^{-2\gamma_rt}+\frac{2\gamma k_BT}{m^2}\int_0^te^{-2\gamma_r(t-t')}(t-t')^2dt'
    \end{equation*}

    In the limit for $t\to\infty$ the decreasing exponential will dominate in the first term, which will send everything to zero. As for the second term, instead, we will have to explicitly solve the integral and then find the value at the limit; setting $\tau=t-t'$, we find:

    \begin{align*}
        -\int_t^0e^{-2\gamma_r\tau}\tau^2d\tau&=\int_0^te^{-2\gamma_r\tau}\tau^2d\tau=-\frac{1}{2\gamma_r}e^{-2\gamma_r\tau}\tau^2\bigg|_0^t+\frac{2}{2\gamma_r}\int_0^te^{-2\gamma_r\tau}\tau d\tau=\\
        &=-\frac{1}{2\gamma_r}e^{-2\gamma_rt}t^2-\frac{1}{2\gamma_r^2}e^{-2\gamma_r\tau}\tau\bigg|_0^t+\frac{1}{2\gamma_r^2}\int_0^te^{-2\gamma_r\tau}d\tau=\\
        &=-\frac{1}{2\gamma_r}e^{-2\gamma_rt}t^2-\frac{1}{2\gamma_r^2}e^{-2\gamma_rt}t-\frac{1}{4\gamma_r^3}e^{-2\gamma_r\tau}\bigg|_0^t=\\
        &=-\frac{1}{2\gamma_r}\left(t^2+\frac{1}{\gamma_r}t+\frac{1}{4\gamma_r^3}\right)e^{-2\gamma_rt}+\frac{1}{4\gamma_r^3}
    \end{align*}

    At the limit the exponential goes to zero, therefore

    \begin{equation*}
        \left\langle x(t)^2\right\rangle \ \xrightarrow{t\to\infty} \ \frac{2\gamma k_BT}{m^2}\cdot\frac{1}{4\gamma_r^3}=\frac{\gamma k_BT}{2m^2\gamma_r^3}
    \end{equation*}

    Being $\gamma_r=\frac{\gamma}{2m}$:

    \begin{equation}
        \left\langle x(t)^2\right\rangle \ \xrightarrow{t\to\infty} \ \frac{8m^3\gamma k_BT}{2m^2\gamma^3}=\frac{4mk_BT}{\gamma^2}
    \end{equation}

    \item **Underdamped case**

    Let's calculate $\left\langle x^{om}\left(t_1\right)x^{om}\left(t_2\right)\right\rangle$:

    \footnotesize

    \begin{equation*}
        \left[A\cos\left(\sqrt{\abs{\Omega}}t_1\right)+B\sin\left(\sqrt{\abs{\Omega}}t_1\right)\right]\left[A\cos\left(\sqrt{\abs{\Omega}}t_2\right)+B\sin\left(\sqrt{\abs{\Omega}}t_2\right)\right]e^{-\gamma_r\left(t_1+t_2\right)}
    \end{equation*}

    \normalsize

    Let's use the notation $\sqrt{\Omega}t=\tau$:

    \begin{equation*}
        \left[A\cos\left(\tau_1\right)+B\sin\left(\tau_1\right)\right]\left[A\cos\left(\tau_2\right)+B\sin\left(\tau_2\right)\right]e^{-\gamma_r\left(t_1+t_2\right)}
    \end{equation*}

    Let's make the trigonometric terms given by the products explicit:

    \begin{equation*}
        \begin{cases}
            \cos\left(\tau_1\right)\cos\left(\tau_2\right)=\frac{1}{2}\left[\cos\left(\tau_1-\tau_2\right)+\cos\left(\tau_1+\tau_2\right)\right]\\
            \cos\left(\tau_1\right)\sin\left(\tau_2\right)+\sin\left(\tau_1\right)\cos\left(\tau_2\right)=\sin\left(\tau_1+\tau_2\right)\\
            \sin\left(\tau_1\right)\sin\left(\tau_2\right)=\frac{1}{2}\left[\cos\left(\tau_1-\tau_2\right)-\cos\left(\tau_1+\tau_2\right)\right]
        \end{cases}
    \end{equation*}

    Let's substitute, without momentarily considering the exponential:

    \scriptsize

    \begin{align*}
        &\frac{A^2}{2}\left[\cos\left(\tau_1-\tau_2\right)+\cos\left(\tau_1+\tau_2\right)\right]+AB\sin\left(\tau_1+\tau_2\right)+\frac{B^2}{2}\left[\cos\left(\tau_1-\tau_2\right)-\cos\left(\tau_1+\tau_2\right)\right]=\\
        &=\frac{A^2+B^2}{2}\cos\left(\tau_1-\tau_2\right)+\frac{A^2-B^2}{2}\cos\left(\tau_1+\tau_2\right)+AB\sin\left(\tau_1+\tau_2\right)
    \end{align*}

    \normalsize

    Substituting in the initial formula:

    \tiny

    \begin{equation*}
        \left[\frac{A^2+B^2}{2}\cos\left(\sqrt{\abs{\Omega}}\left(t_1-t_2\right)\right)+\frac{A^2-B^2}{2}\cos\left(\sqrt{\abs{\Omega}}\left(t_1+t_2\right)\right)+AB\sin\left(\sqrt{\abs{\Omega}}\left(t_1+t_2\right)\right)\right]e^{-\gamma_r\left(t_1+t_2\right)}
    \end{equation*}

    \normalsize

    Let's now calculate $\left\langle x^s\left(t_1\right)x^s\left(t_2\right)\right\rangle$:

    \footnotesize

    \begin{align*}
        &\frac{2\gamma k_BT}{m^2\abs{\Omega}}\int_0^{\min\left(t_1,t_2\right)}e^{-\gamma_r\left(t_1-t'+t_2-t'\right)}\sin\left(\sqrt{\abs{\Omega}}\left(t_1-t'\right)\right)\sin\left(\sqrt{\abs{\Omega}}\left(t_2-t'\right)\right)dt'=\\
        &=\frac{2\gamma k_BT}{m^2\abs{\Omega}}\int_0^{\min\left(t_1,t_2\right)}e^{-\gamma_r\left(t_1+t_2-2t'\right)}\sin\left(\sqrt{\abs{\Omega}}\left(t_1-t'\right)\right)\sin\left(\sqrt{\abs{\Omega}}\left(t_2-t'\right)\right)dt'
    \end{align*}

    \normalsize

    We can calculate the variance by setting $t_1=t_2=t$:

    \small

    \begin{align*}
        \left\langle x(t)^2\right\rangle=&\left[\frac{A^2+B^2}{2}+\frac{A^2-B^2}{2}\cos\left(2\sqrt{\abs{\Omega}}t\right)+AB\sin\left(2\sqrt{\abs{\Omega}}t\right)\right]e^{-2\gamma_rt}+\\
        &+\frac{2\gamma k_BT}{m^2\Omega}\int_0^te^{-2\gamma_r\left(t-t'\right)}\sin^2\left(\sqrt{\abs{\Omega}}\left(t-t'\right)\right)dt'
    \end{align*}

    \normalsize

    In the limit for $t\to\infty$ the first term decays exponentially to zero, while as for the second term we will have to solve the integral; setting $\tau=t-t'$, we will have:

    \begin{equation*}
        -\int_t^0e^{-2\gamma_r\tau}\sin^2\left(\sqrt{\abs{\Omega}}\tau\right)d\tau=\int_0^te^{-2\gamma_r\tau}\sin^2\left(\sqrt{\abs{\Omega}}\tau\right)d\tau
    \end{equation*}

    Let's proceed with the calculation remembering the trigonometric relation $\sin^2x=\frac{1-\cos(2x)}{2}$:

    \begin{align*}
        \int_0^te^{-2\gamma_r\tau}\sin^2\left(\sqrt{\abs{\Omega}}\tau\right)d\tau&=\int_0^te^{-2\gamma_r\tau}\cdot\frac{1-\cos\left(2\sqrt{\abs{\Omega}}\tau\right)}{2}d\tau=\\
        &=\frac{1}{2}\int_0^te^{-2\gamma_r\tau}d\tau-\frac{1}{2}\int_0^te^{-2\gamma_r\tau}\cos\left(2\sqrt{\abs{\Omega}}\tau\right)d\tau
    \end{align*}

    Let's denote the first integral by $I_1$ and the second by $I_2$ and calculate them individually; let's start with the first:

    \begin{equation*}
        I_1=\frac{1}{2}\int_0^te^{-2\gamma_r\tau}d\tau=-\frac{1}{4\gamma_r}e^{-2\gamma_r\tau}\bigg|_0^t=-\frac{e^{-2\gamma_rt}-1}{4\gamma_r}=\frac{1-e^{-2\gamma_rt}}{4\gamma_r}
    \end{equation*}

    For the second, instead, let's integrate by parts:

    \begin{align*}
        I_2&=-\frac{1}{2}\int_0^te^{-2\gamma_r\tau}\cos\left(2\sqrt{\abs{\Omega}}\tau\right)d\tau=\\
        &=\frac{1}{4\gamma_r}e^{-2\gamma_r\tau}\cos\left(2\sqrt{\abs{\Omega}}\tau\right)\bigg|_0^t+\frac{\sqrt{\abs{\Omega}}}{2\gamma_r}\int_0^te^{-2\gamma_r\tau}\sin\left(2\sqrt{\abs{\Omega}}\tau\right)d\tau=\\
        &=\frac{e^{-2\gamma_rt}\cos\left(2\sqrt{\abs{\Omega}}t\right)-1}{4\gamma_r}-\frac{\sqrt{\abs{\Omega}}}{4\gamma_r^2}e^{-2\gamma_r\tau}\sin\left(2\sqrt{\abs{\Omega}}\tau\right)\bigg|_0^t+\frac{\abs{\Omega}}{\cancel{2}\gamma_r^2}\left(-\cancel{2}I_2\right)
    \end{align*}

    Bringing the last term to the first member:

    \begin{equation*}
        \left(1+\frac{\abs{\Omega}}{\gamma_r^2}\right)I_2=\frac{e^{-2\gamma_rt}\cos\left(2\sqrt{\abs{\Omega}}t\right)-1}{4\gamma_r}-\frac{\sqrt{\abs{\Omega}}e^{-2\gamma_rt}\sin\left(2\sqrt{\abs{\Omega}}t\right)}{4\gamma_r^2}
    \end{equation*}

    \begin{equation*}
        \left(\frac{\gamma_r^2+\abs{\Omega}}{\gamma_r^2}\right)I_2=\frac{e^{-2\gamma_rt}\cos\left(2\sqrt{\abs{\Omega}}t\right)-1}{4\gamma_r}-\frac{\sqrt{\abs{\Omega}}e^{-2\gamma_rt}\sin\left(2\sqrt{\abs{\Omega}}t\right)}{4\gamma_r^2}
    \end{equation*}

    \begin{equation*}
        I_2=\frac{\gamma_r\left[e^{-2\gamma_rt}\cos\left(2\sqrt{\abs{\Omega}}t\right)-1\right]}{4\left(\gamma_r^2+\abs{\Omega}\right)}-\frac{\sqrt{\abs{\Omega}}e^{-2\gamma_rt}\sin\left(2\sqrt{\abs{\Omega}}t\right)}{4\left(\gamma_r^2+\abs{\Omega}\right)}
    \end{equation*}

    In the limit for $t\to\infty$:

    \begin{equation*}
        I_1+I_2 \ \xrightarrow{t\to\infty} \ \frac{1}{4\gamma_r}-\frac{\gamma_r}{4\left(\gamma_r^2+\abs{\Omega}\right)}=\frac{\cancel{\gamma_r^2}+\abs{\Omega}\cancel{-\gamma_r^2}}{4\gamma_r\left(\gamma_r^2+\abs{\Omega}\right)}
    \end{equation*}

    It follows that:

    \begin{equation*}
        \left\langle x(t)^2\right\rangle \ \xrightarrow{t\to\infty} \ \frac{2\gamma k_BT}{m^2\cancel{\abs{\Omega}}}\cdot\frac{\cancel{\abs{\Omega}}}{4\gamma_r\left(\gamma_r^2+\abs{\Omega}\right)}=\frac{\gamma k_BT}{2m^2\gamma_r\left(\gamma_r^2+\abs{\Omega}\right)}
    \end{equation*}

    Substituting $\frac{\gamma}{2m}=\gamma_r$:

    \begin{equation*}
        \left\langle x(t)^2\right\rangle \ \xrightarrow{t\to\infty} \ \frac{\cancel{\gamma_r}k_BT}{m\cancel{\gamma_r}\left(\gamma_r^2+\abs{\Omega}\right)}
    \end{equation*}

    Since $\abs{\Omega}=-\Omega=\omega_0^2-\gamma_r^2$:

    \begin{equation*}
        \left\langle x(t)^2\right\rangle \ \xrightarrow{t\to\infty} \ \frac{k_BT}{m\left(\cancel{\gamma_r}+\omega_0^2\cancel{-\gamma_r^2}\right)}=\frac{k_BT}{m\omega_0^2}=\frac{k_BT}{k}
    \end{equation*}

    which is the same result obtained in the overdamped case.
\end{enumerate}
