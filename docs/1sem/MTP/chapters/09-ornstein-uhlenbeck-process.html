11 Ornstein-Uhlenbeck Process

To conclude the study of stochastic processes, which began with the analysis of diffusion processes, let’s take as an example a model that we will study with the various tools we have learned in the past chapters. The process in question is the Ornstein-Uhlenbeck process, which, to be honest, we have already introduced in exercise (ex:processo-di-ornstein-uhlenbeck) following the introduction of the Wiener process.

11.1 Sample trajectory

Formally, the process is defined by the following differential equation:

\[\begin{equation} \begin{cases} dx=-\mu xdt+\sigma dB\\ x(0)=x_0 \end{cases} \end{equation}\]

To find a general solution, it is useful to define the following function:

\[\begin{equation} y(t)=e^{\mu t}x(t) \end{equation}\]

where \(x(t)\) is the solution of the equation. The reason why this is convenient is soon told by applying the chain rule (equation (eq:sde-chain-rule-ito)), having set \(\mu(x,t)=-\mu x\) and \(\sigma(x,t)=\sigma\) for consistency with the Langevin equation:

\[\begin{align*} dy&=\left(\frac{\partial y}{\partial t}+\frac{\partial y}{\partial x}\mu(x,t)+\frac{1}{2}\frac{\partial^2y}{\partial x^2}\sigma^2\right)dt+\frac{\partial y}{\partial x}\sigma(x,t) dB=\\ &=\left(\cancel{\mu e^{\mu t}x}\cancel{-\mu xe^{\mu t}}+0\right)dt+\sigma e^{\mu t}dB \end{align*}\]

The remaining equation is easy to integrate:

\[\begin{equation*} dy=\sigma e^{\mu t}dB \ \Longrightarrow \ \ y(t)=y(0)+\sigma\int_0^te^{\mu t'}dB(t') \end{equation*}\]

Returning to the expression in \(x(t)\):

\[\begin{equation*} e^{\mu t}x(t)=e^{\mu\cdot0}x(0)+\sigma\int_0^te^{\mu t'}dB(t')=x_0+\sigma\int_0^te^{\mu t'}dB(t') \end{equation*}\]

We thus find the explicit form of the solution:

\[\begin{equation} \boxed{x(t)=x_0e^{-\mu t}+\sigma e^{-\mu t}\int_0^te^{\mu t'}dB(t')} \end{equation}\]

Calculate the solution of the following stochastic differential equation:

\begin{equation*}
    \begin{cases}
        dx=(\beta-\mu x)dt+\sigma dB\\           x(0)=x_0
    \end{cases}
\end{equation*}

We will have to follow the same procedure just carried out, with the only difference that this time $\mu(x,t)=-\mu x+\beta$. In this way the coefficient of $dt$ will not be canceled, since a surviving contribution equal to $\beta e^{\mu t}$ will remain:

\begin{equation*}
    dy=\beta e^{\mu t}dt+\sigma e^{\mu t}dB
\end{equation*}

Integrating both members:

\begin{equation*}
    y(t)=y(0)+\beta\int_0^te^{\mu t'}dt'+\sigma\int_0^te^{\mu t'}dB(t')
\end{equation*}

Solution. Let’s solve the first integral:

\begin{equation*}
    \int_0^te^{\mu t'}dt'=\frac{1}{\mu}e^{\mu t'}\bigg|_0^t=\frac{1}{\mu}\left(e^{\mu t}-1\right)
\end{equation*}

Let's now substitute $y(t)=e^{\mu t}x(t)$ and return to the complete solution:

\begin{equation*}
    e^{\mu t}x(t)=e^{\mu\cdot 0}x(0)+\frac{\beta}{\mu}\left(e^{\mu t}-1\right)+\sigma\int_0^te^{\mu t'}dB(t')
\end{equation*}

Let's therefore multiply both members by $e^{-\mu t}$ to obtain the final solution:

\begin{equation*}
    x(t)=x_0e^{-\mu t}+e^{-\mu t}\cdot\frac{\beta}{\mu}\left(e^{\mu t}-1\right)+\sigma e^{-\mu t}\int_0^te^{\mu t'}dB(t')
\end{equation*}

that is

\begin{equation*}
    x(t)=x_0e^{-\mu t}+\frac{\beta}{\mu}\left(1-e^{-\mu t}\right)+\sigma e^{-\mu t}\int_0^te^{\mu t'}dB(t')
\end{equation*}

11.1.1 Variance

We know that one of the main aspects of a realization of a stochastic process is its variance, in order to describe how fluctuations tend to change over time.

Let’s start from the calculation of the average value of \(x(t)^2\), which is obtained starting from the series expansion of the differential of \(x\) stopped at the second order, since - as already repeated several times - we have \(dB^2=dt\):

\[\begin{equation*} d\left(x^2\right)=\frac{d\left(x^2\right)}{dx}dx+\frac{1}{2}\frac{d^2\left(x^2\right)}{dx^2}dx^2=2xdx+\frac{1}{\cancel{2}}\cdot\cancel{2}dx^2 \end{equation*}\]

Let’s substitute the expression for \(dx\) given by the SDE:

\[\begin{equation*} d\left(x^2\right)=2x\left(-\mu xdt+\sigma dB\right)+\left(-\mu xdt+\sigma dB\right)^2 \end{equation*}\]

Of the square, only \(\sigma^2dB^2=\sigma^2dt\) remains:

\[\begin{equation*} d\left(x^2\right)=-2\mu x^2dt+2\sigma xdB+\sigma^2dt=\left(-2\mu x^2+\sigma^2\right)dt+2\sigma xdB \end{equation*}\]

Let’s consider the expected value on both sides:

\[\begin{equation*} d\left\langle x^2\right\rangle=\left(-2\mu\left\langle x^2\right\rangle+\sigma^2\right)dt+2\sigma\langle xdB\rangle \end{equation*}\]

According to the Itô convention \(\langle xdB\rangle=\langle x\rangle\cancel{\langle dB\rangle}=0\), from which the differential equation follows

\[\begin{equation} \frac{d}{dt}\left\langle x^2\right\rangle=-2\mu\left\langle x^2\right\rangle+\sigma^2 \end{equation}\]

This is a non-homogeneous ordinary differential equation. To find a solution we must first solve the associated homogeneous equation:

\[\begin{equation*} \frac{d}{dt}\left\langle x^2\right\rangle=-2\mu\left\langle x^2\right\rangle \ \Longrightarrow \ \ \left\langle x(t)^2\right\rangle=ke^{-2\mu t} \end{equation*}\]

To find the solution to the non-homogeneous equation ((eq:processo-u-o-varianza-equazione-non-omogenea)) we will have to add a constant contribution \(c\) to the solution found, such that

\[\begin{equation*} \cancel{\frac{d}{dt}c}=-2\mu c+\sigma^2 \ \iff \ 2\mu c=\sigma^2 \ \iff \ c=\frac{\sigma^2}{2\mu} \end{equation*}\]

Summing the two contributions, we will find:

\[\begin{equation*} \left\langle x(t)^2\right\rangle=ke^{-2\mu t}+\frac{\sigma^2}{2\mu} \end{equation*}\]

It remains to find the constant \(k\) through the initial condition (\(\left\langle x(0)^2\right\rangle=x_0^2\)):

\[\begin{equation*} \left\langle x(0)^2\right\rangle=k+\frac{\sigma^2}{2\mu}=x_0^2 \ \Longrightarrow \ k=x_0^2-\frac{\sigma^2}{2\mu} \end{equation*}\]

Ultimately:

\[\begin{equation} \left\langle x(t)^2\right\rangle=\left(x_0^2-\frac{\sigma^2}{2\mu}\right)e^{-2\mu t}+\frac{\sigma^2}{2\mu} \end{equation}\]

For the variance we must find \(\langle x(t)\rangle\), which is easily found starting from (eq:processo-u-o-soluzione-generale):

\[\begin{equation*} \langle x(t)\rangle=x_0e^{-\mu t}+\sigma e^{-\mu t}\left\langle\int_0^te^{\mu t'}dB(t')\right\rangle=x_0e^{-\mu t}+\sigma e^{-\mu t}\int_0^t\left\langle e^{\mu t'}\right\rangle\cancel{\langle dB(t')\rangle} \end{equation*}\]

We can now calculate the variance associated with the process:

\[\begin{align*} Var[x(t)]&=\left\langle x(t)^2\right\rangle-\langle x(t)\rangle^2=\left(x_0^2-\frac{\sigma^2}{2\mu}\right)e^{-2\mu t}+\frac{\sigma^2}{2\mu}-x_0^2e^{-\mu t}=\\ &=\left(\cancel{x_0^2}-\frac{\sigma^2}{2\mu}\cancel{-x_0^2}\right)e^{-\mu t}+\frac{\sigma^2}{2\mu} \end{align*}\]

that is

\[\begin{equation} \boxed{Var[x(t)]=\frac{\sigma^2}{2\mu}\left(1-e^{-\mu t}\right)} \end{equation}\]

This is a function that is zero for \(t=0\) and progressively increasing, stabilizing at the value \(\frac{\sigma^2}{2\mu}\) for large \(t\).

11.1.2 Connected auto-correlation

Another important aspect is the correlation of the trajectory with itself at different instants of time, called auto-correlation:

\[\begin{equation} \left\langle x\left(t_1\right)x\left(t_2\right)\right\rangle \end{equation}\]

If we remove the effects given by the global mean, we obtain what is called the connected auto-correlation:

\[\begin{equation} c\left(t_1,t_2\right)=\left\langle x\left(t_1\right)x\left(t_2\right)\right\rangle-\left\langle x\left(t_1\right)\right\rangle\left\langle x\left(t_2\right)\right\rangle \end{equation}\]

Let’s calculate the first term by substituting (eq:processo-u-o-soluzione-generale):

\[\begin{equation*} \left\langle x\left(t_1\right)x\left(t_2\right)\right\rangle=\left\langle\left(x_0e^{-\mu t_1}+\sigma e^{-\mu t_1}\int_0^{t_1}e^{\mu t'}dB(t')\right)\left(x_0e^{-\mu t_2}+\sigma e^{-\mu t_2}\int_0^{t_2}e^{\mu t'}dB(t')\right)\right\rangle \end{equation*}\]

From the products, the common factor \(e^{-\mu\left(t_1+t_2\right)}\) emerges, which we can take out:

\[\begin{align*} \left\langle x\left(t_1\right)x\left(t_2\right)\right\rangle=e^{-\mu\left(t_1+t_2\right)}\bigg\langle&x_0^2+\\ &+x_0\sigma\int_0^{t_1}e^{\mu t'}dB(t')+\\ &+x_0\sigma\int_0^{t_2}e^{\mu t'}dB(t')+\\ &+\sigma^2\int_0^{t_1}e^{\mu t'}dB(t')\int_0^{t_2}e^{\mu t'}dB(t')\bigg\rangle \end{align*}\]

Distributing the average value over the four terms, the two central ones will be zero, since \(\langle dB(t)\rangle\) is so. What remains is:

\[\begin{equation} \left\langle x\left(t_1\right)x\left(t_2\right)\right\rangle=e^{-\mu\left(t_1+t_2\right)}\left(x_0^2+\sigma^2\left\langle\int_0^{t_1}e^{\mu t'}dB(t')\int_0^{t_2}e^{\mu t'}dB(t')\right\rangle\right) \end{equation}\]

To find the remaining expected value, it is useful to calculate more generally

\[\begin{equation*} \left\langle\int_0^{t_1}f(t')dB(t')\int_0^{t_2}g(t')dB(t')\right\rangle \end{equation*}\]

Let’s assume that \(t_2>t_1\) and let’s discretize the interval \(\left[0,t_2\right]\) in the following way:

\[\begin{equation*} \left\{t_0=0,t_1,\dots,t_{m-1},t_m=\boldsymbol{t_1},t_{m+1},\dots,t_{n-1},t_n=\boldsymbol{t_2}\right\} \end{equation*}\]

At this point, we can discretize the integrals as follows:

\[\begin{equation*} \left\langle\sum_{i=1}^mf_{i-1}\Delta B_i\sum_{j=1}^ng_{j-1}\Delta B_j\right\rangle \end{equation*}\]

Let’s split the second summation into the intervals \([1,m]\) and \([m+1,n]\):

\[\begin{align*} &\left\langle\sum_{i=1}^mf_{i-1}\Delta B_i\left[\sum_{j=1}^mg_{j-1}\Delta B_j+\sum_{j=m+1}^ng_{j-1}\Delta B_j\right]\right\rangle=\\ &=\left\langle\sum_{i=1}^mf_{i-1}\Delta B_i\sum_{j=1}^mg_{j-1}\Delta B_j\right\rangle+\left\langle\sum_{i=1}^mf_{i-1}\Delta B_i\sum_{j=m+1}^ng_{j-1}\Delta B_j\right\rangle \end{align*}\]

The summations of the second term refer to different intervals, so they are uncorrelated quantities:

\[\begin{align*} \left\langle\sum_{i=1}^mf_{i-1}\Delta B_i\sum_{j=m+1}^ng_{j-1}\Delta B_j\right\rangle&=\left\langle\sum_{i=1}^mf_{i-1}\Delta B_i\right\rangle\left\langle\sum_{j=m+1}^ng_{j-1}\Delta B_j\right\rangle=\\ &=\sum_{i=1}^m\left\langle f_{i-1}\right\rangle\cancel{\left\langle\Delta B_i\right\rangle}\sum_{j=m+1}^n\left\langle g_{j-1}\right\rangle\cancel{\left\langle\Delta B_j\right\rangle}=0 \end{align*}\]

What remains is therefore the first term, where the contributions of the summation can be divided into those in which \(i=j\) and those in which \(i\neq j\):

\[\begin{align*} \left\langle\sum_{i=1}^m\sum_{j=1}^mf_{i-1}g_{j-1}\Delta B_i\Delta B_j\right\rangle&=\left\langle\sum_{i=1}^mf_{i-1}g_{i-1}\Delta B_i^2\right\rangle+2\left\langle\sum_{i>j}f_{i-1}g_{j-1}\Delta B_i\Delta B_j\right\rangle=\\ &=\sum_{i=1}^m\left\langle f_{i-1}g_{i-1}\right\rangle\left\langle\Delta B_i^2\right\rangle+2\sum_{i>j}\left\langle f_{i-1}g_{j-1}\right\rangle\cancel{\left\langle\Delta B_i\right\rangle}\cancel{\left\langle\Delta B_j\right\rangle}=\\ &=\sum_{i=1}^m\left\langle f_{i-1}g_{i-1}\right\rangle\Delta t_i \end{align*}\]

We thus find that, in the limit:

\[\begin{equation*} \sum_{i=1}^m\left\langle f_{i-1}g_{i-1}\right\rangle\Delta t_i \ \longrightarrow \ \int_0^{t_1}\langle f(t')g(t')\rangle dt' \end{equation*}\]

If we had initially assumed \(t_1>t_2\) we would have obtained the same result, but with the upper limit of integration equal to \(t_2\). It follows that

\[\begin{equation*} \left\langle\int_0^{t_1}f(t')dB(t')\int_0^{t_2}g(t')dB(t')\right\rangle=\int_0^{\min\left(t_1,t_2\right)}\langle f(t')g(t')\rangle dt' \end{equation*}\]

Let’s go back to (eq:processo-u-o-auto-correlazione-calcolo-1) and substitute the result found:

\[\begin{equation*} \left\langle x\left(t_1\right)x\left(t_2\right)\right\rangle=e^{-\mu\left(t_1+t_2\right)}\left(x_0^2+\sigma^2\int_0^{\min\left(t_1t_2\right)}e^{2\mu t'}dt'\right) \end{equation*}\]

Let’s solve the integral:

\[\begin{align*} \left\langle x\left(t_1\right)x\left(t_2\right)\right\rangle&=e^{-\mu\left(t_1+t_2\right)}\left(x_0^2+\frac{\sigma^2}{2\mu}e^{2\mu t'}\bigg|_0^{\min\left(t_1,t_2\right)}\right)=\\ &=e^{-\mu\left(t_1+t_2\right)}\left[x_0^2+\frac{\sigma^2}{2\mu}\left(e^{2\mu\min\left(t_1,t_2\right)}-1\right)\right] \end{align*}\]

Let’s substitute in (eq:processo-u-o-auto-correlazione-connessa-definizione), remembering that \(\langle x(t)\rangle=x_0e^{-\mu t}\):

\[\begin{align*} c\left(t_1,t_2\right)&=e^{-\mu\left(t_1+t_2\right)}\left[x_0^2+\frac{\sigma^2}{2\mu}\left(e^{2\mu\min\left(t_1,t_2\right)}-1\right)\right]-x_0e^{-\mu t_1}\cdot x_0e^{-\mu t_2}=\\ &=\cancel{x_0^2e^{-\mu\left(t_1+t_2\right)}}+\frac{\sigma^2}{2\mu}\left(e^{2\mu\min\left(t_1,t_2\right)}-1\right)e^{-\mu\left(t_1+t_2\right)}\cancel{-x_0^2e^{-\mu\left(t_1+t_2\right)}} \end{align*}\]

We find:

\[\begin{equation*} c\left(t_1,t_2\right)=\frac{\sigma^2}{2\mu}\left[e^{\mu\left(2\min\left(t_1,t_2\right)-t_1-t_2\right)}-e^{-\mu\left(t_1+t_2\right)}\right] \end{equation*}\]

Let’s evaluate the two cases:

\[\begin{align*} &\min\left(t_1,t_2\right)=t_1 \ \Longrightarrow \ 2t_1-t_1-t_2=t_1-t_2\\ &\min\left(t_1,t_2\right)=t_2 \ \Longrightarrow \ 2t_2-t_1-t_2=-t_1+t_2 \end{align*}\]

This means that the exponent \(2\min\left(t_1,t_2\right)-t_1-t_2\) can be rewritten as \(-\abs{t_1-t_2}\):

\[\begin{equation} \boxed{c\left(t_1,t_2\right)=\frac{\sigma^2}{2\mu}\left[e^{-\mu\abs{t_1-t_2}}-e^{-\mu\left(t_1+t_2\right)}\right]} \end{equation}\]

We can find an even more general formula if we assume that at the initial instant the system is not entirely concentrated at \(x_0\) but is distributed according to a function \(p\left(x_0\right)\). In this case, we have that \(\langle x(t)\rangle=\left\langle x_0\right\rangle e^{-\mu t}\), so by substituting the results in (eq:processo-u-o-auto-correlazione-connessa-definizione) we will have:

\[\begin{align*} c\left(t_1,t_2\right)&=\left\langle x_0^2\right\rangle e^{-\mu\left(t_1+t_2\right)}+\frac{\sigma^2}{2\mu}\left[e^{-\mu\abs{t_1-t_2}}-e^{-\mu\left(t_1+t_2\right)}\right]-\left\langle x_0\right\rangle^2e^{-\mu\left(t_1+t_2\right)}=\\ &=\left(\left\langle x_0^2\right\rangle-\left\langle x_0\right\rangle^2\right)e^{-\mu\left(t_1+t_2\right)}+\frac{\sigma^2}{2\mu}\left[e^{-\mu\abs{t_1-t_2}}-e^{-\mu\left(t_1+t_2\right)}\right] \end{align*}\]

From which:

\[\begin{equation} \boxed{c\left(t_1,t_2\right)=Var\left(x_0\right)e^{-\mu\left(t_1+t_2\right)}+\frac{\sigma^2}{2\mu}\left[e^{-\mu\abs{t_1-t_2}}-e^{-\mu\left(t_1+t_2\right)}\right]} \end{equation}\]

We note that, in the limit for \(t_1,t_2\to\infty\) but keeping the difference \(t_1-t_2\) constant, we find the stationary connected auto-correlation, since it is independent of the individual values \(t_1\) and \(t_2\) but only of their difference in modulus:

\[\begin{equation} \boxed{c_{stat}\left(t_1,t_2\right)=\frac{\sigma^2}{2\mu}e^{-\mu\abs{t_1-t_2}}} \end{equation}\]

11.2 Propagator

Another fundamental characteristic of a stochastic process is its propagator, which tells us how the pdf evolves over time.

It can be found starting from the Fokker-Planck equation ((eq:fokker-planck-equazione)), which in our case is written as

\[\begin{equation} \frac{\partial p}{\partial t}=-\frac{\partial}{\partial x}(-\mu xp)+\frac{1}{2}\frac{\partial^2}{\partial x^2}\left(\sigma^2p\right)=\mu\frac{\partial}{\partial x}(xp)+\frac{\sigma^2}{2}\frac{\partial^2p}{\partial x^2} \end{equation}\]

To find the solution, it is convenient to apply the Fourier transform to both members:

\[\begin{equation} \red{\mathcal{F}\left[\frac{\partial p}{\partial t}\right]}=\mu\orange{\mathcal{F}\left[\frac{\partial}{\partial x}(xp)\right]}+\frac{\sigma^2}{2}\green{\mathcal{F}\left[\frac{\partial^2p}{\partial x^2}\right]} \end{equation}\]

Let’s calculate the various transformations individually.

Let’s substitute the results obtained in (eq:processo-u-o-propagatore-equazione-differenziale-fourier):

\[\begin{equation*} \frac{\partial\tilde{p}}{\partial t}=-\mu\omega\frac{\partial\tilde{p}}{\partial\omega}-\frac{\sigma^2}{2}\omega^2\tilde{p}(\omega,t) \end{equation*}\]

This is a homogeneous first-order partial differential equation, in which \(\tilde{p}(\omega,t)\) is derived once with respect to \(t\) and another time with respect to \(t\):

\[\begin{equation} \frac{\partial\tilde{p}}{\partial t}+\mu\omega\frac{\partial\tilde{p}}{\partial\omega}+\frac{\sigma^2}{2}\omega^2\tilde{p}(\omega,t)=0 \end{equation}\]

To solve this equation we will use a method known as the method of characteristics, which makes use of an auxiliary variable \(s\) such as to redefine the function in the following way:

\[\begin{equation*} \tilde{p}(s)\equiv\tilde{p}(\omega(s),t(s)) \end{equation*}\]

By deriving with respect to \(s\) we obtain:

\[\begin{equation} \frac{d\tilde{p}}{ds}=\frac{\partial\tilde{p}}{\partial\omega}\frac{d\omega}{ds}+\frac{\partial\tilde{p}}{\partial t}\frac{dt}{ds} \end{equation}\]

Referring to equation (eq:processo-u-o-propagatore-equazione-differenziale-post-fourier), we note that it can be rewritten in the following system of differential equations:

\[\begin{equation} \begin{cases} \frac{d\omega}{ds}=\mu\omega\\ \frac{dt}{ds}=1\\ \frac{d\tilde{p}}{ds}+\frac{\sigma^2}{2}\omega^2\tilde{p}(s)=0 \end{cases} \end{equation}\]

We have thus passed from a partial differential equation to a system of ordinary differential equations. The method consists in identifying curves, also called characteristics, belonging to the space described by the coordinates \(\left(\omega,t,\tilde{p}\right)\) which are constrained on surfaces

\[\begin{equation} \begin{cases} u\left(\omega,t,\tilde{p}\right)=A\\ v\left(\omega,t,\tilde{p}\right)=B \end{cases} \end{equation}\]

where \(A\) and \(B\) are arbitrary constants.

The functions \(u\) and \(v\) are obtained directly from the differential equations written above. In particular, from the first we find:

\[\begin{equation*} \int\frac{d\omega}{\omega}=\mu\int ds \ \Longrightarrow \ \omega=\omega(0)e^{\mu s} \end{equation*}\]

From the third, instead:

\[\begin{equation*} \int\frac{d\tilde{p}}{\tilde{p}}=-\frac{\sigma^2}{2}\int\omega^2ds=-\frac{\sigma^2\omega(0)^2}{2}\int e^{2\mu s}ds \ \Longrightarrow \ \tilde{p}=\tilde{p}(0)e^{-\frac{\sigma^2\omega(0)^2}{4\mu}e^{2\mu s}} \end{equation*}\]

Since \(\omega(0)^2e^{2\mu s}=\omega^2\) then the two solutions are the following:

\[\begin{equation*} \begin{cases} \omega=\omega(0)e^{\mu s}\\ \tilde{p}=\tilde{p}(0)e^{-\frac{\sigma^2}{2\mu}\omega^2} \end{cases} \end{equation*}\]

From the second equation of the system (eq:processo-u-o-sistema-equazioni-differenziali), finally, it results:

\[\begin{equation*} \int dt=\int s \ \Longrightarrow \ t=t_0+s \ \iff \ s=t-t_0 \end{equation*}\]

This also means that \(\omega(s=0)=\omega\left(t=t_0\right)=\omega_0\), so finally by substituting:

\[\begin{equation} \begin{cases} \omega=\omega_0e^{\mu\left(t-t_0\right)}\\ \tilde{p}=\tilde{p}(0)e^{-\frac{\sigma^2}{4\mu}\omega^2} \end{cases} \end{equation}\]

As said, the constraints must be constant. For this reason, by isolating \(\omega_0\) from the first and \(\tilde{p}(0)\) from the second, we obtain:

\[\begin{equation*} \begin{cases} \omega_0=\omega e^{-\mu\left(t-t_0\right)}\\ \tilde{p}(0)=\tilde{p}e^{\frac{\sigma^2}{4\mu}\omega^2} \end{cases} \end{equation*}\]

By setting \(A=\omega_0\) and \(B=\tilde{p}(0)\) we have found the functions \(u\) and \(v\):

\[\begin{equation} \begin{cases} u\left(\omega,t,\tilde{p}\right)=\omega e^{-\mu\left(t-t_0\right)}\\ v\left(\omega,t,\tilde{p}\right)=\tilde{p}e^{\frac{\sigma^2}{4\mu}\omega^2} \end{cases} \end{equation}\]

It can be shown that \(u\) and \(v\) are related by the relation

\[\begin{equation} v=g(u) \end{equation}\]

where \(g\) is an unknown functional to be found. Substituting:

\[\begin{equation} \tilde{p}e^{\frac{\sigma^2}{4\mu}\omega^2}=g\left(\omega e^{-\mu\left(t-t_0\right)}\right) \ \Longrightarrow \ \tilde{p}(\omega,t)=e^{-\frac{\sigma^2}{4\mu}\omega^2}g\left(\omega e^{-\mu\left(t-t_0\right)}\right) \end{equation}\]

To find the functional \(g\) we must impose the initial condition:

\[\begin{equation} \tilde{p}(\omega,0)=e^{-\frac{\sigma^2}{4\mu}\omega^2}g(\omega) \ \Longrightarrow \ g(\omega)=\tilde{p}(\omega,0)e^{\frac{\sigma^2}{4\mu}\omega^2} \end{equation}\]

Assuming that the initial condition is \(p(x,0)=\delta\left(x-x_0\right)\), then \(\tilde{p}(\omega,0)\) is given by the Fourier transform:

\[\begin{equation*} \tilde{p}(\omega,0)=\int_{-\infty}^{+\infty}p(x,0)e^{i\omega x}dx=\int_{-\infty}^{+\infty}\delta\left(x-x_0\right)e^{i\omega x}dx=e^{i\omega x_0} \end{equation*}\]

Let’s substitute in (eq:processo-u-o-funzionale-g-condizione-iniziale):

\[\begin{equation} g(\omega)=e^{i\omega x_0}e^{\frac{\sigma^2}{4\mu}\omega^2}=e^{\frac{\sigma^2}{4\mu}\omega^2+ix_0\omega} \end{equation}\]

Having found the expression for \(g(\omega)\) we can finally obtain \(\tilde{p}(\omega,t)\) in its explicit form starting from (eq:processo-u-o-espression-tilde-p-funzione-di-g):

\[\begin{equation*} \tilde{p}(\omega,t)=e^{-\frac{\sigma^2}{4\mu}\omega^2}e^{\frac{\sigma^2}{4\mu}\omega^2e^{-2\mu\left(t-t_0\right)}+ix_0\omega e^{-\mu\left(t-t_0\right)}}=e^{\frac{\sigma^2}{4\mu}\left(1-e^{-2\mu\left(t-t_0\right)}\right)\omega^2+ix_0e^{-\mu\left(t-t_0\right)}\omega} \end{equation*}\]

The propagator \(p(x,t)\) is none other than the inverse transform of \(\tilde{p}(\omega,t)\):

\[\begin{align*} p(x,t)&=\frac{1}{2\pi}\int_{-\infty}^{+\infty}\tilde{p}(\omega,t)e^{-i\omega x}d\omega=\\ &=\frac{1}{2\pi}\int_{-\infty}^{+\infty}e^{\frac{\sigma^2}{4\mu}\left(1-e^{-2\mu\left(t-t_0\right)}\right)\omega^2+ix_0e^{-\mu\left(t-t_0\right)}\omega}e^{-ix\omega}d\omega=\\ &=\frac{1}{2\pi}\int_{-\infty}^{+\infty}e^{\frac{\sigma^2}{4\mu}\left(1-e^{-2\mu\left(t-t_0\right)}\right)\omega^2-i\left(x-x_0e^{-\mu\left(t-t_0\right)}\right)\omega}d\omega \end{align*}\]

We recognize the form of the Gaussian integral that we know how to solve:

\[\begin{equation*} p(x,t)=\frac{1}{2\pi}\sqrt{\frac{2\pi}{\frac{\sigma^2}{2\mu}\left(1-e^{-2\mu\left(t-t_0\right)}\right)}}e^{-\frac{\left(x-x_0e^{-\mu\left(t-t_0\right)}\right)^2}{2\cdot\frac{\sigma^2}{2\mu}\left(1-e^{-2\mu\left(t-t_0\right)}\right)}} \end{equation*}\]

Simplifying, we obtain the final form of the propagator:

\[\begin{equation} \boxed{p(x,t)=\sqrt{\frac{\mu}{\pi\sigma^2\left(1-e^{-2\mu\left(t-t_0\right)}\right)}}e^{-\frac{\mu\left(x-x_0e^{-\mu\left(t-t_0\right)}\right)^2}{\sigma^2\left(1-e^{-2\mu\left(t-t_0\right)}\right)}}} \end{equation}\]

In the stationary limit, for \(t\to\infty\), we find

\[\begin{equation} \boxed{p^{(st)}(x)=\sqrt{\frac{\mu}{\pi\sigma^2}}e^{-\frac{\mu x^2}{\sigma^2}}} \end{equation}\]

that is, a Gaussian centered at zero and with variance \(\frac{\sigma^2}{\mu}\).

The general formula was given by (eq:fokker-planck-soluzione-all-equilibrio-forma-finale), and we note that by substituting \(x_{min}=0\), \(\mu(x)=-\mu x\) and \(\sigma(x)=\sigma\) we obtain:

\[\begin{equation*} p^{(st)}(x)=\frac{1}{Z\sigma^2}e^{2\int_0^x\frac{-\mu x'}{\sigma^2}dx'}=\frac{1}{Z\sigma^2}e^{-\frac{\cancel{2}\mu}{\sigma^2}\cdot\frac{x^2}{\cancel{2}}}=\frac{1}{Z\sigma^2}e^{-\frac{\mu x^2}{\sigma^2}} \end{equation*}\]

From which the normalization factor follows:

\[\begin{equation*} \frac{1}{Z^2\sigma^2}\sqrt{\frac{\cancel{2}\pi}{\frac{\cancel{2}\mu}{\sigma^2}}}=1 \ \iff \ \frac{1}{Z\sigma^2}=\sqrt{\frac{\mu}{\pi\sigma^2}} \end{equation*}\]

which is exactly what we obtained in (eq:processo-u-o-propagatore-stazionario) by taking the propagator to the limit for \(t\to\infty\).

Show that the equation for the variance of the Ornstein-Uhlenbeck process obtained from the stochastic differential equation is the same as that obtained from the Fokker-Planck equation. Also verify that in the stationary limit it assumes the value \(\frac{\sigma^2}{2\mu}\).

Solution. We have already derived the equation for the variance through the differential equation, obtaining equation (eq:processo-u-o-varianza-equazione-non-omogenea).

As for the derivation with the Fokker-Planck equation, we will have to multiply both members of (eq:processo-u-o-equazione-fokker-planck) by $x^2$ and integrate:

\begin{equation*}
    \int_{-\infty}^{+\infty}x^2\frac{\partial}{\partial t}p(x,t)dx=\mu\int_{-\infty}^{+\infty}x^2\frac{\partial}{\partial x}(xp)dx+\frac{\sigma^2}{2}\int_{-\infty}^{+\infty}x^2\frac{\partial^2}{\partial x^2}p(x,t)dx
\end{equation*}

On the first member we can simply take the derivative out of the integral:

\begin{equation*}
    \int_{-\infty}^{+\infty}x^2\frac{\partial}{\partial t}p(x,t)dx=\frac{\partial}{\partial t}\int_{-\infty}^{+\infty}x^2p(x,t)dx
\end{equation*}

On the second member, instead, we will have to integrate by parts, the first term once and the second twice. The first will give:

\begin{equation*}
    \int_{-\infty}^{+\infty}x^2\frac{\partial}{\partial x}(xp)dx=\cancel{x^3p(x,t)\big|_{-\infty}^{+\infty}}-2\int_{-\infty}^{+\infty}x^2p(x,t)dx
\end{equation*}

The second, instead:

\begin{align*}
    \int_{-\infty}^{+\infty}x^2\frac{\partial^2}{\partial x^2}p(x,t)dx&=\cancel{x^2\frac{\partial}{\partial x}p(x,t)\bigg|_{-\infty}^{+\infty}}-2\int_{-\infty}^{+\infty}x\frac{\partial}{\partial x}p(x,t)dx=\\
    &=-2\cancel{xp(x,t)\big|_{-\infty}^{+\infty}}+2\int_{-\infty}^{+\infty}p(x,t)dx=2
\end{align*}

Substituting in the starting equation:

\begin{equation*}
    \frac{\partial}{\partial t}\int_{-\infty}^{+\infty}x^2p(x,t)dx=-2\mu\int_{-\infty}^{+\infty}x^2p(x,t)dx+\frac{\sigma^2}{\cancel{2}}\cdot\cancel{2}
\end{equation*}

Being by definition

\begin{equation*}
    \left\langle x^2\right\rangle=\int_{-\infty}^{+\infty}x^2p(x,t)dx
\end{equation*}

we therefore find:

\begin{equation*}
    \frac{d}{dt}\left\langle x^2\right\rangle=-2\mu\left\langle x^2\right\rangle+\sigma^2
\end{equation*}

which is what is written in (eq:processo-u-o-varianza-equazione-non-omogenea).

In the stationary limit, the variance will be independent of time, therefore:

\begin{equation*}
    \frac{d}{dt}\left\langle x^2\right\rangle=0 \ \iff \ -2\mu\left\langle x^2\right\rangle+\sigma^2=0 \ \Longrightarrow \ \left\langle x^2\right\rangle=\frac{\sigma^2}{2\mu}
\end{equation*}

just as predicted by the assignment.

# Ornstein-Uhlenbeck Process

To conclude the study of stochastic processes, which began with the analysis of diffusion processes, let's take as an example a model that we will study with the various tools we have learned in the past chapters. The process in question is the **Ornstein-Uhlenbeck process**, which, to be honest, we have already introduced\footnote{Only in a specific case, as we will see better below.} in exercise (ex:processo-di-ornstein-uhlenbeck) following the introduction of the Wiener process.

## Sample trajectory

Formally, the process is defined by the following differential equation:

\begin{equation}
    \begin{cases}
        dx=-\mu xdt+\sigma dB\\        x(0)=x_0
    \end{cases}
\end{equation}

To find a general solution, it is useful to define the following function:

\begin{equation}
    y(t)=e^{\mu t}x(t)
\end{equation}

where $x(t)$ is the solution of the equation. The reason why this is convenient is soon told by applying the chain rule (equation (eq:sde-chain-rule-ito)), having set $\mu(x,t)=-\mu x$ and $\sigma(x,t)=\sigma$ for consistency with the Langevin equation:

\begin{align*}
    dy&=\left(\frac{\partial y}{\partial t}+\frac{\partial y}{\partial x}\mu(x,t)+\frac{1}{2}\frac{\partial^2y}{\partial x^2}\sigma^2\right)dt+\frac{\partial y}{\partial x}\sigma(x,t) dB=\\
    &=\left(\cancel{\mu e^{\mu t}x}\cancel{-\mu xe^{\mu t}}+0\right)dt+\sigma e^{\mu t}dB
\end{align*}

The remaining equation is easy to integrate:

\begin{equation*}
    dy=\sigma e^{\mu t}dB \ \Longrightarrow \ \ y(t)=y(0)+\sigma\int_0^te^{\mu t'}dB(t')
\end{equation*}

Returning to the expression in $x(t)$:

\begin{equation*}
    e^{\mu t}x(t)=e^{\mu\cdot0}x(0)+\sigma\int_0^te^{\mu t'}dB(t')=x_0+\sigma\int_0^te^{\mu t'}dB(t')
\end{equation*}

We thus find the explicit form of the solution:

\begin{equation}
    \boxed{x(t)=x_0e^{-\mu t}+\sigma e^{-\mu t}\int_0^te^{\mu t'}dB(t')}
\end{equation}

    We will have to follow the same procedure just carried out, with the only difference that this time $\mu(x,t)=-\mu x+\beta$. In this way the coefficient of $dt$ will not be canceled, since a surviving contribution equal to $\beta e^{\mu t}$ will remain:

    \end{equation*}

    \end{equation*}

    \end{equation*}

    Let's now substitute $y(t)=e^{\mu t}x(t)$ and return to the complete solution:

    \begin{equation*}

    \end{equation*}

    that is

### Variance

We know that one of the main aspects of a realization of a stochastic process is its variance, in order to describe how fluctuations tend to change over time.

Let's start from the calculation of the average value of $x(t)^2$, which is obtained starting from the series expansion of the differential of $x$ stopped at the second order, since - as already repeated several times - we have $dB^2=dt$:

\begin{equation*}
    d\left(x^2\right)=\frac{d\left(x^2\right)}{dx}dx+\frac{1}{2}\frac{d^2\left(x^2\right)}{dx^2}dx^2=2xdx+\frac{1}{\cancel{2}}\cdot\cancel{2}dx^2
\end{equation*}

Let's substitute the expression for $dx$ given by the SDE:

\begin{equation*}
    d\left(x^2\right)=2x\left(-\mu xdt+\sigma dB\right)+\left(-\mu xdt+\sigma dB\right)^2
\end{equation*}

Of the square, only $\sigma^2dB^2=\sigma^2dt$ remains:

\begin{equation*}
    d\left(x^2\right)=-2\mu x^2dt+2\sigma xdB+\sigma^2dt=\left(-2\mu x^2+\sigma^2\right)dt+2\sigma xdB
\end{equation*}

Let's consider the expected value on both sides:\footnote{We note the difference with what was obtained in equation (eq:sde-differenziale-x-quadro-sbagliato): here we find an additional contribution due to the expansion up to the second order, justified by the relation $dB^2=dt$.}

\begin{equation*}
    d\left\langle x^2\right\rangle=\left(-2\mu\left\langle x^2\right\rangle+\sigma^2\right)dt+2\sigma\langle xdB\rangle
\end{equation*}

According to the Itô convention $\langle xdB\rangle=\langle x\rangle\cancel{\langle dB\rangle}=0$, from which the differential equation follows

\begin{equation}
    \frac{d}{dt}\left\langle x^2\right\rangle=-2\mu\left\langle x^2\right\rangle+\sigma^2
\end{equation}

This is a non-homogeneous ordinary differential equation. To find a solution we must first solve the associated homogeneous equation:

\begin{equation*}
    \frac{d}{dt}\left\langle x^2\right\rangle=-2\mu\left\langle x^2\right\rangle \ \Longrightarrow \ \ \left\langle x(t)^2\right\rangle=ke^{-2\mu t}
\end{equation*}

To find the solution to the non-homogeneous equation ((eq:processo-u-o-varianza-equazione-non-omogenea)) we will have to add a constant contribution $c$ to the solution found, such that

\begin{equation*}
    \cancel{\frac{d}{dt}c}=-2\mu c+\sigma^2 \ \iff \ 2\mu c=\sigma^2 \ \iff \ c=\frac{\sigma^2}{2\mu}
\end{equation*}

Summing the two contributions, we will find:

\begin{equation*}
    \left\langle x(t)^2\right\rangle=ke^{-2\mu t}+\frac{\sigma^2}{2\mu}
\end{equation*}

It remains to find the constant $k$ through the initial condition ($\left\langle x(0)^2\right\rangle=x_0^2$):

\begin{equation*}
    \left\langle x(0)^2\right\rangle=k+\frac{\sigma^2}{2\mu}=x_0^2 \ \Longrightarrow \ k=x_0^2-\frac{\sigma^2}{2\mu}
\end{equation*}

Ultimately:

\begin{equation}
    \left\langle x(t)^2\right\rangle=\left(x_0^2-\frac{\sigma^2}{2\mu}\right)e^{-2\mu t}+\frac{\sigma^2}{2\mu}
\end{equation}

For the variance we must find $\langle x(t)\rangle$, which is easily found starting from (eq:processo-u-o-soluzione-generale):

\begin{equation*}
    \langle x(t)\rangle=x_0e^{-\mu t}+\sigma e^{-\mu t}\left\langle\int_0^te^{\mu t'}dB(t')\right\rangle=x_0e^{-\mu t}+\sigma e^{-\mu t}\int_0^t\left\langle e^{\mu t'}\right\rangle\cancel{\langle dB(t')\rangle}
\end{equation*}

We can now calculate the variance associated with the process:

\begin{align*}
    Var[x(t)]&=\left\langle x(t)^2\right\rangle-\langle x(t)\rangle^2=\left(x_0^2-\frac{\sigma^2}{2\mu}\right)e^{-2\mu t}+\frac{\sigma^2}{2\mu}-x_0^2e^{-\mu t}=\\
    &=\left(\cancel{x_0^2}-\frac{\sigma^2}{2\mu}\cancel{-x_0^2}\right)e^{-\mu t}+\frac{\sigma^2}{2\mu}
\end{align*}

that is

\begin{equation}
    \boxed{Var[x(t)]=\frac{\sigma^2}{2\mu}\left(1-e^{-\mu t}\right)}
\end{equation}

This is a function that is zero for $t=0$ and progressively increasing, stabilizing at the value $\frac{\sigma^2}{2\mu}$ for large $t$.\footnote{At this point it is interesting to note one thing: the solution $e^{-t}B\left(e^{2t}\right)$, with which the process in question was first introduced, is not easily reducible to what is written in (eq:processo-u-o-soluzione-generale). The Wikipedia page (\url{https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ornstein-Uhlenbeck_process}) clarifies that the *temporally homogeneous* process can be written as $\frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{2\mu}}e^{-\mu t}B\left(e^{2\mu t}\right)$, so the solution written by the professor would be the one relative to the case $\mu=1$ and $\sigma=\sqrt{2}$, but the variance - easily calculable - turns out to be constant and equal to $1$, and not $1-e^{-\mu t}$ as the formula (eq:processo-u-o-varianza) would like. What I believe is that this solution is valid only in the limit for $t\to\infty$, being precisely relative to the *temporally homogeneous* process, and represents the particular case in which the distribution becomes a standard Gaussian.}

### Connected auto-correlation

Another important aspect is the correlation of the trajectory with itself at different instants of time, called **auto-correlation**:

\begin{equation}
    \left\langle x\left(t_1\right)x\left(t_2\right)\right\rangle
\end{equation}

If we remove the effects given by the global mean, we obtain what is called the *connected auto-correlation*:\footnote{Speaking of the Wiener process, we called this quantity the *correlation function* (paragraph (sec:funzione-di-correlazione)); from a statistical point of view, these are different quantities, but the professor uses them (I believe improperly) as synonyms: the correlation function would correspond to the auto-correlation, while the covariance to the connected auto-correlation.}

\begin{equation}
    c\left(t_1,t_2\right)=\left\langle x\left(t_1\right)x\left(t_2\right)\right\rangle-\left\langle x\left(t_1\right)\right\rangle\left\langle x\left(t_2\right)\right\rangle
\end{equation}

Let's calculate the first term by substituting (eq:processo-u-o-soluzione-generale):

\footnotesize

\begin{equation*}
    \left\langle x\left(t_1\right)x\left(t_2\right)\right\rangle=\left\langle\left(x_0e^{-\mu t_1}+\sigma e^{-\mu t_1}\int_0^{t_1}e^{\mu t'}dB(t')\right)\left(x_0e^{-\mu t_2}+\sigma e^{-\mu t_2}\int_0^{t_2}e^{\mu t'}dB(t')\right)\right\rangle
\end{equation*}

\normalsize

From the products, the common factor $e^{-\mu\left(t_1+t_2\right)}$ emerges, which we can take out:

\begin{align*}
    \left\langle x\left(t_1\right)x\left(t_2\right)\right\rangle=e^{-\mu\left(t_1+t_2\right)}\bigg\langle&x_0^2+\\
    &+x_0\sigma\int_0^{t_1}e^{\mu t'}dB(t')+\\
    &+x_0\sigma\int_0^{t_2}e^{\mu t'}dB(t')+\\
    &+\sigma^2\int_0^{t_1}e^{\mu t'}dB(t')\int_0^{t_2}e^{\mu t'}dB(t')\bigg\rangle
\end{align*}

Distributing the average value over the four terms, the two central ones will be zero, since $\langle dB(t)\rangle$ is so. What remains is:

\begin{equation}
    \left\langle x\left(t_1\right)x\left(t_2\right)\right\rangle=e^{-\mu\left(t_1+t_2\right)}\left(x_0^2+\sigma^2\left\langle\int_0^{t_1}e^{\mu t'}dB(t')\int_0^{t_2}e^{\mu t'}dB(t')\right\rangle\right)
\end{equation}

To find the remaining expected value, it is useful to calculate more generally

\begin{equation*}
    \left\langle\int_0^{t_1}f(t')dB(t')\int_0^{t_2}g(t')dB(t')\right\rangle
\end{equation*}

Let's assume that $t_2>t_1$ and let's discretize the interval $\left[0,t_2\right]$ in the following way:\footnote{In the discretization there is an evident abuse of notation, since by setting the index as a subscript we can indicate with $t_1$ and $t_2$ two different quantities. However, it is not difficult to understand from the context what is being referred to.}

\begin{equation*}
    \left\{t_0=0,t_1,\dots,t_{m-1},t_m=\boldsymbol{t_1},t_{m+1},\dots,t_{n-1},t_n=\boldsymbol{t_2}\right\}
\end{equation*}

At this point, we can discretize the integrals as follows:

\begin{equation*}
    \left\langle\sum_{i=1}^mf_{i-1}\Delta B_i\sum_{j=1}^ng_{j-1}\Delta B_j\right\rangle
\end{equation*}

Let's split the second summation into the intervals $[1,m]$ and $[m+1,n]$:

\begin{align*}
    &\left\langle\sum_{i=1}^mf_{i-1}\Delta B_i\left[\sum_{j=1}^mg_{j-1}\Delta B_j+\sum_{j=m+1}^ng_{j-1}\Delta B_j\right]\right\rangle=\\
    &=\left\langle\sum_{i=1}^mf_{i-1}\Delta B_i\sum_{j=1}^mg_{j-1}\Delta B_j\right\rangle+\left\langle\sum_{i=1}^mf_{i-1}\Delta B_i\sum_{j=m+1}^ng_{j-1}\Delta B_j\right\rangle
\end{align*}

The summations of the second term refer to different intervals, so they are uncorrelated quantities:

\begin{align*}
    \left\langle\sum_{i=1}^mf_{i-1}\Delta B_i\sum_{j=m+1}^ng_{j-1}\Delta B_j\right\rangle&=\left\langle\sum_{i=1}^mf_{i-1}\Delta B_i\right\rangle\left\langle\sum_{j=m+1}^ng_{j-1}\Delta B_j\right\rangle=\\
    &=\sum_{i=1}^m\left\langle f_{i-1}\right\rangle\cancel{\left\langle\Delta B_i\right\rangle}\sum_{j=m+1}^n\left\langle g_{j-1}\right\rangle\cancel{\left\langle\Delta B_j\right\rangle}=0
\end{align*}

What remains is therefore the first term, where the contributions of the summation can be divided into those in which $i=j$ and those in which $i\neq j$:

\footnotesize

\begin{align*}
    \left\langle\sum_{i=1}^m\sum_{j=1}^mf_{i-1}g_{j-1}\Delta B_i\Delta B_j\right\rangle&=\left\langle\sum_{i=1}^mf_{i-1}g_{i-1}\Delta B_i^2\right\rangle+2\left\langle\sum_{i>j}f_{i-1}g_{j-1}\Delta B_i\Delta B_j\right\rangle=\\
    &=\sum_{i=1}^m\left\langle f_{i-1}g_{i-1}\right\rangle\left\langle\Delta B_i^2\right\rangle+2\sum_{i>j}\left\langle f_{i-1}g_{j-1}\right\rangle\cancel{\left\langle\Delta B_i\right\rangle}\cancel{\left\langle\Delta B_j\right\rangle}=\\
    &=\sum_{i=1}^m\left\langle f_{i-1}g_{i-1}\right\rangle\Delta t_i
\end{align*}

\normalsize

We thus find that, in the limit:

\begin{equation*}
    \sum_{i=1}^m\left\langle f_{i-1}g_{i-1}\right\rangle\Delta t_i \ \longrightarrow \ \int_0^{t_1}\langle f(t')g(t')\rangle dt'
\end{equation*}

If we had initially assumed $t_1>t_2$ we would have obtained the same result, but with the upper limit of integration equal to $t_2$. It follows that

\begin{equation*}
    \left\langle\int_0^{t_1}f(t')dB(t')\int_0^{t_2}g(t')dB(t')\right\rangle=\int_0^{\min\left(t_1,t_2\right)}\langle f(t')g(t')\rangle dt'
\end{equation*}

Let's go back to (eq:processo-u-o-auto-correlazione-calcolo-1) and substitute the result found:

\begin{equation*}
    \left\langle x\left(t_1\right)x\left(t_2\right)\right\rangle=e^{-\mu\left(t_1+t_2\right)}\left(x_0^2+\sigma^2\int_0^{\min\left(t_1t_2\right)}e^{2\mu t'}dt'\right)
\end{equation*}

Let's solve the integral:

\begin{align*}
    \left\langle x\left(t_1\right)x\left(t_2\right)\right\rangle&=e^{-\mu\left(t_1+t_2\right)}\left(x_0^2+\frac{\sigma^2}{2\mu}e^{2\mu t'}\bigg|_0^{\min\left(t_1,t_2\right)}\right)=\\
    &=e^{-\mu\left(t_1+t_2\right)}\left[x_0^2+\frac{\sigma^2}{2\mu}\left(e^{2\mu\min\left(t_1,t_2\right)}-1\right)\right]
\end{align*}

Let's substitute in (eq:processo-u-o-auto-correlazione-connessa-definizione), remembering that $\langle x(t)\rangle=x_0e^{-\mu t}$:

\begin{align*}
    c\left(t_1,t_2\right)&=e^{-\mu\left(t_1+t_2\right)}\left[x_0^2+\frac{\sigma^2}{2\mu}\left(e^{2\mu\min\left(t_1,t_2\right)}-1\right)\right]-x_0e^{-\mu t_1}\cdot x_0e^{-\mu t_2}=\\
    &=\cancel{x_0^2e^{-\mu\left(t_1+t_2\right)}}+\frac{\sigma^2}{2\mu}\left(e^{2\mu\min\left(t_1,t_2\right)}-1\right)e^{-\mu\left(t_1+t_2\right)}\cancel{-x_0^2e^{-\mu\left(t_1+t_2\right)}}
\end{align*}

We find:

\begin{equation*}
    c\left(t_1,t_2\right)=\frac{\sigma^2}{2\mu}\left[e^{\mu\left(2\min\left(t_1,t_2\right)-t_1-t_2\right)}-e^{-\mu\left(t_1+t_2\right)}\right]
\end{equation*}

Let's evaluate the two cases:

\begin{align*}
    &\min\left(t_1,t_2\right)=t_1 \ \Longrightarrow \ 2t_1-t_1-t_2=t_1-t_2\\
    &\min\left(t_1,t_2\right)=t_2 \ \Longrightarrow \ 2t_2-t_1-t_2=-t_1+t_2
\end{align*}

This means that the exponent $2\min\left(t_1,t_2\right)-t_1-t_2$ can be rewritten as $-\abs{t_1-t_2}$:

\begin{equation}
    \boxed{c\left(t_1,t_2\right)=\frac{\sigma^2}{2\mu}\left[e^{-\mu\abs{t_1-t_2}}-e^{-\mu\left(t_1+t_2\right)}\right]}
\end{equation}

We can find an even more general formula if we assume that at the initial instant the system is not entirely concentrated at $x_0$ but is distributed according to a function $p\left(x_0\right)$. In this case, we have that $\langle x(t)\rangle=\left\langle x_0\right\rangle e^{-\mu t}$, so by substituting the results in (eq:processo-u-o-auto-correlazione-connessa-definizione) we will have:

\begin{align*}
    c\left(t_1,t_2\right)&=\left\langle x_0^2\right\rangle e^{-\mu\left(t_1+t_2\right)}+\frac{\sigma^2}{2\mu}\left[e^{-\mu\abs{t_1-t_2}}-e^{-\mu\left(t_1+t_2\right)}\right]-\left\langle x_0\right\rangle^2e^{-\mu\left(t_1+t_2\right)}=\\
    &=\left(\left\langle x_0^2\right\rangle-\left\langle x_0\right\rangle^2\right)e^{-\mu\left(t_1+t_2\right)}+\frac{\sigma^2}{2\mu}\left[e^{-\mu\abs{t_1-t_2}}-e^{-\mu\left(t_1+t_2\right)}\right]
\end{align*}

From which:

\begin{equation}
    \boxed{c\left(t_1,t_2\right)=Var\left(x_0\right)e^{-\mu\left(t_1+t_2\right)}+\frac{\sigma^2}{2\mu}\left[e^{-\mu\abs{t_1-t_2}}-e^{-\mu\left(t_1+t_2\right)}\right]}
\end{equation}

We note that, in the limit for $t_1,t_2\to\infty$ but keeping the difference $t_1-t_2$ constant, we find the **stationary** connected auto-correlation, since it is independent of the individual values $t_1$ and $t_2$ but only of their difference in modulus:\footnote{As a proof of what was stated in the previous notes, we note that this result is the same as that obtained in exercise (ex:processo-di-ornstein-uhlenbeck) by setting $\mu=1$ and $\sigma=\sqrt{2}$.}

\begin{equation}
    \boxed{c_{stat}\left(t_1,t_2\right)=\frac{\sigma^2}{2\mu}e^{-\mu\abs{t_1-t_2}}}
\end{equation}

## Propagator

Another fundamental characteristic of a stochastic process is its **propagator**, which tells us how the pdf evolves over time.

It can be found starting from the Fokker-Planck equation ((eq:fokker-planck-equazione)), which in our case is written as

\begin{equation}
    \frac{\partial p}{\partial t}=-\frac{\partial}{\partial x}(-\mu xp)+\frac{1}{2}\frac{\partial^2}{\partial x^2}\left(\sigma^2p\right)=\mu\frac{\partial}{\partial x}(xp)+\frac{\sigma^2}{2}\frac{\partial^2p}{\partial x^2}
\end{equation}

To find the solution, it is convenient to apply the Fourier transform to both members:

\begin{equation}
    \red{\mathcal{F}\left[\frac{\partial p}{\partial t}\right]}=\mu\orange{\mathcal{F}\left[\frac{\partial}{\partial x}(xp)\right]}+\frac{\sigma^2}{2}\green{\mathcal{F}\left[\frac{\partial^2p}{\partial x^2}\right]}
\end{equation}

Let's calculate the various transformations individually.

\begin{enumerate}
    \item $\red{\mathcal{F}\left[\frac{\partial p}{\partial t}\right]}$

    \begin{equation*}
        \mathcal{F}\left[\frac{\partial p}{\partial t}\right]=\int_{-\infty}^{+\infty}\frac{\partial p}{\partial t}e^{i\omega x}dx=\frac{\partial}{\partial t}\int_{-\infty}^{+\infty}p(x,t)e^{i\omega x}dx=\frac{\partial\tilde{p}}{\partial t}
    \end{equation*}

    \item $\orange{\mathcal{F}\left[\frac{\partial}{\partial x}(xp)\right]}$

    \begin{align*}
        \mathcal{F}\left[\frac{\partial}{\partial x}(xp)\right]&=\int_{-\infty}^{+\infty}\frac{\partial}{\partial x}(xp)e^{i\omega x}dx=\\
        &=\cancel{xp(x,t)e^{i\omega x}\bigg|_{-\infty}^{+\infty}}-\int_{-\infty}^{+\infty}xp(x,t)\frac{\partial}{\partial x}e^{i\omega x}dx=\\
        &=-i\omega\int_{-\infty}^{+\infty}xp(x,t)e^{i\omega x}dx
    \end{align*}

    We note that $\frac{\partial}{\partial\omega}e^{i\omega x}=ixe^{i\omega x}$:

    \begin{align*}
        \mathcal{F}\left[\frac{\partial}{\partial x}(xp)\right]&=-\omega\int_{-\infty}^{+\infty}p(x,t)\cdot ixe^{i\omega x}dx=-\omega\int_{-\infty}^{+\infty}p(x,t)\frac{\partial}{\partial\omega}e^{i\omega x}dx=\\
        &=-\omega\frac{\partial}{\partial\omega}\int_{-\infty}^{+\infty}p(x,t)e^{i\omega x}dx=-\omega\frac{\partial\tilde{p}}{\partial\omega}
    \end{align*}

    \item $\green{\mathcal{F}\left[\frac{\partial^2p}{\partial x^2}\right]}$

    \begin{align*}
        \mathcal{F}\left[\frac{\partial^2p}{\partial x^2}\right]&=\int_{-\infty}^{+\infty}\frac{\partial^2p}{\partial x^2}e^{i\omega x}dx=\cancel{\frac{\partial p}{\partial x}e^{i\omega x}\bigg|_{-\infty}^{+\infty}}-\int_{-\infty}^{+\infty}\frac{\partial p}{\partial x}i\omega e^{i\omega x}dx=\\
        &=-i\omega\cancel{p(x,t)e^{i\omega x}\bigg|_{-\infty}^{+\infty}}+i\omega\int_{-\infty}^{+\infty}p(x,t)i\omega e^{i\omega x}dx=\\
        &=-\omega^2\int_{-\infty}^{+\infty}p(x,t)e^{i\omega x}dx=-\omega^2\tilde{p}(\omega,t)
    \end{align*}
\end{enumerate}

Let's substitute the results obtained in (eq:processo-u-o-propagatore-equazione-differenziale-fourier):

\begin{equation*}
    \frac{\partial\tilde{p}}{\partial t}=-\mu\omega\frac{\partial\tilde{p}}{\partial\omega}-\frac{\sigma^2}{2}\omega^2\tilde{p}(\omega,t)
\end{equation*}

This is a homogeneous first-order partial differential equation, in which $\tilde{p}(\omega,t)$ is derived once with respect to $t$ and another time with respect to $t$:

\begin{equation}
    \frac{\partial\tilde{p}}{\partial t}+\mu\omega\frac{\partial\tilde{p}}{\partial\omega}+\frac{\sigma^2}{2}\omega^2\tilde{p}(\omega,t)=0
\end{equation}

To solve this equation we will use a method known as the **method of characteristics**, which makes use of an auxiliary variable $s$ such as to redefine the function in the following way:

\begin{equation*}
    \tilde{p}(s)\equiv\tilde{p}(\omega(s),t(s))
\end{equation*}

By deriving with respect to $s$ we obtain:

\begin{equation}
    \frac{d\tilde{p}}{ds}=\frac{\partial\tilde{p}}{\partial\omega}\frac{d\omega}{ds}+\frac{\partial\tilde{p}}{\partial t}\frac{dt}{ds}
\end{equation}

Referring to equation (eq:processo-u-o-propagatore-equazione-differenziale-post-fourier), we note that it can be rewritten in the following system of differential equations:

\begin{equation}
    \begin{cases}
        \frac{d\omega}{ds}=\mu\omega\\       \frac{dt}{ds}=1\\       \frac{d\tilde{p}}{ds}+\frac{\sigma^2}{2}\omega^2\tilde{p}(s)=0
    \end{cases}
\end{equation}

We have thus passed from a partial differential equation to a system of ordinary differential equations. The method consists in identifying curves, also called *characteristics*, belonging to the space described by the coordinates $\left(\omega,t,\tilde{p}\right)$ which are constrained on surfaces

\begin{equation}
    \begin{cases}
        u\left(\omega,t,\tilde{p}\right)=A\\       v\left(\omega,t,\tilde{p}\right)=B
    \end{cases}
\end{equation}

where $A$ and $B$ are arbitrary constants.

The functions $u$ and $v$ are obtained directly from the differential equations written above. In particular, from the first we find:

\begin{equation*}
    \int\frac{d\omega}{\omega}=\mu\int ds \ \Longrightarrow \ \omega=\omega(0)e^{\mu s}
\end{equation*}

From the third, instead:

\begin{equation*}
    \int\frac{d\tilde{p}}{\tilde{p}}=-\frac{\sigma^2}{2}\int\omega^2ds=-\frac{\sigma^2\omega(0)^2}{2}\int e^{2\mu s}ds \ \Longrightarrow \ \tilde{p}=\tilde{p}(0)e^{-\frac{\sigma^2\omega(0)^2}{4\mu}e^{2\mu s}}
\end{equation*}

Since $\omega(0)^2e^{2\mu s}=\omega^2$ then the two solutions are the following:

\begin{equation*}
    \begin{cases}
        \omega=\omega(0)e^{\mu s}\\       \tilde{p}=\tilde{p}(0)e^{-\frac{\sigma^2}{2\mu}\omega^2}
    \end{cases}
\end{equation*}

From the second equation of the system (eq:processo-u-o-sistema-equazioni-differenziali), finally, it results:

\begin{equation*}
    \int dt=\int s \ \Longrightarrow \ t=t_0+s \ \iff \ s=t-t_0
\end{equation*}

This also means that $\omega(s=0)=\omega\left(t=t_0\right)=\omega_0$, so finally by substituting:

\begin{equation}
    \begin{cases}
        \omega=\omega_0e^{\mu\left(t-t_0\right)}\\       \tilde{p}=\tilde{p}(0)e^{-\frac{\sigma^2}{4\mu}\omega^2}
    \end{cases}
\end{equation}

As said, the constraints must be constant. For this reason, by isolating $\omega_0$ from the first and $\tilde{p}(0)$ from the second, we obtain:

\begin{equation*}
    \begin{cases}
        \omega_0=\omega e^{-\mu\left(t-t_0\right)}\\       \tilde{p}(0)=\tilde{p}e^{\frac{\sigma^2}{4\mu}\omega^2}
    \end{cases}
\end{equation*}

By setting $A=\omega_0$ and $B=\tilde{p}(0)$ we have found the functions $u$ and $v$:

\begin{equation}
    \begin{cases}
        u\left(\omega,t,\tilde{p}\right)=\omega e^{-\mu\left(t-t_0\right)}\\       v\left(\omega,t,\tilde{p}\right)=\tilde{p}e^{\frac{\sigma^2}{4\mu}\omega^2}
    \end{cases}
\end{equation}

It can be shown that $u$ and $v$ are related by the relation

\begin{equation}
    v=g(u)
\end{equation}

where $g$ is an unknown functional to be found. Substituting:

\begin{equation}
    \tilde{p}e^{\frac{\sigma^2}{4\mu}\omega^2}=g\left(\omega e^{-\mu\left(t-t_0\right)}\right) \ \Longrightarrow \ \tilde{p}(\omega,t)=e^{-\frac{\sigma^2}{4\mu}\omega^2}g\left(\omega e^{-\mu\left(t-t_0\right)}\right)
\end{equation}

To find the functional $g$ we must impose the initial condition:

\begin{equation}
    \tilde{p}(\omega,0)=e^{-\frac{\sigma^2}{4\mu}\omega^2}g(\omega) \ \Longrightarrow \ g(\omega)=\tilde{p}(\omega,0)e^{\frac{\sigma^2}{4\mu}\omega^2}
\end{equation}

Assuming that the initial condition is $p(x,0)=\delta\left(x-x_0\right)$, then $\tilde{p}(\omega,0)$ is given by the Fourier transform:

\begin{equation*}
    \tilde{p}(\omega,0)=\int_{-\infty}^{+\infty}p(x,0)e^{i\omega x}dx=\int_{-\infty}^{+\infty}\delta\left(x-x_0\right)e^{i\omega x}dx=e^{i\omega x_0}
\end{equation*}

Let's substitute in (eq:processo-u-o-funzionale-g-condizione-iniziale):

\begin{equation}
    g(\omega)=e^{i\omega x_0}e^{\frac{\sigma^2}{4\mu}\omega^2}=e^{\frac{\sigma^2}{4\mu}\omega^2+ix_0\omega}
\end{equation}

Having found the expression for $g(\omega)$ we can finally obtain $\tilde{p}(\omega,t)$ in its explicit form starting from (eq:processo-u-o-espression-tilde-p-funzione-di-g):

\begin{equation*}
    \tilde{p}(\omega,t)=e^{-\frac{\sigma^2}{4\mu}\omega^2}e^{\frac{\sigma^2}{4\mu}\omega^2e^{-2\mu\left(t-t_0\right)}+ix_0\omega e^{-\mu\left(t-t_0\right)}}=e^{\frac{\sigma^2}{4\mu}\left(1-e^{-2\mu\left(t-t_0\right)}\right)\omega^2+ix_0e^{-\mu\left(t-t_0\right)}\omega}
\end{equation*}

The propagator $p(x,t)$ is none other than the inverse transform of $\tilde{p}(\omega,t)$:

\begin{align*}
    p(x,t)&=\frac{1}{2\pi}\int_{-\infty}^{+\infty}\tilde{p}(\omega,t)e^{-i\omega x}d\omega=\\
    &=\frac{1}{2\pi}\int_{-\infty}^{+\infty}e^{\frac{\sigma^2}{4\mu}\left(1-e^{-2\mu\left(t-t_0\right)}\right)\omega^2+ix_0e^{-\mu\left(t-t_0\right)}\omega}e^{-ix\omega}d\omega=\\
    &=\frac{1}{2\pi}\int_{-\infty}^{+\infty}e^{\frac{\sigma^2}{4\mu}\left(1-e^{-2\mu\left(t-t_0\right)}\right)\omega^2-i\left(x-x_0e^{-\mu\left(t-t_0\right)}\right)\omega}d\omega
\end{align*}

We recognize the form of the Gaussian integral that we know how to solve:

\begin{equation*}
    p(x,t)=\frac{1}{2\pi}\sqrt{\frac{2\pi}{\frac{\sigma^2}{2\mu}\left(1-e^{-2\mu\left(t-t_0\right)}\right)}}e^{-\frac{\left(x-x_0e^{-\mu\left(t-t_0\right)}\right)^2}{2\cdot\frac{\sigma^2}{2\mu}\left(1-e^{-2\mu\left(t-t_0\right)}\right)}}
\end{equation*}

Simplifying, we obtain the final form of the propagator:

\begin{equation}
    \boxed{p(x,t)=\sqrt{\frac{\mu}{\pi\sigma^2\left(1-e^{-2\mu\left(t-t_0\right)}\right)}}e^{-\frac{\mu\left(x-x_0e^{-\mu\left(t-t_0\right)}\right)^2}{\sigma^2\left(1-e^{-2\mu\left(t-t_0\right)}\right)}}}
\end{equation}

In the stationary limit, for $t\to\infty$, we find

\begin{equation}
    \boxed{p^{(st)}(x)=\sqrt{\frac{\mu}{\pi\sigma^2}}e^{-\frac{\mu x^2}{\sigma^2}}}
\end{equation}

that is, a Gaussian centered at zero and with variance $\frac{\sigma^2}{\mu}$.

The general formula was given by (eq:fokker-planck-soluzione-all-equilibrio-forma-finale), and we note that by substituting $x_{min}=0$, $\mu(x)=-\mu x$ and $\sigma(x)=\sigma$ we obtain:

\begin{equation*}
    p^{(st)}(x)=\frac{1}{Z\sigma^2}e^{2\int_0^x\frac{-\mu x'}{\sigma^2}dx'}=\frac{1}{Z\sigma^2}e^{-\frac{\cancel{2}\mu}{\sigma^2}\cdot\frac{x^2}{\cancel{2}}}=\frac{1}{Z\sigma^2}e^{-\frac{\mu x^2}{\sigma^2}}
\end{equation*}

From which the normalization factor follows:

\begin{equation*}
    \frac{1}{Z^2\sigma^2}\sqrt{\frac{\cancel{2}\pi}{\frac{\cancel{2}\mu}{\sigma^2}}}=1 \ \iff \ \frac{1}{Z\sigma^2}=\sqrt{\frac{\mu}{\pi\sigma^2}}
\end{equation*}

which is exactly what we obtained in (eq:processo-u-o-propagatore-stazionario) by taking the propagator to the limit for $t\to\infty$.
