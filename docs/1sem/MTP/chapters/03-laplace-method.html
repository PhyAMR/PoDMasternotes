5 The Laplace method

In many situations it is necessary to calculate complicated integrals whose form is

\[\begin{equation}{} I(\lambda)=\int_{x_1}^{x_2}g(x)e^{\lambda f(x)}dx \end{equation}\]

where \(f(x)\) and \(g(x)\) are real and smooth functions and \(\lambda\) is a real parameter.

Although it is impossible to calculate \(I(\lambda)\) with a fixed \(\lambda\), under appropriate conditions it is possible to approximate it well in the limit for \(\lambda\to\infty\): the idea of the Laplace method, in fact, is that the major contribution to the integral as \(\lambda\) increases comes from the neighborhood of the point where \(f(x)\) assumes its maximum value.

5.1 First case: internal point

Let \(x_1<x_0<x_2\) with \(f'\left(x_0\right)=0\) being the maximum point for the function in the whole interval; for the same reason we will have that \(f''\left(x_0\right)<0\).

As we expect, the major contribution will come in the neighborhood of \(x_0\), so it is useful to expand \(f(x)\) in series precisely at that point:

\[\begin{equation*} f(x)=f\left(x_0\right)+\cancel{f'\left(x_0\right)\left(x-x_0\right)}+\frac{f''\left(x_0\right)}{2}\left(x-x_0\right)^2+\mathcal{O}\left(\abs{x-x_0}^3\right) \end{equation*}\]

Substituting in (eq:metodo-di-laplace-integrale-iniziale):

\[\begin{equation*} I(\lambda)=\int_{x_1}^{x_2}g(x)e^{\lambda\left[f\left(x_0\right)+\frac{f''\left(x_0\right)}{2}\left(x-x_0\right)^2+\mathcal{O}\left(\abs{x-x_0}^3\right)\right]}dx \end{equation*}\]

In the limit for \(x\to x_0\) we will have \(g(x)\to g\left(x_0\right)\) and all the terms \(\mathcal{O}\left(\abs{x-x_0}^3\right)\) can be considered null:

\[\begin{equation*} I(\lambda)\simeq g\left(x_0\right)\int_{x_1}^{x_2}e^{\lambda\left[f\left(x_0\right)+\frac{f''\left(x_0\right)}{2}\left(x-x_0\right)^2\right]}dx=g\left(x_0\right)e^{\lambda f\left(x_0\right)}\int_{x_1}^{x_2}e^{\frac{\lambda f''\left(x_0\right)}{2}\left(x-x_0\right)^2}dx \end{equation*}\]

Furthermore, remembering that \(f''\left(x_0\right)<0\), we can write:

\[\begin{equation*} I(\lambda)\simeq g\left(x_0\right)e^{\lambda f\left(x_0\right)}\int_{x_1}^{x_2}e^{-\frac{\lambda \abs{f''\left(x_0\right)}}{2}\left(x-x_0\right)^2}dx \end{equation*}\]

At this point we set

\[\begin{equation}{} s=\sqrt{\frac{\lambda\abs{f''\left(x_0\right)}}{2}}\left(x-x_0\right) \end{equation}\]

so as to have a Gaussian-type exponential in the integral:

\[\begin{equation}{} I(\lambda)\simeq g\left(x_0\right)e^{\lambda f\left(x_0\right)}\sqrt{\frac{2}{\lambda\abs{f''\left(x_0\right)}}}\int_{s_1}^{s_2}e^{-s^2}ds \end{equation}\]

The limits of the integral are given by the substitution in equation (eq:metodo-di-laplace-sostituzione-s) of \(x=x_1\) in one case and of \(x=x_2\) in the other, so we will have \(s_1<0\) and \(s_2>0\). Since the two limits are proportional to \(\sqrt{\lambda}\) in the limit for \(\lambda\to\infty\) we have:

\[\begin{equation*} \int_{s_1}^{s_2}e^{-s^2}ds \ \xrightarrow{\lambda\to\infty} \ \int_{-\infty}^{+\infty}e^{-s^2}ds=\sqrt{\pi} \end{equation*}\]

We can therefore approximate the integral to

\[\begin{equation}{} \boxed{I(\lambda)\simeq g\left(x_0\right)e^{\lambda f\left(x_0\right)}\sqrt{\frac{2\pi}{\lambda\abs{f''\left(x_0\right)}}}} \end{equation}\]

This is the dominant term, that is, the one that provides the major contribution in the limit for \(\lambda\to\infty\): considering the successive terms in the expansion of \(f(x)\) and \(g(x)\) it is also possible to obtain the other contributions, as we will see below in exercise (ex:metodo-di-laplace-sviluppi-successivi).

*Show that a better approximation of equation (eq:metodo-di-laplace-termine-dominante* is given by)

\begin{equation}{}
    I(\lambda)\simeq e^{\lambda f\left(x_0\right)}\sqrt{\frac{2\pi}{\lambda\abs{f''\left(x_0\right)}}}\left(g\left(x_0\right)+\frac{c}{\lambda}\right)
\end{equation}

*where* $c$ *is a constant that depends on the derivatives of* $f(x)$ *up to the fourth order (calculated at* $x_0$*), on* $g\left(x_0\right)$ *and on* $g'\left(x_0\right)$.

I start from the assumption that the text is actually *misleading*, in the sense that the constant $c$ does not depend only on $g\left(x_0\right)$ and $g'\left(x_0\right)$ but also on $g''\left(x_0\right)$, as we will see below due to the series expansions of the functions.

For simplicity of writing I will use the following notations:

\begin{itemize}
    \item $x-x_0=z$
    \item $f\left(x_0\right)=f_0$
\end{itemize}

As suggested by the text, let's expand both $f(x)$ and $g(x)$ in series around $x_0$, remembering that $f_0'=0$ and that $f_0''=-\abs{f_0''}$:

\begin{align*}
    &f(x)=f_0-\frac{\abs{f_0''}}{2}z^2+\frac{f_0'''}{6}z^3+\frac{f_0^{IV}}{24}z^4+\dots\\
    &g(x)=g_0+g_0'z+\frac{g_0''}{2}z^2+\dots
\end{align*}

Let's now make the integral explicit as a function of $\lambda$:

\begin{equation*}
    I(\lambda)=\int_{z_1}^{z_2}\left[g_0+g_0'z+\frac{g_0''}{2}z^2+\dots\right]e^{\lambda\left[f_0-\frac{\abs{f_0''}}{2}z^2+\frac{f_0'''}{6}z^3+\frac{f_0^{IV}}{24}z^4+\dots\right]}dz
\end{equation*}

Let's now focus only on the exponential, which we can also write as

\begin{equation*}
    e^{\lambda\left[f_0-\frac{\abs{f_0''}}{2}z^2+\frac{f_0'''}{6}z^3+\frac{f_0^{IV}}{24}z^4+\dots\right]}=e^{\lambda f_0}e^{-\frac{\lambda\abs{f_0''}}{2}z^2}e^{\lambda\left[\frac{f_0'''}{6}z^3+\frac{f_0^{IV}}{24}z^4+\dots\right]}
\end{equation*}

The term $e^{\lambda f_0}e^{-\frac{\lambda\abs{f_0''}}{2}z^2}$ we had already obtained in the previous calculation; to this is added the last exponential, which we can include in the calculation by expanding it in series:

\begin{align*}
    &e^{\lambda\left[\frac{f_0'''}{6}z^3+\frac{f_0^{IV}}{24}z^4+\dots\right]}=\\
    &=1+\lambda\left[\frac{f_0'''}{6}z^3+\frac{f_0^{IV}}{24}z^4+\dots\right]+\frac{\lambda^2}{2}\left[\frac{f_0'''}{6}z^3+\frac{f_0^{IV}}{24}z^4+\dots\right]^2+\dots
\end{align*}

Let's remember that all these terms must be multiplied by the expansion of $g(x)$, by $e^{\lambda f_0}$ and by $e^{-\frac{\lambda\abs{f_0''}}{2}z^2}$. Net of multiplicative factors, we will find ourselves solving integrals of the type:

\begin{equation*}
    \int_{-\infty}^{+\infty}z^ne^{-\frac{\lambda\abs{f_0''}}{2}z^2}dz
\end{equation*}

already considering the limit $\lambda\to\infty$.

We immediately understand that if $n$ is odd the integral will be zero, as it is an integral in a symmetric interval of an odd function.

Considering therefore only even powers, we can rewrite the integral in the following way:

\begin{align*}
    \int_{-\infty}^{+\infty}z^ne^{-\frac{a}{2}z^2}dz&=\frac{\partial^{\frac{n}{2}}}{\partial\left(-\frac{a}{2}\right)^{\frac{n}{2}}}\int_{-\infty}^{+\infty}e^{-\frac{a}{2}z^2}dz=(-2)^{\frac{n}{2}}\frac{\partial^{\frac{n}{2}}}{\partial a^{\frac{n}{2}}}\int_{-\infty}^{+\infty}e^{-\frac{a}{2}z^2}dz=\\
    &=(-2)^{\frac{n}{2}}\frac{\partial^{\frac{n}{2}}}{\partial a^{\frac{n}{2}}}\sqrt{\frac{2\pi}{a}}
\end{align*}

having set $a=\lambda\abs{f_0''}$.

To find the general formula, let's calculate the results for the smallest $n$, remembering that $n$ can only be even:

\begin{align*}
    &n=0 \ \Longrightarrow \ (-2)^0\frac{\partial^0}{\partial a^0}\sqrt{\frac{2\pi}{a}}=\sqrt{\frac{2\pi}{a}}\\
    &n=2 \ \Longrightarrow \ (-2)\frac{\partial}{\partial a}\sqrt{\frac{2\pi}{a}}=-2\left(-\frac{1}{2}\right)\sqrt{\frac{2\pi}{a^3}}=\sqrt{\frac{2\pi}{a^3}}\\
    &n=4 \ \Longrightarrow \ (-2)^2\frac{\partial^2}{\partial a^2}\sqrt{\frac{2\pi}{a}}=4\left(-\frac{1}{2}\right)\left(-\frac{3}{2}\right)\sqrt{\frac{2\pi}{a^5}}=3\sqrt{\frac{2\pi}{a^5}}\\
    &n=6 \ \Longrightarrow \ (-2)^3\frac{\partial^3}{\partial a^3}\sqrt{\frac{2\pi}{a}}=-8\left(-\frac{1}{2}\right)\left(-\frac{3}{2}\right)\left(-\frac{5}{2}\right)\sqrt{\frac{2\pi}{a^7}}=15\sqrt{\frac{2\pi}{a^7}}
\end{align*}

In general it will result

\begin{equation}
    \int_{-\infty}^{+\infty}z^ne^{-\frac{a}{2}z^2}dz=(n-1)!!\sqrt{\frac{2\pi}{a^{n+1}}}=\frac{(n-1)!!}{a^{\frac{n}{2}}}\sqrt{\frac{2\pi}{a}}
\end{equation}

Substituting $a=\lambda\abs{f_0''}$ we find

\begin{equation}{}
    \int_{-\infty}^{+\infty}z^ne^{-\frac{\lambda\abs{f_0''}}{2}z^2}dz=\frac{(n-1)!!}{\lambda^{\frac{n}{2}}\abs{f_0''}^{\frac{n}{2}}}\sqrt{\frac{2\pi}{\lambda\abs{f_0''}}}
\end{equation}

Looking at the form we need to arrive at (equation (eq:metodo-di-laplace-soluzione-esercizio-famigerato)), we note that the term $e^{\lambda f_0}$ and the term $\sqrt{\frac{2\pi}{\lambda\abs{f_0''}}}$ (which results from each integral, and we can therefore factor out for all contributions) are already made explicit in front of the factor. This means that we will only have to focus on the rest, that is, on the products that come out of the expansion of $g(x)$ and the expansion of $e^{\lambda\left[\frac{f_0'''}{6}z^3+\frac{f_0^{IV}}{24}z^4+\dots\right]}$:

\begin{align*}
    &\left[g_0+g_0'z+\frac{g_0''}{2}z^2+\dots\right]\cdot1+\\
    &+\left[g_0+g_0'z+\frac{g_0''}{2}z^2+\dots\right]\cdot\lambda\left[\frac{f_0'''}{6}z^3+\frac{f_0^{IV}}{24}z^4+\dots\right]+\\
    &+\left[g_0+g_0'z+\frac{g_0''}{2}z^2+\dots\right]\cdot\frac{\lambda^2}{2}\left[\frac{f_0'''}{6}z^3+\frac{f_0^{IV}}{24}z^4+\dots\right]^2+\\
    &+\dots
\end{align*}

From (eq:metodo-di-laplace-esercizio-famigerato-relazione-z-lambda) we know that from a term of the type $\propto z^n$ a result $\propto\lambda^{-\frac{n}{2}}$ comes out. Since we only want the terms up to $\frac{1}{\lambda}$ then

\begin{itemize}
    \item of all the terms multiplied by $\lambda^0$ we will have to consider only those up to $z^2$
    \item of all the terms multiplied by $\lambda^1$ we will have to consider only those up to $z^4$
    \item of all the terms multiplied by $\lambda^2$ we will have to consider only those up to $z^6$
\end{itemize}

Remembering that all odd powers give a null contribution, it will result:

\begin{align*}
    &\left[g_0+\cancel{g_0'z}+\frac{g_0''}{2}z^2\right]+\\
    &+\lambda\left[\cancel{\frac{g_0f_0'''}{6}z^3}+\frac{g_0f_0^{IV}}{24}z^4+\frac{g_0'f_0'''}{6}z^4\right]+\\
    &+\frac{\lambda^2}{2}\left[\frac{g_0\left(f_0'''\right)^2}{36}z^6\right]
\end{align*}

Let's write the results by referring to (eq:metodo-di-laplace-esercizio-famigerato-relazione-z-lambda):

\begin{align*}
    &g_0+\frac{g_0''}{2\lambda\abs{f_0''}}+\lambda\left[\frac{3g_0f_0^{IV}}{24\lambda^2\abs{f_0''}^2}+\frac{3g_0'f_0'''}{6\lambda^2\abs{f_0''}^2}\right]+\frac{\lambda^2}{2}\left[\frac{15g_0\left(f_0'''\right)^2}{36\lambda^3\abs{f_0''}^3}\right]=\\
    &=g_0+\frac{g_0''}{2\lambda\abs{f_0''}}+\frac{g_0f_0^{IV}}{8\lambda\abs{f_0''}^2}+\frac{g_0'f_0'''}{2\lambda\abs{f_0''}^2}+\frac{5g_0\left(f_0'''\right)^2}{24\lambda\abs{f_0''}^3}
\end{align*}

Putting all the terms together, it finally results:

\begin{equation*}
    I(\lambda)=e^{\lambda f_0}\sqrt{\frac{2\pi}{\lambda\abs{f_0''}}}\left[g_0+\frac{1}{\lambda}\left(\frac{g_0''}{2\abs{f_0''}}+\frac{g_0f_0^{IV}}{8\abs{f_0''}^2}+\frac{g_0'f_0'''}{2\abs{f_0''}^2}+\frac{5g_0\left(f_0'''\right)^2}{24\abs{f_0''}^3}\right)\right]
\end{equation*}

From which we find a constant

\begin{equation*}
    c=\frac{1}{2\abs{f''\left(x_0\right)}}\left[\frac{g\left(x_0\right)}{4}\left(\frac{5f'''\left(x_0\right)^2}{3\abs{f''\left(x_0\right)}^2}+\frac{f^{IV}\left(x_0\right)}{\abs{f''\left(x_0\right)}}\right)+\frac{g'\left(x_0\right)f'''\left(x_0\right)}{\abs{f''\left(x_0\right)}}+g''\left(x_0\right)\right]
\end{equation*}

5.2 Second case: end point

Let \(x_0=x_1\) or \(x_0=x_2\) with, again, \(f'\left(x_0\right)=0\) and \(f''\left(x_0\right)<0\).

The steps followed will be the same as in the previous case with the only difference that, looking at the substitution in (eq:metodo-di-laplace-sostituzione-s), one limit will go to zero while the other to infinity. This means that the Gaussian integral is half the result obtained previously, in fact:

\[\begin{equation*} \int_0^{+\infty}e^{-s^2}ds=\int_{-\infty}^0e^{-s^2}ds=\frac{\sqrt{\pi}}{2} \end{equation*}\]

Substituting in equation (eq:metodo-di-laplace-formula-con-integrale-gaussiano) we find the result:

\[\begin{equation}{} \boxed{I(\lambda)\simeq g\left(x_0\right)e^{\lambda f\left(x_0\right)}\sqrt{\frac{\pi}{2\lambda\abs{f''\left(x_0\right)}}}} \end{equation}\]

The modified Bessel function of the second kind is a special function that is useful in many applications. This function has the following integral representation:

Solution. \[\begin{equation*} K_\nu(x)=\int_0^\infty e^{-x\cosh t}\cosh(\nu t)dt \ \text{ *with*} \ x>0 \end{equation*}\]

*We want to calculate* $K_\nu(x)$ *in the limit for* $x\to\infty$ *for* $\nu$ *fixed. Show that:*

\begin{equation*}
    K_\nu(x)\simeq e^{-x}\sqrt{\frac{\pi}{2x}}\left(1+\frac{c(\nu)}{x}+\dots\right) \ \     \text{  *with*} \ c(\nu)=\frac{4\nu^2-1}{8}
\end{equation*}

Let's consider as function $f(t)=-\cosh t$ which in the interval $[0,\infty)$ has its maximum at $t=0$, where it assumes the value

\begin{equation*}
    f(0)=-\cosh(0)=-1
\end{equation*}

Since the maximum point coincides with one of the ends of the interval then, in the limit for $x\to\infty$, it is necessary to resort to (eq:metodo-di-laplace-caso-2):

\begin{equation*}
    K_\nu(x)\simeq\cosh(0)e^{-x}\sqrt{\frac{\pi}{2x\abs{-\cosh(0)}}}=e^{-x}\sqrt{\frac{\pi}{2x}}
\end{equation*}

This is the dominant term, so we must resort to the successive expansions to obtain the result required by the assignment. The functions and derivatives calculated at $t=0$ will be

\begin{minipage}{0.48\textwidth}
    \begin{equation*}
        \begin{cases}
            f(0)&=-\cosh(0)=-1\\
            f'(0)&=-\sinh(0)=0\\
            f''(0)&=-\cosh(0)=-1\\
            f'''(0)&=-\sinh(0)=0\\
            f^{IV}(0)&=-\cosh(0)=-1
        \end{cases}
    \end{equation*}
\end{minipage}
\hfill
\begin{minipage}{0.48\textwidth}
    \begin{equation*}
        \begin{cases}
            g(0)&=\cosh(0)=1\\
            g'(0)&=\nu\sinh(0)=0\\
            g''(0)&=\nu^2\cosh(0)=\nu^2
        \end{cases}
    \end{equation*}
\end{minipage}

Substituting in the solution of exercise (ex:metodo-di-laplace-sviluppi-successivi) we find the constant

\begin{equation*}
    c(\nu)=\frac{1}{2}\left[\frac{1}{4}\left(0-1\right)+0+\nu^2\right]=-\frac{1}{8}+\frac{\nu^2}{2}=\frac{4\nu^2-1}{8}
\end{equation*}

just as predicted by the assignment.

5.3 Third case: non-stationary end point

Let \(x_0=x_1\) or \(x_0=x_2\) but with \(f'\left(x_0\right)\neq0\).

Since this time the first derivative does not vanish at \(x_0\), the expansion of the function can be stopped at the first order:

\[\begin{equation*} f(x)=f\left(x_0\right)+f'\left(x_0\right)\left(x-x_0\right)+\mathcal{O}\left(\abs{x-x_0}^2\right) \end{equation*}\]

At this point we proceed as in the previous cases. First, let’s go to the limit for \(x\to x_0\):

\[\begin{equation*} I(\lambda)\simeq g\left(x_0\right)e^{\lambda f\left(x_0\right)}\int_{x_1}^{x_2}e^{\lambda f'\left(x_0\right)\left(x-x_0\right)}dx \end{equation*}\]

Without loss of generality, let’s assume that \(f'\left(x_0\right)<0\):

\[\begin{equation*} I(\lambda)\simeq g\left(x_0\right)e^{\lambda f\left(x_0\right)}\int_{x_1}^{x_2}e^{-\lambda\abs{f'\left(x_0\right)}\left(x-x_0\right)}dx \end{equation*}\]

Now let’s set

\[\begin{equation}{} s=\lambda\abs{f'\left(x_0\right)}\left(x-x_0\right) \end{equation}\]

so as to have:

\[\begin{equation*} I(\lambda)\simeq g\left(x_0\right)\frac{e^{\lambda f\left(x_0\right)}}{\lambda\abs{f'\left(x_0\right)}}\int_{s_1}^{s_2}e^{-s}ds \end{equation*}\]

Let’s now assume that \(x_0=x_1\); from (eq:metodo-di-laplace-sostituzione-s-caso-3) it is clear that \(s_1=0\), and by sending \(\lambda\to\infty\) we have instead that \(s_2\to\infty\):

\[\begin{equation*} I(\lambda)\simeq g\left(x_0\right)\frac{e^{\lambda f\left(x_0\right)}}{\lambda\abs{f'\left(x_0\right)}}\int_0^\infty e^{-s}ds=g\left(x_0\right)\frac{e^{\lambda f\left(x_0\right)}}{\lambda\abs{f'\left(x_0\right)}}\left(-e^{-s}\right)\bigg|_0^\infty \end{equation*}\]

From which the final formula is obtained:

\[\begin{equation}{} \boxed{I(\lambda)\simeq g\left(x_0\right)\frac{e^{\lambda f\left(x_0\right)}}{\lambda\abs{f'\left(x_0\right)}}} \end{equation}\]

Obtain the dominant order approximation (for \(\lambda\to\infty\)) of:

Solution. \[\begin{equation*} \int_{-2}^0 x^m h(x)dx \ \ *where* \ h(x)=e^{\lambda\left(2x^3+3x^2\right)} *and* \ m *is a fixed positive parameter.* \end{equation*}\]

First, let's observe where the stationary points of $f(x)=2x^3+3x^2$ are by seeing where its derivative vanishes:

\begin{equation*}
    f'(x)=6x\left(x+1\right)=0 \ \Longrightarrow \ x=-1,0
\end{equation*}

To understand if they are maximum or minimum points, let's evaluate the sign that the second derivative assumes at these points:

\begin{equation*}
    f''(x)=6(2x+1) \ \Longrightarrow \ f''(-1)=-6, \ f''(0)=6
\end{equation*}

This means that the relative maximum point for $f(x)$ is $x_0=-1$.

Let's then evaluate the first integral. Since for $x\to-\infty$ the behavior of the function is $f(x)\to-\infty$ then the function grows up to $x_0=-1$, so $x_0$ will be an internal maximum point in the interval $[-2,0]$; this means that we fall into the first case and we will have to apply the formula (eq:metodo-di-laplace-termine-dominante).

\begin{equation*}
    \int_{-2}^0 x^m h(x)dx\simeq(-1)^me^\lambda\sqrt{\frac{2\pi}{6\lambda}}=(-1)^me^\lambda\sqrt{\frac{\pi}{3\lambda}}
\end{equation*}

In the second interval considered, $x_0=-1$ constitutes an end, so we fall into the second case and therefore we will have to apply the formula (eq:metodo-di-laplace-caso-2):

\begin{equation*}
    \int_{-1}^0 x^m h(x)dx\simeq(-1)^me^\lambda\sqrt{\frac{\pi}{12\lambda}}
\end{equation*}

Since $x=0$ is a minimum point, then $f(x)$ in the interval $[0,1]$ is increasing, so the maximum point will be precisely the end $x=1$ which is not, however, a stationary point for the function. We are therefore in the third case and we must apply the (eq:metodo-di-laplace-caso-3):

\begin{equation*}
    \int_0^1 x^m h(x)dx\simeq\frac{e^{5\lambda}}{12\lambda}
\end{equation*}

5.4 Applications of the Laplace method

5.4.1 Stirling’s formula

The Laplace method is also useful for understanding how quickly the factorial of a number \(n\) grows as \(n\) itself grows.

The analytical definition of the factorial is given by the Euler’s Gamma function:

\[\begin{equation} \Gamma(\lambda+1)=\int_0^\infty e^{-x}x^\lambda dx \end{equation}\]

Show that

Solution. \[\begin{equation*} \Gamma(\lambda+1)=\lambda\Gamma(\lambda) \end{equation*}\]

*from which it follows* $\lambda!=\Gamma(\lambda+1)$.

Integrating by parts, one obtains:

\begin{equation*}
    \Gamma(\lambda+1)=\int_0^\infty e^{-x}x^\lambda dx=\cancel{-e^{-x}x^\lambda\bigg|_0^\infty}+\lambda\int_0^\infty e^{-x}x^{\lambda-1}dx=\lambda\Gamma(\lambda)
\end{equation*}

To return to the cases seen in the Laplace method, it is necessary to write \(x^\lambda\) in exponential form:

\[\begin{equation}{} \Gamma(\lambda+1)=\int_0^\infty e^{-x}e^{\lambda\ln x} dx \end{equation}\]

The point is that by choosing \(g(x)=e^{-x}\) and \(f(x)=\ln x\) it is not possible to apply the Laplace method, because the logarithm does not have a specific peak but grows indefinitely as \(x\) grows.

What is convenient to do is to set \(x=\lambda t\) so as to have

\[\begin{equation*} \Gamma(\lambda+1)=\int_0^\infty e^{-\lambda t}\lambda^\lambda t^\lambda\lambda dt=\lambda^{\lambda+1}\int_0^\infty t^\lambda e^{-\lambda t}dt \end{equation*}\]

In this case too, the problem arises when considering \(f(t)=-t\). However, if we repeat the step written in (eq:metodo-di-laplace-formula-di-stirling-base-comune) we will have:

\[\begin{equation*} \Gamma(\lambda+1)=\lambda^{\lambda+1}\int_0^\infty e^{\lambda\ln t}e^{-\lambda t}dt=\lambda^{\lambda+1}\int_0^\infty e^{\lambda(\ln t-t)}dt \end{equation*}\]

We note that \(f(t)=\ln t-t\) is a decreasing function for \(t\to\infty\) but has a stationary point at

\[\begin{equation*} f'(t)=\frac{1}{t}-1=0 \ \Longrightarrow \ t=1 \end{equation*}\]

It is easily verified that this point is a maximum, being

\[\begin{equation*} f''(t)=-\frac{1}{t^2} \ \Longrightarrow \ f''(1)=-1 \end{equation*}\]

This means that we can consider \(g(t)=1\) and \(f(t)=\ln t-t\) and apply the Laplace method which falls into the first case:

\[\begin{equation} \boxed{\lambda!=\Gamma(\lambda+1)\simeq\lambda^{\lambda+1}e^{-\lambda}\sqrt{\frac{2\pi}{\lambda}}} \end{equation}\]

Using the solution of exercise (ex:metodo-di-laplace-sviluppi-successivi) we can improve the estimate:

\[\begin{equation*} \lambda!\simeq\lambda^{\lambda+1}e^{-\lambda}\sqrt{\frac{2\pi}{\lambda}}\left(1+\frac{1}{2\lambda}\left[\frac{1}{4}\left(\frac{5\cdot4}{3}+\frac{-6}{1}\right)\right]\right)=\lambda^{\lambda+1}e^{-\lambda}\sqrt{\frac{2\pi}{\lambda}}\left(1+\frac{1}{12\lambda}\right) \end{equation*}\]

5.4.2 Lp norm

From real analysis we know that the \(p\)-norm of a function \(g(t)\) belonging to the space \(L^p([a,b])\) is defined as

\[\begin{equation}{} \norm{h}_p=\left(\int_a^b\abs{h(x)}^pdx\right)^{\frac{1}{p}} \end{equation}\]

We will see how through the Laplace method we are able to study the behavior of this norm in the limit for \(p\to\infty\), that is, when the space becomes infinite-dimensional.

First, let’s define the integral:

\[\begin{equation*} I(p)=\int_a^b\abs{h(x)}^pdx \end{equation*}\]

from which it follows that \(\norm{h}_p=I(p)^{\frac{1}{p}}\).

Again, it is useful to write the function in exponential form:

\[\begin{equation*} I(p)=\int_a^be^{p\ln\abs{h(x)}}dx \end{equation*}\]

To apply the Laplace method, it is necessary that \(\ln\abs{h(x)}\) be differentiable in the whole interval \([a,b]\), but this turns out to be true in the case where \(h(x)\) does not vanish at some point, since the logarithm is not defined at \(0\). However, returning to equation (eq:metodo-di-laplace-norma-p), we note that for very small \(h(x)\) the contribution to the integral becomes negligible in the limit for \(p\to\infty\), so we will neglect this discontinuity at the first order of the expansion.

Let’s therefore proceed with the calculation. First, let’s calculate the second derivative of \(f(x)=\ln\abs{h(x)}\):

\[\begin{equation*} f'(x)=\frac{h'(x)}{h(x)} \ \Longrightarrow \ f''(x)=\frac{h''(x)}{h(x)}-\frac{h'(x)^2}{h(x)^2}=\frac{h''(x)h(x)-h'(x)^2}{h(x)^2} \end{equation*}\]

Remembering that \(h'\left(x_0\right)=0\):

\[\begin{equation*} \abs{f''\left(x_0\right)}=\abs{\frac{h''\left(x_0\right)}{h\left(x_0\right)}}=\frac{\abs{h''\left(x_0\right)}}{\abs{h\left(x_0\right)}} \end{equation*}\]

Since the logarithm is a monotonically increasing function, the maximum \(x_0\) for \(h(x)\) will also be so for \(\ln\abs{h(x)}\). Assuming that this maximum is within \([a,b]\), we can substitute the functions \(g(x)=1\) and \(f(x)=\ln\abs{h(x)}\) in equation (eq:metodo-di-laplace-termine-dominante).

\[\begin{equation*} I(p)\simeq e^{p\ln\abs{h\left(x_0\right)}}\sqrt{\frac{2\pi\abs{h\left(x_0\right)}}{p\abs{h''\left(x_0\right)}}}=\abs{h\left(x_0\right)}^p\sqrt{\frac{2\pi\abs{h\left(x_0\right)}}{p\abs{h''\left(x_0\right)}}} \end{equation*}\]

Let’s now try to calculate the norm:

\[\begin{equation*} \norm{g}_p=I(p)^{\frac{1}{p}}\simeq\abs{h\left(x_o\right)}\left(\sqrt{\frac{2\pi\abs{h\left(x_0\right)}}{p\abs{h''\left(x_0\right)}}}\right)^{\frac{1}{p}} \end{equation*}\]

Let’s set \(\alpha=\sqrt{\frac{2\pi\abs{h\left(x_0\right)}}{\abs{h''\left(x_0\right)}}}\) and express in exponential form all the terms with an exponent:

\[\begin{equation*} \norm{g}_p\simeq\abs{h\left(x_0\right)}\alpha^{\frac{1}{p}}p^{-\frac{1}{2p}}\simeq\abs{h\left(x_0\right)}e^{\frac{\ln\alpha}{p}}e^{-\frac{\ln p}{2p}} \end{equation*}\]

Let’s expand the exponentials in series, stopping at the first order:

\[\begin{align*} \norm{g}_p&\simeq\abs{h\left(x_0\right)}\left(1+\frac{\ln\alpha}{p}+\mathcal{O}\left(\frac{1}{p^2}\right)\right)\left(1-\frac{\ln p}{2p}+\mathcal{O}\left(\frac{1}{p^2}\right)\right)=\\ &=\abs{h\left(x_0\right)}\left(1-\frac{\ln p}{2p}+\mathcal{O}\left(\frac{\ln p}{p}\right)\right) \end{align*}\]

We find

\[\begin{equation*} \norm{g}_p\simeq\max_{x\in[a,b]}\\abs{h(x)}\left(1-\frac{\ln p}{2p}+\mathcal{O}\left(\frac{\ln p}{p}\right)\right) \end{equation*}\]

which corresponds precisely to the norm of the space \(L^\infty\), also considering the additional corrective terms due to the series expansions.

# The Laplace method

In many situations it is necessary to calculate complicated integrals whose form is

\begin{equation}{}
    I(\lambda)=\int_{x_1}^{x_2}g(x)e^{\lambda f(x)}dx
\end{equation}

where $f(x)$ and $g(x)$ are real and smooth functions\footnote{By *smooth* we mean functions that are infinitely differentiable.} and $\lambda$ is a real parameter.

Although it is impossible to calculate $I(\lambda)$ with a fixed $\lambda$, under appropriate conditions it is possible to approximate it well in the limit for $\lambda\to\infty$: the idea of the **Laplace method**, in fact, is that the major contribution to the integral as $\lambda$ increases comes from the neighborhood of the point where $f(x)$ assumes its maximum value.

## First case: internal point

Let $x_1<x_0<x_2$ with $f'\left(x_0\right)=0$ being the maximum point for the function in the whole interval; for the same reason we will have that $f''\left(x_0\right)<0$.

<!-- TikZ Figure: ../images/Figure/metodo-di-laplace-lambda-crescente.tex -->

As we expect, the major contribution will come in the neighborhood of $x_0$, so it is useful to expand $f(x)$ in series precisely at that point:

\begin{equation*}
    f(x)=f\left(x_0\right)+\cancel{f'\left(x_0\right)\left(x-x_0\right)}+\frac{f''\left(x_0\right)}{2}\left(x-x_0\right)^2+\mathcal{O}\left(\abs{x-x_0}^3\right)
\end{equation*}

Substituting in (eq:metodo-di-laplace-integrale-iniziale):

\begin{equation*}
    I(\lambda)=\int_{x_1}^{x_2}g(x)e^{\lambda\left[f\left(x_0\right)+\frac{f''\left(x_0\right)}{2}\left(x-x_0\right)^2+\mathcal{O}\left(\abs{x-x_0}^3\right)\right]}dx
\end{equation*}

In the limit for $x\to x_0$ we will have $g(x)\to g\left(x_0\right)$ and all the terms $\mathcal{O}\left(\abs{x-x_0}^3\right)$ can be considered null:

\begin{equation*}
    I(\lambda)\simeq g\left(x_0\right)\int_{x_1}^{x_2}e^{\lambda\left[f\left(x_0\right)+\frac{f''\left(x_0\right)}{2}\left(x-x_0\right)^2\right]}dx=g\left(x_0\right)e^{\lambda f\left(x_0\right)}\int_{x_1}^{x_2}e^{\frac{\lambda f''\left(x_0\right)}{2}\left(x-x_0\right)^2}dx
\end{equation*}

Furthermore, remembering that $f''\left(x_0\right)<0$, we can write:

\begin{equation*}
    I(\lambda)\simeq g\left(x_0\right)e^{\lambda f\left(x_0\right)}\int_{x_1}^{x_2}e^{-\frac{\lambda \abs{f''\left(x_0\right)}}{2}\left(x-x_0\right)^2}dx
\end{equation*}

At this point we set

\begin{equation}{}
    s=\sqrt{\frac{\lambda\abs{f''\left(x_0\right)}}{2}}\left(x-x_0\right)
\end{equation}

so as to have a Gaussian-type exponential in the integral:

\begin{equation}{}
    I(\lambda)\simeq g\left(x_0\right)e^{\lambda f\left(x_0\right)}\sqrt{\frac{2}{\lambda\abs{f''\left(x_0\right)}}}\int_{s_1}^{s_2}e^{-s^2}ds
\end{equation}

The limits of the integral are given by the substitution in equation (eq:metodo-di-laplace-sostituzione-s) of $x=x_1$ in one case and of $x=x_2$ in the other, so we will have $s_1<0$ and $s_2>0$. Since the two limits are proportional to $\sqrt{\lambda}$ in the limit for $\lambda\to\infty$ we have:

\begin{equation*}
    \int_{s_1}^{s_2}e^{-s^2}ds \ \xrightarrow{\lambda\to\infty} \ \int_{-\infty}^{+\infty}e^{-s^2}ds=\sqrt{\pi}
\end{equation*}

We can therefore approximate the integral to

\begin{equation}{}
    \boxed{I(\lambda)\simeq g\left(x_0\right)e^{\lambda f\left(x_0\right)}\sqrt{\frac{2\pi}{\lambda\abs{f''\left(x_0\right)}}}}
\end{equation}

This is the dominant term, that is, the one that provides the major contribution in the limit for $\lambda\to\infty$: considering the successive terms in the expansion of $f(x)$ and $g(x)$ it is also possible to obtain the other contributions, as we will see below in exercise (ex:metodo-di-laplace-sviluppi-successivi).

    \begin{equation*}
        \int_{-\infty}^{+\infty}z^ne^{-\frac{\lambda\abs{f_0''}}{2}z^2}dz
    \end{equation*}

    already considering the limit $\lambda\to\infty$.

    We immediately understand that if $n$ is odd the integral will be zero, as it is an integral in a symmetric interval of an odd function.

    Considering therefore only even powers, we can rewrite the integral in the following way:

    \begin{align*}
        \int_{-\infty}^{+\infty}z^ne^{-\frac{a}{2}z^2}dz&=\frac{\partial^{\frac{n}{2}}}{\partial\left(-\frac{a}{2}\right)^{\frac{n}{2}}}\int_{-\infty}^{+\infty}e^{-\frac{a}{2}z^2}dz=(-2)^{\frac{n}{2}}\frac{\partial^{\frac{n}{2}}}{\partial a^{\frac{n}{2}}}\int_{-\infty}^{+\infty}e^{-\frac{a}{2}z^2}dz=\\
        &=(-2)^{\frac{n}{2}}\frac{\partial^{\frac{n}{2}}}{\partial a^{\frac{n}{2}}}\sqrt{\frac{2\pi}{a}}
    \end{align*}

    having set $a=\lambda\abs{f_0''}$.

    To find the general formula, let's calculate the results for the smallest $n$, remembering that $n$ can only be even:

    \begin{align*}
        &n=0 \ \Longrightarrow \ (-2)^0\frac{\partial^0}{\partial a^0}\sqrt{\frac{2\pi}{a}}=\sqrt{\frac{2\pi}{a}}\\
        &n=2 \ \Longrightarrow \ (-2)\frac{\partial}{\partial a}\sqrt{\frac{2\pi}{a}}=-2\left(-\frac{1}{2}\right)\sqrt{\frac{2\pi}{a^3}}=\sqrt{\frac{2\pi}{a^3}}\\
        &n=4 \ \Longrightarrow \ (-2)^2\frac{\partial^2}{\partial a^2}\sqrt{\frac{2\pi}{a}}=4\left(-\frac{1}{2}\right)\left(-\frac{3}{2}\right)\sqrt{\frac{2\pi}{a^5}}=3\sqrt{\frac{2\pi}{a^5}}\\
        &n=6 \ \Longrightarrow \ (-2)^3\frac{\partial^3}{\partial a^3}\sqrt{\frac{2\pi}{a}}=-8\left(-\frac{1}{2}\right)\left(-\frac{3}{2}\right)\left(-\frac{5}{2}\right)\sqrt{\frac{2\pi}{a^7}}=15\sqrt{\frac{2\pi}{a^7}}
    \end{align*}

    \end{equation}

    Substituting $a=\lambda\abs{f_0''}$ we find

    \begin{equation}{}
        \int_{-\infty}^{+\infty}z^ne^{-\frac{\lambda\abs{f_0''}}{2}z^2}dz=\frac{(n-1)!!}{\lambda^{\frac{n}{2}}\abs{f_0''}^{\frac{n}{2}}}\sqrt{\frac{2\pi}{\lambda\abs{f_0''}}}
    \end{equation}

## Third case: non-stationary end point

Let $x_0=x_1$ or $x_0=x_2$ but with $f'\left(x_0\right)\neq0$.

Since this time the first derivative does not vanish at $x_0$, the expansion of the function can be stopped at the first order:

\begin{equation*}
    f(x)=f\left(x_0\right)+f'\left(x_0\right)\left(x-x_0\right)+\mathcal{O}\left(\abs{x-x_0}^2\right)
\end{equation*}

At this point we proceed as in the previous cases. First, let's go to the limit for $x\to x_0$:

\begin{equation*}
    I(\lambda)\simeq g\left(x_0\right)e^{\lambda f\left(x_0\right)}\int_{x_1}^{x_2}e^{\lambda f'\left(x_0\right)\left(x-x_0\right)}dx
\end{equation*}

Without loss of generality, let's assume that $f'\left(x_0\right)<0$:

\begin{equation*}
    I(\lambda)\simeq g\left(x_0\right)e^{\lambda f\left(x_0\right)}\int_{x_1}^{x_2}e^{-\lambda\abs{f'\left(x_0\right)}\left(x-x_0\right)}dx
\end{equation*}

Now let's set

\begin{equation}{}
    s=\lambda\abs{f'\left(x_0\right)}\left(x-x_0\right)
\end{equation}

so as to have:

\begin{equation*}
    I(\lambda)\simeq g\left(x_0\right)\frac{e^{\lambda f\left(x_0\right)}}{\lambda\abs{f'\left(x_0\right)}}\int_{s_1}^{s_2}e^{-s}ds
\end{equation*}

Let's now assume that $x_0=x_1$; from (eq:metodo-di-laplace-sostituzione-s-caso-3) it is clear that $s_1=0$, and by sending $\lambda\to\infty$ we have instead that $s_2\to\infty$:

\begin{equation*}
    I(\lambda)\simeq g\left(x_0\right)\frac{e^{\lambda f\left(x_0\right)}}{\lambda\abs{f'\left(x_0\right)}}\int_0^\infty e^{-s}ds=g\left(x_0\right)\frac{e^{\lambda f\left(x_0\right)}}{\lambda\abs{f'\left(x_0\right)}}\left(-e^{-s}\right)\bigg|_0^\infty
\end{equation*}

\end{equation}

## Applications of the Laplace method

### Stirling's formula

The Laplace method is also useful for understanding how quickly the factorial of a number $n$ grows as $n$ itself grows.

The analytical definition of the factorial is given by the **Euler's Gamma function**:

\begin{equation}
    \Gamma(\lambda+1)=\int_0^\infty e^{-x}x^\lambda dx
\end{equation}

::: {.exercise}
*Show that*

:::

::: {.solution}
\begin{equation*}
        \Gamma(\lambda+1)=\lambda\Gamma(\lambda)
    \end{equation*}

    *from which it follows* $\lambda!=\Gamma(\lambda+1)$.

    Integrating by parts, one obtains:

    \begin{equation*}
        \Gamma(\lambda+1)=\int_0^\infty e^{-x}x^\lambda dx=\cancel{-e^{-x}x^\lambda\bigg|_0^\infty}+\lambda\int_0^\infty e^{-x}x^{\lambda-1}dx=\lambda\Gamma(\lambda)
    \end{equation*}

:::

To return to the cases seen in the Laplace method, it is necessary to write $x^\lambda$ in exponential form:

\begin{equation}{}
    \Gamma(\lambda+1)=\int_0^\infty e^{-x}e^{\lambda\ln x} dx
\end{equation}

The point is that by choosing $g(x)=e^{-x}$ and $f(x)=\ln x$ it is not possible to apply the Laplace method, because the logarithm does not have a specific peak but grows indefinitely as $x$ grows.

What is convenient to do is to set $x=\lambda t$ so as to have

\begin{equation*}
    \Gamma(\lambda+1)=\int_0^\infty e^{-\lambda t}\lambda^\lambda t^\lambda\lambda dt=\lambda^{\lambda+1}\int_0^\infty t^\lambda e^{-\lambda t}dt
\end{equation*}

In this case too, the problem arises when considering $f(t)=-t$. However, if we repeat the step written in (eq:metodo-di-laplace-formula-di-stirling-base-comune) we will have:

\begin{equation*}
    \Gamma(\lambda+1)=\lambda^{\lambda+1}\int_0^\infty e^{\lambda\ln t}e^{-\lambda t}dt=\lambda^{\lambda+1}\int_0^\infty e^{\lambda(\ln t-t)}dt
\end{equation*}

We note that $f(t)=\ln t-t$ is a decreasing function for $t\to\infty$ but has a stationary point at

\begin{equation*}
    f'(t)=\frac{1}{t}-1=0 \ \Longrightarrow \ t=1
\end{equation*}

It is easily verified that this point is a maximum, being

\begin{equation*}
    f''(t)=-\frac{1}{t^2} \ \Longrightarrow \ f''(1)=-1
\end{equation*}

This means that we can consider $g(t)=1$ and $f(t)=\ln t-t$ and apply the Laplace method which falls into the first case:

\begin{equation}
    \boxed{\lambda!=\Gamma(\lambda+1)\simeq\lambda^{\lambda+1}e^{-\lambda}\sqrt{\frac{2\pi}{\lambda}}}
\end{equation}

Using the solution of exercise (ex:metodo-di-laplace-sviluppi-successivi) we can improve the estimate:

\begin{equation*}
    \lambda!\simeq\lambda^{\lambda+1}e^{-\lambda}\sqrt{\frac{2\pi}{\lambda}}\left(1+\frac{1}{2\lambda}\left[\frac{1}{4}\left(\frac{5\cdot4}{3}+\frac{-6}{1}\right)\right]\right)=\lambda^{\lambda+1}e^{-\lambda}\sqrt{\frac{2\pi}{\lambda}}\left(1+\frac{1}{12\lambda}\right)
\end{equation*}

### Lp norm

From real analysis we know that the $p$-norm of a function $g(t)$ belonging to the space $L^p([a,b])$ is defined as

\begin{equation}{}
    \norm{h}_p=\left(\int_a^b\abs{h(x)}^pdx\right)^{\frac{1}{p}}
\end{equation}

We will see how through the Laplace method we are able to study the behavior of this norm in the limit for $p\to\infty$, that is, when the space becomes infinite-dimensional.

First, let's define the integral:

\begin{equation*}
    I(p)=\int_a^b\abs{h(x)}^pdx
\end{equation*}

from which it follows that $\norm{h}_p=I(p)^{\frac{1}{p}}$.

Again, it is useful to write the function in exponential form:

\begin{equation*}
    I(p)=\int_a^be^{p\ln\abs{h(x)}}dx
\end{equation*}

To apply the Laplace method, it is necessary that $\ln\abs{h(x)}$ be differentiable in the whole interval $[a,b]$, but this turns out to be true in the case where $h(x)$ does not vanish at some point, since the logarithm is not defined at $0$. However, returning to equation (eq:metodo-di-laplace-norma-p), we note that for very small $h(x)$ the contribution to the integral becomes negligible in the limit for $p\to\infty$, so we will neglect this discontinuity at the first order of the expansion.

Let's therefore proceed with the calculation. First, let's calculate the second derivative of $f(x)=\ln\abs{h(x)}$:

\begin{equation*}
    f'(x)=\frac{h'(x)}{h(x)} \ \Longrightarrow \ f''(x)=\frac{h''(x)}{h(x)}-\frac{h'(x)^2}{h(x)^2}=\frac{h''(x)h(x)-h'(x)^2}{h(x)^2}
\end{equation*}

Remembering that $h'\left(x_0\right)=0$:

\begin{equation*}
    \abs{f''\left(x_0\right)}=\abs{\frac{h''\left(x_0\right)}{h\left(x_0\right)}}=\frac{\abs{h''\left(x_0\right)}}{\abs{h\left(x_0\right)}}
\end{equation*}

Since the logarithm is a monotonically increasing function, the maximum $x_0$ for $h(x)$ will also be so for $\ln\abs{h(x)}$. Assuming that this maximum is within $[a,b]$, we can substitute the functions $g(x)=1$ and $f(x)=\ln\abs{h(x)}$ in equation (eq:metodo-di-laplace-termine-dominante).

\begin{equation*}
    I(p)\simeq e^{p\ln\abs{h\left(x_0\right)}}\sqrt{\frac{2\pi\abs{h\left(x_0\right)}}{p\abs{h''\left(x_0\right)}}}=\abs{h\left(x_0\right)}^p\sqrt{\frac{2\pi\abs{h\left(x_0\right)}}{p\abs{h''\left(x_0\right)}}}
\end{equation*}

Let's now try to calculate the norm:

\begin{equation*}
    \norm{g}_p=I(p)^{\frac{1}{p}}\simeq\abs{h\left(x_o\right)}\left(\sqrt{\frac{2\pi\abs{h\left(x_0\right)}}{p\abs{h''\left(x_0\right)}}}\right)^{\frac{1}{p}}
\end{equation*}

Let's set $\alpha=\sqrt{\frac{2\pi\abs{h\left(x_0\right)}}{\abs{h''\left(x_0\right)}}}$ and express in exponential form all the terms with an exponent:

\begin{equation*}
    \norm{g}_p\simeq\abs{h\left(x_0\right)}\alpha^{\frac{1}{p}}p^{-\frac{1}{2p}}\simeq\abs{h\left(x_0\right)}e^{\frac{\ln\alpha}{p}}e^{-\frac{\ln p}{2p}}
\end{equation*}

Let's expand the exponentials in series, stopping at the first order:

\begin{align*}
    \norm{g}_p&\simeq\abs{h\left(x_0\right)}\left(1+\frac{\ln\alpha}{p}+\mathcal{O}\left(\frac{1}{p^2}\right)\right)\left(1-\frac{\ln p}{2p}+\mathcal{O}\left(\frac{1}{p^2}\right)\right)=\\
    &=\abs{h\left(x_0\right)}\left(1-\frac{\ln p}{2p}+\mathcal{O}\left(\frac{\ln p}{p}\right)\right)
\end{align*}

We find

\begin{equation*}
    \norm{g}_p\simeq\max_{x\in[a,b]}\\abs{h(x)}\left(1-\frac{\ln p}{2p}+\mathcal{O}\left(\frac{\ln p}{p}\right)\right)
\end{equation*}

which corresponds precisely to the norm of the space $L^\infty$, also considering the additional corrective terms due to the series expansions.\footnote{A curious fact is that the first corrective term does not depend on the point $x_0$, since this is contained in $\alpha$, and it is necessary to go to the second order to find a dependence on it.}
