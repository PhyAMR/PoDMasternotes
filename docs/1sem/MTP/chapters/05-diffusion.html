7 Diffusion

To introduce the stochastic processes that we will see later, it is necessary to delve deeper into the phenomenon of diffusion, which we have already mentioned in the previous chapter. In particular, we will derive the diffusion equation in different contexts and through two independent methods: the one related to the mesoscopic scale, which describes observable phenomena such as Brownian motion, and the one related to the microscopic scale, which will lead directly to one of the pillars in the study of stochastic processes, the random walk.

7.1 Einstein’s derivation

Let’s consider a long, thin tube in the \(x\) direction filled with water, into which ink is injected at the position \(x=0\) at time \(t=0\).

Let’s focus on a generic region \(V(x,t)\) inside the tube, so that we can define the following quantity:

\[\begin{equation} w(x,t)=\frac{\text{Number of particles in} \ V(x,t)}{V(x,t)} \end{equation}\]

This is therefore the particle density of ink at position \(x\) at time \(t\), assuming that \(V(x,t)\) is small enough to be identified by a single value of \(x\).

We can define the probability that a particle is in a certain region \(A\) as

\[\begin{equation} \int_Aw(x,t)dx \end{equation}\]

For it to actually correspond to a probability, it must be normalized, therefore it is necessary that

\[\begin{equation*} \int_{-\infty}^{+\infty}w(x,t)dx=1 \end{equation*}\]

We can also easily establish the initial condition knowing that at time \(t=0\) the entire ink is concentrated at \(x=0\):

\[\begin{equation} w(x,0)=\delta(x) \end{equation}\]

Our goal is to find a law that tells us how \(w(x,t)\) varies over time. We will therefore have to derive a formula that can express \(w(x,t+\Delta t)\) as a function of a quantity dependent on \(t\), and to do this we will need some important assumptions that we will see below.

Let’s consider the density \(w(x-y,t)\) associated with all the particles located at a distance \(y\) from the coordinate \(x\) at time \(t\). The first necessary assumption is the following:

The probability that a particle moves a distance \(y\) in a time interval \(\Delta t\) is independent of the starting point considered

Let’s denote by \(f(y,\Delta t)\) the pdf relative to this probability, which as we have said depends only on \(y\) and not also on \(x\). In this case, the density \(w(x,t+\Delta t)\) can easily be expressed as

\[\begin{equation} w(x,t+\Delta t)=\int_{-\infty}^{+\infty}w(x-y,t)f(y,\Delta t)dy \end{equation}\]

Intuitively we can say that the density will be obtained from the contribution of all possible values of \(y\), each of which is expressed by the product of the density of the particles at \(x-y\) multiplied by the probability \(f(y,\Delta t)dy\) that they move by \(y\).

To solve the integral equation we make a second assumption:

The function \(f(y,\Delta t)\) decays quickly as \(y\) increases

This means that the major contributions come from the regions near \(x\), since at greater distances the probability of a displacement occurring becomes very low. This justifies us in expanding \(w(x-y,t)\) in a Taylor series with respect to \(x\):

\[\begin{equation*} w(x-y,t)=w(x,t)-\frac{\partial w(x,t)}{\partial x}y+\frac{1}{2}\frac{\partial^2w(x,t)}{\partial x^2}y^2+\mathcal{O}\left(y^3\right) \end{equation*}\]

Let’s substitute on the right-hand side of (eq:diffusione-derivazione-einstein-equazione-integrale), temporarily ignoring the higher-order terms:

\[\begin{equation*} w(x,t)\int_{-\infty}^{+\infty}f(y,\Delta t)dy-\frac{\partial w}{\partial x}\int_{-\infty}^{+\infty}yf(y,\Delta t)dy+\frac{1}{2}\frac{\partial^2w}{\partial x^2}\int_{-\infty}^{+\infty}y^2f(y,\Delta t)dy \end{equation*}\]

The first integral is clearly equal to \(1\) since \(f(y,\Delta t)dy\) is a probability. As for the other two integrals, however, two other assumptions come to our aid; the first is the following:

Displacements with respect to a coordinate \(x\) are equally probable from the left and from the right

Translated into a formula:

\[\begin{equation*} f(-y,\Delta t)=f(y,\Delta t) \end{equation*}\]

Since it is a symmetric function in \(y\), therefore, the integral with the linear term in \(y\) will clearly be zero.

As for the last one, instead, the fourth and final assumption is the following:

The second moment of the distribution \(f(y,\Delta t)\) is directly proportional to \(\Delta t\)

Let \(D\) be the proportionality constant, it follows that

\[\begin{equation} \int_{-\infty}^{+\infty}y^2f(y,\Delta t)dy=D\Delta t \end{equation}\]

Putting everything together we get

\[\begin{equation*} w(x,t+\Delta t)=w(x,t)+\frac{1}{2}\frac{\partial^2w}{\partial x^2}D\Delta t+\mathcal{O}\left(y^3\right) \end{equation*}\]

Let’s isolate the incremental ratio with respect to time on the left-hand side:

\[\begin{equation} \frac{w(x,t+\Delta t)-w(x,t)}{\Delta t}=\frac{D}{2}\frac{\partial^2w}{\partial x^2}+\mathcal{O}\left(y^3\right) \end{equation}\]

At this point, going to the limit \(\Delta t\to0\) we obtain the diffusion equation, also called the heat equation because it describes the analogous thermodynamic phenomenon:

\[\begin{equation} \boxed{\frac{\partial w}{\partial t}=\frac{D}{2}\frac{\partial^2w}{\partial x^2}} \end{equation}\]

This is a partial differential equation (PDE), which can be solved by imposing the initial condition seen in equation (eq:diffusione-derivazione-einstein-condizione-iniziale).

All the reasoning done can be easily extended to a greater number of spatial dimensions, obtaining the formula

\[\begin{equation} \boxed{\frac{\partial w}{\partial t}=\frac{D}{2}\nabla^2w} \end{equation}\]

7.1.1 Solution with dilation symmetry

Finding the solution of a PDE, in general, is not simple; to facilitate the calculations it is possible to exploit a symmetry of the system, called dilation symmetry, for which the solution to (eq:diffusione-derivazione-einstein-equazione-generale) is such as to preserve the ratio \(\frac{x}{\sqrt{t}}\).

To prove this, let’s suppose we go from the pair of coordinates \((x,t)\) to the pair of coordinates \(\left(\lambda x,\lambda^2t\right)\) and substitute in (eq:diffusione-derivazione-einstein-equazione-1D):

\[\begin{equation*} \frac{\partial}{\partial t}w\left(\lambda x,\lambda^2t\right)=\frac{D}{2}\frac{\partial^2}{\partial x^2}w\left(\lambda x,\lambda^2t\right) \end{equation*}\]

Let’s use the chain rule on both the left and the right:

\[\begin{equation*} \frac{\partial}{\partial t}=\frac{\partial\left(\lambda^2t\right)}{\partial t}\frac{\partial}{\partial\left(\lambda^2t\right)}=\lambda^2\frac{\partial}{\partial\left(\lambda^2t\right)} \frac{\partial}{\partial x}=\frac{\partial(\lambda x)}{\partial x}\frac{\partial }{\partial(\lambda x)}=\lambda\frac{\partial}{\partial(\lambda x)} \end{equation*}\]

Substituting:

\[\begin{equation*} \cancel{\lambda^2}\frac{\partial}{\partial\left(\lambda^2 t\right)}w\left(\lambda x,\lambda^2t\right)=\frac{D}{2}\cancel{\lambda^2}\frac{\partial^2}{\partial (\lambda x)^2}w\left(\lambda x,\lambda^2t\right) \end{equation*}\]

which is the same form as the starting one. This means that both \(w(x,t)\) and \(w\left(\lambda x,\lambda^2t\right)\) are solutions of the differential equation, and therefore the density function is such as to leave the ratio \(\frac{x^2}{t}\) unchanged since

\[\begin{equation*} \frac{x'^2}{t'}=\frac{\cancel{\lambda^2}x^2}{\cancel{\lambda^2}t}=\frac{x^2}{t} \end{equation*}\]

or, analogously, the ratio \(\frac{x}{\sqrt{t}}\), as written above.

This invariance constrains the functional form of \(w(x,t)\) to the dependence on the parameter \(\frac{x}{\sqrt{t}}\), which can be considered as a single variable:

\[\begin{equation*} w(x,t)\equiv v\left(\frac{x}{\sqrt{t}}\right) \end{equation*}\]

Let’s therefore rewrite the derivatives by setting \(z=\frac{x}{\sqrt{t}}\):

\[\begin{equation*} \frac{\partial}{\partial t}w\equiv\frac{\partial}{\partial t}v=\frac{dv}{dz}\frac{\partial z}{\partial t}=-\frac{x}{2\sqrt{t^3}}\frac{dv}{dz} \end{equation*}\]

\[\begin{equation*} \frac{\partial^2}{\partial x^2}w\equiv\frac{\partial^2}{\partial x^2}v=\frac{\partial}{\partial x}\left(\frac{\partial}{\partial x}v\right)=\frac{\partial}{\partial x}\left(\frac{dv}{dz}\frac{\partial z}{\partial x}\right)=\frac{d^2v}{dz^2}\left(\frac{\partial z}{\partial x}\right)^2+\frac{dv}{dz}\cancel{\frac{\partial^2z}{\partial x^2}}=\frac{1}{t}\frac{d^2v}{dz^2} \end{equation*}\]

Let’s substitute in the differential equation:

\[\begin{equation*} -\frac{x}{2\sqrt{t^3}}\frac{dv}{dz}=\frac{D}{2t}\frac{d^2v}{dz^2} \ \Longrightarrow \ \ -\frac{x}{\sqrt{t}}\frac{dv}{dz}=D\frac{d^2v}{dz^2} \end{equation*}\]

Remembering that \(\frac{x}{\sqrt{t}}=z\):

\[\begin{equation} \boxed{\frac{d^2v}{dz^2}+\frac{z}{D}\frac{dv}{dz}=0} \end{equation}\]

This is an ordinary differential equation (ODE), which is much simpler to solve. We just need to set \(\rho=\frac{dv}{dz}\) to obtain a first-order differential equation:

\[\begin{equation*} \frac{d\rho}{dz}+\frac{z}{D}\rho=0 \ \Longrightarrow \ \ \int\frac{d\rho}{\rho}=-\frac{1}{D}\int zdz \ \Longrightarrow \ \ \log\rho=-\frac{z^2}{2D}+c \ \Longrightarrow \ \rho=ae^{-\frac{z^2}{2D}} \end{equation*}\]

being \(a=e^c\).

Returning to \(v\):

\[\begin{equation*} \frac{dv}{dz}=ae^{-\frac{z^2}{2D}} \ \Longrightarrow \ \ v(z)=a\int_0^ze^{-\frac{z'^2}{2D}}dz'+b \end{equation*}\]

But knowing that \(v(z)\equiv w(x,t)\):

\[\begin{equation} \boxed{w(x,t)=\int_0^{\frac{x}{\sqrt{t}}}e^{-\frac{z^2}{2D}}dz+b} \end{equation}\]

The integral is not solvable, so there is no real analytical form of the solution, but in any case it is a valid solution for the equation (eq:diffusione-derivazione-einstein-equazione-1D).

We can still calculate specific values, such as the initial density for \(t\to0\):

\[\begin{equation*} \lim_{t\to0}w(x,t)=\lim_{t\to0}\int_0^{\frac{x}{\sqrt{t}}}e^{-\frac{z^2}{2D}}dz+b=\int_0^\infty e^{-\frac{z^2}{2D}}dz+b=a\sqrt{\frac{\pi}{2D}}+b \end{equation*}\]

At \(x=0\), instead, the density is equal to

\[\begin{equation*} w(0,t)=\cancel{\int_0^0e^{-\frac{z^2}{2D}}dz}+b=b \end{equation*}\]

This solution, however, is not what is called fundamental, in the sense that it is not the one that allows all possible configurations: just note that the initial condition \(w(x,0)=\delta\left(x-x_0\right)\) is not possible with any combination of \(a\) and \(b\), since \(a\sqrt{\frac{\pi}{2D}}+b\) is a constant quantity unlike the Dirac delta. Later we will derive the fundamental solution, but first we need to explore the microscopic point of view.

7.2 Microscopic derivation

Moving from a mesoscopic to a microscopic scale, we will no longer approach a continuous system but a discrete one. Let’s therefore consider a one-dimensional lattice of step \(\Delta\ell\) in which a particle can only move at a unit of time distant \(\Delta t\) from the previous one.

Having discretized both time and space, we can write the two coordinates in the following way:

\[\begin{equation*} x_i=i\Delta\ell, i=0,\pm1,\pm2,\dots t_k=k\Delta t, k=0,1,2,\dots \end{equation*}\]

Let’s introduce the following probabilities:

Let’s impose that

\[\begin{equation} p_++p_-=1 \end{equation}\]

that is, that at time \(t_{k+1}\) the particle must necessarily move either one position back or one position forward, without any other possibility. In this case, for a particle to reach the position \(x_i\) at time \(t_{k+1}\) there are only two possibilities

Summing the probabilities relative to the two cases we obtain what is called the master equation:

\[\begin{equation} \boxed{W_i(k+1)=W_{i-1}(k)p_++W_{i+1}(k)p_-} \end{equation}\]

The process described here is commonly called a random walk, since it follows the random path of a particle that has a certain probability of moving in one direction or the other at each step. The master equation of a random walk does nothing more than make explicit how the probability for a coordinate \(x_i\) changes over time.

An important property to keep in mind is that the master equation must conserve normalization, that is, the sum \(\sum_iW_i(k)\) must be equal to \(1\) at all times \(t_k\). To verify this, we just need to place the summation in (eq:diffusion-master-equation):

\[\begin{equation*} \sum_{i=-\infty}^{+\infty}W_i(k+1)=\sum_{i=-\infty}^{+\infty}W_{i-1}(k)p_++\sum_{i=-\infty}^{+\infty}W_{i+1}(k)p_- \end{equation*}\]

Since \(i\) runs over infinite values, we can indicate both \(i+1\) and \(i-1\) with the same index \(j\):

\[\begin{equation*} \sum_{i=-\infty}^{+\infty}W_i(k+1)=\sum_{j=-\infty}^{+\infty}W_j(k)p_++\sum_{j=-\infty}^{+\infty}W_j(k)p_-=\left(p_++p_-\right)\sum_{j=-\infty}^{+\infty}W_j(k) \end{equation*}\]

From (eq:diffusione-normalizzazione-p-+-p–) it follows

\[\begin{equation*} \sum_{i=-\infty}^{+\infty}W_i(k+1)=\sum_{j=-\infty}^{+\infty}W_j(k) \end{equation*}\]

which is the initial thesis. This means that in a random walk, particles are not born from nothing and do not disappear into nothing, but remain within the lattice for the entire evolution of the system.

In general, a random walk is very simple to simulate by computer. By imposing the same parameters and the same initial conditions, in fact, the probability \(W_i(k)\) can be estimated as

\[\begin{equation*} W_i(k)=\frac{r}{R} \end{equation*}\]

where \(r\) is the number of simulations in which the particle is at \(x_i\) at time \(t_k\) and \(R\) is the total number of simulations.

Despite the simplicity with respect to numerical calculation, we will see below how to derive \(W_i(k)\) analytically in general, thus formally solving the master equation.

7.2.1 Solution with generating functions

Let’s define the following generating function:

\[\begin{equation} \tilde{W}(z,k)=\sum_{i=-\infty}^{+\infty}z^iW_i(k) \end{equation}\]

To write the master equation as a function of the generating function, we must multiply both members by \(z^i\) and sum over all possible indices \(i\):

\[\begin{equation*} \sum_{i=-\infty}^{+\infty}z^iW_i(k+1)=\sum_{i=-\infty}^{+\infty}z^iW_{i-1}(k)p_++\sum_{i=-\infty}^{+\infty}z^iW_{i+1}(k)p_- \end{equation*}\]

On the right-hand side, let’s multiply and divide each term by \(z\) so as to have the exponent \(i-1\) in one case and \(i+1\) in the other:

\[\begin{equation*} \sum_{i=-\infty}^{+\infty}z^iW_i(k+1)=zp_+\sum_{i=-\infty}^{+\infty}z^{i-1}W_{i-1}(k)+\frac{p_-}{z}\sum_{i=-\infty}^{+\infty}z^{i+1}W_{i+1}(k) \end{equation*}\]

At this point we can substitute the definition (eq:diffusione-funzione-generatrice-definizione):

\[\begin{equation*} \tilde{W}(z,k+1)=zp_+\tilde{W}(z,k)+\frac{p_-}{z}\tilde{W}(z,k)=\left(zp_++\frac{p_-}{z}\right)\tilde{W}(z,k) \end{equation*}\]

From this law it follows iteratively:

\[\begin{equation*} \tilde{W}(z,k+1)=\left(zp_++\frac{p_-}{z}\right)\tilde{W}(z,k)=\left(zp_++\frac{p_-}{z}\right)^2\tilde{W}(z,k-1)=\dots \end{equation*}\]

And therefore, in general:

\[\begin{equation} \tilde{W}(z,k)=\left(zp_++\frac{p_-}{z}\right)^k\tilde{W}(z,0) \end{equation}\]

Let’s remember that the variable \(k\) refers to the time coordinate \(t_k\). If the particle is at the coordinate \(x_{i_0}\) at time \(t=t_0\) then

\[\begin{equation*} W_i(0)=\delta_{i,i_0} \ \Longrightarrow \ \ \tilde{W}(z,0)=\sum_{i=-\infty}^{+\infty}z^i\delta_{i,i_0}=z^{i_0} \end{equation*}\]

We thus find the following expression for the generating function:

\[\begin{equation} \boxed{\tilde{W}(z,k)=\left(zp_++\frac{p_-}{z}\right)^kz^{i_0}} \end{equation}\]

It goes without saying that, by setting \(i_0=0\), it results

\[\begin{equation} \tilde{W}(z,k)=\left(zp_++\frac{p_-}{z}\right)^k \end{equation}\]

Having found an analytical expression for \(\tilde{W}(z,k)\) independent of \(W_i(k)\), we can use this to calculate the mean and variance associated with the probability distribution relative to the particle’s position.

First of all, let’s note that by deriving the generating function starting from the definition, it results:

\[\begin{equation*} \frac{d\tilde{W}}{dz}=\sum_{i=-\infty}^{+\infty}iz^{i-1}W_i(k) \end{equation*}\]

By setting \(z=1\) we find the average position:

\[\begin{equation} \frac{d\tilde{W}}{dz}\bigg|_{z=1}=\sum_{i=-\infty}^{+\infty}iW_i(k)=\langle i(k)\rangle\equiv\mu(k) \end{equation}\]

Let’s calculate the first member starting from (eq:diffusione-funzione-generatrice-espressione):

\[\begin{equation} \frac{d\tilde{W}}{dz}=k\left(zp_++\frac{p_-}{z}\right)^{k-1}\left(p_+-\frac{p_-}{z^2}\right)z^{i_0}+\left(zp_++\frac{p_-}{z}\right)^ki_0z^{i_0-1} \end{equation}\]

\[\begin{equation*} \frac{d\tilde{W}}{dz}\bigg|_{z=1}=k\left(p_++p_-\right)^{k-1}\left(p_+-p_-\right)+\left(p_++p_-\right)^ki_0 \end{equation*}\]

Remembering that \(p_++p_-=1\):

\[\begin{equation} \boxed{\mu(k)=k(p_+-p_-)+i_0} \end{equation}\]

Intuitively, the particle will tend to move over time by a quantity proportional to \(p_+-p_-\) with respect to the starting coordinate \(x_{i_0}\).

Let’s now move on to the second derivative of \(\tilde{W}(z,k)\):

\[\begin{equation*} \frac{d^2\tilde{W}}{dz^2}=\sum_{i=-\infty}^{+\infty}i(i-1)z^{i-2}W_i(k) \end{equation*}\]

From which it follows

\[\begin{equation*} \frac{d^2\tilde{W}}{dz^2}\bigg|_{z=1}=\sum_{i=-\infty}^{+\infty}i(i-1)W_i(k)=\langle i(i-1)\rangle=\left\langle i(k)^2-i(k)\right\rangle=\left\langle i(k)^2\right\rangle-\langle i(k)\rangle \end{equation*}\]

We know that the variance is given by:

\[\begin{equation*} \sigma(k)^2=\left\langle i(k)^2\right\rangle-\langle i(k)\rangle^2 \end{equation*}\]

Therefore, by adding and subtracting \(\langle i(k)\rangle\):

\[\begin{equation*} \sigma(k)^2=\left\langle i(k)^2\right\rangle-\langle i(k)\rangle+\langle i(k)\rangle-\langle i(k)\rangle^2=\frac{d^2\tilde{W}}{dz^2}\bigg|_{z=1}+\langle i(k)\rangle-\langle i(k)\rangle^2 \end{equation*}\]

All we have to do, therefore, is to calculate the second derivative of \(\tilde{W}(z,k)\); let’s start directly from (eq:diffusione-funzione-generatrice-derivata-prima), coloring each term differently to better visualize each contribution dependent on \(z\):

\[\begin{equation*} \frac{d}{dz}\left(\frac{d\tilde{W}}{dz}\right)=\frac{d}{dz}\left[\red{k\left(zp_++\frac{p_-}{z}\right)^{k-1}}\green{\left(p_+-\frac{p_-}{z^2}\right)}\orange{z^{i_0}}+\blue{\left(zp_++\frac{p_-}{z}\right)^k}\purple{i_0z^{i_0-1}}\right] \end{equation*}\]

Let’s now calculate each contribution separately:

\[\begin{align*} &\red{\raisebox{.5pt}{\textcircled{\raisebox{-.9pt} {1}}}} k\red{(k-1)\left(zp_++\frac{p_-}{z}\right)^{k-2}}\left(p_+-\frac{p_-}{z^2}\right)^{\red{2}}z^{i_0} \\ &\green{\raisebox{.5pt}{\textcircled{\raisebox{-.9pt} {2}}}} k\left(zp_++\frac{p_-}{z}\right)^{k-1}\green{\left(\frac{2p_-}{z^3}\right)}z^{i_0} \\ &\orange{\raisebox{.5pt}{\textcircled{\raisebox{-.9pt} {3}}}} k\left(zp_++\frac{p_-}{z}\right)^{k-1}\left(p_+-\frac{p_-}{z^2}\right)\orange{i_0z^{i_0-1}} \\ &\blue{\raisebox{.5pt}{\textcircled{\raisebox{-.9pt} {4}}}} \blue{k\left(zp_++\frac{p_-}{z}\right)^{k-1}\left(p_+-\frac{p_-}{z^2}\right)}i_0z^{i_0-1} \\ &\purple{\raisebox{.5pt}{\textcircled{\raisebox{-.9pt} {5}}}} \left(zp_++\frac{p_-}{z}\right)^k\purple{i_0\left(i_0-1\right)z^{i_0-2}} \end{align*}\]

Let’s now set \(z=1\):

\[\begin{align*} &\red{\raisebox{.5pt}{\textcircled{\raisebox{-.9pt} {1}}}} k(k-1)\left(p_++p_-\right)^{k-2}\left(p_+-p_-\right)^2=k(k-1)\left(p_+-p_-\right)^2 \\ &\green{\raisebox{.5pt}{\textcircled{\raisebox{-.9pt} {2}}}} k\left(p_++p_-\right)^{k-1}2p_-=2kp_- \\ &\orange{\raisebox{.5pt}{\textcircled{\raisebox{-.9pt} {3}}}} k\left(p_++p_-\right)^{k-1}\left(p_+-p_-\right)i_0=k\left(p_+-p_-\right)i_0 \\ &\blue{\raisebox{.5pt}{\textcircled{\raisebox{-.9pt} {4}}}} k\left(p_++p_-\right)^{k-1}\left(p_+-p_-\right)i_0=k\left(p_+-p_-\right)i_0 \\ &\purple{\raisebox{.5pt}{\textcircled{\raisebox{-.9pt} {5}}}} \left(p_++p_-\right)^ki_0\left(i_0-1\right)=i_0\left(i_0-1\right) \\ \end{align*}\]

Returning to the expression for \(\sigma(k)^2\), we will have to sum all these contributions together with \(\mu(k)\) and \(-\mu(k)^2\):

\[\begin{align*} \sigma(k)^2=&k(k-1)\left(p_+-p_-\right)^2+2kp_-\cancel{+2k\left(p_+-p_-\right)i_0}\cancel{+i_0^2}\cancel{-i_0}+\\ &+k\left(p_+-p_-\right)\cancel{+i_0}-k^2\left(p_+-p_-\right)^2\\ & \cancel{-2k\left(p_+-p_-\right)i_0}\\ & \cancel{-i_0^2} \end{align*}\]

What remains is:

\[\begin{equation*} \sigma(k)^2=\cancel{k^2\left(p_+-p_-\right)^2}-k\left(p_+-p_-\right)^2+2kp_-+k\left(p_+-p_-\right)\cancel{-k^2\left(p_+-p_-\right)^2} \end{equation*}\]

\[\begin{equation*} \sigma(k)^2=2kp_-+k\left(p_+-p_-\right)\left[1-\left(p_+-p_-\right)\right] \end{equation*}\]

Knowing that \(1=p_++p_-\):

\[\begin{equation*} 1-\left(p_+-p_-\right)=\cancel{p_+}+p_- \cancel{-p_+}+p_-=2p_- \ \Longrightarrow \ \ \sigma(k)^2=2kp_-+2k_-(p_+-p_-) \end{equation*}\]

We thus find:

\[\begin{equation*} \sigma(k)^2=2kp_-(1+p_+-p_-) \end{equation*}\]

Substituting once again \(1=p_++p_-\):

\[\begin{equation} \sigma(k)^2=2kp_-(p_+\cancel{+p_-}+p_+\cancel{-p_-})\ \Longrightarrow \ \boxed{\sigma(k)^2=4kp_+p_-} \end{equation}\]

The variance therefore also increases proportionally to time, in this case by a factor given by the product \(p_+p_-\).

At this point, let’s try to calculate the distribution. Let’s go back to equation (eq:diffusione-funzione-generatrice-espressione) and write the binomial power using the binomial theorem:

\[\begin{equation*} \tilde{W}(z,k)=z^{i_0}\sum_{n=0}^k\binom{k}{n}\left(zp_+\right)^n\left(\frac{p_-}{z}\right)^{k-n} \end{equation*}\]

We want the summation index to run over a symmetric interval, thus going from \(n\in[0,k]\) to \(i\in[-k,k]\). Let’s therefore set \(i=2n-k\) so that \(n=0\Rightarrow i=-k\) and that \(n=k\Rightarrow i=k\), obtaining:

\[\begin{equation*} n=\frac{k+i}{2} \ \Longrightarrow \ \ \tilde{W}(z,k)=z^{i_0}\sum_{i=-k}^k\binom{k}{\frac{k+i}{2}}\left(zp_+\right)^{\frac{k+i}{2}}\left(\frac{p_-}{z}\right)^{k-\frac{k+i}{2}} \end{equation*}\]

Let’s make the factor \(z\) explicit:

\[\begin{align*} \tilde{W}(z,k)&=z^{i_0}\sum_{i=-k}^kz^{\frac{k+i}{2}}z^{-\frac{k-i}{2}}\binom{k}{\frac{k+i}{2}}\left(p_+\right)^{\frac{k+i}{2}}\left(p_-\right)^{\frac{k-i}{2}}=\\ &=z^{i_0}\sum_{i=-k}^kz^i\binom{k}{\frac{k+i}{2}}\left(p_+\right)^{\frac{k+i}{2}}\left(p_-\right)^{\frac{k-i}{2}}\\ &=\sum_{i=-k}^kz^{i+i_0}\binom{k}{\frac{k+i}{2}}\left(p_+\right)^{\frac{k+i}{2}}\left(p_-\right)^{\frac{k-i}{2}} \end{align*}\]

Let’s set \(j=i+i_0\) so as to have only the index as the exponent of \(z\):

\[\begin{equation*} \tilde{W}(z,k)=\sum_{j=-k+i_0}^{k+i_0}z^{j}\binom{k}{\frac{k+j-i_0}{2}}\left(p_+\right)^{\frac{k+\left(j-i_0\right)}{2}}\left(p_-\right)^{\frac{k-\left(j-i_0\right)}{2}} \end{equation*}\]

Let’s make a comparison with (eq:diffusione-funzione-generatrice-definizione):

\[\begin{equation*} \sum_{i=-\infty}^{+\infty}z^iW_i(k)=\sum_{i=-k+i_0}^{k+i_0}z^i\binom{k}{\frac{k+i-i_0}{2}}\left(p_+\right)^{\frac{k+\left(i-i_0\right)}{2}}\left(p_-\right)^{\frac{k-\left(i-i_0\right)}{2}} \end{equation*}\]

We thus find that

\[\begin{equation} \boxed{W_i(k)= \ \begin{cases} \binom{k}{\frac{k+i-i_0}{2}}\left(p_+\right)^{\frac{k+\left(i-i_0\right)}{2}}\left(p_-\right)^{\frac{k-\left(i-i_0\right)}{2}} \ \ &i\in[-k+i_0,k+i_0]\\ 0 \ & \text{otherwise} \end{cases}} \end{equation}\]}} \end{equation}

*Find the solution* $W_i(k)$ *by imposing a generic initial condition* $W_i(0)=g_i$.

If $W_i(0)$ then

\begin{equation*}
    \tilde{W}(z,0)=\sum_{i=-\infty}^{+\infty}z^iW_i(0)=\sum_{i=-\infty}^{+\infty}z^ig_i
\end{equation*}

From (eq:diffusione-funzione-generatrice-funzione-di-k=0) it follows that

\begin{equation*}
    \tilde{W}(z,k)=\left(zp_++\frac{p_-}{z}\right)^k\sum_{j=-\infty}^{+\infty}z^jg_j
\end{equation*}

Let's use the binomial theorem again this time:

\begin{equation*}
    \tilde{W}(z,k)=\sum_{n=0}^k\binom{k}{n}\left(zp_+\right)^n\left(\frac{p_-}{z}\right)^{k-n}\sum_{j=-\infty}^{+\infty}z^jg_j
\end{equation*}

Let's make the power of $z$ explicit:

\begin{equation*}
    \tilde{W}(z,k)=\sum_{j=-\infty}^{+\infty}\sum_{n=0}^kz^{j+n-k+n}g_j\binom{k}{n}\left(p_+\right)^n\left(p_-\right)^{k-n}
\end{equation*}

Let's pay attention to the fact that the term in $z$ is

\begin{equation*}
    z^{j+2n-k}
\end{equation*}

while we want it to be a single index as an exponent. For this reason we set:

\begin{equation*}
    i=j+2n-k
\end{equation*}

The extremes of the index $i$ will be determined by those of the index $n$:

\begin{align*}
    &n=0 \ \Longrightarrow \ \ i=j-k\\
    &n=k \ \Longrightarrow \ \ i=j+k
\end{align*}

Furthermore, to be able to make the powers of $p_+$ and $p_-$ explicit, it is convenient to derive $n$ and $k-n$ as a function of this new index:

\begin{align*}
    &2n+j-k=i \ \Longrightarrow \ \ n=\frac{k+i-j}{2}\\
    &k-n=k-\frac{k+i-j}{2}=\frac{k-(i-j)}{2}
\end{align*}

Substituting:

\begin{equation*}
    \tilde{W}(z,k)=\sum_{j=-\infty}^{+\infty}\sum_{i=j-k}^{j+k}z^ig_j\binom{k}{\frac{k+i-j}{2}}\left(p_+\right)^{\frac{k+i-j}{2}}\left(p_-\right)^{\frac{k-(i-j)}{2}}
\end{equation*}

We note that we can invert the indices $i$ and $j$ in the two summations without modifying the summation as a whole. This is because $j$ is an index that runs through all the relative integers, and therefore since $i=j+2n-k$ then $i$ will also run in the same way through all of $\mathbb{Z}$. It is therefore clear that any pair $(i,j)$ is the same whether we consider $i\in[j-k,j+k]$ or whether we consider $j\in[i-k,i+k]$, therefore

\begin{align*}
    \tilde{W}(z,k)&=\sum_{j=-\infty}^{+\infty}\sum_{i=j-k}^{j+k}z^ig_j\binom{k}{\frac{k+i-j}{2}}\left(p_+\right)^{\frac{k+i-j}{2}}\left(p_-\right)^{\frac{k-(i-j)}{2}}\\
    &=\sum_{i=-\infty}^{+\infty}\sum_{j=i-k}^{i+k}z^ig_j\binom{k}{\frac{k+i-j}{2}}\left(p_+\right)^{\frac{k+i-j}{2}}\left(p_-\right)^{\frac{k-(i-j)}{2}}
\end{align*}

Let's also make a comparison this time with (eq:diffusione-funzione-generatrice-definizione):

\begin{equation*}
    \sum_{i=-\infty}^{+\infty}z^iW_i(k)=\sum_{i=-\infty}^{+\infty}z^i\sum_{j=i-k}^{i+k}g_j\binom{k}{\frac{k+i-j}{2}}\left(p_+\right)^{\frac{k+i-j}{2}}\left(p_-\right)^{\frac{k-(i-j)}{2}}
\end{equation*}

From which it follows that

\begin{equation}
    \boxed{W_i(k)=\sum_{j=i-k}^{i+k}g_j\binom{k}{\frac{k+i-j}{2}}\left(p_+\right)^{\frac{k+i-j}{2}}\left(p_-\right)^{\frac{k-(i-j)}{2}}}
\end{equation}

We note that by imposing $g_j=\delta_{j,i_0}$ we find exactly (eq:diffusione-distribuzione-finale):

\begin{align*}
    W_i(k)&=\sum_{j=i-k}^{i+k}\delta_{j,i_0}\binom{k}{\frac{k+i-j}{2}}\left(p_+\right)^{\frac{k+i-j}{2}}\left(p_-\right)^{\frac{k-(i-j)}{2}}=\\
    &=\binom{k}{\frac{k+i-i_0}{2}}\left(p_+\right)^{\frac{k+i-i_0}{2}}\left(p_-\right)^{\frac{k-\left(i-i_0\right)}{2}}
\end{align*}

and this result is possible only if $i_0\in[i-k,i+k]$, or in other words if

\begin{equation*}
    i-k\leqslant i_0\leqslant i+k \ \Longrightarrow \ \
    \begin{cases}
        i-k\leqslant i_0\\
        i_0\leqslant i+k
    \end{cases}
    \iff \ \
    \begin{cases}
        i\leqslant k+i_0\\
        i\geqslant -k+i_0
    \end{cases}
\end{equation*}

that is $i\in[-k+i_0,k+i_0]$, since otherwise the contribution is not present in the summation and will therefore be $0$.
*Calculate the mean* $\mu(k)$ *and the variance* $\sigma(k)^2$ *directly from the distribution (eq:diffusione-distribuzione-finale*).

The mean $\mu(k)$, by definition, is given by

\begin{equation*}
    \mu(k)=\sum_{i}iW_i(k)=\sum_{i=-k+i_0}^{k+i_0}i\binom{k}{\frac{k+i-i_0}{2}}\left(p_+\right)^{\frac{k+\left(i-i_0\right)}{2}}\left(p_-\right)^{\frac{k-\left(i-i_0\right)}{2}}
\end{equation*}

Let's go back to the starting index $n=\frac{k+i-i_0}{2}$:

\begin{align*}
    \mu(k)&=\sum_{n=0}^k\left(2n-k+i_0\right)\binom{k}{n}\left(p_+\right)^n\left(p_-\right)^{k-n}\\
    &=2\sum_{n=0}^kn\binom{k}{n}\left(p_+\right)^n\left(p_-\right)^{k-n}+\left(-k+i_0\right)\sum_{n=0}^k\binom{k}{n}\left(p_+\right)^n\left(p_-\right)^{k-n}
\end{align*}

The quantity $\binom{k}{n}\left(p_+\right)^n\left(p_-\right)^{k-n}$ is none other than a binomial distribution, which will therefore be normalized to $1$. The first summation will therefore be the mean relative to this distribution, equal to $kp_+$:

\begin{equation*}
    \mu(k)=2kp_+-k+i_0=k\left(2p_+-1\right)+i_0
\end{equation*}

Remembering that $1=p_++p_-$:

\begin{equation*}
    \mu(k)=k\left(2p_+-p_+-p_-\right)+i_0=k\left(p_+-p_-\right)+i_0
\end{equation*}

To determine the variance, we must first calculate the moment $\left\langle i(k)^2\right\rangle$:

\[\begin{align*} \left\langle i(k)^2\right\rangle &= \sum_{i=-k+i_0}^{k+i_0} i^2 W_i(k) = \sum_{i=-k+i_0}^{k+i_0} i^2 \binom{k}{\frac{k+i-i_0}{2}} \left(p_+\right)^{\frac{k+(i-i_0)}{2}} \left(p_-\right)^{\frac{k-(i-i_0)}{2}} \\ &= \sum_{n=0}^k \left(2n-k+i_0\right)^2 \binom{k}{n} \left(p_+\right)^n \left(p_-\right)^{k-n} \end{align*}\]

Let's separate the various contributions based on the powers of $n$, keeping in mind that the variance of a binomial is equal to $kp_+p_-$, and therefore

\begin{equation*}
    Var(n)=\mathbb{E}\left(n^2\right)-\mathbb{E}(n)^2 \ \iff \ \mathbb{E}\left(n^2\right)=Var(n)+\mathbb{E}(n)^2
\end{equation*}

We will have:

\begin{align*}
    n^2 \ \Longrightarrow \ \ &4\sum_{n=0}^kn^2\binom{k}{n}\left(p_+\right)^n\left(p_-\right)^{k-n}=4kp_+p_-+4k^2p_+^2\\
    n^1 \ \Longrightarrow \ \ &4\left(-k+i_0\right)\sum_{n=0}^kn\binom{k}{n}\left(p_+\right)^n\left(p_-\right)^{k-n}=4k\left(-k+i_0\right)p_+\\
    n^0 \ \Longrightarrow \ \ &\left(k^2-2ki_0+i_0^2\right)\sum_{n=0}^k\binom{k}{n}\left(p_+\right)^n\left(p_-\right)^{k-n}=k^2-2ki_0+i_0^2
\end{align*}

For simplicity, let's write the entire moment in the form of a polynomial in $k$:

\begin{equation*}
    \left\langle i(k)^2\right\rangle=k^2\left(4p_+^2-4p_++1\right)+k\left(4p_+p_-+4p_+i_0-2i_0\right)+i_0^2
\end{equation*}

All we have to do is to expand $\mu(k)^2$, starting - for simplicity - from its exclusive form in $p_+$, that is, without the term $p_-$:

\begin{equation*}
    \mu(k)^2=k^2\left(4p_+^2-4p_++1\right)+k\left(4p_+i_0-2i_0\right)+i_0^2
\end{equation*}

We note that $\left\langle i(k)^2\right\rangle$ and $\mu(k)^2$ are equal except for a term $4kp_+p_-$. The difference will therefore give

\begin{equation*}
    \sigma(k)^2=\left\langle i(k)^2\right\rangle-\mu(k)^2=4kp_+p_-
\end{equation*}

7.3 The continuous limit

Having reached this point, it is not yet completely clear why the random walk should be linked to the diffusion process described at the beginning of the chapter.

The means that allows us to pass from one scale (the microscopic one) to the other (the mesoscopic one) is the continuous limit, in which the quantities \(\Delta\ell\) and \(\Delta t\) are progressively reduced until they become infinitesimal.

We will therefore have to rewrite equation (eq:diffusion-master-equation) by making these quantities explicit; we will use the following conventions:

\[\begin{align*} x_i\equiv x \ \Longrightarrow \ \ &x_{i\pm1}=x_i\pm\Delta\ell\equiv x\pm\Delta\ell\\ t_i\equiv t \ \Longrightarrow \ \ &t_{i\pm1}=t_i\pm\Delta t\equiv t\pm\Delta t \end{align*}\]

We can therefore write:

\[\begin{equation} W(x,t+\Delta t)=W(x-\Delta\ell,t)p_++W(x+\Delta\ell,t)p_- \end{equation}\]

At this point we can expand the functions on the right-hand side in series, assuming that \(x\gg\Delta\ell\) so that we can stop the expansion at the first order while still ensuring a good approximation:

\[\begin{align*} &W(x-\Delta\ell,t)=W(x,t)-\Delta\ell\frac{\partial W}{\partial x}+\frac{\Delta\ell^2}{2}\frac{\partial^2W}{\partial x^2}+\mathcal{O}\left(\Delta\ell^3\right)\\ &W(x+\Delta\ell,t)=W(x,t)+\Delta\ell\frac{\partial W}{\partial x}+\frac{\Delta\ell^2}{2}\frac{\partial^2W}{\partial x^2}+\mathcal{O}\left(\Delta\ell^3\right) \end{align*}\]

Let’s sum the two terms, remembering that the first is multiplied by \(p_+\) and the second by \(p_-\):

\[\begin{equation*} \left(p_++p_-\right)W(x,t)-\left(p_+-p_-\right)\Delta\ell\frac{\partial W}{\partial x}+\left(p_++p_-\right)\frac{\Delta\ell^2}{2}\frac{\partial^2W}{\partial x^2}+\mathcal{O}\left(\Delta\ell^3\right) \end{equation*}\]

Let’s substitute in (eq:diffusione-limite-continuo-1) remembering that \(p_++p_-=1\):

\[\begin{equation*} W(x,t+\Delta t)=W(x,t)-\left(p_+-p_-\right)\Delta\ell\frac{\partial W}{\partial x}+\frac{\Delta\ell^2}{2}\frac{\partial^2W}{\partial x^2}+\mathcal{O}\left(\Delta\ell^3\right) \end{equation*}\]

Let’s bring \(W(x,t)\) to the left-hand side and divide by \(\Delta t\):

\[\begin{equation} \frac{W(x,t+\Delta t)-W(x,t)}{\Delta t}=-\left(p_+-p_-\right)\frac{\Delta\ell}{\Delta t}\frac{\partial W}{\partial x}+\frac{\Delta\ell^2}{2\Delta t}\frac{\partial^2W}{\partial x^2}+\mathcal{O}\left(\Delta\ell^3\right) \end{equation}\]

It is clear at this point that, going to the limit for \(\Delta t\to0\), the left-hand side would be reduced to the derivative of \(W(x,t)\) with respect to time, provided that \(t\gg\Delta t\); at the same time, as said before, we will also have to send \(\Delta\ell\to0\). We note that on the right we have two ratios dependent on \(\Delta\ell\) and \(\Delta t\), which are not defined when we pass to the limit.

Let’s therefore define the following quantities:

\[\begin{equation} \begin{cases} \left(p_+-p_-\right)\lim\limits_{\Delta\ell,\Delta t\to0}\frac{\Delta\ell}{\Delta t}=v\\ \lim\limits_{\Delta\ell,\Delta t\to0}\frac{\Delta\ell^2}{\Delta t}=D \end{cases} \end{equation}\]

In other words, we are stating that the ratio between \(\Delta\ell\) and \(\Delta t\), as well as that between \(\Delta\ell^2\) and the same \(\Delta t\), is kept constant, so that physical meaning is guaranteed to the model.

The equation in its final form will therefore be

\[\begin{equation} \boxed{\frac{\partial W}{\partial t}=-v\frac{\partial W}{\partial x}+\frac{D}{2}\frac{\partial^2W}{\partial x^2}} \end{equation}\]

The quantity \(v\) has been so named precisely because it has the dimensions of a velocity, and represents the displacement of the particle to the left or to the right: we note in fact that for \(p_+=p_-=\frac{1}{2}\) the velocity is zero, since there is no tendency in one direction with respect to the other; in the case where \(p_+\neq p_-\) there will be a drift to the right if \(p_+>p_-\) or to the left if \(p_+<p_-\).

In this way we have rediscovered equation (eq:diffusione-derivazione-einstein-equazione-generale), generalizing to the case where \(v\neq0\).

7.3.1 Fundamental solution of the diffusion equation

Having found the most general expression for the diffusion equation, we now want to calculate the fundamental solution, that is, the one that is able to solve almost any diffusion problem starting from a generic initial condition. Since we are going to solve a partial differential equation, a condition that must necessarily be respected is the boundedness of the solution: this would not be necessary in a finite interval, but since it is formally defined in all of \(\mathbb{R}\) then it is necessary that it also be bounded, since otherwise it would not be a unique solution.

In particular, we will impose that

\[\begin{equation*} w(x,0)=\delta\left(x-x_0\right) \end{equation*}\]

Although the Dirac delta \(\delta\left(x-x_0\right)\) is a function that diverges at \(x=x_0\), in fact, what is important is that its integral over all of \(\mathbb{R}\) is finite: in this way the evolution over time of the distribution can spread but certainly not condense to the point of diverging at some point, since the area underneath is still finite.

Let’s now proceed to the solution, for which it is necessary to resort to the Fourier transform with respect to the spatial coordinate:

\[\begin{equation} \tilde{w}(\omega,t)=\int_{-\infty}^{+\infty}w(x,t)e^{i\omega x}dx \end{equation}\]

First we will see the simplest case in which \(v=0\), while later we will also calculate the solution in the more general case with \(v\neq0\).

Let’s apply the transform to the first member:

\[\begin{equation*} \mathcal{F}\left(\frac{\partial w}{\partial t}\right)=\int_{-\infty}^{+\infty}\frac{\partial w}{\partial t}e^{i\omega x}dx=\frac{\partial}{\partial t}\int_{-\infty}^{+\infty}w(x,t)e^{i\omega x}dx=\frac{\partial\tilde{\omega}}{\partial t} \end{equation*}\]

Let’s now apply it to the second member:

\[\begin{equation*} \frac{D}{2}\mathcal{F}\left(\frac{\partial^2w}{\partial x^2}\right)=\frac{D}{2}\int_{-\infty}^{+\infty}\frac{\partial^2w}{\partial x^2}e^{i\omega x}dx \end{equation*}\]

To solve this integral we must integrate by parts twice:

\[\begin{equation*} \int_{-\infty}^{+\infty}\frac{\partial^2w}{\partial x^2}e^{i\omega x}dx=\frac{\partial w}{\partial x}e^{i\omega x}\bigg|_{-\infty}^{+\infty}-\int_{-\infty}^{+\infty}\frac{\partial w}{\partial x}\frac{\partial e^{i\omega x}}{\partial x}dx \end{equation*}\]

Let’s remember that \(w(x,t)\) is nothing more than a distribution, so it is legitimate to assume that for \(x\to\pm\infty\) it (and its two successive derivatives) tend to zero. In this way the first term will be zero and we can repeat the same reasoning with the second term:

\[\begin{align*} \int_{-\infty}^{+\infty}\frac{\partial^2w}{\partial x^2}e^{i\omega x}dx&=-i\omega\int_{-\infty}^{+\infty}\frac{\partial w}{\partial x}e^{i\omega x}dx=\\ &=-i\omega\left[\cancel{w(x,t)e^{i\omega x}\big|_{-\infty}^{+\infty}}-\int_{-\infty}^{+\infty}w(x,t)\frac{\partial e^{i\omega x}}{\partial x}dx\right]=\\ &=-i\omega\left[-i\omega\int_{-\infty}^{+\infty}w(x,t)e^{i\omega x}dx\right]=-\omega^2\tilde{w}(\omega,t) \end{align*}\]

We thus obtain the following first-order differential equation:

\[\begin{equation} \frac{\partial\tilde{\omega}}{\partial t}=-\frac{\omega^2D}{2}\tilde{w}(\omega,t) \end{equation}\]

It is solvable by separation of variables:

\[\begin{equation*} \frac{d\tilde{w}}{dt}=-\frac{\omega^2D}{2}\tilde{w} \ \Longrightarrow \ \ \int\frac{d\tilde{w}}{\tilde{w}}=-\frac{\omega^2D}{2}\int dt \ \Longrightarrow \ \ \log\tilde{w}=-\frac{\omega^2D}{2}t+c \end{equation*}\]

The solution, therefore, will be

\[\begin{equation} \tilde{w}(\omega,t)=ke^{-\frac{\omega^2D}{2}t} \end{equation}\]

By imposing \(w(x,0)=\delta\left(x-x_0\right)\) we can apply the initial condition to obtain the unique solution:

\[\begin{equation*} \tilde{w}(\omega,0)=\int_{-\infty}^{+\infty}w(x,0)e^{i\omega x}dx=\int_{-\infty}^{+\infty}\delta\left(x-x_0\right)e^{i\omega x}dx=e^{i\omega x_0} \end{equation*}\]

Ultimately, it results:

\[\begin{equation} \tilde{w}(\omega,t)=e^{i\omega x_0-\frac{\omega^2D}{2}t} \end{equation}\]

To return to the form \(w(x,t)\) we will have to resort to the inverse transform:

\[\begin{equation} w(x,t)=\frac{1}{2\pi}\int_{-\infty}^{+\infty}\tilde{w}(\omega,t)e^{-i\omega x}d\omega=\frac{1}{2\pi}\int_{-\infty}^{+\infty}e^{-\frac{\omega^2D}{2}t-i\omega\left(x-x_0\right)}d\omega \end{equation}\]

This integral is none other than a Gaussian integral in the most general form seen in equation (eq:integrali-gaussiani-forma-generale), in which \(a=Dt\) and \(b=-i\left(x-x_0\right)\), from which the solution follows:

\[\begin{equation} w(x,t)=\frac{1}{2\pi}\sqrt{\frac{2\pi}{Dt}}e^{-\frac{\left(x-x_0\right)^2}{2Dt}} \ \Longrightarrow \ \boxed{w(x,t)=\frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi Dt}}e^{-\frac{\left(x-x_0 \right)^2}{2Dt}}} \end{equation}\]

The most important thing to note is that \(w(x,t)\) is normalized over the entire spatial interval at any instant of time, in fact:

\[\begin{equation*} \int_{-\infty}^{+\infty}w(x,t)dx=\frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi Dt}}\int_{-\infty}^{+\infty}e^{-\frac{\left(x-x_0 \right)^2}{2Dt}}dx=\frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi Dt}}\sqrt{2\pi Dt}=1 \end{equation*}\]

This means that we can interpret \(w(x,t)\) as a probability density function, and this will be useful in the calculations that we will see below.

So far we have assumed for simplicity that the diffusion process started from the instant \(t=0\). To extend the reasoning to a generic initial instant \(t_0\) we will have to rewrite the initial condition as

\[\begin{equation*} w\left(x,t_0\right)=\delta\left(x-x_0\right) \end{equation*}\]

The procedure, on the whole, does not change; the only real difference is from (eq:diffusione-limite-continuo-eq-diff-trasformata), from which the constant \(k\) will take the value

\[\begin{equation*} \tilde{w}\left(\omega,t_0\right)=e^{i\omega x_0} \ \Longrightarrow \ \ e^{i\omega x_0}=ke^{-\frac{\omega^2D}{2}t_0} \ \iff \ \ k=e^{i\omega x_0+\frac{\omega^2D}{2}t_0} \end{equation*}\]

We thus find:

\[\begin{equation} \tilde{w}(\omega,t)=e^{i\omega x_0-\frac{\omega^2D}{2}\left(t-t_0\right)} \end{equation}\]

At this point the inverse transform will not change going from \(t\) to \(t-t_0\), so the final solution will be

\[\begin{equation} \boxed{w(x,t)=\frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi D\left(t-t_0\right)}}e^{-\frac{\left(x-x_0\right)^2}{2D\left(t-t_0\right)}}} \end{equation}\]

This relationship demonstrates the translational invariance of the solution with respect to both space and time. The meaning is that this quantity depends only on the difference between the spatial coordinates \(x-x_0\) and the temporal ones \(t-t_0\), and there are no privileged positions or privileged instants of time with respect to others; mathematically, in fact, we can state that

\[\begin{equation} w\left(x,t|x_0,t_0 \right)=w\left(x-x_0,t-t_0|0,0 \right) \end{equation}\]

If we assume that \(v\neq0\) then, when we apply the Fourier transform, on the right-hand side of the diffusion equation we will also have to consider the contribution

\[\begin{equation*} -v\mathcal{F}\left(\frac{\partial w}{\partial x}\right) \end{equation*}\]

From the calculations seen above we know that

\[\begin{equation*} \mathcal{F}\left(\frac{\partial w}{\partial x}\right)=-i\omega\mathcal{F}(w) \end{equation*}\]

from which it follows:

\[\begin{equation*} \frac{\partial\tilde{\omega}}{\partial t}=-\left(\frac{\omega^2D}{2}-i\omega v\right)\tilde{w}(\omega,t) \end{equation*}\]

From (eq:diffusione-estensione-a-tempo-t_0) we can easily understand how the solution of this differential equation is

\[\begin{equation*} \tilde{w}(\omega,t)=e^{i\omega x_0-\left(\frac{\omega^2D}{2}-i\omega v\right)\left(t-t_0 \right)} \end{equation*}\]

Let’s now perform the inverse transform:

\[\begin{equation*} w(x,t)=\frac{1}{2\pi}\int_{-\infty}^{+\infty}\tilde{w}(\omega,t)e^{-i\omega x}d\omega=\frac{1}{2\pi}\int_{-\infty}^{+\infty}e^{-\frac{\omega^2D}{2}\left(t-t_0 \right)-i\omega\left[x-x_0-v\left(t-t_0 \right)\right]}d\omega \end{equation*}\]

The Gaussian integral will give:

\[\begin{equation} \boxed{w(x,t)=\frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi D\left(t-t_0 \right)}}e^{-\frac{\left[x-x_0-v\left(t-t_0 \right)\right]^2}{2D\left(t-t_0 \right)}}} \end{equation}\]

The results obtained can be generalized to a generic number of dimensions greater than one. Interpreting \(w\left(\vec{x},t\right)\) as the probability distribution associated with the particles in a system of (hyper)volume \(V\) and (hyper)surface \(\Sigma(V)\), and indicating with \(\vec{J}(\vec{x},t)\) the flux density of the particles themselves, from the continuity equation it results:

\[\begin{equation*} \frac{d}{dt}\int_Vw\left(\vec{x},t\right)d^nx=-\int_{\Sigma(V)}\vec{J}\left(\vec{x},t\right)\cdot\hat{n}dS \end{equation*}\]

where \(\hat{n}\) is the unit vector orthogonal to the surface unit \(dS\).

From the divergence theorem, moreover, we can also reduce to a volume integral for \(\vec{J}\):

\[\begin{equation*} \int_{\Sigma(V)}\vec{J}\left(\vec{x},t\right)\cdot\hat{n}dS=\int_V\vec{\nabla}\cdot\vec{J}\left(\vec{x},t\right)d^nx \end{equation*}\]

Substituting in the continuity equation and bringing everything to the left-hand side inside a single integral:

\[\begin{equation*} \int_V\left(\frac{\partial w}{\partial t}+\vec{\nabla}\cdot\vec{J}\right)d^nx=0 \end{equation*}\]

From this it follows that

\[\begin{equation} \frac{\partial w}{\partial t}+\vec{\nabla}\cdot\vec{J}=0 \ \Longrightarrow \ \frac{\partial w}{\partial t}=-\vec{\nabla}\cdot\vec{J} \end{equation}\]

Since there are no external fields, we can assume that in a first approximation the flux density is proportional to the opposite of the gradient of the particle density, since they tend to diffuse from regions of high density to those of low density. For reasons of dimension, the proportionality coefficient is the diffusion coefficient \(D\):

\[\begin{equation} \vec{J}\left(\vec{x},t\right)\simeq-D\vec{\nabla}w\left(\vec{x},t\right) \end{equation}\]

This approximation is known as Fick’s law, which substituted in (eq:diffusione-dimensioni-maggiori-1) returns

\[\begin{equation*} \frac{\partial w}{\partial t}\simeq\vec{\nabla}\left[D\vec{\nabla}w\left(\vec{x},t\right)\right] \end{equation*}\]

Assuming \(D\) is constant, finally:

\[\begin{equation} \boxed{\frac{\partial w}{\partial t}\simeq D\nabla^2w\left(\vec{x},t\right)} \end{equation}\]

We thus rediscover the formula (eq:diffusione-derivazione-einstein-equazione-generale-dim>1) that we had already anticipated, except for a factor of \(\frac{1}{2}\) due to a simple approximation of Fick’s law.

7.3.2 Solutions via propagator

The peculiarity of the fundamental solution written in (eq:diffusione-soluzione-fondamentale-generale) is that it describes how the initial information from a point \(x_0\) in time; for this reason it is also called the propagator of the diffusion process.

As reported previously, the fundamental solution is so called because from it all the solutions of the diffusion equation can be derived starting from any initial condition, provided - as said - that the boundedness of the function is guaranteed. In other words, we can say that all solutions can be written starting from the propagator of the relative equation.

It is not difficult to verify that the function

\[\begin{equation} W(x,t)=\int_{-\infty}^{+\infty}f(y)w\left(x,t|y,t_0\right)dy \end{equation}\]

is a solution of the problem

\[\begin{equation} \begin{cases} \frac{\partial W}{\partial t}=\frac{D}{2}\frac{\partial^2W}{\partial x^2}\\ W\left(x,t_0\right)=f(x) \end{cases} \end{equation}\]

assuming that \(f\in C^{\infty}(\mathbb{R})\).

To prove this, let’s calculate the time derivative of \(W(x,t)\):

\[\begin{equation*} \frac{\partial W}{\partial t}=\frac{\partial}{\partial t}\int_{-\infty}^{+\infty}f(y)w\left(x,t|y,t_0\right)dy=\int_{-\infty}^{+\infty}f(y)\frac{\partial w\left(x,t|y,t_0\right)}{\partial t}dy \end{equation*}\]

From the diffusion equation (with zero drift velocity):

\[\begin{equation*} \frac{\partial w}{\partial t}=\frac{D}{2}\frac{\partial^2w}{\partial x^2} \ \Longrightarrow \ \frac{\partial W}{\partial t}=\int_{-\infty}^{+\infty}f(y)\frac{D}{2}\frac{\partial^2w\left(x,t|y,t_0\right)}{\partial x^2}dy \end{equation*}\]

From which we find:

\[\begin{equation*} \frac{\partial W}{\partial t}=\frac{D}{2}\frac{\partial^2}{\partial x^2}\int_{-\infty}^{+\infty}f(y)w\left(x,t|y,t_0\right)dy=\frac{D}{2}\frac{\partial^2W}{\partial x^2} \end{equation*}\]

which is precisely the equation of the system (eq:diffusione-limite-continuo-problema-generale).

By making \(W\left(x,t_0\right)\) explicit, moreover, we have

\[\begin{equation*} W\left(x,t_0\right)=\int_{-\infty}^{+\infty}f(y)w\left(x,t_0|y,t_0\right)dy=\int_{-\infty}^{+\infty}f(y)\delta(x-y)dy=f(x) \end{equation*}\]

which is the expected initial condition.

The reasoning can be generalized for \(v\neq0\), in which case (eq:diffusione-limite-continuo-soluzione-generale) is a solution of the system

\[\begin{equation} \begin{cases} \frac{\partial W}{\partial t}=-v\frac{\partial W}{\partial x}+\frac{D}{2}\frac{\partial^2W}{\partial x^2}\\ W\left(x,t_0\right)=f(x) \end{cases} \end{equation}\]

7.3.3 Generation of moments via propagator

As anticipated, the normalization of the propagator with respect to the whole space, which implies its role as a pdf, is useful for the calculation of quantities such as the average position or the variance with respect to the coordinates \(x\), or more generally for the generation of moments.

We can in fact write:

\[\begin{equation} \langle f(x)\rangle_t=\int_{-\infty}^{+\infty}f(x)w\left(x,t|x_0,t_0\right)dx \end{equation}\]

To easily calculate the moments up to the second order, we can calculate the derivative with respect to time:

\[\begin{equation*} \frac{d}{dt}\langle f(x)\rangle_t=\int_{-\infty}^{+\infty}f(x)\frac{\partial w}{\partial t}dx=-v\int_{-\infty}^{+\infty}f(x)\frac{\partial w}{\partial x}dx+\frac{D}{2}\int_{-\infty}^{+\infty}f(x)\frac{\partial^2w}{\partial x^2}dx \end{equation*}\]

Remembering that \(w\left(x,t|x_0,t_0\right)\) converges to zero at infinity, both on one side and on the other, we can integrate the first integral by parts and repeat the same thing twice for the second, exactly as done during the calculations that led to equation (eq:diffusione-limite-continuo-equazione-differenziale-primo-ordine-trasformata). We will obtain:

\[\begin{equation} \boxed{\frac{d}{dt}\langle f(x)\rangle_t=v\int_{-\infty}^{+\infty}\frac{df}{dx}w\left(x,t|x_0,t_0\right)dx+\frac{D}{2}\int_{-\infty}^{+\infty}\frac{d^2f}{dx^2}w\left(x,t|x_0,t_0\right)dx} \end{equation}\]

We can calculate the average position of the distribution by setting \(f(x)=x\):

\[\begin{equation} \frac{d}{dt}\langle x\rangle_t=v\int_{-\infty}^{+\infty}w\left(x,t|x_0,t_0\right)dx+0=v \ \Longrightarrow \ \boxed{\langle x\rangle_t=x_0+v\left(t-t_0\right)} \end{equation}\]

To calculate the variance we must first calculate the second-order moment:

\[\begin{equation*} \frac{d}{dt}\left\langle x^2\right\rangle_t=v\int_{-\infty}^{+\infty}2xw\left(x,t|x_0,t_0\right)dx+\frac{D}{2}\int_{-\infty}^{+\infty}2w\left(x,t|x_0,t_0\right)dx=2v\langle x\rangle_t+D \end{equation*}\]

Substituting \(\langle x\rangle_t\) from (eq:diffusione-media-posizione):

\[\begin{equation*} \frac{d}{dt}\left\langle x^2\right\rangle_t=2vx_0+2v^2\left(t-t_0 \right)+D \end{equation*}\]

Integrating:

\[\begin{equation} \left\langle x^2\right\rangle_t=x_0^2+2vx_0\left(t-t_0 \right)+v^2\left(t-t_0 \right)^2+D(t-t_0) \end{equation}\]

At this point we can calculate the variance:

\[\begin{align*} &Var(x)=\left\langle x^2\right\rangle_t-\langle x\rangle_t=\\ &=\cancel{x_0^2}\cancel{+2vx_0\left(t-t_0 \right)}\cancel{+v^2\left(t-t_0 \right)^2}+D(t-t_0)\cancel{-x_0^2}\cancel{-2vx_0\left(t-t_0 \right)}\cancel{-v^2\left(t-t_0 \right)^2} \end{align*}\]

What remains is therefore

\[\begin{equation} \boxed{Var(x)=D\left(t-t_0 \right)} \end{equation}\]

Both results were actually predictable: looking at the expression (eq:diffusione-soluzione-fondamentale-generale) we notice that it is a Gaussian centered at \(x_0+v\left(t-t\right)\) with standard deviation \(\sqrt{D\left(t-t_0\right)}\). It is, in practice, a Gaussian initially centered at \(x_0\), which tends to move over time with constant velocity \(v\), and whose initial null variance (being a Dirac delta) then tends to increase proportionally to \(D\), thus spreading the distribution throughout space.

7.4 Diffusion in a semi-infinite domain

So far we have considered diffusion problems that involved all of infinite space. For the last part of this chapter we will also explore two distinct cases of semi-infinite spaces, that is, spaces bounded by a wall beyond which unlimited space extends.

We will see how delimiting the space in this way implies the addition of a new constraint to be respected for the propagator of the diffusion process. The calculations will be done assuming the simplest case in which \(v=0\) and \(t_0=0\), as the results are then easily generalizable to the case with \(v\neq0\) and \(t_0\neq0\).

7.4.1 Absorbing wall

Let’s assume as a first case that the wall has absorbing properties, that is, that every particle that reaches this wall is absorbed by it and therefore removed from the system.

For simplicity of calculation, let’s assume that this wall is located at \(x=0\) and that the possible space for the diffusion of particles is \(\mathbb{R}^+\). The new constraint to be imposed, therefore, is the following:

\[\begin{equation*} w(0,t)=0 \end{equation*}\]

which translated indicates that at any instant of time there are no particles at the coordinate \(x=0\), as they are absorbed and removed from the system.

Indicating with \(w_a(x,t)\) the propagator relative to this problem, the system to be solved is therefore

\[\begin{equation} \begin{cases} \frac{\partial w_a}{\partial t}=\frac{D}{2}\frac{\partial^2w_a}{\partial x^2}\\ w_a(x,0)=\delta\left(x-x_0\right)\\ w_a(0,t)=0 \end{cases} \end{equation}\]

The goal, therefore, is to find a solution that is fixed at zero at the origin during the entire evolution and that at the same time satisfies the conditions already seen previously. One idea is to take two distributions that are symmetric with respect to the origin, that evolve in the same way, and subtract them from each other. If we want the distribution to be centered at \(x_0\), for example, we will have to consider the one centered at \(-x_0\), thus obtaining

\[\begin{equation} \boxed{w_a\left(x,t|x_0,0 \right)=\frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi Dt}}\left(e^{-\frac{\left(x-x_0 \right)^2}{2Dt}}-e^{-\frac{\left(x+x_0 \right)^2}{2Dt}}\right)} \end{equation}\]

We can prove that this is a valid solution of the system (eq:diffusione-parete-assorbente-sistema). In the first place, both distributions are solutions of the differential equation; moreover, since the equation is linear, a linear combination of solutions is in turn a solution of the same equation, and (eq:diffusione-parete-assorbente-propagatore) is precisely a linear combination of two valid solutions.

As for the initial condition, it will be given by the difference of the Dirac deltas \(\delta\left(x-x_0\right)-\delta\left(x+x_0\right)\). This is not a problem, as the space considered is only that of the positive coordinates, so one of the two deltas will not contribute:

\[\begin{equation*} \int_0^{+\infty}w\left(x,0|x_0,0 \right)dx=\int_0^{+\infty}\delta\left(x-x_0 \right)dx-\cancel{\int_0^{+\infty}\delta\left(x+x_0 \right)dx}=1 \end{equation*}\]

This guarantees the normalization of the distribution at the initial instant of time, so even if the initial condition of the system (eq:diffusione-parete-assorbente-sistema) is not formally respected, it is still a valid solution for it.

Having found the propagator of the system, the general solution with initial condition \(f(x)\) will therefore be given by

\[\begin{equation} W_a(x,t)=\int_0^{+\infty}f(y)w_a\left(x,t|y,0\right)dy \end{equation}\]

The main difference of the propagator found with respect to the propagator in infinite space is related to its normalization: \(w_a(x,t)\), in fact, is normalized only at the instant \(t=0\), while for \(t>0\) its integral gives as a result a number less than \(1\).

To observe this we must make this integral explicit, which we indicate with \(P(t)\) as it is dependent on time:

\[\begin{align*} P(t)&=\int_0^{+\infty}w_a\left(x,t|x_0,0\right)dx=\\ &=\int_0^{+\infty}\frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi Dt}}e^{-\frac{\left(x-x_0 \right)^2}{2Dt}}dx-\int_0^{+\infty}\frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi Dt}}e^{-\frac{\left(x+x_0 \right)^2}{2Dt}}dx \end{align*}\]

Let’s perform a change of variable for both integrals:

\[\begin{equation*} \begin{cases} u=\frac{x-x_0}{\sqrt{Dt}}\\ v=\frac{x+x_0}{\sqrt{Dt}} \end{cases} \Longrightarrow \ \begin{cases} du=\frac{dx}{\sqrt{Dt}}\\ dv=\frac{dx}{\sqrt{Dt}} \end{cases} \end{equation*}\] \end{equation*}

\[\begin{equation*} P(t)=\int_{-\frac{x_0}{\sqrt{Dt}}}^{+\infty}\frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}}e^{-\frac{u^2}{2}}du-\int_{\frac{x_0}{\sqrt{Dt}}}^{+\infty}\frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}}e^{-\frac{v^2}{2}}dv \end{equation*}\]

Let’s rewrite the first integral as the difference between the integral over the whole space and the complementary interval:

\[\begin{equation*} P(t)=\int_{-\infty}^{+\infty}\frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}}e^{-\frac{u^2}{2}}du-\int_{-\infty}^{-\frac{x_0}{\sqrt{Dt}}}\frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}}e^{-\frac{u^2}{2}}du-\int_{\frac{x_0}{\sqrt{Dt}}}^{+\infty}\frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}}e^{-\frac{v^2}{2}}dv \end{equation*}\]

The first integral is a normalized Gaussian integral; in the second integral, since the integrand function is symmetric, we can the integration interval, obtaining:

\[\begin{equation*} P(t)=1-\int_{\frac{x_0}{\sqrt{Dt}}}^{+\infty}\frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}}e^{-\frac{v^2}{2}}dv-\int_{\frac{x_0}{\sqrt{Dt}}}^{+\infty}\frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}}e^{-\frac{v^2}{2}}dv=1-2\int_{\frac{x_0}{\sqrt{Dt}}}^{+\infty}\frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}}e^{-\frac{v^2}{2}}dv \end{equation*}\]

Since the resulting integral is positive for \(t>0\), we have therefore shown that

\[\begin{equation*} P(t>1)<1 \end{equation*}\]

just as stated initially. This quantity is also called survival probability as it expresses the probability that a particle has not been absorbed at time \(t\).

From \(P(t)\) we can also calculate the lifetime distribution, indicated with \(q(t)\), which corresponds to the probability distribution associated with the absorption of a particle. In particular, the decrease of \(P(t)\) over time indicates an increase in particles that are absorbed, therefore

\[\begin{equation*} q(t)=-\frac{dP}{dt} \end{equation*}\]

The problem at this point is that calculating the derivative of \(P(t)\) is not trivial, because the Gaussian integral is not solvable in the interval \(\left[\frac{x_0}{\sqrt{Dt}},+\infty\right)\). This forces us to look for another way to calculate the derivative of the survival probability.

The idea is to make the probability \(P\) explicit according to the definition and exploit the diffusion equation associated with the propagator:

\[\begin{align*} \frac{dP}{dt}&=\frac{\partial}{\partial t}\int_0^{+\infty}w_a\left(x,t|x_0,0\right)dx=\int_0^{+\infty}\frac{\partial w_a}{\partial t}dx=\frac{D}{2}\int_0^{+\infty}\frac{\partial^2w_a}{\partial x^2}dx=\\ &=\frac{D}{2}\frac{\partial w_a}{\partial x}\bigg|_0^{+\infty}=0-\frac{D}{2}\frac{\partial w_a}{\partial x}\bigg|_{x=0}=\\ &=-\frac{D}{2}\frac{\partial}{\partial x}\left[\frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi Dt}}\left(e^{-\frac{\left(x-x_0 \right)^2}{2Dt}}-e^{-\frac{\left(x+x_0 \right)^2}{2Dt}}\right)\right]\bigg|_{x=0}=\\ &=-\frac{1}{2}\sqrt{\frac{D}{2\pi t}}\left(-\frac{x-x_0}{Dt}e^{-\frac{\left(x-x_0 \right)^2}{2Dt}}\bigg|_{x=0}+\frac{x+x_0}{Dt}e^{-\frac{\left(x+x_0 \right)^2}{2Dt}}\bigg|_{x=0}\right)=\\ &=-\frac{1}{2}\sqrt{\frac{D}{2\pi t}}\frac{x_0}{Dt}\left(e^{-\frac{x_0^2}{2Dt}}+e^{-\frac{x_0^2}{2Dt}}\right)=-\frac{1}{\cancel{2}}\frac{x_0}{\sqrt{2\pi Dt^3}}\cdot\cancel{2}e^{-\frac{x_0^2}{2Dt}} \end{align*}\]

From which it follows:

\[\begin{equation} \boxed{q(t)=-\frac{dP}{dt}=\frac{x_0}{\sqrt{2\pi Dt^3}}e^{-\frac{x_0^2}{2Dt}}} \end{equation}\]

In particular, we find that

\[\begin{equation} q(t)\sim t^{-\frac{3}{2}} \end{equation}\]

This trend is characteristic of the diffusion process we are studying, namely Brownian motion.

7.4.2 Reflecting wall

Let’s now imagine that the wall at \(x=0\) is reflecting, that is, capable of sending back every particle that happens to hit it. To do this we must impose that the flow of particles at \(x=0\) is zero, in the sense that every particle that reaches the wall at the instant \(t\), not being able to cross it, no longer has any to the left, but on the contrary is to the right, and because of this symmetry the net flow is zero:

\[\begin{equation*} J(x=0,t)=0 \end{equation*}\]

From Fick’s law (equation (eq:diffusione-legge-di-fick)) we know that the flow is given by

\[\begin{equation*} J(x,t)=-\frac{D}{2}\frac{\partial w}{\partial x} \end{equation*}\]

Therefore the condition to be imposed will be:

\[\begin{equation} \frac{\partial w_r}{\partial x}\bigg|_{x=0}=0 \end{equation}\]

At this point we proceed as in the absorbing case: we exploit the symmetry of the system and consider a linear combination of the two distributions centered one at \(x_0\) and the other at \(-x_0\).

\[\begin{equation*} w_r\left(x,t|x_0,0\right)=\frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi Dt}}\left(\alpha e^{-\frac{\left(x-x_0 \right)^2}{2Dt}}+\beta e^{-\frac{\left(x+x_0 \right)^2}{2Dt}}\right) \end{equation*}\]

The goal is to find the coefficients \(\alpha\) and \(\beta\). We therefore impose the condition (eq:diffusione-parete-riflettente-condizione):

\[\begin{align*} \frac{\partial w_r}{\partial x}\bigg|_{x=0}&=\frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi Dt}}\left(-\alpha\frac{x-x_0}{Dt}e^{-\frac{\left(x-x_0 \right)^2}{2Dt}}-\beta\frac{x+x_0}{Dt}e^{-\frac{\left(x+x_0 \right)^2}{2Dt}}\right)\bigg|_{x=0}=\\ &=\frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi Dt}}\left(\frac{\alpha x_0}{Dt}e^{-\frac{x_0^2}{2Dt}}-\frac{\beta x_0}{Dt}e^{-\frac{x_0^2}{2Dt}}\right)=\frac{x_0}{\sqrt{2\pi D^3t^3}}(\alpha-\beta)e^{-\frac{x_0^2}{2Dt}} \end{align*}\]

This implies that

\[\begin{equation*} \frac{\partial w_r}{\partial x}\bigg|_{x=0}=0 \ \iff \ \alpha=\beta \end{equation*}\]

By setting \(\alpha=\beta=1\) the solution will therefore be given by

\[\begin{equation*} \boxed{w_r\left(x,t|x_0,0\right)=\frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi Dt}}\left(e^{-\frac{\left(x-x_0 \right)^2}{2Dt}}+e^{-\frac{\left(x+x_0 \right)^2}{2Dt}}\right)} \end{equation*}\]

Again, having found the propagator, we can write the general solution:

\[\begin{equation} W_r(x,t)=\int_0^{+\infty}f(y)w_r\left(x,t|x_0,0\right)dy \end{equation}\]

# Diffusion

To introduce the stochastic processes that we will see later, it is necessary to delve deeper into the phenomenon of *diffusion*, which we have already mentioned in the previous chapter. In particular, we will derive the *diffusion equation* in different contexts and through two independent methods: the one related to the *mesoscopic* scale, which describes observable phenomena such as *Brownian motion*, and the one related to the *microscopic* scale, which will lead directly to one of the pillars in the study of stochastic processes, the *random walk*.

## Einstein's derivation

Let's consider a long, thin tube in the $x$ direction filled with water, into which ink is injected at the position $x=0$ at time $t=0$.

<!-- TikZ Figure: ../images/Figure/diffusione-tubo-acqua-inchiostro.tex -->

Let's focus on a generic region $V(x,t)$ inside the tube, so that we can define the following quantity:

\begin{equation}
    w(x,t)=\frac{\text{Number of particles in} \ V(x,t)}{V(x,t)}
\end{equation}

This is therefore the *particle density* of ink at position $x$ at time $t$, assuming that $V(x,t)$ is small enough to be identified by a single value of $x$.

We can define the probability that a particle is in a certain region $A$ as

\begin{equation}
    \int_Aw(x,t)dx
\end{equation}

For it to actually correspond to a probability, it must be normalized, therefore it is necessary that

\begin{equation*}
    \int_{-\infty}^{+\infty}w(x,t)dx=1
\end{equation*}

We can also easily establish the initial condition knowing that at time $t=0$ the entire ink is concentrated at $x=0$:

\begin{equation}
    w(x,0)=\delta(x)
\end{equation}

Our goal is to find a law that tells us how $w(x,t)$ varies over time. We will therefore have to derive a formula that can express $w(x,t+\Delta t)$ as a function of a quantity dependent on $t$, and to do this we will need some important assumptions that we will see below.

Let's consider the density $w(x-y,t)$ associated with all the particles located at a distance $y$ from the coordinate $x$ at time $t$. The first necessary assumption is the following:

\noindent \red{\raisebox{.5pt}{\textcircled{\raisebox{-.9pt} {1}}}} The probability that a particle moves a distance $y$ in a time interval $\Delta t$ is *independent* of the starting point considered

Let's denote by $f(y,\Delta t)$ the pdf relative to this probability, which as we have said depends only on $y$ and not also on $x$. In this case, the density $w(x,t+\Delta t)$ can easily be expressed as

\begin{equation}
    w(x,t+\Delta t)=\int_{-\infty}^{+\infty}w(x-y,t)f(y,\Delta t)dy
\end{equation}

Intuitively we can say that the density will be obtained from the contribution of all possible values of $y$, each of which is expressed by the product of the density of the particles at $x-y$ multiplied by the probability $f(y,\Delta t)dy$ that they move by $y$.

To solve the integral equation we make a second assumption:

\noindent \red{\raisebox{.5pt}{\textcircled{\raisebox{-.9pt} {2}}}} The function $f(y,\Delta t)$ *decays quickly* as $y$ increases

This means that the major contributions come from the regions near $x$, since at greater distances the probability of a displacement occurring becomes very low. This justifies us in expanding $w(x-y,t)$ in a Taylor series with respect to $x$:

\begin{equation*}
    w(x-y,t)=w(x,t)-\frac{\partial w(x,t)}{\partial x}y+\frac{1}{2}\frac{\partial^2w(x,t)}{\partial x^2}y^2+\mathcal{O}\left(y^3\right)
\end{equation*}

Let's substitute on the right-hand side of (eq:diffusione-derivazione-einstein-equazione-integrale), temporarily ignoring the higher-order terms:

\begin{equation*}
    w(x,t)\int_{-\infty}^{+\infty}f(y,\Delta t)dy-\frac{\partial w}{\partial x}\int_{-\infty}^{+\infty}yf(y,\Delta t)dy+\frac{1}{2}\frac{\partial^2w}{\partial x^2}\int_{-\infty}^{+\infty}y^2f(y,\Delta t)dy
\end{equation*}

The first integral is clearly equal to $1$ since $f(y,\Delta t)dy$ is a probability. As for the other two integrals, however, two other assumptions come to our aid; the first is the following:

\noindent \red{\raisebox{.5pt}{\textcircled{\raisebox{-.9pt} {3}}}} Displacements with respect to a coordinate $x$ are *equally probable* from the left and from the right

Translated into a formula:

\begin{equation*}
    f(-y,\Delta t)=f(y,\Delta t)
\end{equation*}

Since it is a symmetric function in $y$, therefore, the integral with the linear term in $y$ will clearly be zero.

As for the last one, instead, the fourth and final assumption is the following:

\noindent \red{\raisebox{.5pt}{\textcircled{\raisebox{-.9pt} {4}}}} The second moment of the distribution $f(y,\Delta t)$ is directly proportional to $\Delta t$

Let $D$ be the proportionality constant, it follows that

\begin{equation}
    \int_{-\infty}^{+\infty}y^2f(y,\Delta t)dy=D\Delta t
\end{equation}

Putting everything together we get

\begin{equation*}
    w(x,t+\Delta t)=w(x,t)+\frac{1}{2}\frac{\partial^2w}{\partial x^2}D\Delta t+\mathcal{O}\left(y^3\right)
\end{equation*}

Let's isolate the incremental ratio with respect to time on the left-hand side:

\begin{equation}
    \frac{w(x,t+\Delta t)-w(x,t)}{\Delta t}=\frac{D}{2}\frac{\partial^2w}{\partial x^2}+\mathcal{O}\left(y^3\right)
\end{equation}

At this point, going to the limit $\Delta t\to0$ we obtain the **diffusion equation**, also called the **heat equation** because it describes the analogous thermodynamic phenomenon:

\begin{equation}
    \boxed{\frac{\partial w}{\partial t}=\frac{D}{2}\frac{\partial^2w}{\partial x^2}}
\end{equation}

This is a **partial differential equation** (*PDE*), which can be solved by imposing the initial condition seen in equation (eq:diffusione-derivazione-einstein-condizione-iniziale).

All the reasoning done can be easily extended to a greater number of spatial dimensions, obtaining the formula

\begin{equation}
    \boxed{\frac{\partial w}{\partial t}=\frac{D}{2}\nabla^2w}
\end{equation}

### Solution with dilation symmetry

Finding the solution of a PDE, in general, is not simple; to facilitate the calculations it is possible to exploit a symmetry of the system, called **dilation symmetry**, for which the solution to (eq:diffusione-derivazione-einstein-equazione-generale) is such as to preserve the ratio $\frac{x}{\sqrt{t}}$.

To prove this, let's suppose we go from the pair of coordinates $(x,t)$ to the pair of coordinates $\left(\lambda x,\lambda^2t\right)$ and substitute in (eq:diffusione-derivazione-einstein-equazione-1D):

\begin{equation*}
    \frac{\partial}{\partial t}w\left(\lambda x,\lambda^2t\right)=\frac{D}{2}\frac{\partial^2}{\partial x^2}w\left(\lambda x,\lambda^2t\right)
\end{equation*}

Let's use the chain rule on both the left and the right:

\begin{equation*}
    \frac{\partial}{\partial t}=\frac{\partial\left(\lambda^2t\right)}{\partial t}\frac{\partial}{\partial\left(\lambda^2t\right)}=\lambda^2\frac{\partial}{\partial\left(\lambda^2t\right)}   \frac{\partial}{\partial x}=\frac{\partial(\lambda x)}{\partial x}\frac{\partial }{\partial(\lambda x)}=\lambda\frac{\partial}{\partial(\lambda x)}
\end{equation*}

Substituting:

\begin{equation*}
    \cancel{\lambda^2}\frac{\partial}{\partial\left(\lambda^2 t\right)}w\left(\lambda x,\lambda^2t\right)=\frac{D}{2}\cancel{\lambda^2}\frac{\partial^2}{\partial (\lambda x)^2}w\left(\lambda x,\lambda^2t\right)
\end{equation*}

which is the same form as the starting one. This means that both $w(x,t)$ and $w\left(\lambda x,\lambda^2t\right)$ are solutions of the differential equation, and therefore the density function is such as to leave the ratio $\frac{x^2}{t}$ unchanged since

\begin{equation*}
    \frac{x'^2}{t'}=\frac{\cancel{\lambda^2}x^2}{\cancel{\lambda^2}t}=\frac{x^2}{t}
\end{equation*}

or, analogously, the ratio $\frac{x}{\sqrt{t}}$, as written above.

This invariance constrains the functional form of $w(x,t)$ to the dependence on the parameter $\frac{x}{\sqrt{t}}$, which can be considered as a single variable:

\begin{equation*}
    w(x,t)\equiv v\left(\frac{x}{\sqrt{t}}\right)
\end{equation*}

Let's therefore rewrite the derivatives by setting $z=\frac{x}{\sqrt{t}}$:

\begin{equation*}
    \frac{\partial}{\partial t}w\equiv\frac{\partial}{\partial t}v=\frac{dv}{dz}\frac{\partial z}{\partial t}=-\frac{x}{2\sqrt{t^3}}\frac{dv}{dz}
\end{equation*}

\begin{equation*}
    \frac{\partial^2}{\partial x^2}w\equiv\frac{\partial^2}{\partial x^2}v=\frac{\partial}{\partial x}\left(\frac{\partial}{\partial x}v\right)=\frac{\partial}{\partial x}\left(\frac{dv}{dz}\frac{\partial z}{\partial x}\right)=\frac{d^2v}{dz^2}\left(\frac{\partial z}{\partial x}\right)^2+\frac{dv}{dz}\cancel{\frac{\partial^2z}{\partial x^2}}=\frac{1}{t}\frac{d^2v}{dz^2}
\end{equation*}

Let's substitute in the differential equation:

\begin{equation*}
    -\frac{x}{2\sqrt{t^3}}\frac{dv}{dz}=\frac{D}{2t}\frac{d^2v}{dz^2} \ \Longrightarrow \ \ -\frac{x}{\sqrt{t}}\frac{dv}{dz}=D\frac{d^2v}{dz^2}
\end{equation*}

Remembering that $\frac{x}{\sqrt{t}}=z$:

\begin{equation}
    \boxed{\frac{d^2v}{dz^2}+\frac{z}{D}\frac{dv}{dz}=0}
\end{equation}

This is an **ordinary differential equation** (ODE), which is much simpler to solve. We just need to set $\rho=\frac{dv}{dz}$ to obtain a first-order differential equation:

\begin{equation*}
    \frac{d\rho}{dz}+\frac{z}{D}\rho=0 \ \Longrightarrow \ \ \int\frac{d\rho}{\rho}=-\frac{1}{D}\int zdz \ \Longrightarrow \ \ \log\rho=-\frac{z^2}{2D}+c \ \Longrightarrow \ \rho=ae^{-\frac{z^2}{2D}}
\end{equation*}

being $a=e^c$.

Returning to $v$:

\begin{equation*}
    \frac{dv}{dz}=ae^{-\frac{z^2}{2D}} \ \Longrightarrow \ \ v(z)=a\int_0^ze^{-\frac{z'^2}{2D}}dz'+b
\end{equation*}

But knowing that $v(z)\equiv w(x,t)$:

\begin{equation}
    \boxed{w(x,t)=\int_0^{\frac{x}{\sqrt{t}}}e^{-\frac{z^2}{2D}}dz+b}
\end{equation}

The integral is not solvable, so there is no real analytical form of the solution, but in any case it is a valid solution for the equation (eq:diffusione-derivazione-einstein-equazione-1D).

We can still calculate specific values, such as the initial density for $t\to0$:

\begin{equation*}
    \lim_{t\to0}w(x,t)=\lim_{t\to0}\int_0^{\frac{x}{\sqrt{t}}}e^{-\frac{z^2}{2D}}dz+b=\int_0^\infty e^{-\frac{z^2}{2D}}dz+b=a\sqrt{\frac{\pi}{2D}}+b
\end{equation*}

At $x=0$, instead, the density is equal to

\begin{equation*}
    w(0,t)=\cancel{\int_0^0e^{-\frac{z^2}{2D}}dz}+b=b
\end{equation*}

This solution, however, is not what is called *fundamental*, in the sense that it is not the one that allows all possible configurations: just note that the initial condition $w(x,0)=\delta\left(x-x_0\right)$ is not possible with any combination of $a$ and $b$, since $a\sqrt{\frac{\pi}{2D}}+b$ is a constant quantity unlike the Dirac delta. Later we will derive the *fundamental solution*, but first we need to explore the microscopic point of view.

## Microscopic derivation

Moving from a mesoscopic to a microscopic scale, we will no longer approach a continuous system but a *discrete* one. Let's therefore consider a one-dimensional lattice of step $\Delta\ell$ in which a particle can only move at a unit of time distant $\Delta t$ from the previous one.

Having discretized both time and space, we can write the two coordinates in the following way:

\begin{equation*}
    x_i=i\Delta\ell,   i=0,\pm1,\pm2,\dots   t_k=k\Delta t,   k=0,1,2,\dots
\end{equation*}

Let's introduce the following probabilities:

\begin{itemize}
    \item $W_i(k)$ the probability that the particle is at $x_i$ at time $t_k$
    \item $p_+$ the probability that the particle goes from $x_i$ at time $t_k$ to $x_{i+1}$ at time $t_{k+1}$
    \item $p_-$ the probability that the particle goes from $x_i$ at time $t_k$ to $x_{i-1}$ at time $t_{k+1}$
\end{itemize}

Let's impose that

\begin{equation}
    p_++p_-=1
\end{equation}

that is, that at time $t_{k+1}$ the particle must necessarily move either one position back or one position forward, without any other possibility. In this case, for a particle to reach the position $x_i$ at time $t_{k+1}$ there are only two possibilities

\begin{enumerate}
    \item the particle is at $x_{i-1}$ and moves forward
    \item the particle is at $x_{i+1}$ and moves backward
\end{enumerate}

Summing the probabilities relative to the two cases we obtain what is called the **master equation**:

\begin{equation}
    \boxed{W_i(k+1)=W_{i-1}(k)p_++W_{i+1}(k)p_-}
\end{equation}

The process described here is commonly called a **random walk**, since it follows the random path of a particle that has a certain probability of moving in one direction or the other at each step. The master equation of a random walk does nothing more than make explicit how the probability for a coordinate $x_i$ changes over time.

An important property to keep in mind is that the master equation must *conserve normalization*, that is, the sum $\sum_iW_i(k)$ must be equal to $1$ at all times $t_k$. To verify this, we just need to place the summation in (eq:diffusion-master-equation):

\begin{equation*}
    \sum_{i=-\infty}^{+\infty}W_i(k+1)=\sum_{i=-\infty}^{+\infty}W_{i-1}(k)p_++\sum_{i=-\infty}^{+\infty}W_{i+1}(k)p_-
\end{equation*}

Since $i$ runs over infinite values, we can indicate both $i+1$ and $i-1$ with the same index $j$:

\begin{equation*}
    \sum_{i=-\infty}^{+\infty}W_i(k+1)=\sum_{j=-\infty}^{+\infty}W_j(k)p_++\sum_{j=-\infty}^{+\infty}W_j(k)p_-=\left(p_++p_-\right)\sum_{j=-\infty}^{+\infty}W_j(k)
\end{equation*}

From (eq:diffusione-normalizzazione-p-+-p--) it follows

\begin{equation*}
    \sum_{i=-\infty}^{+\infty}W_i(k+1)=\sum_{j=-\infty}^{+\infty}W_j(k)
\end{equation*}

which is the initial thesis. This means that in a random walk, particles are not born from nothing and do not disappear into nothing, but remain within the lattice for the entire evolution of the system.

In general, a random walk is very simple to simulate by computer. By imposing the same parameters and the same initial conditions, in fact, the probability $W_i(k)$ can be estimated as

\begin{equation*}
    W_i(k)=\frac{r}{R}
\end{equation*}

where $r$ is the number of simulations in which the particle is at $x_i$ at time $t_k$ and $R$ is the total number of simulations.

Despite the simplicity with respect to numerical calculation, we will see below how to derive $W_i(k)$ analytically in general, thus formally solving the master equation.

### Solution with generating functions

Let's define the following generating function:

\begin{equation}
    \tilde{W}(z,k)=\sum_{i=-\infty}^{+\infty}z^iW_i(k)
\end{equation}

To write the master equation as a function of the generating function, we must multiply both members by $z^i$ and sum over all possible indices $i$:

\begin{equation*}
    \sum_{i=-\infty}^{+\infty}z^iW_i(k+1)=\sum_{i=-\infty}^{+\infty}z^iW_{i-1}(k)p_++\sum_{i=-\infty}^{+\infty}z^iW_{i+1}(k)p_-
\end{equation*}

On the right-hand side, let's multiply and divide each term by $z$ so as to have the exponent $i-1$ in one case and $i+1$ in the other:

\begin{equation*}
    \sum_{i=-\infty}^{+\infty}z^iW_i(k+1)=zp_+\sum_{i=-\infty}^{+\infty}z^{i-1}W_{i-1}(k)+\frac{p_-}{z}\sum_{i=-\infty}^{+\infty}z^{i+1}W_{i+1}(k)
\end{equation*}

At this point we can substitute the definition (eq:diffusione-funzione-generatrice-definizione):

\begin{equation*}
    \tilde{W}(z,k+1)=zp_+\tilde{W}(z,k)+\frac{p_-}{z}\tilde{W}(z,k)=\left(zp_++\frac{p_-}{z}\right)\tilde{W}(z,k)
\end{equation*}

From this law it follows iteratively:

\begin{equation*}
    \tilde{W}(z,k+1)=\left(zp_++\frac{p_-}{z}\right)\tilde{W}(z,k)=\left(zp_++\frac{p_-}{z}\right)^2\tilde{W}(z,k-1)=\dots
\end{equation*}

And therefore, in general:

\begin{equation}
    \tilde{W}(z,k)=\left(zp_++\frac{p_-}{z}\right)^k\tilde{W}(z,0)
\end{equation}

Let's remember that the variable $k$ refers to the time coordinate $t_k$. If the particle is at the coordinate $x_{i_0}$ at time $t=t_0$ then

\begin{equation*}
    W_i(0)=\delta_{i,i_0} \ \Longrightarrow \ \ \tilde{W}(z,0)=\sum_{i=-\infty}^{+\infty}z^i\delta_{i,i_0}=z^{i_0}
\end{equation*}

We thus find the following expression for the generating function:

\begin{equation}
    \boxed{\tilde{W}(z,k)=\left(zp_++\frac{p_-}{z}\right)^kz^{i_0}}
\end{equation}

It goes without saying that, by setting $i_0=0$, it results

\begin{equation}
    \tilde{W}(z,k)=\left(zp_++\frac{p_-}{z}\right)^k
\end{equation}

Having found an analytical expression for $\tilde{W}(z,k)$ independent of $W_i(k)$, we can use this to calculate the mean and variance associated with the probability distribution relative to the particle's position.

First of all, let's note that by deriving the generating function starting from the definition, it results:

\begin{equation*}
    \frac{d\tilde{W}}{dz}=\sum_{i=-\infty}^{+\infty}iz^{i-1}W_i(k)
\end{equation*}

By setting $z=1$ we find the average position:

\begin{equation}
    \frac{d\tilde{W}}{dz}\bigg|_{z=1}=\sum_{i=-\infty}^{+\infty}iW_i(k)=\langle i(k)\rangle\equiv\mu(k)
\end{equation}

Let's calculate the first member starting from (eq:diffusione-funzione-generatrice-espressione):

\begin{equation}
    \frac{d\tilde{W}}{dz}=k\left(zp_++\frac{p_-}{z}\right)^{k-1}\left(p_+-\frac{p_-}{z^2}\right)z^{i_0}+\left(zp_++\frac{p_-}{z}\right)^ki_0z^{i_0-1}
\end{equation}

\begin{equation*}
    \frac{d\tilde{W}}{dz}\bigg|_{z=1}=k\left(p_++p_-\right)^{k-1}\left(p_+-p_-\right)+\left(p_++p_-\right)^ki_0
\end{equation*}

Remembering that $p_++p_-=1$:

\begin{equation}
    \boxed{\mu(k)=k(p_+-p_-)+i_0}
\end{equation}

Intuitively, the particle will tend to move over time by a quantity proportional to $p_+-p_-$ with respect to the starting coordinate $x_{i_0}$.

Let's now move on to the second derivative of $\tilde{W}(z,k)$:

\begin{equation*}
    \frac{d^2\tilde{W}}{dz^2}=\sum_{i=-\infty}^{+\infty}i(i-1)z^{i-2}W_i(k)
\end{equation*}

From which it follows

\begin{equation*}
    \frac{d^2\tilde{W}}{dz^2}\bigg|_{z=1}=\sum_{i=-\infty}^{+\infty}i(i-1)W_i(k)=\langle i(i-1)\rangle=\left\langle i(k)^2-i(k)\right\rangle=\left\langle i(k)^2\right\rangle-\langle i(k)\rangle
\end{equation*}

We know that the variance is given by:

\begin{equation*}
    \sigma(k)^2=\left\langle i(k)^2\right\rangle-\langle i(k)\rangle^2
\end{equation*}

Therefore, by adding and subtracting $\langle i(k)\rangle$:

\begin{equation*}
    \sigma(k)^2=\left\langle i(k)^2\right\rangle-\langle i(k)\rangle+\langle i(k)\rangle-\langle i(k)\rangle^2=\frac{d^2\tilde{W}}{dz^2}\bigg|_{z=1}+\langle i(k)\rangle-\langle i(k)\rangle^2
\end{equation*}

All we have to do, therefore, is to calculate the second derivative of $\tilde{W}(z,k)$; let's start directly from (eq:diffusione-funzione-generatrice-derivata-prima), coloring each term differently to better visualize each contribution dependent on $z$:

\begin{equation*}
    \frac{d}{dz}\left(\frac{d\tilde{W}}{dz}\right)=\frac{d}{dz}\left[\red{k\left(zp_++\frac{p_-}{z}\right)^{k-1}}\green{\left(p_+-\frac{p_-}{z^2}\right)}\orange{z^{i_0}}+\blue{\left(zp_++\frac{p_-}{z}\right)^k}\purple{i_0z^{i_0-1}}\right]
\end{equation*}

Let's now calculate each contribution separately:

\begin{align*}
    &\red{\raisebox{.5pt}{\textcircled{\raisebox{-.9pt} {1}}}}   k\red{(k-1)\left(zp_++\frac{p_-}{z}\right)^{k-2}}\left(p_+-\frac{p_-}{z^2}\right)^{\red{2}}z^{i_0} \\
    &\green{\raisebox{.5pt}{\textcircled{\raisebox{-.9pt} {2}}}}   k\left(zp_++\frac{p_-}{z}\right)^{k-1}\green{\left(\frac{2p_-}{z^3}\right)}z^{i_0} \\
    &\orange{\raisebox{.5pt}{\textcircled{\raisebox{-.9pt} {3}}}}   k\left(zp_++\frac{p_-}{z}\right)^{k-1}\left(p_+-\frac{p_-}{z^2}\right)\orange{i_0z^{i_0-1}} \\
    &\blue{\raisebox{.5pt}{\textcircled{\raisebox{-.9pt} {4}}}}   \blue{k\left(zp_++\frac{p_-}{z}\right)^{k-1}\left(p_+-\frac{p_-}{z^2}\right)}i_0z^{i_0-1} \\
    &\purple{\raisebox{.5pt}{\textcircled{\raisebox{-.9pt} {5}}}}   \left(zp_++\frac{p_-}{z}\right)^k\purple{i_0\left(i_0-1\right)z^{i_0-2}}
\end{align*}

Let's now set $z=1$:

\begin{align*}
    &\red{\raisebox{.5pt}{\textcircled{\raisebox{-.9pt} {1}}}}   k(k-1)\left(p_++p_-\right)^{k-2}\left(p_+-p_-\right)^2=k(k-1)\left(p_+-p_-\right)^2 \\
    &\green{\raisebox{.5pt}{\textcircled{\raisebox{-.9pt} {2}}}}   k\left(p_++p_-\right)^{k-1}2p_-=2kp_- \\
    &\orange{\raisebox{.5pt}{\textcircled{\raisebox{-.9pt} {3}}}}   k\left(p_++p_-\right)^{k-1}\left(p_+-p_-\right)i_0=k\left(p_+-p_-\right)i_0 \\
    &\blue{\raisebox{.5pt}{\textcircled{\raisebox{-.9pt} {4}}}}   k\left(p_++p_-\right)^{k-1}\left(p_+-p_-\right)i_0=k\left(p_+-p_-\right)i_0 \\
    &\purple{\raisebox{.5pt}{\textcircled{\raisebox{-.9pt} {5}}}}   \left(p_++p_-\right)^ki_0\left(i_0-1\right)=i_0\left(i_0-1\right) \\
\end{align*}

Returning to the expression for $\sigma(k)^2$, we will have to sum all these contributions together with $\mu(k)$ and $-\mu(k)^2$:

\begin{align*}
    \sigma(k)^2=&k(k-1)\left(p_+-p_-\right)^2+2kp_-\cancel{+2k\left(p_+-p_-\right)i_0}\cancel{+i_0^2}\cancel{-i_0}+\\
    &+k\left(p_+-p_-\right)\cancel{+i_0}-k^2\left(p_+-p_-\right)^2\\
    & \cancel{-2k\left(p_+-p_-\right)i_0}\\
    & \cancel{-i_0^2}
\end{align*}

What remains is:

\begin{equation*}
    \sigma(k)^2=\cancel{k^2\left(p_+-p_-\right)^2}-k\left(p_+-p_-\right)^2+2kp_-+k\left(p_+-p_-\right)\cancel{-k^2\left(p_+-p_-\right)^2}
\end{equation*}

\begin{equation*}
    \sigma(k)^2=2kp_-+k\left(p_+-p_-\right)\left[1-\left(p_+-p_-\right)\right]
\end{equation*}

Knowing that $1=p_++p_-$:

\begin{equation*}
    1-\left(p_+-p_-\right)=\cancel{p_+}+p_- \cancel{-p_+}+p_-=2p_- \ \Longrightarrow \ \ \sigma(k)^2=2kp_-+2k_-(p_+-p_-)
\end{equation*}

We thus find:

\begin{equation*}
    \sigma(k)^2=2kp_-(1+p_+-p_-)
\end{equation*}

Substituting once again $1=p_++p_-$:

\begin{equation}
    \sigma(k)^2=2kp_-(p_+\cancel{+p_-}+p_+\cancel{-p_-})\ \Longrightarrow \ \boxed{\sigma(k)^2=4kp_+p_-}
\end{equation}

The variance therefore also increases proportionally to time, in this case by a factor given by the product $p_+p_-$.

At this point, let's try to calculate the distribution. Let's go back to equation (eq:diffusione-funzione-generatrice-espressione) and write the binomial power using the binomial theorem:

\begin{equation*}
    \tilde{W}(z,k)=z^{i_0}\sum_{n=0}^k\binom{k}{n}\left(zp_+\right)^n\left(\frac{p_-}{z}\right)^{k-n}
\end{equation*}

We want the summation index to run over a symmetric interval, thus going from $n\in[0,k]$ to $i\in[-k,k]$. Let's therefore set $i=2n-k$ so that $n=0\Rightarrow i=-k$ and that $n=k\Rightarrow i=k$, obtaining:\footnote{Although not made explicit by the professor, not all values of $i$ in $[-k,k]$ are possible, but only those such that $k+i$ is an even number, since otherwise the factorial would include a fractional number.}

\begin{equation*}
    n=\frac{k+i}{2} \ \Longrightarrow \ \ \tilde{W}(z,k)=z^{i_0}\sum_{i=-k}^k\binom{k}{\frac{k+i}{2}}\left(zp_+\right)^{\frac{k+i}{2}}\left(\frac{p_-}{z}\right)^{k-\frac{k+i}{2}}
\end{equation*}

Let's make the factor $z$ explicit:

\begin{align*}
    \tilde{W}(z,k)&=z^{i_0}\sum_{i=-k}^kz^{\frac{k+i}{2}}z^{-\frac{k-i}{2}}\binom{k}{\frac{k+i}{2}}\left(p_+\right)^{\frac{k+i}{2}}\left(p_-\right)^{\frac{k-i}{2}}=\\
    &=z^{i_0}\sum_{i=-k}^kz^i\binom{k}{\frac{k+i}{2}}\left(p_+\right)^{\frac{k+i}{2}}\left(p_-\right)^{\frac{k-i}{2}}\\
    &=\sum_{i=-k}^kz^{i+i_0}\binom{k}{\frac{k+i}{2}}\left(p_+\right)^{\frac{k+i}{2}}\left(p_-\right)^{\frac{k-i}{2}}
\end{align*}

Let's set $j=i+i_0$ so as to have only the index as the exponent of $z$:\footnote{The same argument made in the previous note applies: $j$ must be such that $k+j-i_0$ is even.}

\begin{equation*}
    \tilde{W}(z,k)=\sum_{j=-k+i_0}^{k+i_0}z^{j}\binom{k}{\frac{k+j-i_0}{2}}\left(p_+\right)^{\frac{k+\left(j-i_0\right)}{2}}\left(p_-\right)^{\frac{k-\left(j-i_0\right)}{2}}
\end{equation*}

Let's make a comparison with (eq:diffusione-funzione-generatrice-definizione):

\begin{equation*}
    \sum_{i=-\infty}^{+\infty}z^iW_i(k)=\sum_{i=-k+i_0}^{k+i_0}z^i\binom{k}{\frac{k+i-i_0}{2}}\left(p_+\right)^{\frac{k+\left(i-i_0\right)}{2}}\left(p_-\right)^{\frac{k-\left(i-i_0\right)}{2}}
\end{equation*}

We thus find that

\begin{equation}
    \boxed{W_i(k)= \
    \begin{cases}
        \binom{k}{\frac{k+i-i_0}{2}}\left(p_+\right)^{\frac{k+\left(i-i_0\right)}{2}}\left(p_-\right)^{\frac{k-\left(i-i_0\right)}{2}} \ \  &i\in[-k+i_0,k+i_0]\\
                0 \ &   \text{otherwise}
    \end{cases}}
\end{equation}

        &=\sum_{i=-\infty}^{+\infty}\sum_{j=i-k}^{i+k}z^ig_j\binom{k}{\frac{k+i-j}{2}}\left(p_+\right)^{\frac{k+i-j}{2}}\left(p_-\right)^{\frac{k-(i-j)}{2}}
    \end{align*}

    Let's also make a comparison this time with (eq:diffusione-funzione-generatrice-definizione):

    \begin{equation*}
        \sum_{i=-\infty}^{+\infty}z^iW_i(k)=\sum_{i=-\infty}^{+\infty}z^i\sum_{j=i-k}^{i+k}g_j\binom{k}{\frac{k+i-j}{2}}\left(p_+\right)^{\frac{k+i-j}{2}}\left(p_-\right)^{\frac{k-(i-j)}{2}}
    \end{equation*}

    From which it follows that

    \begin{equation}
        \boxed{W_i(k)=\sum_{j=i-k}^{i+k}g_j\binom{k}{\frac{k+i-j}{2}}\left(p_+\right)^{\frac{k+i-j}{2}}\left(p_-\right)^{\frac{k-(i-j)}{2}}}
    \end{equation}

    We note that by imposing $g_j=\delta_{j,i_0}$ we find exactly (eq:diffusione-distribuzione-finale):

    \begin{align*}
        W_i(k)&=\sum_{j=i-k}^{i+k}\delta_{j,i_0}\binom{k}{\frac{k+i-j}{2}}\left(p_+\right)^{\frac{k+i-j}{2}}\left(p_-\right)^{\frac{k-(i-j)}{2}}=\\
        &=\binom{k}{\frac{k+i-i_0}{2}}\left(p_+\right)^{\frac{k+i-i_0}{2}}\left(p_-\right)^{\frac{k-\left(i-i_0\right)}{2}}
    \end{align*}

    and this result is possible only if $i_0\in[i-k,i+k]$, or in other words if

    \begin{equation*}
        i-k\leqslant i_0\leqslant i+k \ \Longrightarrow \ \
        \begin{cases}
            i-k\leqslant i_0\\
            i_0\leqslant i+k
        \end{cases}
        \iff \ \
        \begin{cases}
            i\leqslant k+i_0\\
            i\geqslant -k+i_0
        \end{cases}
    \end{equation*}

    that is $i\in[-k+i_0,k+i_0]$, since otherwise the contribution is not present in the summation and will therefore be $0$.

## The continuous limit

Having reached this point, it is not yet completely clear why the random walk should be linked to the diffusion process described at the beginning of the chapter.

The means that allows us to pass from one scale (the microscopic one) to the other (the mesoscopic one) is the **continuous limit**, in which the quantities $\Delta\ell$ and $\Delta t$ are progressively reduced until they become infinitesimal.

We will therefore have to rewrite equation (eq:diffusion-master-equation) by making these quantities explicit; we will use the following conventions:

\begin{align*}
    x_i\equiv x \ \Longrightarrow \ \ &x_{i\pm1}=x_i\pm\Delta\ell\equiv x\pm\Delta\ell\\
    t_i\equiv t \ \Longrightarrow \ \ &t_{i\pm1}=t_i\pm\Delta t\equiv t\pm\Delta t
\end{align*}

We can therefore write:

\begin{equation}
    W(x,t+\Delta t)=W(x-\Delta\ell,t)p_++W(x+\Delta\ell,t)p_-
\end{equation}

At this point we can expand the functions on the right-hand side in series, assuming that $x\gg\Delta\ell$ so that we can stop the expansion at the first order while still ensuring a good approximation:

\begin{align*}
    &W(x-\Delta\ell,t)=W(x,t)-\Delta\ell\frac{\partial W}{\partial x}+\frac{\Delta\ell^2}{2}\frac{\partial^2W}{\partial x^2}+\mathcal{O}\left(\Delta\ell^3\right)\\
    &W(x+\Delta\ell,t)=W(x,t)+\Delta\ell\frac{\partial W}{\partial x}+\frac{\Delta\ell^2}{2}\frac{\partial^2W}{\partial x^2}+\mathcal{O}\left(\Delta\ell^3\right)
\end{align*}

Let's sum the two terms, remembering that the first is multiplied by $p_+$ and the second by $p_-$:

\begin{equation*}
    \left(p_++p_-\right)W(x,t)-\left(p_+-p_-\right)\Delta\ell\frac{\partial W}{\partial x}+\left(p_++p_-\right)\frac{\Delta\ell^2}{2}\frac{\partial^2W}{\partial x^2}+\mathcal{O}\left(\Delta\ell^3\right)
\end{equation*}

Let's substitute in (eq:diffusione-limite-continuo-1) remembering that $p_++p_-=1$:

\begin{equation*}
    W(x,t+\Delta t)=W(x,t)-\left(p_+-p_-\right)\Delta\ell\frac{\partial W}{\partial x}+\frac{\Delta\ell^2}{2}\frac{\partial^2W}{\partial x^2}+\mathcal{O}\left(\Delta\ell^3\right)
\end{equation*}

Let's bring $W(x,t)$ to the left-hand side and divide by $\Delta t$:

\begin{equation}
    \frac{W(x,t+\Delta t)-W(x,t)}{\Delta t}=-\left(p_+-p_-\right)\frac{\Delta\ell}{\Delta t}\frac{\partial W}{\partial x}+\frac{\Delta\ell^2}{2\Delta t}\frac{\partial^2W}{\partial x^2}+\mathcal{O}\left(\Delta\ell^3\right)
\end{equation}

It is clear at this point that, going to the limit for $\Delta t\to0$, the left-hand side would be reduced to the derivative of $W(x,t)$ with respect to time, provided that $t\gg\Delta t$; at the same time, as said before, we will also have to send $\Delta\ell\to0$. We note that on the right we have two ratios dependent on $\Delta\ell$ and $\Delta t$, which are not defined when we pass to the limit.

Let's therefore define the following quantities:

\begin{equation}
    \begin{cases}
        \left(p_+-p_-\right)\lim\limits_{\Delta\ell,\Delta t\to0}\frac{\Delta\ell}{\Delta t}=v\\
        \lim\limits_{\Delta\ell,\Delta t\to0}\frac{\Delta\ell^2}{\Delta t}=D
    \end{cases}
\end{equation}

In other words, we are stating that the ratio between $\Delta\ell$ and $\Delta t$, as well as that between $\Delta\ell^2$ and the same $\Delta t$, is kept constant, so that physical meaning is guaranteed to the model.

The equation in its final form will therefore be

\begin{equation}
    \boxed{\frac{\partial W}{\partial t}=-v\frac{\partial W}{\partial x}+\frac{D}{2}\frac{\partial^2W}{\partial x^2}}
\end{equation}

The quantity $v$ has been so named precisely because it has the dimensions of a velocity, and represents the displacement of the particle to the left or to the right: we note in fact that for $p_+=p_-=\frac{1}{2}$ the velocity is zero, since there is no tendency in one direction with respect to the other; in the case where $p_+\neq p_-$ there will be a *drift* to the right if $p_+>p_-$ or to the left if $p_+<p_-$.

In this way we have rediscovered equation (eq:diffusione-derivazione-einstein-equazione-generale), generalizing to the case where $v\neq0$.

### Fundamental solution of the diffusion equation

Having found the most general expression for the diffusion equation, we now want to calculate the *fundamental solution*, that is, the one that is able to solve almost any diffusion problem starting from a generic initial condition. Since we are going to solve a partial differential equation, a condition that must necessarily be respected is the *boundedness* of the solution: this would not be necessary in a finite interval, but since it is formally defined in all of $\mathbb{R}$ then it is necessary that it also be bounded, since otherwise it would not be a *unique* solution.

In particular, we will impose that

\begin{equation*}
    w(x,0)=\delta\left(x-x_0\right)
\end{equation*}

Although the Dirac delta $\delta\left(x-x_0\right)$ is a function that diverges at $x=x_0$, in fact, what is important is that its integral over all of $\mathbb{R}$ is *finite*: in this way the evolution over time of the distribution can spread but certainly not condense to the point of diverging at some point, since the area underneath is still finite.

Let's now proceed to the solution, for which it is necessary to resort to the **Fourier transform** with respect to the spatial coordinate:

\begin{equation}
    \tilde{w}(\omega,t)=\int_{-\infty}^{+\infty}w(x,t)e^{i\omega x}dx
\end{equation}

First we will see the simplest case in which $v=0$, while later we will also calculate the solution in the more general case with $v\neq0$.

Let's apply the transform to the first member:

\begin{equation*}
    \mathcal{F}\left(\frac{\partial w}{\partial t}\right)=\int_{-\infty}^{+\infty}\frac{\partial w}{\partial t}e^{i\omega x}dx=\frac{\partial}{\partial t}\int_{-\infty}^{+\infty}w(x,t)e^{i\omega x}dx=\frac{\partial\tilde{\omega}}{\partial t}
\end{equation*}

Let's now apply it to the second member:

\begin{equation*}
    \frac{D}{2}\mathcal{F}\left(\frac{\partial^2w}{\partial x^2}\right)=\frac{D}{2}\int_{-\infty}^{+\infty}\frac{\partial^2w}{\partial x^2}e^{i\omega x}dx
\end{equation*}

To solve this integral we must integrate by parts twice:

\begin{equation*}
    \int_{-\infty}^{+\infty}\frac{\partial^2w}{\partial x^2}e^{i\omega x}dx=\frac{\partial w}{\partial x}e^{i\omega x}\bigg|_{-\infty}^{+\infty}-\int_{-\infty}^{+\infty}\frac{\partial w}{\partial x}\frac{\partial e^{i\omega x}}{\partial x}dx
\end{equation*}

Let's remember that $w(x,t)$ is nothing more than a distribution, so it is legitimate to assume that for $x\to\pm\infty$ it (and its two successive derivatives) tend to zero. In this way the first term will be zero and we can repeat the same reasoning with the second term:

\begin{align*}
    \int_{-\infty}^{+\infty}\frac{\partial^2w}{\partial x^2}e^{i\omega x}dx&=-i\omega\int_{-\infty}^{+\infty}\frac{\partial w}{\partial x}e^{i\omega x}dx=\\
    &=-i\omega\left[\cancel{w(x,t)e^{i\omega x}\big|_{-\infty}^{+\infty}}-\int_{-\infty}^{+\infty}w(x,t)\frac{\partial e^{i\omega x}}{\partial x}dx\right]=\\
    &=-i\omega\left[-i\omega\int_{-\infty}^{+\infty}w(x,t)e^{i\omega x}dx\right]=-\omega^2\tilde{w}(\omega,t)
\end{align*}

We thus obtain the following first-order differential equation:

\begin{equation}
    \frac{\partial\tilde{\omega}}{\partial t}=-\frac{\omega^2D}{2}\tilde{w}(\omega,t)
\end{equation}

It is solvable by separation of variables:

\begin{equation*}
    \frac{d\tilde{w}}{dt}=-\frac{\omega^2D}{2}\tilde{w} \ \Longrightarrow \ \ \int\frac{d\tilde{w}}{\tilde{w}}=-\frac{\omega^2D}{2}\int dt \ \Longrightarrow \ \ \log\tilde{w}=-\frac{\omega^2D}{2}t+c
\end{equation*}

The solution, therefore, will be

\begin{equation}
    \tilde{w}(\omega,t)=ke^{-\frac{\omega^2D}{2}t}
\end{equation}

By imposing $w(x,0)=\delta\left(x-x_0\right)$ we can apply the initial condition to obtain the unique solution:

\begin{equation*}
    \tilde{w}(\omega,0)=\int_{-\infty}^{+\infty}w(x,0)e^{i\omega x}dx=\int_{-\infty}^{+\infty}\delta\left(x-x_0\right)e^{i\omega x}dx=e^{i\omega x_0}
\end{equation*}

Ultimately, it results:

\begin{equation}
    \tilde{w}(\omega,t)=e^{i\omega x_0-\frac{\omega^2D}{2}t}
\end{equation}

To return to the form $w(x,t)$ we will have to resort to the inverse transform:

\begin{equation}
    w(x,t)=\frac{1}{2\pi}\int_{-\infty}^{+\infty}\tilde{w}(\omega,t)e^{-i\omega x}d\omega=\frac{1}{2\pi}\int_{-\infty}^{+\infty}e^{-\frac{\omega^2D}{2}t-i\omega\left(x-x_0\right)}d\omega
\end{equation}

This integral is none other than a Gaussian integral in the most general form seen in equation (eq:integrali-gaussiani-forma-generale), in which $a=Dt$ and $b=-i\left(x-x_0\right)$, from which the solution follows:

\begin{equation}
    w(x,t)=\frac{1}{2\pi}\sqrt{\frac{2\pi}{Dt}}e^{-\frac{\left(x-x_0\right)^2}{2Dt}} \ \Longrightarrow \ \boxed{w(x,t)=\frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi Dt}}e^{-\frac{\left(x-x_0 \right)^2}{2Dt}}}
\end{equation}

The most important thing to note is that $w(x,t)$ is normalized over the entire spatial interval at any instant of time, in fact:

\begin{equation*}
    \int_{-\infty}^{+\infty}w(x,t)dx=\frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi Dt}}\int_{-\infty}^{+\infty}e^{-\frac{\left(x-x_0 \right)^2}{2Dt}}dx=\frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi Dt}}\sqrt{2\pi Dt}=1
\end{equation*}

This means that we can interpret $w(x,t)$ as a *probability density function*, and this will be useful in the calculations that we will see below.

\subsubsection{Extension to an initial time instant $t=t_0$}

So far we have assumed for simplicity that the diffusion process started from the instant $t=0$. To extend the reasoning to a generic initial instant $t_0$ we will have to rewrite the initial condition as

\begin{equation*}
    w\left(x,t_0\right)=\delta\left(x-x_0\right)
\end{equation*}

The procedure, on the whole, does not change; the only real difference is from (eq:diffusione-limite-continuo-eq-diff-trasformata), from which the constant $k$ will take the value

\begin{equation*}
    \tilde{w}\left(\omega,t_0\right)=e^{i\omega x_0} \ \Longrightarrow \ \ e^{i\omega x_0}=ke^{-\frac{\omega^2D}{2}t_0} \ \iff \ \ k=e^{i\omega x_0+\frac{\omega^2D}{2}t_0}
\end{equation*}

We thus find:

\begin{equation}
    \tilde{w}(\omega,t)=e^{i\omega x_0-\frac{\omega^2D}{2}\left(t-t_0\right)}
\end{equation}

At this point the inverse transform will not change going from $t$ to $t-t_0$, so the final solution will be

\begin{equation}
    \boxed{w(x,t)=\frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi D\left(t-t_0\right)}}e^{-\frac{\left(x-x_0\right)^2}{2D\left(t-t_0\right)}}}
\end{equation}

This relationship demonstrates the **translational invariance** of the solution with respect to both space and time. The meaning is that this quantity depends only on the difference between the spatial coordinates $x-x_0$ and the temporal ones $t-t_0$, and there are no privileged positions or privileged instants of time with respect to others; mathematically, in fact, we can state that

\begin{equation}
    w\left(x,t|x_0,t_0
\right)=w\left(x-x_0,t-t_0|0,0
\right)
\end{equation}

\subsubsection{Extension to $v\neq0$}

If we assume that $v\neq0$ then, when we apply the Fourier transform, on the right-hand side of the diffusion equation we will also have to consider the contribution

\begin{equation*}
    -v\mathcal{F}\left(\frac{\partial w}{\partial x}\right)
\end{equation*}

From the calculations seen above we know that

\begin{equation*}
    \mathcal{F}\left(\frac{\partial w}{\partial x}\right)=-i\omega\mathcal{F}(w)
\end{equation*}

from which it follows:

\begin{equation*}
    \frac{\partial\tilde{\omega}}{\partial t}=-\left(\frac{\omega^2D}{2}-i\omega v\right)\tilde{w}(\omega,t)
\end{equation*}

From (eq:diffusione-estensione-a-tempo-t_0) we can easily understand how the solution of this differential equation is

\begin{equation*}
    \tilde{w}(\omega,t)=e^{i\omega x_0-\left(\frac{\omega^2D}{2}-i\omega v\right)\left(t-t_0 \right)}
\end{equation*}

Let's now perform the inverse transform:

\begin{equation*}
    w(x,t)=\frac{1}{2\pi}\int_{-\infty}^{+\infty}\tilde{w}(\omega,t)e^{-i\omega x}d\omega=\frac{1}{2\pi}\int_{-\infty}^{+\infty}e^{-\frac{\omega^2D}{2}\left(t-t_0 \right)-i\omega\left[x-x_0-v\left(t-t_0 \right)\right]}d\omega
\end{equation*}

The Gaussian integral will give:

\begin{equation}
\boxed{w(x,t)=\frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi D\left(t-t_0
\right)}}e^{-\frac{\left[x-x_0-v\left(t-t_0
\right)\right]^2}{2D\left(t-t_0
\right)}}}
\end{equation}

\subsubsection{Extension to a higher number of dimensions}

The results obtained can be generalized to a generic number of dimensions greater than one. Interpreting $w\left(\vec{x},t\right)$ as the probability distribution associated with the particles in a system of (hyper)volume $V$ and (hyper)surface $\Sigma(V)$, and indicating with $\vec{J}(\vec{x},t)$ the *flux density* of the particles themselves, from the continuity equation it results:

\begin{equation*}
    \frac{d}{dt}\int_Vw\left(\vec{x},t\right)d^nx=-\int_{\Sigma(V)}\vec{J}\left(\vec{x},t\right)\cdot\hat{n}dS
\end{equation*}

where $\hat{n}$ is the unit vector orthogonal to the surface unit $dS$.

From the divergence theorem, moreover, we can also reduce to a volume integral for $\vec{J}$:

\begin{equation*}
    \int_{\Sigma(V)}\vec{J}\left(\vec{x},t\right)\cdot\hat{n}dS=\int_V\vec{\nabla}\cdot\vec{J}\left(\vec{x},t\right)d^nx
\end{equation*}

Substituting in the continuity equation and bringing everything to the left-hand side inside a single integral:

\begin{equation*}
    \int_V\left(\frac{\partial w}{\partial t}+\vec{\nabla}\cdot\vec{J}\right)d^nx=0
\end{equation*}

From this it follows that

\begin{equation}
    \frac{\partial w}{\partial t}+\vec{\nabla}\cdot\vec{J}=0 \ \Longrightarrow \ \frac{\partial w}{\partial t}=-\vec{\nabla}\cdot\vec{J}
\end{equation}

Since there are no external fields, we can assume that in a first approximation the flux density is proportional to the opposite of the gradient of the particle density, since they tend to diffuse from regions of high density to those of low density. For reasons of dimension, the proportionality coefficient is the diffusion coefficient $D$:

\begin{equation}
    \vec{J}\left(\vec{x},t\right)\simeq-D\vec{\nabla}w\left(\vec{x},t\right)
\end{equation}

This approximation is known as **Fick's law**, which substituted in (eq:diffusione-dimensioni-maggiori-1) returns

\begin{equation*}
    \frac{\partial w}{\partial t}\simeq\vec{\nabla}\left[D\vec{\nabla}w\left(\vec{x},t\right)\right]
\end{equation*}

Assuming $D$ is constant, finally:

\begin{equation}
    \boxed{\frac{\partial w}{\partial t}\simeq D\nabla^2w\left(\vec{x},t\right)}
\end{equation}

We thus rediscover the formula (eq:diffusione-derivazione-einstein-equazione-generale-dim>1) that we had already anticipated, except for a factor of $\frac{1}{2}$ due to a simple approximation of Fick's law.

### Solutions via propagator

The peculiarity of the fundamental solution written in (eq:diffusione-soluzione-fondamentale-generale) is that it describes how the initial information \q{propagates} from a point $x_0$ in time; for this reason it is also called the **propagator** of the diffusion process.

As reported previously, the fundamental solution is so called because from it all the solutions of the diffusion equation can be derived starting from any initial condition, provided - as said - that the boundedness of the function is guaranteed. In other words, we can say that all solutions can be written starting from the *propagator* of the relative equation.

It is not difficult to verify that the function\footnote{Previously we indicated with $w$ the probability density relative to the mesoscopic derivation and with $W$ that relative to the microscopic derivation; from now on we will indicate with $w$ the fundamental solution and with $W$ the general solution of the diffusion problem.}

\begin{equation}
    W(x,t)=\int_{-\infty}^{+\infty}f(y)w\left(x,t|y,t_0\right)dy
\end{equation}

is a solution of the problem

\begin{equation}
    \begin{cases}
        \frac{\partial W}{\partial t}=\frac{D}{2}\frac{\partial^2W}{\partial x^2}\\
        W\left(x,t_0\right)=f(x)
    \end{cases}
\end{equation}

assuming that $f\in C^{\infty}(\mathbb{R})$.

To prove this, let's calculate the time derivative of $W(x,t)$:

\begin{equation*}
    \frac{\partial W}{\partial t}=\frac{\partial}{\partial t}\int_{-\infty}^{+\infty}f(y)w\left(x,t|y,t_0\right)dy=\int_{-\infty}^{+\infty}f(y)\frac{\partial w\left(x,t|y,t_0\right)}{\partial t}dy
\end{equation*}

From the diffusion equation (with zero drift velocity):

\begin{equation*}
    \frac{\partial w}{\partial t}=\frac{D}{2}\frac{\partial^2w}{\partial x^2} \ \Longrightarrow \ \frac{\partial W}{\partial t}=\int_{-\infty}^{+\infty}f(y)\frac{D}{2}\frac{\partial^2w\left(x,t|y,t_0\right)}{\partial x^2}dy
\end{equation*}

From which we find:

\begin{equation*}
    \frac{\partial W}{\partial t}=\frac{D}{2}\frac{\partial^2}{\partial x^2}\int_{-\infty}^{+\infty}f(y)w\left(x,t|y,t_0\right)dy=\frac{D}{2}\frac{\partial^2W}{\partial x^2}
\end{equation*}

which is precisely the equation of the system (eq:diffusione-limite-continuo-problema-generale).

By making $W\left(x,t_0\right)$ explicit, moreover, we have

\begin{equation*}
    W\left(x,t_0\right)=\int_{-\infty}^{+\infty}f(y)w\left(x,t_0|y,t_0\right)dy=\int_{-\infty}^{+\infty}f(y)\delta(x-y)dy=f(x)
\end{equation*}

which is the expected initial condition.

The reasoning can be generalized for $v\neq0$, in which case (eq:diffusione-limite-continuo-soluzione-generale) is a solution of the system

\begin{equation}
    \begin{cases}
        \frac{\partial W}{\partial t}=-v\frac{\partial W}{\partial x}+\frac{D}{2}\frac{\partial^2W}{\partial x^2}\\
        W\left(x,t_0\right)=f(x)
    \end{cases}
\end{equation}

### Generation of moments via propagator

As anticipated, the normalization of the propagator with respect to the whole space, which implies its role as a pdf, is useful for the calculation of quantities such as the average position or the variance with respect to the coordinates $x$, or more generally for the generation of *moments*.

We can in fact write:

\begin{equation}
    \langle f(x)\rangle_t=\int_{-\infty}^{+\infty}f(x)w\left(x,t|x_0,t_0\right)dx
\end{equation}

To easily calculate the moments up to the second order, we can calculate the derivative with respect to time:

\begin{equation*}
    \frac{d}{dt}\langle f(x)\rangle_t=\int_{-\infty}^{+\infty}f(x)\frac{\partial w}{\partial t}dx=-v\int_{-\infty}^{+\infty}f(x)\frac{\partial w}{\partial x}dx+\frac{D}{2}\int_{-\infty}^{+\infty}f(x)\frac{\partial^2w}{\partial x^2}dx
\end{equation*}

Remembering that $w\left(x,t|x_0,t_0\right)$ converges to zero at infinity, both on one side and on the other, we can integrate the first integral by parts and repeat the same thing twice for the second, exactly as done during the calculations that led to equation (eq:diffusione-limite-continuo-equazione-differenziale-primo-ordine-trasformata). We will obtain:

\begin{equation}
    \boxed{\frac{d}{dt}\langle f(x)\rangle_t=v\int_{-\infty}^{+\infty}\frac{df}{dx}w\left(x,t|x_0,t_0\right)dx+\frac{D}{2}\int_{-\infty}^{+\infty}\frac{d^2f}{dx^2}w\left(x,t|x_0,t_0\right)dx}
\end{equation}

We can calculate the average position of the distribution by setting $f(x)=x$:

\begin{equation}
    \frac{d}{dt}\langle x\rangle_t=v\int_{-\infty}^{+\infty}w\left(x,t|x_0,t_0\right)dx+0=v \ \Longrightarrow \ \boxed{\langle x\rangle_t=x_0+v\left(t-t_0\right)}
\end{equation}

To calculate the variance we must first calculate the second-order moment:

\begin{equation*}
    \frac{d}{dt}\left\langle x^2\right\rangle_t=v\int_{-\infty}^{+\infty}2xw\left(x,t|x_0,t_0\right)dx+\frac{D}{2}\int_{-\infty}^{+\infty}2w\left(x,t|x_0,t_0\right)dx=2v\langle x\rangle_t+D
\end{equation*}

Substituting $\langle x\rangle_t$ from (eq:diffusione-media-posizione):

\begin{equation*}
    \frac{d}{dt}\left\langle x^2\right\rangle_t=2vx_0+2v^2\left(t-t_0 \right)+D
\end{equation*}

Integrating:

\begin{equation}
    \left\langle x^2\right\rangle_t=x_0^2+2vx_0\left(t-t_0
\right)+v^2\left(t-t_0
\right)^2+D(t-t_0)
\end{equation}

At this point we can calculate the variance:

\begin{align*}
    &Var(x)=\left\langle x^2\right\rangle_t-\langle x\rangle_t=\\
    &=\cancel{x_0^2}\cancel{+2vx_0\left(t-t_0
\right)}\cancel{+v^2\left(t-t_0
\right)^2}+D(t-t_0)\cancel{-x_0^2}\cancel{-2vx_0\left(t-t_0
\right)}\cancel{-v^2\left(t-t_0
\right)^2}
\end{align*}

What remains is therefore

\begin{equation}
    \boxed{Var(x)=D\left(t-t_0 \right)}
\end{equation}

Both results were actually predictable: looking at the expression (eq:diffusione-soluzione-fondamentale-generale) we notice that it is a Gaussian centered at $x_0+v\left(t-t\right)$ with standard deviation $\sqrt{D\left(t-t_0\right)}$. It is, in practice, a Gaussian initially centered at $x_0$, which tends to move over time with constant velocity $v$, and whose initial null variance (being a Dirac delta) then tends to increase proportionally to $D$, thus spreading the distribution throughout space.

## Diffusion in a semi-infinite domain

So far we have considered diffusion problems that involved all of infinite space. For the last part of this chapter we will also explore two distinct cases of semi-infinite spaces, that is, spaces bounded by a *wall* beyond which unlimited space extends.

We will see how delimiting the space in this way implies the addition of a new constraint to be respected for the propagator of the diffusion process. The calculations will be done assuming the simplest case in which $v=0$ and $t_0=0$, as the results are then easily generalizable to the case with $v\neq0$ and $t_0\neq0$.

### Absorbing wall

Let's assume as a first case that the wall has *absorbing* properties, that is, that every particle that reaches this wall is absorbed by it and therefore *removed* from the system.

For simplicity of calculation, let's assume that this wall is located at $x=0$ and that the possible space for the diffusion of particles is $\mathbb{R}^+$. The new constraint to be imposed, therefore, is the following:

\begin{equation*}
    w(0,t)=0
\end{equation*}

which translated indicates that at any instant of time there are no particles at the coordinate $x=0$, as they are absorbed and removed from the system.

Indicating with $w_a(x,t)$ the propagator relative to this problem, the system to be solved is therefore

\begin{equation}
    \begin{cases}
        \frac{\partial w_a}{\partial t}=\frac{D}{2}\frac{\partial^2w_a}{\partial x^2}\\
        w_a(x,0)=\delta\left(x-x_0\right)\\
        w_a(0,t)=0
    \end{cases}
\end{equation}

The goal, therefore, is to find a solution that is fixed at zero at the origin during the entire evolution and that at the same time satisfies the conditions already seen previously. One idea is to take two distributions that are symmetric with respect to the origin, that evolve in the same way, and subtract them from each other. If we want the distribution to be centered at $x_0$, for example, we will have to consider the one centered at $-x_0$, thus obtaining

\begin{equation}
    \boxed{w_a\left(x,t|x_0,0
\right)=\frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi Dt}}\left(e^{-\frac{\left(x-x_0
\right)^2}{2Dt}}-e^{-\frac{\left(x+x_0
\right)^2}{2Dt}}\right)}
\end{equation}

We can prove that this is a valid solution of the system (eq:diffusione-parete-assorbente-sistema). In the first place, both distributions are solutions of the differential equation; moreover, since the equation is *linear*, a linear combination of solutions is in turn a solution of the same equation, and (eq:diffusione-parete-assorbente-propagatore) is precisely a linear combination of two valid solutions.

As for the initial condition, it will be given by the difference of the Dirac deltas $\delta\left(x-x_0\right)-\delta\left(x+x_0\right)$. This is not a problem, as the space considered is only that of the positive coordinates, so one of the two deltas will not contribute:

\begin{equation*}
    \int_0^{+\infty}w\left(x,0|x_0,0
\right)dx=\int_0^{+\infty}\delta\left(x-x_0
\right)dx-\cancel{\int_0^{+\infty}\delta\left(x+x_0
\right)dx}=1
\end{equation*}

This guarantees the normalization of the distribution at the initial instant of time, so even if the initial condition of the system (eq:diffusione-parete-assorbente-sistema) is not formally respected, it is still a valid solution for it.\footnote{As the professor writes: <<[...] *then* $\delta\left(x+x_0\right)$ *\q{does not matter* because it is in} $\mathbb{R}^-$.>>}

Having found the propagator of the system, the general solution with initial condition $f(x)$ will therefore be given by

\begin{equation}
    W_a(x,t)=\int_0^{+\infty}f(y)w_a\left(x,t|y,0\right)dy
\end{equation}

\subsubsection{Lifetime distribution}

The main difference of the propagator found with respect to the propagator in infinite space is related to its normalization: $w_a(x,t)$, in fact, is normalized only at the instant $t=0$, while for $t>0$ its integral gives as a result a number less than $1$.

To observe this we must make this integral explicit, which we indicate with $P(t)$ as it is dependent on time:

\begin{align*}
    P(t)&=\int_0^{+\infty}w_a\left(x,t|x_0,0\right)dx=\\
    &=\int_0^{+\infty}\frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi Dt}}e^{-\frac{\left(x-x_0
\right)^2}{2Dt}}dx-\int_0^{+\infty}\frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi Dt}}e^{-\frac{\left(x+x_0
\right)^2}{2Dt}}dx
\end{align*}

Let's perform a change of variable for both integrals:

\begin{equation*}
    \begin{cases}
        u=\frac{x-x_0}{\sqrt{Dt}}\\
        v=\frac{x+x_0}{\sqrt{Dt}}
    \end{cases}
    \Longrightarrow \
    \begin{cases}
        du=\frac{dx}{\sqrt{Dt}}\\
        dv=\frac{dx}{\sqrt{Dt}}
    \end{cases}
\end{equation*}

\begin{equation*}
    P(t)=\int_{-\frac{x_0}{\sqrt{Dt}}}^{+\infty}\frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}}e^{-\frac{u^2}{2}}du-\int_{\frac{x_0}{\sqrt{Dt}}}^{+\infty}\frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}}e^{-\frac{v^2}{2}}dv
\end{equation*}

Let's rewrite the first integral as the difference between the integral over the whole space and the complementary interval:

\begin{equation*}
    P(t)=\int_{-\infty}^{+\infty}\frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}}e^{-\frac{u^2}{2}}du-\int_{-\infty}^{-\frac{x_0}{\sqrt{Dt}}}\frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}}e^{-\frac{u^2}{2}}du-\int_{\frac{x_0}{\sqrt{Dt}}}^{+\infty}\frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}}e^{-\frac{v^2}{2}}dv
\end{equation*}

The first integral is a normalized Gaussian integral; in the second integral, since the integrand function is symmetric, we can \q{mirror} the integration interval, obtaining:

\begin{equation*}
    P(t)=1-\int_{\frac{x_0}{\sqrt{Dt}}}^{+\infty}\frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}}e^{-\frac{v^2}{2}}dv-\int_{\frac{x_0}{\sqrt{Dt}}}^{+\infty}\frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}}e^{-\frac{v^2}{2}}dv=1-2\int_{\frac{x_0}{\sqrt{Dt}}}^{+\infty}\frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}}e^{-\frac{v^2}{2}}dv
\end{equation*}

Since the resulting integral is positive for $t>0$, we have therefore shown that

\begin{equation*}
    P(t>1)<1
\end{equation*}

just as stated initially. This quantity is also called **survival probability** as it expresses the probability that a particle *has not been absorbed* at time $t$.

From $P(t)$ we can also calculate the **lifetime distribution**, indicated with $q(t)$, which corresponds to the probability distribution associated with the absorption of a particle. In particular, the decrease of $P(t)$ over time indicates an increase in particles that are absorbed, therefore

\begin{equation*}
    q(t)=-\frac{dP}{dt}
\end{equation*}

The problem at this point is that calculating the derivative of $P(t)$ is not trivial, because the Gaussian integral is not solvable in the interval $\left[\frac{x_0}{\sqrt{Dt}},+\infty\right)$. This forces us to look for another way to calculate the derivative of the survival probability.

The idea is to make the probability $P$ explicit according to the definition and exploit the diffusion equation associated with the propagator:

\begin{align*}
    \frac{dP}{dt}&=\frac{\partial}{\partial t}\int_0^{+\infty}w_a\left(x,t|x_0,0\right)dx=\int_0^{+\infty}\frac{\partial w_a}{\partial t}dx=\frac{D}{2}\int_0^{+\infty}\frac{\partial^2w_a}{\partial x^2}dx=\\
    &=\frac{D}{2}\frac{\partial w_a}{\partial x}\bigg|_0^{+\infty}=0-\frac{D}{2}\frac{\partial w_a}{\partial x}\bigg|_{x=0}=\\
    &=-\frac{D}{2}\frac{\partial}{\partial x}\left[\frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi Dt}}\left(e^{-\frac{\left(x-x_0
\right)^2}{2Dt}}-e^{-\frac{\left(x+x_0
\right)^2}{2Dt}}\right)\right]\bigg|_{x=0}=\\
    &=-\frac{1}{2}\sqrt{\frac{D}{2\pi t}}\left(-\frac{x-x_0}{Dt}e^{-\frac{\left(x-x_0
\right)^2}{2Dt}}\bigg|_{x=0}+\frac{x+x_0}{Dt}e^{-\frac{\left(x+x_0
\right)^2}{2Dt}}\bigg|_{x=0}\right)=\\
    &=-\frac{1}{2}\sqrt{\frac{D}{2\pi t}}\frac{x_0}{Dt}\left(e^{-\frac{x_0^2}{2Dt}}+e^{-\frac{x_0^2}{2Dt}}\right)=-\frac{1}{\cancel{2}}\frac{x_0}{\sqrt{2\pi Dt^3}}\cdot\cancel{2}e^{-\frac{x_0^2}{2Dt}}
\end{align*}

From which it follows:

\begin{equation}
    \boxed{q(t)=-\frac{dP}{dt}=\frac{x_0}{\sqrt{2\pi Dt^3}}e^{-\frac{x_0^2}{2Dt}}}
\end{equation}

In particular, we find that

\begin{equation}
    q(t)\sim t^{-\frac{3}{2}}
\end{equation}

This trend is characteristic of the diffusion process we are studying, namely *Brownian motion*.

### Reflecting wall

Let's now imagine that the wall at $x=0$ is *reflecting*, that is, capable of sending back every particle that happens to hit it. To do this we must impose that the flow of particles at $x=0$ is zero, in the sense that every particle that reaches the wall at the instant $t$, not being able to cross it, no longer has any \q{push} to the left, but on the contrary is \q{repelled} to the right, and because of this symmetry the net flow is zero:

\begin{equation*}
    J(x=0,t)=0
\end{equation*}

From Fick's law (equation (eq:diffusione-legge-di-fick)) we know that the flow is given by

\begin{equation*}
    J(x,t)=-\frac{D}{2}\frac{\partial w}{\partial x}
\end{equation*}

Therefore the condition to be imposed will be:

\begin{equation}
    \frac{\partial w_r}{\partial x}\bigg|_{x=0}=0
\end{equation}

At this point we proceed as in the absorbing case: we exploit the symmetry of the system and consider a linear combination of the two distributions centered one at $x_0$ and the other at $-x_0$.

\begin{equation*}
    w_r\left(x,t|x_0,0\right)=\frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi Dt}}\left(\alpha e^{-\frac{\left(x-x_0
\right)^2}{2Dt}}+\beta e^{-\frac{\left(x+x_0
\right)^2}{2Dt}}\right)
\end{equation*}

The goal is to find the coefficients $\alpha$ and $\beta$. We therefore impose the condition (eq:diffusione-parete-riflettente-condizione):

\begin{align*}
    \frac{\partial w_r}{\partial x}\bigg|_{x=0}&=\frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi Dt}}\left(-\alpha\frac{x-x_0}{Dt}e^{-\frac{\left(x-x_0
\right)^2}{2Dt}}-\beta\frac{x+x_0}{Dt}e^{-\frac{\left(x+x_0
\right)^2}{2Dt}}\right)\bigg|_{x=0}=\\
    &=\frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi Dt}}\left(\frac{\alpha x_0}{Dt}e^{-\frac{x_0^2}{2Dt}}-\frac{\beta x_0}{Dt}e^{-\frac{x_0^2}{2Dt}}\right)=\frac{x_0}{\sqrt{2\pi D^3t^3}}(\alpha-\beta)e^{-\frac{x_0^2}{2Dt}}
\end{align*}

This implies that

\begin{equation*}
    \frac{\partial w_r}{\partial x}\bigg|_{x=0}=0 \ \iff \ \alpha=\beta
\end{equation*}

By setting $\alpha=\beta=1$ the solution will therefore be given by

\begin{equation*}
    \boxed{w_r\left(x,t|x_0,0\right)=\frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi Dt}}\left(e^{-\frac{\left(x-x_0
\right)^2}{2Dt}}+e^{-\frac{\left(x+x_0
\right)^2}{2Dt}}\right)}
\end{equation*}

Again, having found the propagator, we can write the general solution:

\begin{equation}
    W_r(x,t)=\int_0^{+\infty}f(y)w_r\left(x,t|x_0,0\right)dy
\end{equation}
