14 Birth and Death Processes

The last category of stochastic processes that we will study are the birth and death processes, so called because they describe a multi-state system where states are progressively populated (a birth phenomenon) or abandoned (a death phenomenon).

14.1 Introduction

Let’s imagine numbering each state with an integer, as shown in figure (fig:processi-di-nascita-e-morte-schema). By convention, we consider the of birth towards states with increasing numbers, from which an inevitable of death towards states with decreasing numbers follows.

We hypothesize that in a time interval \(\Delta t\) an element of the system in a given state \(n\) has only three possibilities: to remain in state \(n\) or to move to one of the two neighboring states, be it \(n-1\) or \(n+1\).

We can now write the master equation that allows us to derive the propagator of the process. Considering state \(n\), at time \(t+\Delta t\) we have three possibilities:

Summing the three contributions:

\[\begin{equation*} p(n,t+\Delta t)=b_{n-1}\Delta tp(n-1,t)+d_{n+1}\Delta tp(n+1,t)+\left[1-\left(b_n+d_n\right)\Delta t\right]p(n,t) \end{equation*}\]

Let’s move \(p(,t)\) to the first member and divide by \(\Delta t\):

\[\begin{equation*} \frac{p(n,t+\Delta t)-p(n,t)}{\Delta t}=b_{n-1}p(n-1,t)+d_{n+1}p(n+1,t)-\left(b_n+d_n\right)p(n,t) \end{equation*}\]

In the limit for \(\Delta t\to0\):

\[\begin{equation} \boxed{\frac{\partial}{\partial t}p(n,t)=b_{n-1}p(n-1,t)+d_{n+1}p(n+1,t)-\left(b_n+d_n\right)p(n,t)} \end{equation}\]

By introducing the initial condition \(p(n,t_0)=\delta_{n,n_0}\), the birth and death process introduced here is also a Markov process, and we can prove it mathematically by verifying the Chapman-Kolmogorov equation ((eq:processi-stocastici-equazione-di-chapman-kolmogorov)), which in this context is written as:

\[\begin{equation*} p\left(n_2,t_2|n_0,t_0\right)=\sum_{n_1}p\left(n_2,t_2|n_1,t_1\right)p\left(n_1,t_1|n_0,t_0\right) \end{equation*}\]

Applying the derivative to both members:

\[\begin{equation*} \frac{\partial}{\partial t_2}p\left(n_2,t_2|n_0,t_0\right)=\frac{\partial}{\partial t_2}\sum_{n_1}p\left(n_2,t_2|n_1,t_1\right)p\left(n_1,t_1|n_0,t_0\right) \end{equation*}\]

Since \(p\left(n_1,t_1|n_0,t_0\right)\) is independent of \(t_2\), it can be excluded from the derivation:

\[\begin{equation} \frac{\partial}{\partial t_2}p\left(n_2,t_2|n_0,t_0\right)=\sum_{n_1}\frac{\partial}{\partial t_2}p\left(n_2,t_2|n_1,t_1\right)p\left(n_1,t_1|n_0,t_0\right) \end{equation}\]

Let’s calculate the derivative on the second member using (eq:processi-nascita-morte-propagatore):

\[\begin{align*} \frac{\partial}{\partial t_2}p\left(n_2,t_2|n_1,t_1\right)=&b_{n_2-1}p\left(n_2-1,t_2|n_1,t_1\right)+\\ &+d_{n_2+1}p\left(n_2+1,t_2|n_1,t_1\right)-\\ &-\left(b_{n_2}+d_{n_2}\right)p\left(n_2,t_2|n_1,t_1\right) \end{align*}\]

Let’s substitute in (eq:processo-nascita-morte-chapman-kolmogorov-calcolo-1):

\[\begin{align*} \frac{\partial}{\partial t_2}p\left(n_2,t_2|n_0,t_0\right)=&b_{n_2-1}\sum_{n_1}p\left(n_2-1,t_2|n_1,t_1\right)p\left(n_1,t_1|n_0,t_0\right)+\\ &+d_{n_2+1}\sum_{n_1}p\left(n_2+1,t_2|n_1,t_1\right)p\left(n_1,t_1|n_0,t_0\right)-\\ &-\left(b_{n_2}+d_{n_2}\right)\sum_{n_1}p\left(n_2,t_2|n_1,t_1\right)p\left(n_1,t_1|n_0,t_0\right) \end{align*}\]

Assuming the Chapman-Kolmogorov equation is valid and applying it to the three terms written above, we find:

\[\begin{align*} \frac{\partial}{\partial t_2}p\left(n_2,t_2|n_0,t_0\right)=&b_{n_2-1}p\left(n_2-1,t_2|n_0,t_0\right)+\\ &+d_{n_2+1}p\left(n_2+1,t_2|n_0,t_0\right)-\\ &-\left(b_{n_2}+d_{n_2}\right)p\left(n_2,t_2|n_0,t_0\right) \end{align*}\]

which is precisely the propagator formula written in (eq:processi-nascita-morte-propagatore).

14.1.2 Matrix formalism

It is possible to obtain a more compact structure by defining the vector

\[\begin{equation} \vec{p}(t)= \begin{pmatrix} p(0,t)\\ p(1,t)\\ p(2,t)\\ \vdots \end{pmatrix} \end{equation}\]

assuming that we have started numbering the states from \(0\) onwards.

To be able to write the master equation, we will therefore have to introduce a matrix \(W\) such that

\[\begin{equation} \frac{\partial}{\partial t}\vec{p}(n,t)=W\vec{p}(n,t) \end{equation}\]

Each component \(p(n,t)\) will therefore be calculated as

\[\begin{equation} \frac{\partial}{\partial t}p(n,t)=\sum_{n'}W_{n,n'}p(n',t) \end{equation}\]

To construct \(W\), let’s consider the \(n\)-th row, which, when multiplied by the vector \(\vec{p}(n,t)\), must return the second member of (eq:processi-nascita-morte-propagatore), therefore every element of the vector other than \(p(n-1,t)\), \(p(n,t)\) and \(p(n+1,t)\) must give a null contribution. It follows that each row of \(W\) contains all zeros except for three consecutive elements; using the Kronecker delta, we can indicate the element of the matrix in the following form:

\[\begin{equation} W_{n,n'}=b_{n-1}\delta_{n-1,n'}+d_{n+1}\delta_{n+1,n'}-(b_n+d_n)\delta_{n,n'} \end{equation}\]

In essence, it is a tridiagonal matrix whose elements on the diagonal are all negative and those on the upper and lower diagonals are instead positive. Furthermore, for the overall flow to be conserved, the sum of the elements along each column must be zero. Intuitively, we can understand this by observing three consecutive rows of the matrix \(W\):

\[\begin{equation*} W= \begin{pmatrix} \ddots & \dots & \dots & \dots & \vdots\\ \vdots & -\left(b_{n-1}+d_{n-1}\right) & d_n & 0 & \vdots\\ \vdots & b_{n-1} & -\left(b_n+d_n\right) & d_{n+1} & \vdots\\ \vdots & 0 & b_n & -\left(b_{n+1}+d_{n+1}\right) & \vdots\\ \vdots & \dots & \dots & \dots & \ddots\\ \end{pmatrix} \end{equation*}\]

Formally, this is verified by imposing that \(\sum\limits_np(n,t)\) is constant:

\[\begin{equation*} \frac{\partial}{\partial t}\sum_np(n,t)=0 \ \iff \ \sum_n\sum_{n'}W_{n,n'}p(n',t)=0 \end{equation*}\]

The second member can be rewritten as

\[\begin{equation*} \sum_{n'}\left(\sum_nW_{n,n'}\right)p(n',t)=0 \end{equation*}\]

from which it follows that

\[\begin{equation*} \sum_nW_{n,n'}=0 \ \ \ \forall n' \end{equation*}\]

Ultimately, the birth and death process can be rewritten in the following compact form:

\[\begin{equation} \boxed{\begin{cases} \frac{\partial}{\partial t}\vec{p}(t)=W\vec{p}\\ \vec{p}(0)=\vec{p}_0 \end{cases} \ \ \text{with} \ \ \begin{cases} W_{n,n'}\geqslant0 \ \ & ext{for} \ n\neq n'\\ \sum\limits_nW_{n,n'}=0 &\forall n' \end{cases}} \end{equation}\]}} \end{equation}

from which the solution follows

\[\begin{equation} \vec{p}(t)=\vec{p}_0e^{Wt} \end{equation}\]

14.2 Process statistics

14.2.1 Expected value

For this calculation, we assume that the number of states is infinite and the index \(n\) ranges over the entire set \(\mathbb{Z}\).

By definition:

\[\begin{equation} \langle n\rangle=\sum_{n=-\infty}^{+\infty}np(n.t) \end{equation}\]

Let’s derive both members with respect to time:

\[\begin{equation} \frac{d}{dt}\langle n\rangle=\frac{d}{dt}\sum_{n=-\infty}^{+\infty}np(n.t)=\sum_{n=-\infty}^{+\infty}n\frac{\partial}{\partial t}p(n.t) \end{equation}\]

Let’s substitute the master equation (equation (eq:processi-nascita-morte-propagatore)):

\[\begin{align*} \frac{d}{dt}\langle n\rangle&=\sum_{n=-\infty}^{+\infty}n\left[b_{n-1}p(n-1,t)+d_{n+1}p(n+1,t)-\left(b_n+d_n\right)p(n,t)\right]=\\ &=\sum_{n=-\infty}^{+\infty}nb_{n-1}p(n-1,t)+\sum_{n=-\infty}^{+\infty}nd_{n+1}p(n+1,t)-\sum_{n=-\infty}^{+\infty}n\left(b_n+d_n\right)p(n,t) \end{align*}\]

We can change the indices of the first two summations so that both include the term \(p(n,t)\):

\[\begin{align*} \frac{d}{dt}\langle n\rangle&=\sum_{n=-\infty}^{+\infty}(n+1)b_np(n,t)+\sum_{n=-\infty}^{+\infty}(n-1)d_np(n,t)-\sum_{n=-\infty}^{+\infty}n\left(b_n+d_n\right)p(n,t)=\\ &=\sum_{n=-\infty}^{+\infty}\left[\cancel{nb_n}+b_np(n,t)\cancel{+nd_n}-d_n\cancel{-nb_n}\cancel{-nd_n}\right]p(n,t)=\\ &=\sum_{n=-\infty}^{+\infty}b_np(n,t)-\sum_{n=-\infty}^{+\infty}d_np(n,t) \end{align*}\]

We find:

\[\begin{equation} \boxed{\frac{d}{dt}\langle n\rangle=\left\langle b_n\right\rangle-\left\langle d_n\right\rangle} \end{equation}\]

The result is intuitive: on the one hand we have \(\langle b_n\rangle\), which is the average net flow to the right, from which the average net flow to the left is subtracted; it is clear that the difference between the two will tell us how the average value of the system varies over time.

14.2.2 Variance

For the variance, we proceed in the same way:

\[\begin{align*} \frac{d}{dt}\left\langle n^2\right\rangle&=\frac{d}{dt}\sum_{n=-\infty}^{+\infty}n^2p(n,t)=\sum_{n=-\infty}^{+\infty}n^2\frac{\partial}{\partial t}p(n,t)=\\ &=\sum_{n=-\infty}^{+\infty}n^2\left[b_{n-1}p(n-1,t)+d_{n+1}p(n+1,t)-\left(b_n+d_n\right)p(n,t)\right]=\\ &=\sum_{n=-\infty}^{+\infty}n^2b_{n-1}p(n-1,t)+\sum_{n=-\infty}^{+\infty}n^2d_{n+1}p(n+1,t)-\sum_{n=-\infty}^{+\infty}n^2\left(b_n+d_n\right)p(n,t)=\\ &=\sum_{n=-\infty}^{+\infty}(n+1)^2b_np(n,t)+\sum_{n=-\infty}^{+\infty}(n-1)^2d_np(n,t)-\sum_{n=-\infty}^{+\infty}n^2\left(b_n+d_n\right)p(n,t)=\\ &=\sum_{n=-\infty}^{+\infty}\left[\left(n^2+2n-1\right)b_n+\left(n^2-2n+1\right)d_n-n^2b_n-n^2d_n\right]p(n,t)=\\ &=\sum_{n=-\infty}^{+\infty}\left[\left(\cancel{n^2}+2n-1\cancel{-n^2}\right)b_n+\left(\cancel{n^2}-2n+1\cancel{-n^2}\right)d_n\right]p(n,t)=\\ &=2\sum_{n=-\infty}^{+\infty}n\left(b_n-d_n\right)p(n,t)+\sum_{n=-\infty}^{+\infty}b_np(n,t)+\sum_{n=-\infty}^{+\infty}d_np(n,t) \end{align*}\]

We find:

\[\begin{equation} \boxed{\frac{d}{dt}\left\langle n^2\right\rangle=2\left\langle n\left(b_n-d_n\right)\right\rangle+\left\langle b_n\right\rangle+\left\langle d_n\right\rangle} \end{equation}\]

14.2.3 Equilibrium distribution

Under the appropriate conditions, we can explicitly calculate the equilibrium distribution for a homogeneous birth and death process.

For simplicity, let’s impose a reflecting state at \(n=0\). At equilibrium, the total flow concerning any state \(n\) - both incoming and outgoing - is zero, therefore:

\[\begin{equation*} \frac{\partial}{\partial t}p(n,t)=0 \ \iff \ b_{n-1}p(n-1)+d_{n+1}p(n+1)-\left(b_n+d_n\right)p(n)=0 \end{equation*}\]

Rearranging the terms:

\[\begin{equation*} \green{b_{n-1}p(n-1)-d_np(n)}-\left[\orange{b_np(n)-d_{n+1}p(n+1)}\right]=0 \end{equation*}\]

The highlighted terms, individually, are nothing more than the net flow between one state and the next: in green, for example, we have the flow from state \(n-1\) to state \(n\) (positive) and that from state \(n\) to state \(n-1\) (negative); in orange, instead, it is the net flow between state \(n\) and state \(n+1\).

Indicating these flows with \(J\), we will therefore write:

\[\begin{equation*} J_{n-1}-J_n=0 \ \Longrightarrow \ J_n=J_{n-1} \end{equation*}\]

Since this relationship holds between generic states, we can iterate the formula:

\[\begin{equation*} J_n=J_{n-1}=J_{n-2}=J_{n-3}=\dots \end{equation*}\]

until we reach \(J_1\), beyond which there is only the state \(n=0\) and therefore the flow \(J_0\) relative to the exchange between states \(n=0\) and \(n=1\). Let’s remember that here we have set a reflecting boundary condition, so by construction \(J_0=0\), but consequently all the other flows will also be zero.

In general, therefore:

\[\begin{equation*} J_n=0 \end{equation*}\]

which translates to

\[\begin{equation} \boxed{b_np^s(n)=d_{n+1}p^s(n+1)} \end{equation}\]

where the \(s\) indicates that the propagator is relative to the stationary condition, that is, at equilibrium.

The expression written above is called detailed balance and it is from this that we can calculate the propagator for any state \(n\) in stationary conditions. We will have in fact that:

\[\begin{equation*} b_{n-1}p^s(n-1)=d_np^s(n) \ \iff \ p^s(n)=\frac{b_{n-1}}{d_n}p^s(n-1) \end{equation*}\]

Iterating the formula:

\[\begin{equation*} p^s(n,t)=\frac{b_{n-1}}{d_n}p^s(n-1)=\frac{b_{n-1}b_{n-2}}{d_nd_{n-1}}p^s(n-2)=\frac{b_{n-1}b_{n-2}b_{n-3}}{d_nd_{n-1}d_{n-2}}p^s(n-3)=\dots \end{equation*}\]

We thus find

\[\begin{equation} \boxed{p^s(n)=\prod_{i=1}^n\frac{b_{i-1}}{d_i}p_0^s} \end{equation}\]

having indicated \(p^s(0)\) with \(p_0^s\).

By calculating \(p_0^s\), therefore, we are able to find all the terms \(p(n)\) at equilibrium. It is obtained by imposing normalization:

\[\begin{equation*} \sum_{n=0}^\infty p^s(n)=1 \ \iff \ \sum_{n=0}^\infty\prod_{i=1}^n\frac{b_{i-1}}{d_i}p_0^s=1 \end{equation*}\]

Since \(p_0^s\) is constant:

\[\begin{equation*} p_0^s\sum_{n=0}^\infty\prod_{i=1}^n\frac{b_{i-1}}{d_i}=1 \end{equation*}\]

For \(n=0\) the product acts on an empty interval (since the index \(i\) should run from \(1\) to \(0\), so it is a non-existent set), therefore the result is the neutral term \(1\):

\[\begin{equation*} p_0^s\left(1+\sum_{n=1}^\infty\prod_{i=1}^n\frac{b_{i-1}}{d_i}\right)=1 \end{equation*}\]

From which we find the final formula:

\[\begin{equation} \boxed{p_0^s=\left(1+\sum_{n=1}^\infty\prod_{i=1}^n\frac{b_{i-1}}{d_i}\right)^{-1}} \end{equation}\]

14.3 Examples of birth and death processes

14.3.1 Poisson process

The Poisson process is the simplest nontrivial example of a birth–death family: it is a pure-birth process obtained by taking a constant birth rate and zero death rate. Concretely, for all states \(n=0,1,2,\dots\) we set \[ b_n=\lambda>0,\qquad d_n=0. \] Thus births occur at a constant rate \(\lambda\) while no deaths are possible. This choice simplifies the master equation and yields the closed-form, well-known Poisson law for the state probabilities.

Master equation. Substituting the rates above into the general master equation gives \[\begin{equation} \dot p_n(t)=\lambda\bigl(p_{n-1}(t)-p_n(t)\bigr),\qquad p_n(0)=\delta_{n,0}, \end{equation}\] where by convention we set \(p_{-1}(t)\equiv0\) so the equation is valid for \(n\ge0\).

Expectation. To obtain the mean count, multiply \(\eqref{eq:poisson-master}\) by \(n\) and sum over \(n\ge0\). Using index shifts and the normalization \(\sum_{n\ge0}p_n(t)=1\) we find \[ \frac{d}{dt}\langle n(t)\rangle =\sum_{n\ge0} n\dot p_n(t) =\lambda\sum_{n\ge0} n\bigl(p_{n-1}-p_n\bigr) =\lambda\sum_{n\ge0} p_n(t) =\lambda. \] Since \(\langle n(0)\rangle=0\), integration yields \[ \boxed{\langle n(t)\rangle=\lambda t.} \] Generating function and explicit solution. A compact route to the full distribution uses the probability generating function \[ g(z,t)=\sum_{n=0}^\infty z^n p_n(t),\qquad |z|\le1. \] Multiplying \(\eqref{eq:poisson-master}\) by \(z^n\) and summing over \(n\ge0\) (again taking \(p_{-1}\equiv0\)) produces the linear first-order PDE \[ \frac{\partial}{\partial t}g(z,t)=\lambda(z-1)g(z,t), \qquad g(z,0)=1,\quad g(1,t)=1. \] Solving this ordinary differential equation in \(t\) (for fixed \(z\)) gives \[ g(z,t)=\exp\bigl(\lambda(z-1)t\bigr). \] Expanding the exponential in powers of \(z\) and matching coefficients yields the state probabilities \[ \boxed{p_n(t)=e^{-\lambda t}\frac{(\lambda t)^n}{n!},\qquad n=0,1,\dots} \] Hence, at time \(t\) the count is Poisson-distributed with parameter \(\lambda t\).

Variance. Differentiating the generating function with respect to \(z\) and evaluating at \(z=1\) provides moments: \[ g_z(z,t)=\lambda t\,e^{\lambda(z-1)t},\qquad g_{zz}(z,t)=(\lambda t)^2e^{\lambda(z-1)t}. \] Therefore \(E[n]=g_z(1,t)=\lambda t\) and \(E[n(n-1)]=g_{zz}(1,t)=(\lambda t)^2\), so \[ \operatorname{Var}(n(t))=E[n^2]-E[n]^2=E[n(n-1)]+E[n]-E[n]^2=\lambda t. \] Thus the variance grows linearly in time and equals the mean: \[ \boxed{\operatorname{Var}(n(t))=\lambda t.} \] Remarks. Summarizing the principal properties and consequences:

A classic example of a birth–death process is radioactive decay. Initially the system contains \(N_0\) identical radioactive particles; each particle decays independently at constant rate \(\gamma>0\). At time \(t\) the system has \(n\) surviving particles; in a short interval \(\Delta t\) the probability that a single surviving particle decays is \(\gamma\Delta t\), hence the probability that one decay occurs (given \(n\) survivors) is \(\gamma n\Delta t\). Thus this is a pure-death process with \[ b_n=0,\qquad d_n=\gamma n. \] Master equation. For \(n=0,1,\dots,N_0\) the forward equation reads \[\begin{equation} \dot P_n(t)=\gamma (n+1)P_{n+1}(t)-\gamma n P_n(t), \end{equation}\] with the convention \(P_{N_0+1}(t)\equiv0\) and initial condition \[ P_n(0)=\delta_{n,N_0}. \] For \(n=N_0\) \(\eqref{eq:radioactive-me}\) reduces to \(\dot P_{N_0}=-\gamma N_0 P_{N_0}\).

Evolution of the mean. From \(\eqref{eq:radioactive-me}\) we obtain \[\begin{align*} \frac{d}{dt}\langle n(t)\rangle &=\sum_{n=0}^{N_0} n\dot P_n(t) =\gamma\sum_{n=0}^{N_0} n\bigl((n+1)P_{n+1}-nP_n\bigr). \end{align*}\] Change index in the first sum by \(m=n+1\): \[ \sum_{n=0}^{N_0} n(n+1)P_{n+1}=\sum_{m=1}^{N_0+1}(m-1)mP_m=\sum_{m=0}^{N_0} (m-1)mP_m, \] (using \(P_{N_0+1}=0\)). Hence \[ \frac{d}{dt}\langle n\rangle =\gamma\sum_{m=0}^{N_0}\bigl((m-1)m-m^2\bigr)P_m =-\gamma\sum_{m=0}^{N_0} m P_m =-\gamma\langle n\rangle. \] Therefore \[ \boxed{\frac{d}{dt}\langle n(t)\rangle=-\gamma\langle n(t)\rangle,\qquad \langle n(t)\rangle=N_0 e^{-\gamma t}.} \] Generating function solution. Define the probability generating function \[ g(z,t)=\sum_{n=0}^{N_0} z^n P_n(t),\qquad g(z,0)=z^{N_0},\quad g(1,t)=1. \] Multiplying \(\eqref{eq:radioactive-me}\) by \(z^n\) and summing yields the first-order PDE \[ \frac{\partial g}{\partial t}=\gamma(1-z)\frac{\partial g}{\partial z}. \] Solving by characteristics (or by observing that \(h(z,t)=1+(z-1)e^{-\gamma t}\) is an invariant curve) gives \[ \boxed{g(z,t)=\bigl(1+(z-1)e^{-\gamma t}\bigr)^{N_0} =\bigl(e^{-\gamma t}z+(1-e^{-\gamma t})\bigr)^{N_0}.} \] Expanding by the binomial theorem and equating coefficients of \(z^n\) yields the closed-form probabilities \[ \boxed{P_n(t)=\binom{N_0}{n}\bigl(e^{-\gamma t}\bigr)^n\bigl(1-e^{-\gamma t}\bigr)^{\,N_0-n},\qquad n=0,\dots,N_0.} \] Interpretation. Each particle survives to time \(t\) independently with probability \(e^{-\gamma t}\); therefore the number of survivors is Binomial\((N_0,e^{-\gamma t})\). From this one immediately recovers \[ \langle n(t)\rangle=N_0 e^{-\gamma t},\qquad \operatorname{Var}(n(t))=N_0 e^{-\gamma t}(1-e^{-\gamma t}). \] ## Furry process The Furry process (also called the Yule or linear birth process) models a multiplicative branching mechanism: each particle independently produces a new particle at rate \(\gamma\), so the total birth rate when there are \(n\) particles is proportional to \(n\). Physically, this can be used as a simple model for an electromagnetic cascade in an absorbing material: a high-energy electron enters the medium, undergoes bremsstrahlung and pair production, and a cascade of secondary particles is generated. Under the idealizing assumptions of independent splitting events and no losses other than branching, the process is a pure-birth process with \[ b_n=\gamma n,\qquad d_n=0,\qquad n\ge1, \] and we consider a single initial particle so that \(p_n(0)=\delta_{n,1}\).

Master equation. For \(n\ge1\) the forward (master) equation is \[\begin{equation} \dot p_n(t)=\gamma (n-1)p_{n-1}(t)-\gamma n p_n(t), \qquad p_n(0)=\delta_{n,1}. \end{equation}\]

Generating function solution. Introduce the probability generating function \[ G(z,t)=\sum_{n=1}^\infty z^n p_n(t),\qquad G(z,0)=z. \] Multiplying \(\eqref{eq:yule-me}\) by \(z^n\) and summing over \(n\ge1\) yields the linear first-order PDE \[ \frac{\partial G}{\partial t}=\gamma z(z-1)\frac{\partial G}{\partial z}, \] which can be solved by the method of characteristics. 14.4 Gillespie’s algorithm

The Gillespie algorithm is an exact stochastic simulation method for Markov jump processes on discrete state spaces. We derive the waiting-time and event-type distributions for a birth–death process and then state the algorithm; finally we give the straightforward generalisation to arbitrary jump rates.

Starting point: the master equation for a birth–death process is \[\begin{equation} \dot p_n(t)=b_{n-1}p_{n-1}(t)+d_{n+1}p_{n+1}(t)-\bigl(b_n+d_n\bigr)p_n(t), \end{equation}\] where \(b_n\) and \(d_n\) are the birth and death rates in state \(n\).

Waiting time to the next event. Let \(q_0(\tau\mid n)\) be the probability that, starting in state \(n\) at time \(0\), no transition occurs in the interval \([0,\tau)\). For a small increment \(\Delta\tau\) we have \[ q_0(\tau+\Delta\tau\mid n)=q_0(\tau\mid n)\bigl(1-(b_n+d_n)\Delta\tau\bigr). \] Passing to the limit \(\Delta\tau\to0\) yields the ODE \[ \frac{d}{d\tau}q_0(\tau\mid n)=-\bigl(b_n+d_n\bigr)q_0(\tau\mid n), \] with solution \[ \boxed{\,q_0(\tau\mid n)=\exp\bigl(-(b_n+d_n)\tau\bigr)\,}. \] Thus the waiting time is exponential with rate \(\lambda_n\equiv b_n+d_n\). The probability density for the time of the next event is \[ \boxed{\,f(\tau\mid n)=\lambda_n e^{-\lambda_n\tau}\,}. \] Type of the next event. Given that an event occurs at time \(\tau\), the conditional probability that it is a birth (rather than a death) depends only on the relative rates: \[ \Pr(\text{birth}\mid\text{event},n)=\frac{b_n}{b_n+d_n},\qquad \Pr(\text{death}\mid\text{event},n)=\frac{d_n}{b_n+d_n}. \] The joint densities for a birth or death at time \(\tau\) are therefore \[ q_+(\tau\mid n)=b_n e^{-\lambda_n\tau},\qquad q_-(\tau\mid n)=d_n e^{-\lambda_n\tau}. \] Gillespie algorithm (birth–death case) Remarks.

General Markov jump processes. For a general jump process with transition rates \(T(n'\mid n)\ge0\) (probability to jump from \(n\) to \(n'\) in small time \(\Delta t\) is \(T(n'\mid n)\Delta t\) for \(n'\neq n\)), define the total exit rate \[ \lambda_n=\sum_{n'\neq n}T(n'\mid n). \] Then the waiting time is exponential with rate \(\lambda_n\), and the probability to jump to a particular target state \(n'\) is \[ \Pr(n\to n')=\frac{T(n'\mid n)}{\lambda_n}\qquad(n'\neq n). \] The general Gillespie step is identical to the birth–death case but with the discrete selection of the target state performed according to the discrete distribution proportional to \(T(n'\mid n)\).

# Birth and Death Processes

The last category of stochastic processes that we will study are the **birth and death processes**, so called because they describe a multi-state system where states are progressively populated (a *birth* phenomenon) or abandoned (a *death* phenomenon).

## Introduction

Let's imagine numbering each state with an integer, as shown in figure (fig:processi-di-nascita-e-morte-schema). By convention, we consider the \q{positive flow} of birth towards states with increasing numbers, from which an inevitable \q{negative flow} of death towards states with decreasing numbers follows.

<!-- TikZ Figure: ../images/Figure/processi-di-nascita-e-morte-schema.tex -->

We hypothesize that in a time interval $\Delta t$ an element of the system in a given state $n$ has only three possibilities: to remain in state $n$ or to move to one of the two neighboring states, be it $n-1$ or $n+1$.

Note that the system described in this way is to all intents and purposes equivalent to the *random walk* introduced in section (sec:derivazione-microscopica), with only two differences:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item Time is not discretized but is *continuous*\footnote{However, this is not the equivalent of the *continuous limit* introduced in section (sec:derivazione-microscopica), because here the states, useful for describing the spatial component of the system, remain numbered in discrete form.}
    \item The element is not obliged to jump to a different state but also has the possibility of remaining in the same state
\end{enumerate}

At this point, let's introduce the *birth rate* $b_n\geqslant0$ and the *death rate* $d_n\geqslant0$ such that, in a sufficiently small time interval $\Delta t$, we can define:

\begin{itemize}
    \item $b_n\Delta t$ as the probability of passing from state $n$ at time to state $n+1$ at time $t+\Delta t$
    \item $d_n\Delta t$ as the probability of passing from state $n$ at time $t$ to state $n-1$ at time $t+\Delta t$
\end{itemize}

We have implicitly assumed here that the rates are *constant* over time, but in principle they could explicitly depend on time. Under this assumption, the process is called **homogeneous**.

We can now write the **master equation** that allows us to derive the *propagator* of the process. Considering state $n$, at time $t+\Delta t$ we have three possibilities:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item The element can pass from state $n-1$ to state $n$ with probability $b_{n-1}\Delta t$
    \item The element can pass from state $n+1$ to state $n$ with probability $d_{n+1}\Delta t$
    \item The element can remain in state $n$ with complementary probability $1-\left(b_n+d_n\right)\Delta t$
\end{enumerate}

Summing the three contributions:

\begin{equation*}
    p(n,t+\Delta t)=b_{n-1}\Delta tp(n-1,t)+d_{n+1}\Delta tp(n+1,t)+\left[1-\left(b_n+d_n\right)\Delta t\right]p(n,t)
\end{equation*}

Let's move $p(,t)$ to the first member and divide by $\Delta t$:

\begin{equation*}
    \frac{p(n,t+\Delta t)-p(n,t)}{\Delta t}=b_{n-1}p(n-1,t)+d_{n+1}p(n+1,t)-\left(b_n+d_n\right)p(n,t)
\end{equation*}

In the limit for $\Delta t\to0$:

\begin{equation}
    \boxed{\frac{\partial}{\partial t}p(n,t)=b_{n-1}p(n-1,t)+d_{n+1}p(n+1,t)-\left(b_n+d_n\right)p(n,t)}
\end{equation}

By introducing the initial condition $p(n,t_0)=\delta_{n,n_0}$, the birth and death process introduced here is also a **Markov process**, and we can prove it mathematically by verifying the Chapman-Kolmogorov equation ((eq:processi-stocastici-equazione-di-chapman-kolmogorov)), which in this context is written as:

\begin{equation*}
    p\left(n_2,t_2|n_0,t_0\right)=\sum_{n_1}p\left(n_2,t_2|n_1,t_1\right)p\left(n_1,t_1|n_0,t_0\right)
\end{equation*}

Applying the derivative to both members:

\begin{equation*}
    \frac{\partial}{\partial t_2}p\left(n_2,t_2|n_0,t_0\right)=\frac{\partial}{\partial t_2}\sum_{n_1}p\left(n_2,t_2|n_1,t_1\right)p\left(n_1,t_1|n_0,t_0\right)
\end{equation*}

Since $p\left(n_1,t_1|n_0,t_0\right)$ is independent of $t_2$, it can be excluded from the derivation:

\begin{equation}
    \frac{\partial}{\partial t_2}p\left(n_2,t_2|n_0,t_0\right)=\sum_{n_1}\frac{\partial}{\partial t_2}p\left(n_2,t_2|n_1,t_1\right)p\left(n_1,t_1|n_0,t_0\right)
\end{equation}

Let's calculate the derivative on the second member using (eq:processi-nascita-morte-propagatore):

\begin{align*}
    \frac{\partial}{\partial t_2}p\left(n_2,t_2|n_1,t_1\right)=&b_{n_2-1}p\left(n_2-1,t_2|n_1,t_1\right)+\\
    &+d_{n_2+1}p\left(n_2+1,t_2|n_1,t_1\right)-\\
    &-\left(b_{n_2}+d_{n_2}\right)p\left(n_2,t_2|n_1,t_1\right)
\end{align*}

Let's substitute in (eq:processo-nascita-morte-chapman-kolmogorov-calcolo-1):

\begin{align*}
    \frac{\partial}{\partial t_2}p\left(n_2,t_2|n_0,t_0\right)=&b_{n_2-1}\sum_{n_1}p\left(n_2-1,t_2|n_1,t_1\right)p\left(n_1,t_1|n_0,t_0\right)+\\
    &+d_{n_2+1}\sum_{n_1}p\left(n_2+1,t_2|n_1,t_1\right)p\left(n_1,t_1|n_0,t_0\right)-\\
    &-\left(b_{n_2}+d_{n_2}\right)\sum_{n_1}p\left(n_2,t_2|n_1,t_1\right)p\left(n_1,t_1|n_0,t_0\right)
\end{align*}

Assuming the Chapman-Kolmogorov equation is valid and applying it to the three terms written above, we find:

\begin{align*}
    \frac{\partial}{\partial t_2}p\left(n_2,t_2|n_0,t_0\right)=&b_{n_2-1}p\left(n_2-1,t_2|n_0,t_0\right)+\\
    &+d_{n_2+1}p\left(n_2+1,t_2|n_0,t_0\right)-\\
    &-\left(b_{n_2}+d_{n_2}\right)p\left(n_2,t_2|n_0,t_0\right)
\end{align*}

which is precisely the propagator formula written in (eq:processi-nascita-morte-propagatore).

### Boundary conditions

As we have done in the past, we can introduce two types of boundary conditions:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item **Reflecting wall**: from a final state $N$ onwards, the \q{positive flow} of birth is interrupted, and the elements that have reached this state can only \q{exit} through the \q{negative flow} of death in the other direction. State $N$ is therefore called *reflecting*.

    In formulas:

    \begin{equation}
        \begin{cases}
            b_n=0 \ \ \text{for} \ n\geqslant N\\
            d_n=0 \ \ \text{for} \ n>N
        \end{cases}
    \end{equation}

    \item **Absorbing wall**: from a final state $N$ onwards, the \q{positive flow} of birth is interrupted, and the elements that have arrived there can no longer leave this state, thus interrupting any \q{flow} out. State $N$ is therefore called *absorbing*.

    In formulas:

    \begin{equation}
        \begin{cases}
            b_n=0 \ \ \text{for} \ n\geqslant N\\
            d_n=0 \ \ \text{for} \ n\geqslant N
        \end{cases}
    \end{equation}
\end{enumerate}

Keep in mind that the master equation written above (equation (eq:processi-nascita-morte-propagatore)) is valid only as long as a boundary state (whether reflecting or absorbing) is not \q{hit}, because in that case it is necessary to modify it appropriately.\footnote{The professor did not go into specifics, but in my opinion it is sufficient to explicitly set the individual rates to zero in the specific cases $n=N-1$, $n=N$ and $n>N$.}

### Matrix formalism

It is possible to obtain a more compact structure by defining the vector

\begin{equation}
    \vec{p}(t)=
    \begin{pmatrix}
        p(0,t)\\
        p(1,t)\\
        p(2,t)\\
        \vdots
    \end{pmatrix}
\end{equation}

assuming that we have started numbering the states from $0$ onwards.

To be able to write the master equation, we will therefore have to introduce a matrix $W$ such that

\begin{equation}
    \frac{\partial}{\partial t}\vec{p}(n,t)=W\vec{p}(n,t)
\end{equation}

Each component $p(n,t)$ will therefore be calculated as

\begin{equation}
    \frac{\partial}{\partial t}p(n,t)=\sum_{n'}W_{n,n'}p(n',t)
\end{equation}

To construct $W$, let's consider the $n$-th row, which, when multiplied by the vector $\vec{p}(n,t)$, must return the second member of (eq:processi-nascita-morte-propagatore), therefore every element of the vector other than $p(n-1,t)$, $p(n,t)$ and $p(n+1,t)$ must give a null contribution. It follows that each row of $W$ contains all zeros except for three consecutive elements; using the Kronecker delta, we can indicate the element of the matrix in the following form:

\begin{equation}
    W_{n,n'}=b_{n-1}\delta_{n-1,n'}+d_{n+1}\delta_{n+1,n'}-(b_n+d_n)\delta_{n,n'}
\end{equation}

In essence, it is a tridiagonal matrix whose elements on the diagonal are all negative and those on the upper and lower diagonals are instead positive. Furthermore, for the overall flow to be conserved, the sum of the elements along each column must be zero. Intuitively, we can understand this by observing three consecutive rows of the matrix $W$:

\begin{equation*}
    W=
    \begin{pmatrix}
        \ddots & \dots & \dots & \dots & \vdots\\
        \vdots & -\left(b_{n-1}+d_{n-1}\right) & d_n &  0 & \vdots\\
        \vdots & b_{n-1} & -\left(b_n+d_n\right) & d_{n+1} & \vdots\\
        \vdots & 0 & b_n & -\left(b_{n+1}+d_{n+1}\right) & \vdots\\
        \vdots & \dots & \dots & \dots & \ddots\\
    \end{pmatrix}
\end{equation*}

Formally, this is verified by imposing that $\sum\limits_np(n,t)$ is constant:\footnote{From this we understand that this constraint is not valid in the presence of absorbing boundary conditions, since the flow, in that case, cannot be constrained to be conserved.}

\begin{equation*}
    \frac{\partial}{\partial t}\sum_np(n,t)=0 \ \iff \ \sum_n\sum_{n'}W_{n,n'}p(n',t)=0
\end{equation*}

The second member can be rewritten as

\begin{equation*}
    \sum_{n'}\left(\sum_nW_{n,n'}\right)p(n',t)=0
\end{equation*}

from which it follows that

\begin{equation*}
    \sum_nW_{n,n'}=0 \ \ \ \forall n'
\end{equation*}

Ultimately, the birth and death process can be rewritten in the following compact form:

\begin{equation}
    \boxed{\begin{cases}
        \frac{\partial}{\partial t}\vec{p}(t)=W\vec{p}\\
        \vec{p}(0)=\vec{p}_0
    \end{cases}
    \ \ \text{with} \ \
    \begin{cases}
        W_{n,n'}\geqslant0 \ \ &    ext{for} \ n\neq n'\\
        \sum\limits_nW_{n,n'}=0 &\forall n'
    \end{cases}}
\end{equation}

from which the solution follows

\begin{equation}
    \vec{p}(t)=\vec{p}_0e^{Wt}
\end{equation}

The result is intuitive: on the one hand we have $\langle b_n\rangle$, which is the *average* net flow to the right, from which the *average* net flow to the left is subtracted; it is clear that the difference between the two will tell us how the average value of the system varies over time.

### Equilibrium distribution

Under the appropriate conditions, we can explicitly calculate the equilibrium distribution for a homogeneous birth and death process.

For simplicity, let's impose a reflecting state at $n=0$. At equilibrium, the total flow concerning any state $n$ - both incoming and outgoing - is zero, therefore:

\begin{equation*}
    \frac{\partial}{\partial t}p(n,t)=0 \ \iff \ b_{n-1}p(n-1)+d_{n+1}p(n+1)-\left(b_n+d_n\right)p(n)=0
\end{equation*}

Rearranging the terms:

\begin{equation*}
    \green{b_{n-1}p(n-1)-d_np(n)}-\left[\orange{b_np(n)-d_{n+1}p(n+1)}\right]=0
\end{equation*}

The highlighted terms, individually, are nothing more than the net flow between one state and the next: in green, for example, we have the flow from state $n-1$ to state $n$ (positive) and that from state $n$ to state $n-1$ (negative); in orange, instead, it is the net flow between state $n$ and state $n+1$.

Indicating these flows with $J$, we will therefore write:

\begin{equation*}
    J_{n-1}-J_n=0 \ \Longrightarrow \ J_n=J_{n-1}
\end{equation*}

Since this relationship holds between generic states, we can iterate the formula:

\begin{equation*}
    J_n=J_{n-1}=J_{n-2}=J_{n-3}=\dots
\end{equation*}

until we reach $J_1$, beyond which there is only the state $n=0$ and therefore the flow $J_0$ relative to the exchange between states $n=0$ and $n=1$. Let's remember that here we have set a *reflecting* boundary condition, so by construction $J_0=0$, but consequently all the other flows will also be zero.

In general, therefore:

\begin{equation*}
    J_n=0
\end{equation*}

which translates to

\begin{equation}
    \boxed{b_np^s(n)=d_{n+1}p^s(n+1)}
\end{equation}

where the $s$ indicates that the propagator is relative to the *stationary condition*, that is, at equilibrium.

The expression written above is called **detailed balance** and it is from this that we can calculate the propagator for any state $n$ in stationary conditions. We will have in fact that:

\begin{equation*}
    b_{n-1}p^s(n-1)=d_np^s(n) \ \iff \ p^s(n)=\frac{b_{n-1}}{d_n}p^s(n-1)
\end{equation*}

Iterating the formula:

\begin{equation*}
    p^s(n,t)=\frac{b_{n-1}}{d_n}p^s(n-1)=\frac{b_{n-1}b_{n-2}}{d_nd_{n-1}}p^s(n-2)=\frac{b_{n-1}b_{n-2}b_{n-3}}{d_nd_{n-1}d_{n-2}}p^s(n-3)=\dots
\end{equation*}

We thus find

\begin{equation}
    \boxed{p^s(n)=\prod_{i=1}^n\frac{b_{i-1}}{d_i}p_0^s}
\end{equation}

having indicated $p^s(0)$ with $p_0^s$.

By calculating $p_0^s$, therefore, we are able to find all the terms $p(n)$ at equilibrium. It is obtained by imposing normalization:

\begin{equation*}
    \sum_{n=0}^\infty p^s(n)=1 \ \iff \ \sum_{n=0}^\infty\prod_{i=1}^n\frac{b_{i-1}}{d_i}p_0^s=1
\end{equation*}

Since $p_0^s$ is constant:

\begin{equation*}
    p_0^s\sum_{n=0}^\infty\prod_{i=1}^n\frac{b_{i-1}}{d_i}=1
\end{equation*}

For $n=0$ the product acts on an empty interval (since the index $i$ should run from $1$ to $0$, so it is a non-existent set), therefore the result is the neutral term $1$:

\begin{equation*}
    p_0^s\left(1+\sum_{n=1}^\infty\prod_{i=1}^n\frac{b_{i-1}}{d_i}\right)=1
\end{equation*}

From which we find the final formula:

\begin{equation}
    \boxed{p_0^s=\left(1+\sum_{n=1}^\infty\prod_{i=1}^n\frac{b_{i-1}}{d_i}\right)^{-1}}
\end{equation}

## Examples of birth and death processes
### Poisson process

The *Poisson process* is the simplest nontrivial example of a birth--death family: it is a pure-birth process obtained by taking a constant birth rate and zero death rate. Concretely, for all states $n=0,1,2,\dots$ we set
$$
b_n=\lambda>0,\qquad d_n=0.
$$
Thus births occur at a constant rate $\lambda$ while no deaths are possible. This choice simplifies the master equation and yields the closed-form, well-known Poisson law for the state probabilities.

Master equation. Substituting the rates above into the general master equation gives
\begin{equation}
\dot p_n(t)=\lambda\bigl(p_{n-1}(t)-p_n(t)\bigr),\qquad p_n(0)=\delta_{n,0},
\end{equation}
where by convention we set $p_{-1}(t)\equiv0$ so the equation is valid for $n\ge0$.

Expectation. To obtain the mean count, multiply \eqref{eq:poisson-master} by $n$ and sum over $n\ge0$. Using index shifts and the normalization $\sum_{n\ge0}p_n(t)=1$ we find
$$
\frac{d}{dt}\langle n(t)\rangle
=\sum_{n\ge0} n\dot p_n(t)
=\lambda\sum_{n\ge0} n\bigl(p_{n-1}-p_n\bigr)
=\lambda\sum_{n\ge0} p_n(t)
=\lambda.
$$
Since $\langle n(0)\rangle=0$, integration yields
$$
\boxed{\langle n(t)\rangle=\lambda t.}
$$
Generating function and explicit solution. A compact route to the full distribution uses the probability generating function
$$
g(z,t)=\sum_{n=0}^\infty z^n p_n(t),\qquad |z|\le1.
$$
Multiplying \eqref{eq:poisson-master} by $z^n$ and summing over $n\ge0$ (again taking $p_{-1}\equiv0$) produces the linear first-order PDE
$$
\frac{\partial}{\partial t}g(z,t)=\lambda(z-1)g(z,t),
\qquad g(z,0)=1,\quad g(1,t)=1.
$$
Solving this ordinary differential equation in $t$ (for fixed $z$) gives
$$
g(z,t)=\exp\bigl(\lambda(z-1)t\bigr).
$$
Expanding the exponential in powers of $z$ and matching coefficients yields the state probabilities
$$
\boxed{p_n(t)=e^{-\lambda t}\frac{(\lambda t)^n}{n!},\qquad n=0,1,\dots}
$$
Hence, at time $t$ the count is Poisson-distributed with parameter $\lambda t$.

Variance. Differentiating the generating function with respect to $z$ and evaluating at $z=1$ provides moments:
$$
g_z(z,t)=\lambda t\,e^{\lambda(z-1)t},\qquad
g_{zz}(z,t)=(\lambda t)^2e^{\lambda(z-1)t}.
$$
Therefore $E[n]=g_z(1,t)=\lambda t$ and $E[n(n-1)]=g_{zz}(1,t)=(\lambda t)^2$, so
$$
\operatorname{Var}(n(t))=E[n^2]-E[n]^2=E[n(n-1)]+E[n]-E[n]^2=\lambda t.
$$
Thus the variance grows linearly in time and equals the mean:
$$
\boxed{\operatorname{Var}(n(t))=\lambda t.}
$$
Remarks. Summarizing the principal properties and consequences:
\begin{itemize}
    \item The Poisson process is a pure-birth counting process with independent increments: counts in disjoint time intervals are independent.
    \item Interarrival times are independent exponential($\lambda$) random variables; consequently the number of events in an interval of length $t$ is Poisson($\lambda t$).
    \item Mean and variance grow linearly with time and coincide: $\mathbb{E}[n(t)]=\operatorname{Var}(n(t))=\lambda t$.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Radioactive decay}

A classic example of a birth--death process is radioactive decay.  Initially the system contains $N_0$ identical radioactive particles; each particle decays independently at constant rate $\gamma>0$.  At time $t$ the system has $n$ surviving particles; in a short interval $\Delta t$ the probability that a single surviving particle decays is $\gamma\Delta t$, hence the probability that one decay occurs (given $n$ survivors) is $\gamma n\Delta t$.  Thus this is a pure-death process with
$$
b_n=0,\qquad d_n=\gamma n.
$$
Master equation. For $n=0,1,\dots,N_0$ the forward equation reads
\begin{equation}
\dot P_n(t)=\gamma (n+1)P_{n+1}(t)-\gamma n P_n(t),
\end{equation}
with the convention $P_{N_0+1}(t)\equiv0$ and initial condition
$$
P_n(0)=\delta_{n,N_0}.
$$
For $n=N_0$ \eqref{eq:radioactive-me} reduces to $\dot P_{N_0}=-\gamma N_0 P_{N_0}$.

Evolution of the mean. From \eqref{eq:radioactive-me} we obtain
\begin{align*}
\frac{d}{dt}\langle n(t)\rangle
&=\sum_{n=0}^{N_0} n\dot P_n(t)
=\gamma\sum_{n=0}^{N_0} n\bigl((n+1)P_{n+1}-nP_n\bigr).
\end{align*}
Change index in the first sum by $m=n+1$:
$$
\sum_{n=0}^{N_0} n(n+1)P_{n+1}=\sum_{m=1}^{N_0+1}(m-1)mP_m=\sum_{m=0}^{N_0} (m-1)mP_m,
$$
(using $P_{N_0+1}=0$). Hence
$$
\frac{d}{dt}\langle n\rangle
=\gamma\sum_{m=0}^{N_0}\bigl((m-1)m-m^2\bigr)P_m
=-\gamma\sum_{m=0}^{N_0} m P_m
=-\gamma\langle n\rangle.
$$
Therefore
$$
\boxed{\frac{d}{dt}\langle n(t)\rangle=-\gamma\langle n(t)\rangle,\qquad
\langle n(t)\rangle=N_0 e^{-\gamma t}.}
$$
Generating function solution. Define the probability generating function
$$
g(z,t)=\sum_{n=0}^{N_0} z^n P_n(t),\qquad g(z,0)=z^{N_0},\quad g(1,t)=1.
$$
Multiplying \eqref{eq:radioactive-me} by $z^n$ and summing yields the first-order PDE
$$
\frac{\partial g}{\partial t}=\gamma(1-z)\frac{\partial g}{\partial z}.
$$
Solving by characteristics (or by observing that $h(z,t)=1+(z-1)e^{-\gamma t}$ is an invariant curve) gives
$$
\boxed{g(z,t)=\bigl(1+(z-1)e^{-\gamma t}\bigr)^{N_0}
=\bigl(e^{-\gamma t}z+(1-e^{-\gamma t})\bigr)^{N_0}.}
$$
Expanding by the binomial theorem and equating coefficients of $z^n$ yields the closed-form probabilities
$$
\boxed{P_n(t)=\binom{N_0}{n}\bigl(e^{-\gamma t}\bigr)^n\bigl(1-e^{-\gamma t}\bigr)^{\,N_0-n},\qquad n=0,\dots,N_0.}
$$
Interpretation. Each particle survives to time $t$ independently with probability $e^{-\gamma t}$; therefore the number of survivors is Binomial$(N_0,e^{-\gamma t})$.  From this one immediately recovers
$$
\langle n(t)\rangle=N_0 e^{-\gamma t},\qquad
\operatorname{Var}(n(t))=N_0 e^{-\gamma t}(1-e^{-\gamma t}).
$$
## Furry process
The Furry process (also called the Yule or linear birth process) models a multiplicative branching mechanism: each particle independently produces a new particle at rate $\gamma$, so the total birth rate when there are $n$ particles is proportional to $n$. Physically, this can be used as a simple model for an electromagnetic cascade in an absorbing material: a high-energy electron enters the medium, undergoes bremsstrahlung and pair production, and a cascade of secondary particles is generated. Under the idealizing assumptions of independent splitting events and no losses other than branching, the process is a pure-birth process with
$$
b_n=\gamma n,\qquad d_n=0,\qquad n\ge1,
$$
and we consider a single initial particle so that $p_n(0)=\delta_{n,1}$.

Master equation. For $n\ge1$ the forward (master) equation is
\begin{equation}
\dot p_n(t)=\gamma (n-1)p_{n-1}(t)-\gamma n p_n(t),
\qquad p_n(0)=\delta_{n,1}.
\end{equation}

Generating function solution. Introduce the probability generating function
$$
G(z,t)=\sum_{n=1}^\infty z^n p_n(t),\qquad G(z,0)=z.
$$
Multiplying \eqref{eq:yule-me} by $z^n$ and summing over $n\ge1$ yields the linear first-order PDE
$$
\frac{\partial G}{\partial t}=\gamma z(z-1)\frac{\partial G}{\partial z},
$$
which can be solved by the method of characteristics. The solution that satisfies $G(z,0)=z$ is
$$
G(z,t)=\frac{e^{-\gamma t}\,z}{1-(1-e^{-\gamma t})z}.
$$
Expanding this geometric series produces the closed-form state probabilities
\begin{equation}
p_n(t)=e^{-\gamma t}\bigl(1-e^{-\gamma t}\bigr)^{\,n-1},\qquad n\ge1,
\end{equation}
which is a geometric distribution (supported on $\{1,2,\dots\}$) with success probability $e^{-\gamma t}$.

Moments. Differentiating the generating function gives the mean and variance. One finds
$$
\boxed{\mathbb{E}[N(t)]=e^{\gamma t},\qquad
\operatorname{Var}(N(t))=e^{\gamma t}\bigl(e^{\gamma t}-1\bigr).}
$$
Remarks. The Furry/Yule process captures the exponential growth of particle number in an idealised branching cascade and yields a simple geometric law for the multiplicity at fixed time. Its assumptions (independent identical branching, no energy loss, no absorption except by branching) limit quantitative accuracy for real showers, but the model is useful for qualitative understanding and as a tractable stochastic example.
## The contact process

The contact process (a mean-field SIS-type epidemic on a finite population) models transmission and recovery on a population of fixed size $N$. Individuals are either infected or susceptible; let $n\in\{0,\dots,N\}$ denote the number of infected individuals and set $x=n/N$.

In a short time interval $\Delta t$ the elementary transitions are
\begin{itemize}
    \item infection: $n\to n+1$ with rate
$$
    b_n=\tilde\beta\;\frac{N-n}{N}\;\frac{n}{N-1},
$$
    obtained by selecting a susceptible uniformly at random (probability $(N-n)/N$) and meeting an infected individual uniformly among the remaining $N-1$ (probability $n/(N-1)$); $\tilde\beta$ is the per-pair transmission rate.
    \item recovery: $n\to n-1$ with rate
$$
    d_n=\gamma n,
$$
    where $\gamma>0$ is the per-individual recovery rate (recovered individuals immediately become susceptible again, SIS dynamics).
\end{itemize}

For large $N$ it is convenient to introduce the intensive variable $x=n/N$. With the scaling
$$
b_x \equiv b_n\Big|_{n= Nx}\approx N\beta\,x(1-x),\qquad \beta\equiv\frac{\tilde\beta}{N-1}\,,
$$
and $d_x\equiv d_n\big|_{n=Nx}=N\gamma x$, the typical step in $x$ is $\Delta x=1/N$, so $x$ changes by $O(1/N)$. This separation of scales (existence of a large typical size $N$) justifies a diffusion approximation (Kurtz / Kramers–Moyal expansion) in the limit $N\gg1$.

Master equation for the discrete probabilities $q_n(t)=\Pr\{n(t)=n\}$ can be written in the rescaled form (indexing by $x=n/N$):
$$
\dot q_n(t)=b_{x-\frac{1}{N}}\,q_{n-1}+d_{x+\frac{1}{N}}\,q_{n+1}-\bigl(b_x+d_x\bigr)\,q_n.
$$
Writing $p(x,t)\approx N q_n(t)$ as the density on the mesh $\Delta x=1/N$ and Taylor-expanding the terms in powers of $1/N$ yields, to second order,
$$
\partial_t p(x,t)=-\frac{1}{N}\frac{\partial}{\partial x}\bigl[(b_x-d_x)\,p(x,t)\bigr]
+\frac{1}{2N^{2}}\frac{\partial^2}{\partial x^2}\bigl[(b_x+d_x)\,p(x,t)\bigr]+o\!\left(\frac{1}{N^{2}}\right).
$$
Substituting $b_x=N\beta x(1-x)$ and $d_x=N\gamma x$ and multiplying by $N$ gives the Fokker--Planck equation in the diffusive scaling
$$
\boxed{\;\partial_t p(x,t)=-\frac{\partial}{\partial x}\Bigl[(\beta x(1-x)-\gamma x)\,p\Bigr]
+\frac{1}{2N}\frac{\partial^2}{\partial x^2}\Bigl[(\beta x(1-x)+\gamma x)\,p\Bigr]\; }.
$$
The corresponding Itô stochastic differential equation for the fraction $x(t)$ is
$$
\boxed{\;dx=\bigl(\beta x(1-x)-\gamma x\bigr)\,dt+\sqrt{\frac{\beta x(1-x)+\gamma x}{N}}\;dB_t\;},
$$
so demographic fluctuations are of order $O(N^{-1/2})$.

Deterministic limit and moment closure.
Taking expectations in the SDE or directly from the master equation gives the exact identity
$$
\frac{d}{dt}\rho(t)=\frac{d}{dt}\mathbb{E}[x(t)]=(\beta-\gamma)\rho(t)-\beta\,\mathbb{E}[x^2(t)],
$$
where $\rho(t)=\mathbb{E}[x(t)]$. Closing the moment hierarchy with the mean-field approximation $\mathbb{E}[x^2]\approx\rho^2$ yields the logistic equation
$$
\boxed{\;\dot\rho=(\beta-\gamma)\rho-\beta\rho^2\;}
$$
with steady states $\rho^*=0$ and $\bar\rho=(\beta-\gamma)/\beta$ (the latter exists only when $\beta>\gamma$). Linear stability analysis gives the Jacobian $J(\rho)=\beta-\gamma-2\beta\rho$, hence
\begin{itemize}
    \item $\rho^*=0$ is stable if $\beta<\gamma$ (extinction) and unstable if $\beta>\gamma$.
    \item $\bar\rho$ is stable when it exists ($\beta>\gamma$).
\end{itemize}
At the critical point $\beta=\gamma$ the deterministic decay is algebraic: $\rho(t)\sim 1/t$.

Comments and limitations.
\begin{itemize}
    \item The diffusion/Fokker--Planck approximation is formally obtained from a Kramers–Moyal expansion; more rigorous treatments use Kurtz theorems, van Kampen's system-size expansion or WKB methods for large deviations.
    \item For finite $N$ stochastic extinction can occur even if $\beta>\gamma$: the deterministic steady state $\bar\rho$ only describes typical behaviour when fluctuations are small compared with the mean.
    \item Near the boundaries $x\approx0$ or $x\approx1$ the diffusion approximation may be inaccurate; boundary-layer or discrete analyses can be required to capture extinction probabilities and fixation times.
\end{itemize}
## Gillespie's algorithm

The Gillespie algorithm is an exact stochastic simulation method for Markov jump processes on discrete state spaces. We derive the waiting-time and event-type distributions for a birth--death process and then state the algorithm; finally we give the straightforward generalisation to arbitrary jump rates.

Starting point: the master equation for a birth--death process is
\begin{equation}
\dot p_n(t)=b_{n-1}p_{n-1}(t)+d_{n+1}p_{n+1}(t)-\bigl(b_n+d_n\bigr)p_n(t),
\end{equation}
where $b_n$ and $d_n$ are the birth and death rates in state $n$.

Waiting time to the next event. Let $q_0(\tau\mid n)$ be the probability that, starting in state $n$ at time $0$, no transition occurs in the interval $[0,\tau)$. For a small increment $\Delta\tau$ we have
$$
q_0(\tau+\Delta\tau\mid n)=q_0(\tau\mid n)\bigl(1-(b_n+d_n)\Delta\tau\bigr).
$$
Passing to the limit $\Delta\tau\to0$ yields the ODE
$$
\frac{d}{d\tau}q_0(\tau\mid n)=-\bigl(b_n+d_n\bigr)q_0(\tau\mid n),
$$
with solution
$$
\boxed{\,q_0(\tau\mid n)=\exp\bigl(-(b_n+d_n)\tau\bigr)\,}.
$$
Thus the waiting time is exponential with rate $\lambda_n\equiv b_n+d_n$. The probability density for the time of the next event is
$$
\boxed{\,f(\tau\mid n)=\lambda_n e^{-\lambda_n\tau}\,}.
$$
Type of the next event. Given that an event occurs at time $\tau$, the conditional probability that it is a birth (rather than a death) depends only on the relative rates:
$$
\Pr(\text{birth}\mid\text{event},n)=\frac{b_n}{b_n+d_n},\qquad
\Pr(\text{death}\mid\text{event},n)=\frac{d_n}{b_n+d_n}.
$$
The joint densities for a birth or death at time $\tau$ are therefore
$$
q_+(\tau\mid n)=b_n e^{-\lambda_n\tau},\qquad
q_-(\tau\mid n)=d_n e^{-\lambda_n\tau}.
$$
Gillespie algorithm (birth--death case)
\begin{enumerate}
  \item Initialize time $t\leftarrow t_0$ and state $n\leftarrow n_0$.
  \item Compute the total rate $\lambda_n=b_n+d_n$. If $\lambda_n=0$ stop (absorbing state).
  \item Draw $r\sim\mathrm{Uniform}(0,1)$ and set
$$
  \tau=-\frac{1}{\lambda_n}\ln r,
  \qquad t\leftarrow t+\tau.
$$
  \item Draw $u\sim\mathrm{Uniform}(0,1)$. If $u<b_n/\lambda_n$ then a birth occurs and $n\leftarrow n+1$, otherwise a death occurs and $n\leftarrow n-1$.
  \item Record $(t,n)$ if desired and return to step 2 until a stopping condition is met (final time, absorption, etc.).
\end{enumerate}

Remarks.
\begin{itemize}
  \item The exponential waiting time and the simple acceptance rule follow from the memoryless property and the decomposition of the total rate into competing channels.
  \item Boundary conditions (reflecting/absorbing) are handled by setting the appropriate rates to zero and treating $\lambda_n=0$ as absorption.
\end{itemize}

General Markov jump processes. For a general jump process with transition rates $T(n'\mid n)\ge0$ (probability to jump from $n$ to $n'$ in small time $\Delta t$ is $T(n'\mid n)\Delta t$ for $n'\neq n$), define the total exit rate
$$
\lambda_n=\sum_{n'\neq n}T(n'\mid n).
$$
Then the waiting time is exponential with rate $\lambda_n$, and the probability to jump to a particular target state $n'$ is
$$
\Pr(n\to n')=\frac{T(n'\mid n)}{\lambda_n}\qquad(n'\neq n).
$$
The general Gillespie step is identical to the birth--death case but with the discrete selection of the target state performed according to the discrete distribution proportional to $T(n'\mid n)$.

Master equation (general form). The forward equation for the probabilities is
$$
\boxed{\,\dot p(n,t)=\sum_{m\neq n} \bigl[T(n\mid m)p(m,t)-T(m\mid n)p(n,t)\bigr]\,,}
$$
which is equivalent to the birth--death master equation when only nearest-neighbour transitions are allowed.
