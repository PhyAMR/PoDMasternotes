15 Ordinary differential equations

In this chapter we will deal with ODEs (Ordinary Differential Equations) related to chemical reactions. Let’s clarify right away that by , in reality, we mean something more than what the term generally suggests: it includes all those systems composed of individuals of a certain type who react with individuals of the same type or of a different type, producing as a product individuals who may or may not be of the same nature.

\[\begin{equation*} \text{Reactant 1} + \text{Reactant 2} \genfrac{}{}{-3pt}{}{\longrightarrow}{\longleftarrow} \text{Products} \end{equation*}\]

In the generic reaction written above, we notice the presence of two arrows that indicate both directions: the reason is that, in the course of the discussion, we will also consider reversible reactions, which can therefore allow the system to return to its original state.

15.1.1 Law of mass action

To calculate the reaction rate, we must first make some assumptions about the system we are considering:

If all these conditions are met, we will say that we are at the dilute limit. Let’s indicate the concentrations of reactants and product with

\[\begin{equation*} x=\frac{n_X}{V} y=\frac{n_Y}{V} z=\frac{n_Z}{V} \end{equation*}\]

For now we will only consider the case with stoichiometric coefficients equal to one; from section (sec:la-legge-di-azione-di-massa-generalizzata) we will extend to a generic number of molecules (or moles) different for the single reaction.

According to what we have written, the reaction rate \(r\) will be a function exclusively of \(x\) and \(y\). Furthermore, since the concentrations are small, we can expand with respect to \((x,y)=(0,0)\), thus approximating the function:

\[\begin{equation*} r(x,y)\simeq r(0,0)+\left(\partial_xr_0\right)x+\left(\partial_yr_0\right)y+\left(\frac{1}{2}\partial_{xx}^2r_0\right)x^2+\left(\partial_{xy}^2r_0\right)xy+\left(\frac{1}{2}\partial_{yy}^2r_0\right)y^2 \end{equation*}\]

having used the notation \(\partial_i r_0=\partial_i r\big|_{(x,y)=(0,0)}\) for simplicity.

We can set

\[\begin{equation*} \begin{cases} r(x,0)=0 \forall x\\ r(0,y)=0 \forall y \end{cases} \end{equation*}\]

since both molecules of \(X\) and molecules of \(Y\) are necessary for the reaction to occur.

This implies that \(r(0,0)=0\). Furthermore, by substituting the two cases in the expansion, we obtain:

\[\begin{equation*} \begin{cases} r(x,0)\simeq\left(\partial_xr_0\right)x+\left(\frac{1}{2}\partial_{xx}^2r_0\right)x^2=0 \forall x\\ r(0,y)\simeq\left(\partial_yr_0\right)y+\left(\frac{1}{2}\partial_{yy}^2r_0\right)y^2=0 \forall y \end{cases} \end{equation*}\]

The fact that the first must hold for every value of \(x\) while the second for every value of \(y\) clarifies that these are identities, not equations. We will therefore have to set the individual coefficients to zero:

\[\begin{equation*} \begin{cases} \partial_xr_0=0, \partial_{xx}^2r_0=0\\ \partial_yr_0=0, \partial_{yy}^2r_0=0 \end{cases} \end{equation*}\]

Returning to the expansion, only one term survives:

\[\begin{equation*} r(x,y)\simeq\left(\partial_{xy}^2r_0\right)xy \end{equation*}\]

Indicating the mixed derivative of the reaction rate calculated at the origin with \(K\), called the kinetic constant, we therefore find that

\[\begin{equation} \boxed{r(x,y)\simeq Kxy} \end{equation}\]

We thus find what is called the law of mass action.

In a first approximation, the **rate** of any **chemical reaction** is proportional to the **product of the concentrations** of the reacting substances.

Let’s remember that this is valid only in the dilute limit regime, in which the \(X\) molecules all have the same probability \(P_{XY}\) of meeting and reacting with the \(Y\) molecules, and the same applies in reverse for \(P_{YX}\); these probabilities, moreover, are independent of each other.

What we have called the kinetic constant \(K\) is actually constant only with respect to the concentrations \(x\) and \(y\), but in general it can depend on other parameters of the system; in chemistry, for example, the Arrhenius equation is known, in which the constant is linked to the temperature \(T\) of the system by the following relation:

\[\begin{equation*} K=K_0e^{-\frac{E}{k_BT}} \end{equation*}\]

Furthermore, since both the concentrations and the reaction rate are positive, then the kinetic constant must also necessarily be positive.

15.1.2 Evolution of concentrations

Starting from this law, it is possible to estimate the evolution of the concentrations of the reactants and the product of a reaction. Knowing the concentrations at time \(t\), in fact, those at time \(t+\Delta t\) will be given by

\[\begin{equation*} \begin{cases} x(t+\Delta t)=x(t)-r(x,y)\Delta t\\ y(t+\Delta t)=y(t)-r(x,y)\Delta t\\ z(t+\Delta t)=z(t)+r(x,y)\Delta t \end{cases} \end{equation*}\]

since the reactants tend to decrease in concentration (being consumed), while the product tends to increase (being synthesized).

Let’s substitute the law of mass action:

\[\begin{equation*} \begin{cases} x(t+\Delta t)\simeq x(t)-Kx(t)y(t)\Delta t\\ y(t+\Delta t)\simeq y(t)-Kx(t)y(t)\Delta t\\ z(t+\Delta t)\simeq z(t)+Kx(t)y(t)\Delta t \end{cases} \Longrightarrow \begin{cases} \frac{x(t+\Delta t)-x(t)}{\Delta t}\simeq-Kx(t)y(t)\\ \frac{y(t+\Delta t)-y(t)}{\Delta t}\simeq-Kx(t)y(t)\\ \frac{z(t+\Delta t)-z(t)}{\Delta t}\simeq Kx(t)y(t) \end{cases} \end{equation*}\]

In the limit for \(t\to0\) it results:

\[\begin{equation} \begin{cases} x'(t)=-Kx(t)y(t)\\ y'(t)=-Kx(t)y(t)\\ z'(t)=Kx(t)y(t) \end{cases} \end{equation}\]

Subtracting the first two equations member by member, we obtain:

\[\begin{equation*} x'(t)-y'(t)=0 \end{equation*}\]

This means that the derivatives of the two functions are equal, so the functions \(x(t)\) and \(y(t)\) differ only by a constant:

\[\begin{equation} x(t)-y(t)=c \ \iff \ y(y)=x(t)-c \end{equation}\]

To calculate \(c\), just consider the boundary conditions; let’s set \(x(0)=x_0\) and \(y(0)=y_0\) obtaining

\[\begin{equation} y_0=x_0-c \ \iff \ c=x_0-y_0 \end{equation}\]

Substituting (eq:odes-x-funzione-di-y) in the differential equation relative to \(x\) we will find

\[\begin{equation} x'(t)=-Kx(t)[x(t)-c] \ \Longrightarrow \ \boxed{x'(t)=Kx(t)[c-x(t)]} \end{equation}\]

The one found is a known differential equation, called the logistic equation. It is solvable by separating the variables:

\[\begin{equation} \frac{dx}{dt}=Kx(c-x) \ \iff \ \frac{1}{x(c-x)}dx=Kdt \end{equation}\]

In order to integrate the first member, we must decompose \(\frac{1}{x(c-x)}\) into partial fractions:

\[\begin{equation*} \frac{1}{x(c-x)}=\frac{A}{x}+\frac{B}{c-x} \end{equation*}\]

Let’s multiply both members by the denominator of the first member:

\[\begin{equation*} 1=A(c-x)+Bx=Ac-Ax+Bx \ \Longrightarrow \ Ac+(B-A)x=1 \end{equation*}\]

For the first and second members to be equal, it is necessary that

\[\begin{equation*} \begin{cases} Ac=1\\ (B-A)x=0 \end{cases} \Longrightarrow \begin{cases} A=\frac{1}{c}\\ B=A=\frac{1}{c} \end{cases} \end{equation*}\]

Returning to (eq:odes-equazione-differenziale-risoluzione-1) we will write:

\[\begin{equation*} \frac{1}{c}\left(\frac{1}{x}+\frac{1}{c-x}\right)dx=Kdt \ \iff \ \left(\frac{1}{x}+\frac{1}{c-x}\right)dx=Kcdt \end{equation*}\]

We can now integrate the two members:

\[\begin{equation*} \log\abs{x}-\log\abs{c-x}=Kct+c' \ \iff \ \log\abs{\frac{x}{c-x}}=Kct+c' \end{equation*}\]

Bringing everything to the power of \(e\):

\[\begin{equation*} \frac{x}{c-x}=e^{Kct+c'}=e^{c'}e^{Kct} \end{equation*}\]

By imposing \(x(0)=x_0\) we find the constant \(e^{c'}\):

\[\begin{equation*} e^{c'}=\frac{x_0}{c-x_0} \ \Longrightarrow \ \frac{x}{c-x}=\frac{x_0}{c-x_0}e^{Kct} \end{equation*}\]

Let’s multiply both members by \(c-x\) to isolate \(x\):

\[\begin{equation*} x=\frac{x_0}{c-x_0}(c-x)e^{Kct}=\frac{cx_0}{c-x_0}e^{Kct}-\frac{x_0}{c-x_0}xe^{Kct} \end{equation*}\]

\[\begin{equation*} \left(1+\frac{x_0}{c-x_0}e^{Kct}\right)x=\frac{cx_0}{c-x_0}e^{Kct} \end{equation*}\]

\[\begin{equation*} \frac{c-x_0+x_0e^{Kct}}{\cancel{c-x_0}}x=\frac{cx_0e^{Kct}}{\cancel{c-x_0}} \end{equation*}\]

\[\begin{equation} \boxed{x(t)=\frac{cx_0e^{Kct}}{c+x_0\left(e^{Kct}-1\right)}} \end{equation}\]

We distinguish two different behaviors based on the sign of \(c\):

Obviously, by solving the differential equation with respect to \(Y\) we will find exactly the opposite behaviors.

It remains to consider the last case, in which \(c=0\), that is, when the molecules of the two substances are initially in the same number. To find the solution in this case, we just need to go back to the differential equation written in (eq:odes-equazione-differenziale-x), from which it follows

\[\begin{equation*} x'(t)=-Kx(x)^2 \ \Longrightarrow \ \frac{1}{x^2}dx=-Kdt \ \Longrightarrow \ -\frac{1}{x}=-Kt+c' \ \Longrightarrow x(t)=\frac{1}{Kt-c'} \end{equation*}\]

In the limit \(t\to\infty\) it results \(x\to0\), therefore - as expected - the entire substance \(X\) is consumed, just as the entire substance \(Y\) is also consumed.

Returning to the differential equations, by summing \(x'(t)\) and \(z'(t)\) we obtain:

\[\begin{equation*} x'(t)+z'(t)=0 \ \Longrightarrow \ x(t)+z(t)=c' \end{equation*}\]

By imposing the initial conditions we find \(c'\):

\[\begin{equation*} c'=x(0)+z(0)=x_0+z_0 \end{equation*}\]

Finally, by substituting (eq:odes-evoluzione-x) we find the formula that describes the evolution of the product concentration:

\[\begin{equation} \boxed{z(t)=z_0+x_0-x(t)=z_0+x_0-\frac{cx_0e^{Kct}}{c+x_0\left(e^{Kct}-1\right)}} \end{equation}\]

From the results seen above, we know that

\[\begin{align*} &c>0 \ \Longrightarrow \ z\xrightarrow{t\to\infty}z_0+x_0-c\\ &c\leqslant0 \ \Longrightarrow \ z\xrightarrow{t\to\infty}z_0+x_0 \end{align*}\]

15.2 Reversible binary systems

Let’s now consider systems of the type

\[\begin{equation} X + Y \ \genfrac{}{}{-3pt}{}{\xrightarrow{K_+}}{\xleftarrow[K_-]{}} \ Z \end{equation}\]

In this case we will not have a simple decrease in the quantity of reactants together with an increase in the quantity of product, but for each concentration we will find a contribution of growth and one of decrease.

\[\begin{equation} \begin{cases} x'(t)=-K_+x(t)y(t)+K_-z(t)\\ y'(t)=-K_+x(t)y(t)+K_-z(t)\\ z'(t)=K_+x(t)y(t)-K_-z(t) \end{cases} \end{equation}\]

To find the evolution of \(x(t)\) again this time, we will have to find a conservation law that links \(x\) with \(y\) and one that links \(x\) with \(z\). To do this, it will be sufficient to subtract the second equation from the first and then the third from the first:

\[\begin{equation*} \begin{cases} x'(t)-y'(t)=0\\ x'(t)+z'(t)=0 \end{cases} \Longrightarrow \begin{cases} x(t)-y(t)=c_1\\ x(t)+z(t)=c_2 \end{cases} \Longrightarrow \begin{cases} y(t)=-\left[c_1-x(t)\right]\\ z(t)=c_2-x(t) \end{cases} \end{equation*}\]

Substituting in the differential equation relative to \(x(t)\):

\[\begin{equation} \boxed{x'(t)=K_+x(t)\left[c_1-x(t)\right]+K_-\left[c_2-x(t)\right]} \end{equation}\]

This is known as the non-homogeneous logistic equation, since the known term \(K_-c_2\) is also added with respect to the one seen in irreversible systems. To solve this equation analytically, we must make the terms with respect to \(x(t)\) explicit and make sure to eliminate the known term to reduce to the solution seen previously (equation (eq:odes-evoluzione-x)):

\[\begin{equation*} x'(t)=-K_+x(t)^2+\left[K_+c_1-K_-\right]x(t)+K_-c_2 \end{equation*}\]

Let’s perform the substitution

\[\begin{equation*} \alpha(t)=x(t)+A \ \Longrightarrow \ \frac{d\alpha}{dt}=\frac{dx}{dt} \end{equation*}\]

obtaining:

\[\begin{align*} \frac{d\alpha}{dt}&=-K_+[\alpha+A]^2+\left[K_+c_1-K_-\right][\alpha+A]+K_-c_2=\\ &=-K_+\alpha^2-2K_+A\alpha\green{-K_+A^2}+K_+c_1\alpha\green{+K_+c_1A}-K_-\alpha\green{-K_-A+K_-c_2} \end{align*}\]

where the terms independent of \(\alpha\) have been highlighted in green, which we want to eliminate. To do this, let’s solve the equation in \(A\) so as to cancel these contributions:

\[\begin{equation*} -K_+A^2+\left(K_+c_1-K_-\right)A+K_-c_2=0 \ \Rightarrow \end{equation*}\]

\[\begin{equation*} \Rightarrow \ A=\frac{-K_+c_1+K_-\pm\sqrt{\left(K_+c_1-K_-\right)^2+4K_+K_-c_2}}{-2K_+} \end{equation*}\]

Imposing

\[\begin{equation} B=\sqrt{\left(K_+c_1-K_-\right)^2+4K_+K_-c_2} \end{equation}\]

we find the following value of \(A\):

\[\begin{equation} A=\frac{K_+c_1-K_-\mp B}{2K_+} \end{equation}\]

Returning to the differential equation in \(\alpha\), what remains is

\[\begin{align*} \frac{d\alpha}{dt}&=-K_+\alpha^2+\left[K_+\left(c_1-2A\right)-K_-\right]\alpha\\ &=-K_+\alpha^2+\left[K_+\left(c_1-\cancel{2}\frac{K_+c_1-K_-\mp B}{\cancel{2}K_+}\right)-K_-\right]\alpha=\\ &=-K_+\alpha^2+\left[\cancel{k_+c_1}-\cancel{k_+c_1}+\cancel{K_-}\pm B-\cancel{K_-}\right]\alpha=K_+\alpha\left(\pm B-\alpha\right) \end{align*}\]

Having reduced to (eq:odes-equazione-differenziale-x) we know that the solutions (equation (eq:odes-evoluzione-x)) are

\[\begin{equation*} \alpha(t)=\frac{\pm B\alpha(0)e^{\pm K_+Bt}}{\pm B+\alpha(0)\left[e^{\pm K_+Bt}-1\right]} \end{equation*}\]

Remembering that \(\alpha(t)=x(t)+A\) the final result is

\[\begin{equation} \boxed{x(t)=\frac{\pm B\,(x_0+A)\,e^{\pm K_+Bt}}{\pm B+(x_0+A)\left(e^{\pm K_+Bt}-1\right)}-A} \end{equation}\]

where \(A\) and \(B\) are given by equations (eq:odes-reversibile-A) and (eq:odes-reversibile-B).

Knowing the relationship between \(x(t)\) and \(z(t)\), we therefore also know that

\[\begin{equation} \boxed{z(t)=z_0+x_0-\frac{\pm B\left(x_0+A\right)e^{\pm K_+Bt}}{\pm B+\left(x_0+A\right)\left(e^{\pm K_+Bt}-1\right)}+A} \end{equation}\]

15.2.1 Stationary concentrations

An interesting aspect of reversible reactions is to calculate what the concentrations will be in equilibrium conditions, that is, when

\[\begin{equation*} \frac{dx}{dt}=\frac{dy}{dt}=\frac{dz}{dt}=0 \end{equation*}\]

By stationarity we do not mean that no more reaction occurs, but that the fluctuations in concentrations are substantially negligible.

From the system of equations (eq:odes-sistemi-reversibili-equazioni) we will have

\[\begin{equation} K_+x\left(\bar{t}\right)y\left(\bar{t}\right)=K_-z\left(\bar{t}\right) \ \Longrightarrow \ \frac{x\left(\bar{t}\right)y\left(\bar{t}\right)}{z\left(\bar{t}\right)}=\frac{K_-}{K_+} \end{equation}\]

Considering also the conservation laws that link \(x(t)\), \(y(t)\) and \(z(t)\) we can find the solutions:

\[\begin{equation*} \begin{cases} \frac{\bar{x}\bar{y}}{\bar{z}}=\frac{K_-}{K_+}\\ \bar{y}=y_0-x_0+\bar{x}\\ \bar{z}=z_0+x_0-\bar{x} \end{cases} \end{equation*}\]

having used for simplicity the notation \(\bar{x}=x\left(\bar{t}\right)\), \(\bar{y}=y\left(\bar{t}\right)\), \(\bar{z}=z\left(\bar{t}\right)\).

A second-degree equation will come out:

\[\begin{equation*} \frac{\bar{x}\left(y_0-x_0+\bar{x}\right)}{z_0+x_0-\bar{x}}=\frac{K_-}{K_+} \ \iff \ K_+\bar{x}^2+\left[K_+\left(y_0-x_0\right)+K_-\right]\bar{x}-K_-(x_0+z_0)=0 \end{equation*}\]

whose solutions are

\[\begin{equation*} \bar{x}=\frac{K_+\left(x_0-y_0\right)-K_-\pm\sqrt{\left[K_+\left(y_0-x_0\right)+K_-\right]^2+4K_+K_-\left(x_0+z_0\right)}}{2K_+} \end{equation*}\]

15.3 The generalized law of mass action

So far we have only considered cases in which the quantities of reactants and products were all in a \(1:1\) ratio with each other. To break this constraint we must introduce the stoichiometric coefficients:

\[\begin{equation} n_XX + n_YY \ \genfrac{}{}{-3pt}{}{\xrightarrow{K_+}}{\xleftarrow[K_-]{}} \ n_ZZ \end{equation}\]

To find the solution in this case we can start from a real case, that of the Haber process, through which it is possible to synthesize ammonia from nitrogen and hydrogen:

\[\begin{equation*} N_2 + 3H_2 \ \genfrac{}{}{-3pt}{}{\xrightarrow{K_+}}{\xleftarrow[K_-]{}} \ 2NH_3 \end{equation*}\]

The equation will therefore be

\[\begin{equation*} X+3Y \ \genfrac{}{}{-3pt}{}{\xrightarrow{K_+}}{\xleftarrow[K_-]{}} \ 2Z \end{equation*}\]

According to the law of mass action, we will have to look at the product of the individual concentrations to obtain the variation of \(x\), \(y\) and \(z\). Let’s consider, for example, the substance \(X\): the written reaction tells us that for each molecule \(X\) the reaction with three molecules \(Y\) is necessary, so the product to consider will be \(xy^3\); similarly, the decay of two molecules \(Z\) is necessary, so the variation of \(x\) will be proportional to \(z^2\). Obviously this reasoning is valid for a single molecule, so in the case of the substance \(Y\) the same relationship must contain a factor of two, since each reaction produces two molecules.

Extending this reasoning we will obtain the following set of equations:

\[\begin{equation*} \begin{cases} x'(t)=-K_+x(t)y(t)^3+K_-z(t)^2\\ y'(t)=-3K_+x(t)y(t)^3+3K_-z(t)^2\\ z'(t)=2K_+x(t)y(t)^3-2K_-z(t)^2 \end{cases} \end{equation*}\]

We thus find a more general form of the law of mass action, extended to any ratio of quantities between reactants and products.

In a first approximation, the **rate** of any **chemical reaction** is proportional to the **product of the concentrations** of the reacting substances, each raised to its respective stoichiometric coefficient which must also be included in the rate with the appropriate sign.

At this point we can write the generic reversible reaction together with the relative differential equations:

\[\begin{equation} aX+bY \ \genfrac{}{}{-3pt}{}{\xrightarrow{K_+}}{\xleftarrow[K_-]{}} \ cZ+dW \ \iff \ \begin{cases} x'(t)=a\left(-K_+x^ay^b+K_-z^cw^d\right)\\ y'(t)=b\left(-K_+x^ay^b+K_-z^cw^d\right)\\ z'(t)=c\left(K_+x^ay^b-K_-z^cw^d\right)\\ w'(t)=d\left(K_+x^ay^b-K_-z^cw^d\right) \end{cases} \end{equation}\]} \end{equation}

At steady state we will have

\[\begin{equation} \boxed{\frac{\bar{x}^a\bar{y}^b}{\bar{z}^c\bar{w}^d}=\frac{K_-}{K_+}} \end{equation}\]

The conservation laws can be found as we have already done previously, that is, by adding and subtracting the differential equations so as to cancel the second member. The conservation laws with respect to \(x(t)\), for example, are given by

\[\begin{equation*} \begin{cases} bx'(t)-ay'(t)=0\\ cx'(t)-az'(t)=0\\ dx'(t)-aw'(t)=0 \end{cases} \Longrightarrow \begin{cases} bx-ay=bx_0-ay_0\\ cx-az=cx_0-az_0\\ dx-aw=dx_0-aw_0 \end{cases} \end{equation*}\]

\[\begin{equation} \boxed{\begin{cases} y=\frac{1}{a}\left[b\left(x-x_0\right)+ay_0\right]\\ z=\frac{1}{a}\left[c\left(x-x_0\right)+az_0\right]\\ w=\frac{1}{a}\left[d\left(x-x_0\right)+aw_0\right] \end{cases}} \end{equation}\]

15.4 General chemical reactions

Now we have all the tools we need to study any set of chemical reactions related to a system.

Let’s consider a total of \(m\) substances \(X_1,\dots,X_m\) involved in a total of \(n\) reactions. The system as a whole will be described by

\[\begin{equation} \sum_{i=1}^ma_{ij}X_i \ \genfrac{}{}{-3pt}{}{\xrightarrow{K_j^+}}{\xleftarrow[K_j^-]{}} \ \sum_{i=1}^mb_{ij}X_i j=1,2,\dots,n \end{equation}\]

where \(a_{ij}\) and \(b_{ij}\) are the stoichiometric coefficients relative to the \(i\)-th substance in the \(j\)-th reaction. If this substance is absent as a reactant then \(a_{ij}=0\), and similarly if it is absent as a product then \(b_{ij}=0\).

Let’s now try to write the evolutions of the concentrations through differential equations. Considering for now only the first reaction (\(j=1\)), we must take into account the difference between the stoichiometric coefficients relative to the substance as a product (\(b_{i1}\)) and to the substance as a reactant (\(a_{i1}\)), which must be factored in front of everything; for the rest we will have to consider two contributions:

Ultimately, we obtain

\[\begin{equation*} \left(b_{i1}-a_{i1}\right)\left(K_1^+\prod_{i=1}^mx_i^{a_{i1}}-K_1^-\prod_{i=1}^mx_i^{b_{i1}}\right) \end{equation*}\]

We will have to consider the contributions from all the \(n\) reactions, so we will finally obtain

\[\begin{equation} \boxed{\frac{dx_i}{dt}=\sum_{j=1}^n\left(b_{ij}-a_{ij}\right)\left(K_j^+\prod_{i=1}^mx_i^{a_{ij}}-K_j^-\prod_{i=j}^mx_i^{b_{ij}}\right)} \end{equation}\]

We can write all the equations in compact form using vector notation, defining \(\vec{x}=\left(x_1,x_2,\dots,x_m\right)\), the stoichiometric matrix \(S\) whose elements \(S_{ij}\) are given by \(b_{ij}-a_{ij}\) and the function \(r_j\left(\vec{x}\right)\) such that

\[\begin{equation*} r_j\left(\vec{x}\right)=K_j^+\prod_{i=1}^mx_i^{a_{ij}}-K_j^-\prod_{i=j}^mx_i^{b_{ij}} \end{equation*}\]

We will write:

\[\begin{equation} \boxed{\frac{d\vec{x}}{dt}=S\vec{r}\left(\vec{x}\right)} \end{equation}\]

15.4.1 Conservation laws

A generic linear conservation law is defined by a constant vector \(\vec{c}\) such that

\[\begin{equation} \vec{c}^{\ T}\cdot\frac{d\vec{x}}{dt}=0 \end{equation}\]

Substituting equation (eq:odes-reazioni-chimiche-generali-forma-compatta):

\[\begin{equation*} \vec{c}^{\ T}\cdot S\vec{r}\left(\vec{x}\right)=0 \end{equation*}\]

If the conservation law is valid, the relationship written above must be valid regardless of the choice of \(\vec{r}\left(\vec{x}\right)\), so we will have to set \(\vec{c}^{\ T}S\) to zero, or equivalently:

\[\begin{equation} \boxed{S^T\vec{c}=\vec{0}} \end{equation}\]

Let’s remember that the set of vectors \(\vec{x}_i\) such that \(A\vec{x}_i=\vec{0}\) constitutes the kernel of the matrix \(A\). Similarly, the vectors \(\vec{c}\) such that \(S^T\vec{c}=\vec{0}\) constitute the kernel of \(S^T\). We can therefore state that

The **coefficients** relative to the **linear conservation laws** belong to the **kernel** of the **transposed stoichiometric matrix**

From this it also follows that the number of independent linear conservation laws is equal to the dimension of the kernel of \(S^T\).

To understand what has been written, we can start from a very simple example, such as the trivial irreversible relation

\[\begin{equation*} X+Y \ \longrightarrow \ Z \end{equation*}\]

The stoichiometric matrix is given by

\[\begin{equation*} S= \begin{pmatrix} 0-1\\ 0-1\\ 1-0 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} -1\\ -1\\ 1 \end{pmatrix} \end{equation*}\]

The kernel of \(S^T\) is

\[\begin{equation*} S^T\vec{c}= \begin{pmatrix} -1 & -1 & 1 \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} c_1\\ c_2\\ c_3 \end{pmatrix} =-c_1-c_2+c_3=0 \end{equation*}\]

One possibility is that

\[\begin{equation*} c_3=c_1+c_2 \ \Longrightarrow \ \vec{c}= \begin{pmatrix} c_1\\ c_2\\ c_1+c_2 \end{pmatrix} = c_1 \begin{pmatrix} 1\\ 0\\ 1 \end{pmatrix} +c_2 \begin{pmatrix} 0\\ 1\\ 1 \end{pmatrix} \end{equation*}\]

By arbitrarily setting \(c_1=c_2=1\) we will find the conservation laws

\[\begin{equation*} \begin{cases} x+z=0\\ y+z=0 \end{cases} \end{equation*}\]

Another possibility is the following:

\[\begin{equation*} c_1=-c_2+c_3 \ \Longrightarrow \ \vec{c}= \begin{pmatrix} -c_2+c_3\\ c_2\\ c_3 \end{pmatrix} =c_2 \begin{pmatrix} -1\\ 1\\ 0 \end{pmatrix} +c_3 \begin{pmatrix} 1\\ 0\\ 1 \end{pmatrix} \end{equation*}\]

In this case the conservation laws are

\[\begin{equation*} \begin{cases} -x+y=0\\ x+z=0 \end{cases} \end{equation*}\]

which are the same ones we used in paragraph (sec:evoluzione-delle-concentrazioni).

15.5 Known models

As anticipated, all the reasoning done can be extended to dynamics completely different from that of actual chemical reactions, such as biological ecosystems, epidemic models, physiological mechanisms and so on. Below we will see some practical examples in which systems of ordinary differential equations come into play.

15.5.1 Lotka-Volterra model

A first system to study is the one related to the predator-prey dynamics. Indicating with \(X\) the prey and with \(Y\) the predator, we can consider three dynamics:

The system of reactions is therefore the following:

\[\begin{equation} \begin{cases} X \ \xrightarrow{K_1} \ 2X\\ X+Y \ \xrightarrow{K_2} \ 2Y\\ Y \ \xrightarrow{K_3} \ \varnothing \end{cases} \end{equation}\]

It is important to understand that in this case these are not real reactions as they are understood in chemistry, since there is not necessarily an interaction between two different species as we observe in the first and third cases. We could in some way speak of an between the individual and the surrounding environment, such as the prey and the food useful for its survival and therefore its subsequent reproduction, but the food in question is not a real element involved in the dynamics of evolution of the species, but only something that is intrinsically part of the system and allows it to exist in the way we observe it.

In any case, the law of mass action can also be extended to cases like these; the differential equations that indicate the variations of the species, in fact, will be given by

\[\begin{equation} \begin{cases} x'(t)=K_1x(t)-K_2x(t)y(t)\\ y'(t)=K_2x(t)y(t)-K_3y(t) \end{cases} \Longrightarrow \boxed{\begin{cases} x'(t)=\left[K_1-K_2y(t)\right]x(t)\\ y'(t)=\left[K_2x(t)-K_3\right]y(t) \end{cases}} \end{equation}\]

These equations are known in the literature as the Lotka-Volterra equations.

At steady state, excluding the trivial solution \(\left(\bar{x},\bar{y}\right)=(0,0)\), it results:

\[\begin{equation*} \begin{cases} x'(t)=0\\ y'(t)=0 \end{cases} \iff \begin{cases} K_1-K_2\bar{y}=0\\ K_2\bar{x}-K_3=0 \end{cases} \Longrightarrow \begin{cases} \bar{y}=\frac{K_1}{K_2}\\ \bar{x}=\frac{K_3}{K_2} \end{cases} \end{equation*}\]

For the dynamics of a physical system of this type, it is useful to calculate the value assumed by the eigenvalue relative to the Jacobian at the stationary point; the possibilities are shown to be five:

Let’s try to evaluate the case of the predator-prey system by calculating the Jacobian of the system:

\[\begin{equation*} J= \begin{pmatrix} \frac{\partial x'}{\partial x} & \frac{\partial x'}{\partial y}\\ \frac{\partial y'}{\partial x} & \frac{\partial y'}{\partial y} \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} K_1-K_2y & -K_2x\\ K_2y & K_2x-K_3 \end{pmatrix} \end{equation*}\]

At the stationary point the Jacobian becomes

\[\begin{equation*} J\left(\bar{x},\bar{y}\right)= \begin{pmatrix} K_1-K_2\cdot\frac{K_1}{K_2} & -K_2\cdot\frac{K_3}{K_2}\\ K_2\cdot\frac{K_1}{K_2} & K_2\cdot\frac{K_3}{K_2}-K_3 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & -K_3\\ K_1 & 0 \end{pmatrix} \end{equation*}\]

The eigenvalues of the matrix are

\[\begin{equation*} \det\left(J\left(\bar{x},\bar{y}\right)-\lambda I\right)= \begin{pmatrix} -\lambda & -K_3\\ K_1 & -\lambda \end{pmatrix} =\lambda^2+K_1K_3=0 \ \Longrightarrow \ \lambda=\pm i\sqrt{K_1K_3} \end{equation*}\]

We are therefore in the last case, in which the system neither converges nor diverges but tends to stabilize in an orbit around the point defined by the kinetic constants \(K_1\), \(K_2\) and \(K_3\).

In this case, calculating the conservation law is not trivial; the quantity that is shown to be conserved is the Lyapunov function:

\[\begin{equation*} V(x,y)=K_2(x+y)-K_3\ln x-K_1\ln y \end{equation*}\]

By deriving, in fact, we obtain

\[\begin{align*} \frac{dV}{dt}&=\frac{\partial V}{\partial x}\frac{\partial x}{\partial t}+\frac{\partial V}{\partial y}\frac{\partial y}{\partial t}=\\ &=\left(K_2-\frac{K_3}{x}\right)\left(K_1-K_2y\right)x+\left(K_2-\frac{K_1}{y}\right)\left(K_2x-K_3\right)y=\\ &=\left(K_2x-K_3\right)\left(K_1-K_2y\right)+\left(K_2y-K_1\right)\left(K_2x-K_3\right)=\\ &=\left(K_2x-K_3\right)\left(\cancel{K_1}-\cancel{K_2y}+\cancel{K_2y}-\cancel{K_1}\right)=0 \end{align*}\]

Let’s always keep in mind that this model can hardly be applied in real contexts, because it ignores many aspects that are very important in an ecosystem that we know to be - in general - very delicate. A trivial example is the number of individuals of a species: according to this model, in fact, in principle a species can have a population of any size, but in reality we know that below a certain threshold a species becomes at serious risk of extinction. The Lotka-Volterra model is however useful as a starting point for developing more elaborate models that can describe real systems.

15.5.2 Brusselator

Another system associated with chaotic behaviors with limit cycles is the brusselator model, described by the following reactions:

\[\begin{equation} \begin{cases} A \ \xrightarrow{K_1} \ X\\ B+X \ \xrightarrow{K_2} \ Y+D\\ 2X+Y \ \xrightarrow{K_3} \ 3X\\ X \ \xrightarrow{K_4} \ E\\ \end{cases} \end{equation}\]

This model was introduced to study autocatalytic reactions, that is, those reactions that have as a product a catalyst capable of accelerating the same. It was conceived by the Nobel Prize winner for chemistry Ilya Prigogine, and was later studied in depth due to its chaotic dynamic characteristics and oscillatory behaviors.

The quantities \(A\), \(B\), \(D\) and \(E\) are considered constant. The remaining concentrations will follow the system of differential equations constructed as we know:

\[\begin{equation*} \begin{cases} x'(t)=K_1A-K_2Bx(t)+K_3x(t)^2y(t)-K_4x(t)\\ y'(t)=K_2Bx(t)-K_3x(t)^2y(t) \end{cases} \end{equation*}\]

which, by ordering the terms, becomes

\[\begin{equation} \boxed{\begin{cases} x'(t)=K_1A-\left[K_2B-K_3x(t)y(t)+K_4\right]x(t)\\ y'(t)=\left[K_2B-K_3x(t)y(t)\right]x(t) \end{cases}} \end{equation}\]

Excluding the obvious solution for the equilibrium that would be had with \(x(t)=0\), as the only other possibility remains

\[\begin{equation*} K_2B-K_3\bar{x}\bar{y}=0 \ \iff \ \bar{y}=\frac{K_2B}{K_3\bar{x}} \end{equation*}\]

in order to cancel \(y'(t)\). Substituting in the differential equation relative to \(x(t)\) results:

\[\begin{equation*} K_1A-\left(K_2B-\cancel{K_3}\bar{x}\frac{K_2B}{\cancel{K_3}\bar{x}}+K_4\right)\bar{x}=0 \ \iff \ K_1A\cancel{-K_2B\bar{x}}\cancel{+K_2B\bar{x}}-K_3\bar{x}=0 \end{equation*}\]

The stationary point will therefore be

\[\begin{equation} \begin{cases} \bar{x}=\frac{K_1}{K_4}A\\ \bar{y}=\frac{K_2K_4}{K_1K_3}\frac{B}{A} \end{cases} \end{equation}\]

15.5.3 Michaelis-Menten kinetics

Moving to the biological field, we can consider an enzymatic reaction, in which an enzyme \(E\) binds to a substrate \(S\) forming what is called an intermediate complex \(ES\), which can subsequently release the same enzyme together with the synthesized protein \(P\):

\[\begin{equation} E+S \ \genfrac{}{}{-3pt}{}{\xrightarrow{K_+}}{\xleftarrow[K_-]{}} \ ES \ \genfrac{}{}{-3pt}{}{\xrightarrow{C_+}}{\xleftarrow[C_-]{}} \ E+P \end{equation}\]

This model is called Michaelis-Menten kinetics because of the two chemists who first proposed it in 1913. We can rewrite it for simplicity as

\[\begin{equation*} X+Y \ \genfrac{}{}{-3pt}{}{\xrightarrow{K_+}}{\xleftarrow[K_-]{}} \ Z \ \genfrac{}{}{-3pt}{}{\xrightarrow{C_+}}{\xleftarrow[C_-]{}} \ X+W \end{equation*}\]

We thus find the following equations:

\[\begin{equation*} \begin{cases} x'(t)=-K_+xy+K_-z+C_+z-C_-xw\\ y'(t)=-K_+xy+K_-z\\ z'(t)=K_+xy-K_-z-C_+z+C_-xw\\ w'(t)=C_+z-C_-xw \end{cases} \end{equation*}\]

which ordered become

\[\begin{equation} \boxed{\begin{cases} x'(t)=-\left(K_+y+C_-w\right)x+\left(K_-+C_+\right)z\\ y'(t)=-K_+xy+K_-z\\ z'(t)=\left(K_+y+C_-w\right)x-\left(K_-+C_+\right)z\\ w'(t)=-C_-xw+C_+z \end{cases}} \end{equation}\]

At equilibrium, the rate of formation of the enzyme-substrate complex must equal the rate of its decomposition, so \(z\) must not change:

\[\begin{equation*} z'(t)=0 \ \iff \ z=\frac{K_+y+C_-w}{K_-+C_+}x \end{equation*}\]

Furthermore, from the equations we note that

\[\begin{equation*} x'(t)-z'(t)=0 \ \Longrightarrow \ x-z=x_0-z_0 \ \iff \ x=z+x_0-z_0 \end{equation*}\]

Let’s substitute:

\[\begin{equation*} z=\frac{K_+y+C_-w}{K_-+C_+}\left(z+x_0-z_0\right) \end{equation*}\]

Let’s calculate \(z\):

\[\begin{equation*} \left(1-\frac{K_+y+C_-w}{K_-+C_+}\right)z=\frac{K_+y+C_-w}{K_-+C_+}\left(x_0-z_0\right) \end{equation*}\]

\[\begin{equation*} \frac{K_-+C_+-K_+y-C_-w}{K_-+C_+}z=\frac{K_+y+C_-w}{K_-+C_+}\left(x_0-z_0\right) \end{equation*}\]

\[\begin{equation*} z=\frac{K_+y+C_-w}{K_-+C_+-K_+y-C_-w}\left(x_0-z_0\right) \end{equation*}\]

Dividing the numerator and denominator by \(K_+y+C_-w\):

\[\begin{equation*} z=\frac{1}{\frac{K_-+C_+}{K_+y+C_-w}-1}\left(x_0-z_0\right) \end{equation*}\]

Indicating the fraction in the denominator with \(K(y,w)\) we can write:

\[\begin{equation} \boxed{z=\frac{1}{K(y,w)-1}\left(x_0-z_0\right)} \end{equation}\]

We have thus managed to find a relationship that links the complex to the concentrations of the substrate and the protein.

15.5.4 Kinetics of gene expression

Still in the biological field, we find another model constructed through reaction: gene expression. This occurs through the transcription of genes into mRNA, which is then translated into a protein; both the mRNA and the protein then decay.

\[\begin{equation} \begin{cases} G \ \xrightarrow{K_c} \ mRNA\\ mRNA \ \xrightarrow{K_d} \ mRNA + P\\ mRNA \ \xrightarrow{\delta_m} \ \varnothing\\ P \ \xrightarrow{\delta_p} \ \varnothing \end{cases} \end{equation}\]

The equations will be

\[\begin{equation} \begin{cases} m'(t)=K_cG\cancel{-K_dm}\cancel{+K_dm}-\delta_mm\\ p'(t)=K_dm-\delta_pp \end{cases} \Longrightarrow \boxed{\begin{cases} m'(t)=K_cG-\delta_mm\\ p'(t)=K_dm-\delta_pp \end{cases}} \end{equation}\]

From this system it is easy to calculate the stationary point:

\[\begin{equation} \bar{m}=\frac{K_cG}{\delta_m} \ \Longrightarrow \ K_d\frac{K_cG}{\delta_m}-\delta_p\bar{p}=0 \ \Longrightarrow \ \begin{cases} \bar{m}=\frac{K_cG}{\delta_m}\\ \bar{p}=\frac{K_cK_dG}{\delta_m\delta_p} \end{cases} \end{equation}\]} \end{equation}

15.6 Fluctuations

15.6.1 Deterministic limit

Molecules (or individuals) are discrete entities that collide at random; when the required colliding set meets, the reaction occurs at some rate. The discrete nature of reacting entities therefore generates random events and hence stochasticity in the dynamics. The deterministic mass–action ODEs derived earlier are recovered only in the limit of large system size (many molecules), when fluctuations become negligible. In what follows we write the chemical Master Equation (CME) for a general well‑mixed system, state the combinatorial propensities, and perform the standard system‑size (Kramers–Moyal) expansion that connects the CME to the deterministic mass‑action ODEs and to a mesoscopic diffusion approximation.

Let \(\vec{n}=(n_1,n_2,\dots,n_S)^\top\) be the vector of molecule counts for species \(X_1,\dots,X_S\). Consider \(R\) reactions. For reaction \(r\) denote the reactant and product stoichiometric coefficients by \(N_{ir}\) and \(M_{ir}\) respectively, and define the stoichiometric change vector \[ \vec{s}_r=(M_{1r}-N_{1r},\;M_{2r}-N_{2r},\;\dots,\;M_{Sr}-N_{Sr})^\top. \]

If reaction \(r\) fires in the forward direction it maps \[ \vec{n}\xrightarrow{T_r^+(\vec{n})}\vec{n}+\vec{s}_r, \] whereas a backward (reverse) firing maps \[ \vec{n}\xrightarrow{T_r^-(\vec{n})}\vec{n}-\vec{s}_r. \]

For a well‑mixed system the combinatorial propensities (transition rates) take the form \[\begin{align} T_r^+(\vec{n}) &= k_r^+ \prod_{i=1}^S \binom{n_i}{N_{ir}}, \\ T_r^-(\vec{n}) &= k_r^- \prod_{i=1}^S \binom{n_i}{M_{ir}}, \end{align}\] because for reaction \(r\) to occur one must choose, for each species \(i\), an unordered set of \(N_{ir}\) reactant molecules out of the \(n_i\) present. These \(T_r^\pm\) are also called propensities.

The chemical Master Equation for the probability \(p(\vec{n},t)\) of being in state \(\vec{n}\) at time \(t\) is \[ \partial_t p(\vec{n},t)=\sum_{r=1}^R\Big[\,T_r^+(\vec{n}-\vec{s}_r)\,p(\vec{n}-\vec{s}_r,t)-T_r^+(\vec{n})\,p(\vec{n},t) +T_r^-(\vec{n}+\vec{s}_r)\,p(\vec{n}+\vec{s}_r,t)-T_r^-(\vec{n})\,p(\vec{n},t)\Big]. \] This CME is an exact description of the stochastic well‑mixed model and can be simulated exactly using Gillespie’s stochastic simulation algorithm.

To obtain the deterministic limit introduce a system‑size parameter \(\Omega\) (for example the physical reaction volume). Define concentrations \[ \vec{x}=\frac{\vec{n}}{\Omega}, \] and assume molecule counts scale with \(\Omega\) so that typical \(n_i=O(\Omega)\) and the net reaction fluxes are extensive. For large \(\Omega\) the combinatorial factors satisfy the leading‑order approximation \[ \binom{\Omega x_i}{N_{ir}}\simeq\frac{(\Omega x_i)^{N_{ir}}}{N_{ir}!}\qquad(\Omega\to\infty), \] hence, with suitably scaled rate constants, \[ T_r^\pm(\Omega\vec{x})\simeq \Omega\,v_r^\pm(\vec{x}), \] where the macroscopic per‑unit‑volume reaction rates are \[\begin{align} v_r^+(\vec{x}) &= k_r^+\prod_{i=1}^S\frac{x_i^{N_{ir}}}{N_{ir}!},\\ v_r^-(\vec{x}) &= k_r^-\prod_{i=1}^S\frac{x_i^{M_{ir}}}{M_{ir}!}, \end{align}\] and the net macroscopic rate per unit volume is \(v_r(\vec{x})\equiv v_r^+(\vec{x})-v_r^-(\vec{x})\).

Multiplying the CME by \(\vec{n}\) and summing over all states gives the exact evolution for the mean counts: \[ \frac{d}{dt}\langle\vec{n}\rangle=\sum_{r=1}^R\vec{s}_r\Big\langle T_r^+(\vec{n})-T_r^-(\vec{n})\Big\rangle. \] In concentrations this reads \[ \frac{d}{dt}\langle\vec{x}\rangle=\sum_{r=1}^R\vec{s}_r\left[\frac{\langle T_r^+(\Omega\vec{x})\rangle}{\Omega}-\frac{\langle T_r^-(\Omega\vec{x})\rangle}{\Omega}\right]. \]

The right‑hand side above must be \(O(1)\) as \(\Omega\to\infty\) for a nontrivial macroscopic limit. Under the scaling stated earlier one finds \[ \frac{\langle T_r^\pm(\Omega\vec{x})\rangle}{\Omega}\simeq v_r^\pm(\langle\vec{x}\rangle)+o(1), \] provided fluctuations are negligible. If correlations and higher cumulants vanish in the large‑\(\Omega\) limit (mean‑field or moment‑closure approximation), \[ \Big\langle\prod_i x_i^{N_{ir}}\Big\rangle \approx \prod_i\langle x_i\rangle^{N_{ir}}, \] and we recover the deterministic mass–action ODEs (the law of large numbers limit): \[ \frac{d}{dt}x_i=\sum_{r=1}^R s_{ir}\,v_r(\vec{x}),\qquad i=1,\dots,S. \] These are the same mass–action ODEs written earlier. The approximation is valid when molecule numbers are large, the system is well mixed, and correlations are negligible.

A more refined approximation is obtained by the Kramers–Moyal (system‑size) expansion of the CME to second order. This yields a chemical Fokker–Planck equation (diffusive approximation) for the probability density of \(\vec{x}\): \[ \partial_t p(\vec{x},t)=-\sum_{i=1}^S\frac{\partial}{\partial x_i}\big[A_i(\vec{x})\,p\big]+\frac{1}{2}\sum_{i,j=1}^S\frac{\partial^2}{\partial x_i\partial x_j}\big[B_{ij}(\vec{x})\,p\big], \] with drift and diffusion coefficients \[\begin{align} A_i(\vec{x}) &= \sum_{r=1}^R s_{ir}\,v_r(\vec{x}), \\ B_{ij}(\vec{x}) &= \frac{1}{\Omega}\sum_{r=1}^R s_{ir}s_{jr}\,\big(v_r^+(\vec{x})+v_r^-(\vec{x})\big). \end{align}\] The drift equals the deterministic mass‑action velocity and the diffusion is of order \(1/\Omega\).

The Fokker–Planck equation corresponds (not uniquely) to the Chemical Langevin Equation (CLE), which provides a mesoscopic stochastic differential equation approximation: \[ dx_i=\sum_{r=1}^R s_{ir}\,v_r(\vec{x})\,dt+\frac{1}{\sqrt{\Omega}}\sum_{r=1}^R s_{ir}\sqrt{v_r^+(\vec{x})+v_r^-(\vec{x})}\;dW_r(t), \] where the \(dW_r\) are independent standard Wiener increments (one noise per reaction channel). The CLE shows that fluctuations around the deterministic trajectory scale as \(\Omega^{-1/2}\).

Remarks and conditions of validity:

In summary, starting from the discrete stochastic description (CME) with combinatorial propensities one recovers the deterministic mass‑action ODEs by (i) introducing concentrations, (ii) imposing the appropriate scaling of rate constants with system size, and (iii) applying a mean‑field closure. The leading stochastic corrections are captured by the Fokker–Planck equation and the CLE, which quantify fluctuations of order \(\Omega^{-1/2}\) about the deterministic trajectory.

# Ordinary differential equations

In this chapter we will deal with **ODEs** (***O**rdinary **D**ifferential **E**quations*) related to chemical reactions. Let's clarify right away that by \q{chemical reactions}, in reality, we mean something more than what the term generally suggests: it includes all those systems composed of *individuals* of a certain type who react with individuals of the same type or of a different type, producing as a product individuals who may or may not be of the same nature.

\begin{equation*}
    \text{Reactant 1}   +   \text{Reactant 2}   \genfrac{}{}{-3pt}{}{\longrightarrow}{\longleftarrow}   \text{Products}
\end{equation*}

In the generic reaction written above, we notice the presence of two arrows that indicate both directions: the reason is that, in the course of the discussion, we will also consider *reversible* reactions, which can therefore allow the system to return to its original state.

The reason why we consider systems of this type is that they form a wide field of applications where both stochastic and deterministic models prove to be very useful. Some specific examples are the following:

\begin{itemize}
    \item **Actual chemical reactions**, involving particles or molecules that transform in case of collisions
    \item **Population systems**, where individuals can be born, generate other individuals, die, feed on each other, immigrate and so on
    \item **Epidemics**, in which diseases are transmitted from individual to individual
    \item **Gene expressions**, such that genes can be transcribed (into mRNA) and translated (into proteins) as a function of some transcription factors
\end{itemize}

The objective is to write ordinary differential equations for the *concentrations* of the reactants and products starting from the rules that we know govern these chemical reactions. Furthermore, we will only deal with the *deterministic* dynamics, thus ignoring the statistical ones.

## Irreversible binary systems

Let's consider a substance of type $X$ and a substance of type $Y$ (reactants) that collide and react, producing a substance of type $Z$ (product):

\begin{equation}
    n_XX+n_YY \ \xrightarrow{K} \ n_ZZ
\end{equation}

In the reaction, two important quantities are made explicit:

\begin{itemize}
    \item **Stoichiometric coefficients** ($n_X$, $n_Y$, $n_Z$): number of reactants consumed and products synthesized
    \item **Kinetic constant** ($K$): parameter proportional to the *rate of occurrence* associated with the reaction, also simply called *reaction rate*
\end{itemize}

### Law of mass action

To calculate the reaction rate, we must first make some assumptions about the system we are considering:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item The reaction takes place in a **finite volume** $V$. Below we will speak explicitly of volume in a physical sense, but it should be borne in mind that, in general, it means any characteristic length that in some way represents the \q{dimension} in which the reaction takes place.
    \item The system must be **well mixed**. This consequently allows us to assume a uniform distribution of the molecules in the volume $V$, and therefore to treat the concentrations as homogeneous quantities.
    \item The **concentrations must be low**. This allows us to neglect the phenomena of *crowding* in which the probability of reaction would not be the same for all molecules.
    \item The **molecules must be in high number**. In this way we can apply *continuous models*, such as - precisely - ordinary differential equations, treating the concentrations as variables of the real field.
\end{enumerate}

If all these conditions are met, we will say that we are at the **dilute limit**. Let's indicate the *concentrations* of reactants and product with

\begin{equation*}
    x=\frac{n_X}{V}   y=\frac{n_Y}{V}   z=\frac{n_Z}{V}
\end{equation*}

For now we will only consider the case with stoichiometric coefficients equal to one; from section (sec:la-legge-di-azione-di-massa-generalizzata) we will extend to a generic number of molecules (or moles) different for the single reaction.

According to what we have written, the reaction rate $r$ will be a function exclusively of $x$ and $y$. Furthermore, since the concentrations are small, we can expand with respect to $(x,y)=(0,0)$, thus approximating the function:

\begin{equation*}
    r(x,y)\simeq r(0,0)+\left(\partial_xr_0\right)x+\left(\partial_yr_0\right)y+\left(\frac{1}{2}\partial_{xx}^2r_0\right)x^2+\left(\partial_{xy}^2r_0\right)xy+\left(\frac{1}{2}\partial_{yy}^2r_0\right)y^2
\end{equation*}

having used the notation $\partial_i r_0=\partial_i r\big|_{(x,y)=(0,0)}$ for simplicity.

We can set

\begin{equation*}
    \begin{cases}
        r(x,0)=0   \forall x\\
        r(0,y)=0   \forall y
    \end{cases}
\end{equation*}

since both molecules of $X$ and molecules of $Y$ are necessary for the reaction to occur.

This implies that $r(0,0)=0$. Furthermore, by substituting the two cases in the expansion, we obtain:

\begin{equation*}
    \begin{cases}
        r(x,0)\simeq\left(\partial_xr_0\right)x+\left(\frac{1}{2}\partial_{xx}^2r_0\right)x^2=0   \forall x\\
        r(0,y)\simeq\left(\partial_yr_0\right)y+\left(\frac{1}{2}\partial_{yy}^2r_0\right)y^2=0   \forall y
    \end{cases}
\end{equation*}

The fact that the first must hold for every value of $x$ while the second for every value of $y$ clarifies that these are *identities*, not equations. We will therefore have to set the individual coefficients to zero:

\begin{equation*}
    \begin{cases}
        \partial_xr_0=0,   \partial_{xx}^2r_0=0\\
        \partial_yr_0=0,   \partial_{yy}^2r_0=0
    \end{cases}
\end{equation*}

Returning to the expansion, only one term survives:

\begin{equation*}
    r(x,y)\simeq\left(\partial_{xy}^2r_0\right)xy
\end{equation*}

Indicating the mixed derivative of the reaction rate calculated at the origin with $K$, called the **kinetic constant**, we therefore find that

\begin{equation}
    \boxed{r(x,y)\simeq Kxy}
\end{equation}

We thus find what is called the **law of mass action**.

::: {.definition}
    In a first approximation, the **rate** of any **chemical reaction** is proportional to the **product of the concentrations** of the reacting substances.

:::

Let's remember that this is valid only in the dilute limit regime, in which the $X$ molecules all have the same probability $P_{XY}$ of meeting and reacting with the $Y$ molecules, and the same applies in reverse for $P_{YX}$; these probabilities, moreover, are *independent* of each other.

What we have called the kinetic constant $K$ is actually constant only with respect to the concentrations $x$ and $y$, but in general it can depend on other parameters of the system; in chemistry, for example, the *Arrhenius equation* is known, in which the constant is linked to the temperature $T$ of the system by the following relation:

\begin{equation*}
    K=K_0e^{-\frac{E}{k_BT}}
\end{equation*}

Furthermore, since both the concentrations and the reaction rate are positive, then the kinetic constant must also necessarily be positive.

### Evolution of concentrations

Starting from this law, it is possible to estimate the evolution of the concentrations of the reactants and the product of a reaction. Knowing the concentrations at time $t$, in fact, those at time $t+\Delta t$ will be given by

\begin{equation*}
    \begin{cases}
        x(t+\Delta t)=x(t)-r(x,y)\Delta t\\
        y(t+\Delta t)=y(t)-r(x,y)\Delta t\\
        z(t+\Delta t)=z(t)+r(x,y)\Delta t
    \end{cases}
\end{equation*}

since the reactants tend to decrease in concentration (being consumed), while the product tends to increase (being synthesized).

Let's substitute the law of mass action:

\begin{equation*}
    \begin{cases}
        x(t+\Delta t)\simeq x(t)-Kx(t)y(t)\Delta t\\
        y(t+\Delta t)\simeq y(t)-Kx(t)y(t)\Delta t\\
        z(t+\Delta t)\simeq z(t)+Kx(t)y(t)\Delta t
    \end{cases}
    \Longrightarrow
    \begin{cases}
        \frac{x(t+\Delta t)-x(t)}{\Delta t}\simeq-Kx(t)y(t)\\
        \frac{y(t+\Delta t)-y(t)}{\Delta t}\simeq-Kx(t)y(t)\\
        \frac{z(t+\Delta t)-z(t)}{\Delta t}\simeq Kx(t)y(t)
    \end{cases}
\end{equation*}

In the limit for $t\to0$ it results:\footnote{From now on, for simplicity, I will not use the symbol of approximate equality but of exact equality, keeping in mind however that it is a result valid up to the first order of approximation.}

\begin{equation}
    \begin{cases}
        x'(t)=-Kx(t)y(t)\\
        y'(t)=-Kx(t)y(t)\\
        z'(t)=Kx(t)y(t)
    \end{cases}
\end{equation}

\subsubsection{Evolution of $x$}

Subtracting the first two equations member by member, we obtain:

\begin{equation*}
    x'(t)-y'(t)=0
\end{equation*}

This means that the derivatives of the two functions are equal, so the functions $x(t)$ and $y(t)$ differ only by a constant:

\begin{equation}
    x(t)-y(t)=c \ \iff \ y(y)=x(t)-c
\end{equation}

To calculate $c$, just consider the boundary conditions; let's set $x(0)=x_0$ and $y(0)=y_0$ obtaining

\begin{equation}
    y_0=x_0-c \ \iff \ c=x_0-y_0
\end{equation}

Substituting (eq:odes-x-funzione-di-y) in the differential equation relative to $x$ we will find

\begin{equation}
    x'(t)=-Kx(t)[x(t)-c] \ \Longrightarrow \ \boxed{x'(t)=Kx(t)[c-x(t)]}
\end{equation}

The one found is a known differential equation, called the **logistic equation**. It is solvable by separating the variables:

\begin{equation}
    \frac{dx}{dt}=Kx(c-x) \ \iff \ \frac{1}{x(c-x)}dx=Kdt
\end{equation}

In order to integrate the first member, we must decompose $\frac{1}{x(c-x)}$ into partial fractions:

\begin{equation*}
    \frac{1}{x(c-x)}=\frac{A}{x}+\frac{B}{c-x}
\end{equation*}

Let's multiply both members by the denominator of the first member:

\begin{equation*}
    1=A(c-x)+Bx=Ac-Ax+Bx \ \Longrightarrow \ Ac+(B-A)x=1
\end{equation*}

For the first and second members to be equal, it is necessary that

\begin{equation*}
    \begin{cases}
        Ac=1\\
        (B-A)x=0
    \end{cases}
    \Longrightarrow
    \begin{cases}
        A=\frac{1}{c}\\
        B=A=\frac{1}{c}
    \end{cases}
\end{equation*}

Returning to (eq:odes-equazione-differenziale-risoluzione-1) we will write:

\begin{equation*}
    \frac{1}{c}\left(\frac{1}{x}+\frac{1}{c-x}\right)dx=Kdt \ \iff \ \left(\frac{1}{x}+\frac{1}{c-x}\right)dx=Kcdt
\end{equation*}

We can now integrate the two members:

\begin{equation*}
    \log\abs{x}-\log\abs{c-x}=Kct+c' \ \iff \ \log\abs{\frac{x}{c-x}}=Kct+c'
\end{equation*}

Bringing everything to the power of $e$:

\begin{equation*}
    \frac{x}{c-x}=e^{Kct+c'}=e^{c'}e^{Kct}
\end{equation*}

By imposing $x(0)=x_0$ we find the constant $e^{c'}$:

\begin{equation*}
    e^{c'}=\frac{x_0}{c-x_0} \ \Longrightarrow \ \frac{x}{c-x}=\frac{x_0}{c-x_0}e^{Kct}
\end{equation*}

Let's multiply both members by $c-x$ to isolate $x$:

\begin{equation*}
    x=\frac{x_0}{c-x_0}(c-x)e^{Kct}=\frac{cx_0}{c-x_0}e^{Kct}-\frac{x_0}{c-x_0}xe^{Kct}
\end{equation*}

\begin{equation*}
    \left(1+\frac{x_0}{c-x_0}e^{Kct}\right)x=\frac{cx_0}{c-x_0}e^{Kct}
\end{equation*}

\begin{equation*}
    \frac{c-x_0+x_0e^{Kct}}{\cancel{c-x_0}}x=\frac{cx_0e^{Kct}}{\cancel{c-x_0}}
\end{equation*}

\begin{equation}
    \boxed{x(t)=\frac{cx_0e^{Kct}}{c+x_0\left(e^{Kct}-1\right)}}
\end{equation}

We distinguish two different behaviors based on the sign of $c$:

\begin{itemize}
    \item $c>0$, that is, initially there is more quantity of $X$ than $Y$.

    \begin{equation*}
        \lim_{t\to\infty}x(t)=c
    \end{equation*}

    Therefore, in the end, the quantity of substance $X$ will be equal to the initial difference from substance $Y$.

    \item $c<0$, that is, initially there is less quantity of $X$ than $Y$.

    \begin{equation*}
        \lim_{t\to\infty}x(t)=0
    \end{equation*}

    Therefore, in the end, the substance $X$ will be completely consumed.
\end{itemize}

Obviously, by solving the differential equation with respect to $Y$ we will find exactly the opposite behaviors.

It remains to consider the last case, in which $c=0$, that is, when the molecules of the two substances are initially in the same number. To find the solution in this case, we just need to go back to the differential equation written in (eq:odes-equazione-differenziale-x), from which it follows

\begin{equation*}
    x'(t)=-Kx(x)^2 \ \Longrightarrow \ \frac{1}{x^2}dx=-Kdt \ \Longrightarrow \ -\frac{1}{x}=-Kt+c' \ \Longrightarrow x(t)=\frac{1}{Kt-c'}
\end{equation*}

In the limit $t\to\infty$ it results $x\to0$, therefore - as expected - the entire substance $X$ is consumed, just as the entire substance $Y$ is also consumed.\footnote{The differential equation relative to $y(t)$, in fact, is the same.}

\subsubsection{Evolution of $z$}

Returning to the differential equations, by summing $x'(t)$ and $z'(t)$ we obtain:

\begin{equation*}
    x'(t)+z'(t)=0 \ \Longrightarrow \ x(t)+z(t)=c'
\end{equation*}

By imposing the initial conditions we find $c'$:

\begin{equation*}
    c'=x(0)+z(0)=x_0+z_0
\end{equation*}

Finally, by substituting (eq:odes-evoluzione-x) we find the formula that describes the evolution of the product concentration:

\begin{equation}
    \boxed{z(t)=z_0+x_0-x(t)=z_0+x_0-\frac{cx_0e^{Kct}}{c+x_0\left(e^{Kct}-1\right)}}
\end{equation}

From the results seen above, we know that

\begin{align*}
    &c>0 \ \Longrightarrow \ z\xrightarrow{t\to\infty}z_0+x_0-c\\
    &c\leqslant0 \ \Longrightarrow \ z\xrightarrow{t\to\infty}z_0+x_0
\end{align*}

## Reversible binary systems

Let's now consider systems of the type

\begin{equation}
    X + Y \ \genfrac{}{}{-3pt}{}{\xrightarrow{K_+}}{\xleftarrow[K_-]{}} \ Z
\end{equation}

In this case we will not have a simple decrease in the quantity of reactants together with an increase in the quantity of product, but for each concentration we will find a contribution of growth and one of decrease.

\begin{equation}
    \begin{cases}
        x'(t)=-K_+x(t)y(t)+K_-z(t)\\
        y'(t)=-K_+x(t)y(t)+K_-z(t)\\
        z'(t)=K_+x(t)y(t)-K_-z(t)
    \end{cases}
\end{equation}

To find the evolution of $x(t)$ again this time, we will have to find a conservation law that links $x$ with $y$ and one that links $x$ with $z$. To do this, it will be sufficient to subtract the second equation from the first and then the third from the first:

\begin{equation*}
    \begin{cases}
        x'(t)-y'(t)=0\\
        x'(t)+z'(t)=0
    \end{cases}
    \Longrightarrow
    \begin{cases}
        x(t)-y(t)=c_1\\
        x(t)+z(t)=c_2
    \end{cases}
    \Longrightarrow
    \begin{cases}
        y(t)=-\left[c_1-x(t)\right]\\
        z(t)=c_2-x(t)
    \end{cases}
\end{equation*}

Substituting in the differential equation relative to $x(t)$:

\begin{equation}
    \boxed{x'(t)=K_+x(t)\left[c_1-x(t)\right]+K_-\left[c_2-x(t)\right]}
\end{equation}

This is known as the **non-homogeneous logistic equation**, since the known term $K_-c_2$ is also added with respect to the one seen in irreversible systems. To solve this equation analytically, we must make the terms with respect to $x(t)$ explicit and make sure to eliminate the known term to reduce to the solution seen previously (equation (eq:odes-evoluzione-x)):

\begin{equation*}
    x'(t)=-K_+x(t)^2+\left[K_+c_1-K_-\right]x(t)+K_-c_2
\end{equation*}

Let's perform the substitution

\begin{equation*}
    \alpha(t)=x(t)+A \ \Longrightarrow \ \frac{d\alpha}{dt}=\frac{dx}{dt}
\end{equation*}

obtaining:

\begin{align*}
    \frac{d\alpha}{dt}&=-K_+[\alpha+A]^2+\left[K_+c_1-K_-\right][\alpha+A]+K_-c_2=\\
    &=-K_+\alpha^2-2K_+A\alpha\green{-K_+A^2}+K_+c_1\alpha\green{+K_+c_1A}-K_-\alpha\green{-K_-A+K_-c_2}
\end{align*}

where the terms independent of $\alpha$ have been highlighted in green, which we want to eliminate. To do this, let's solve the equation in $A$ so as to cancel these contributions:

\begin{equation*}
    -K_+A^2+\left(K_+c_1-K_-\right)A+K_-c_2=0 \ \Rightarrow
\end{equation*}

\begin{equation*}
    \Rightarrow \ A=\frac{-K_+c_1+K_-\pm\sqrt{\left(K_+c_1-K_-\right)^2+4K_+K_-c_2}}{-2K_+}
\end{equation*}

Imposing

\begin{equation}
    B=\sqrt{\left(K_+c_1-K_-\right)^2+4K_+K_-c_2}
\end{equation}

we find the following value of $A$:

\begin{equation}
    A=\frac{K_+c_1-K_-\mp B}{2K_+}
\end{equation}

Returning to the differential equation in $\alpha$, what remains is

\begin{align*}
    \frac{d\alpha}{dt}&=-K_+\alpha^2+\left[K_+\left(c_1-2A\right)-K_-\right]\alpha\\
    &=-K_+\alpha^2+\left[K_+\left(c_1-\cancel{2}\frac{K_+c_1-K_-\mp B}{\cancel{2}K_+}\right)-K_-\right]\alpha=\\
    &=-K_+\alpha^2+\left[\cancel{k_+c_1}-\cancel{k_+c_1}+\cancel{K_-}\pm B-\cancel{K_-}\right]\alpha=K_+\alpha\left(\pm B-\alpha\right)
\end{align*}

Having reduced to (eq:odes-equazione-differenziale-x) we know that the solutions (equation (eq:odes-evoluzione-x)) are

\begin{equation*}
    \alpha(t)=\frac{\pm B\alpha(0)e^{\pm K_+Bt}}{\pm B+\alpha(0)\left[e^{\pm K_+Bt}-1\right]}
\end{equation*}

Remembering that $\alpha(t)=x(t)+A$ the final result is

\begin{equation}
\boxed{x(t)=\frac{\pm B\,(x_0+A)\,e^{\pm K_+Bt}}{\pm B+(x_0+A)\left(e^{\pm K_+Bt}-1\right)}-A}
\end{equation}

where $A$ and $B$ are given by equations (eq:odes-reversibile-A) and (eq:odes-reversibile-B).

Knowing the relationship between $x(t)$ and $z(t)$, we therefore also know that

\begin{equation}
\boxed{z(t)=z_0+x_0-\frac{\pm B\left(x_0+A\right)e^{\pm K_+Bt}}{\pm B+\left(x_0+A\right)\left(e^{\pm K_+Bt}-1\right)}+A}
\end{equation}

### Stationary concentrations

An interesting aspect of reversible reactions is to calculate what the concentrations will be in equilibrium conditions, that is, when

\begin{equation*}
    \frac{dx}{dt}=\frac{dy}{dt}=\frac{dz}{dt}=0
\end{equation*}

By *stationarity* we do not mean that no more reaction occurs, but that the fluctuations in concentrations are substantially negligible.

From the system of equations (eq:odes-sistemi-reversibili-equazioni) we will have

\begin{equation}
    K_+x\left(\bar{t}\right)y\left(\bar{t}\right)=K_-z\left(\bar{t}\right) \ \Longrightarrow \ \frac{x\left(\bar{t}\right)y\left(\bar{t}\right)}{z\left(\bar{t}\right)}=\frac{K_-}{K_+}
\end{equation}

Considering also the conservation laws that link $x(t)$, $y(t)$ and $z(t)$ we can find the solutions:

\begin{equation*}
    \begin{cases}
        \frac{\bar{x}\bar{y}}{\bar{z}}=\frac{K_-}{K_+}\\
        \bar{y}=y_0-x_0+\bar{x}\\
        \bar{z}=z_0+x_0-\bar{x}
    \end{cases}
\end{equation*}

having used for simplicity the notation $\bar{x}=x\left(\bar{t}\right)$, $\bar{y}=y\left(\bar{t}\right)$, $\bar{z}=z\left(\bar{t}\right)$.

A second-degree equation will come out:

\begin{equation*}
        \frac{\bar{x}\left(y_0-x_0+\bar{x}\right)}{z_0+x_0-\bar{x}}=\frac{K_-}{K_+} \ \iff \ K_+\bar{x}^2+\left[K_+\left(y_0-x_0\right)+K_-\right]\bar{x}-K_-(x_0+z_0)=0
\end{equation*}

whose solutions are

\begin{equation*}
\bar{x}=\frac{K_+\left(x_0-y_0\right)-K_-\pm\sqrt{\left[K_+\left(y_0-x_0\right)+K_-\right]^2+4K_+K_-\left(x_0+z_0\right)}}{2K_+}
\end{equation*}

## The generalized law of mass action

So far we have only considered cases in which the quantities of reactants and products were all in a $1:1$ ratio with each other. To break this constraint we must introduce the stoichiometric coefficients:

\begin{equation}
    n_XX + n_YY \ \genfrac{}{}{-3pt}{}{\xrightarrow{K_+}}{\xleftarrow[K_-]{}} \ n_ZZ
\end{equation}

To find the solution in this case we can start from a real case, that of the **Haber process**, through which it is possible to synthesize ammonia from nitrogen and hydrogen:

\begin{equation*}
    N_2 + 3H_2 \ \genfrac{}{}{-3pt}{}{\xrightarrow{K_+}}{\xleftarrow[K_-]{}} \ 2NH_3
\end{equation*}

The equation will therefore be

\begin{equation*}
    X+3Y \ \genfrac{}{}{-3pt}{}{\xrightarrow{K_+}}{\xleftarrow[K_-]{}} \ 2Z
\end{equation*}

According to the law of mass action, we will have to look at the product of the individual concentrations to obtain the variation of $x$, $y$ and $z$. Let's consider, for example, the substance $X$: the written reaction tells us that for each molecule $X$ the reaction with three molecules $Y$ is necessary, so the product to consider will be $xy^3$; similarly, the decay of two molecules $Z$ is necessary, so the variation of $x$ will be proportional to $z^2$. Obviously this reasoning is valid for a single molecule, so in the case of the substance $Y$ the same relationship must contain a factor of two, since each reaction produces two molecules.

Extending this reasoning we will obtain the following set of equations:

\begin{equation*}
    \begin{cases}
        x'(t)=-K_+x(t)y(t)^3+K_-z(t)^2\\
        y'(t)=-3K_+x(t)y(t)^3+3K_-z(t)^2\\
        z'(t)=2K_+x(t)y(t)^3-2K_-z(t)^2
    \end{cases}
\end{equation*}

We thus find a more general form of the law of mass action, extended to any ratio of quantities between reactants and products.

::: {.definition}
    In a first approximation, the **rate** of any **chemical reaction** is proportional to the **product of the concentrations** of the reacting substances, each raised to its respective stoichiometric coefficient which must also be included in the rate with the appropriate sign.

:::

At this point we can write the generic reversible reaction together with the relative differential equations:

\begin{equation}
    aX+bY \ \genfrac{}{}{-3pt}{}{\xrightarrow{K_+}}{\xleftarrow[K_-]{}} \ cZ+dW \ \iff \
    \begin{cases}
        x'(t)=a\left(-K_+x^ay^b+K_-z^cw^d\right)\\
        y'(t)=b\left(-K_+x^ay^b+K_-z^cw^d\right)\\
        z'(t)=c\left(K_+x^ay^b-K_-z^cw^d\right)\\
        w'(t)=d\left(K_+x^ay^b-K_-z^cw^d\right)
    \end{cases}
\end{equation}

At steady state we will have

\begin{equation}
    \boxed{\frac{\bar{x}^a\bar{y}^b}{\bar{z}^c\bar{w}^d}=\frac{K_-}{K_+}}
\end{equation}

The conservation laws can be found as we have already done previously, that is, by adding and subtracting the differential equations so as to cancel the second member. The conservation laws with respect to $x(t)$, for example, are given by

\begin{equation*}
    \begin{cases}
        bx'(t)-ay'(t)=0\\
        cx'(t)-az'(t)=0\\
        dx'(t)-aw'(t)=0
    \end{cases}
    \Longrightarrow
    \begin{cases}
        bx-ay=bx_0-ay_0\\
        cx-az=cx_0-az_0\\
        dx-aw=dx_0-aw_0
    \end{cases}
\end{equation*}

\begin{equation}
    \boxed{\begin{cases}
        y=\frac{1}{a}\left[b\left(x-x_0\right)+ay_0\right]\\
        z=\frac{1}{a}\left[c\left(x-x_0\right)+az_0\right]\\
        w=\frac{1}{a}\left[d\left(x-x_0\right)+aw_0\right]
    \end{cases}}
\end{equation}

## General chemical reactions

Now we have all the tools we need to study any set of chemical reactions related to a system.

Let's consider a total of $m$ substances $X_1,\dots,X_m$ involved in a total of $n$ reactions. The system as a whole will be described by

\begin{equation}
    \sum_{i=1}^ma_{ij}X_i \ \genfrac{}{}{-3pt}{}{\xrightarrow{K_j^+}}{\xleftarrow[K_j^-]{}} \ \sum_{i=1}^mb_{ij}X_i   j=1,2,\dots,n
\end{equation}

where $a_{ij}$ and $b_{ij}$ are the stoichiometric coefficients relative to the $i$-th substance in the $j$-th reaction. If this substance is absent as a reactant then $a_{ij}=0$, and similarly if it is absent as a product then $b_{ij}=0$.

Let's now try to write the evolutions of the concentrations through differential equations. Considering for now only the first reaction ($j=1$), we must take into account the difference between the stoichiometric coefficients relative to the substance as a product ($b_{i1}$) and to the substance as a reactant ($a_{i1}$), which must be factored in front of everything; for the rest we will have to consider two contributions:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item **Production**, in which we will have to multiply $K_+$ by the product of the various concentrations $x_i$, each raised to their respective stoichiometric coefficients $a_{i1}$
    \item **Decay**, in which we will have to multiply $K_-$ by the product of the various concentrations $x_i$, each raised to their respective stoichiometric coefficients $b_{i1}$
\end{enumerate}

Ultimately, we obtain

\begin{equation*}
    \left(b_{i1}-a_{i1}\right)\left(K_1^+\prod_{i=1}^mx_i^{a_{i1}}-K_1^-\prod_{i=1}^mx_i^{b_{i1}}\right)
\end{equation*}

We will have to consider the contributions from all the $n$ reactions, so we will finally obtain

\begin{equation}
    \boxed{\frac{dx_i}{dt}=\sum_{j=1}^n\left(b_{ij}-a_{ij}\right)\left(K_j^+\prod_{i=1}^mx_i^{a_{ij}}-K_j^-\prod_{i=j}^mx_i^{b_{ij}}\right)}
\end{equation}

We can write all the equations in compact form using vector notation, defining $\vec{x}=\left(x_1,x_2,\dots,x_m\right)$, the stoichiometric matrix $S$ whose elements $S_{ij}$ are given by $b_{ij}-a_{ij}$ and the function $r_j\left(\vec{x}\right)$ such that

\begin{equation*}
    r_j\left(\vec{x}\right)=K_j^+\prod_{i=1}^mx_i^{a_{ij}}-K_j^-\prod_{i=j}^mx_i^{b_{ij}}
\end{equation*}

We will write:

\begin{equation}
    \boxed{\frac{d\vec{x}}{dt}=S\vec{r}\left(\vec{x}\right)}
\end{equation}

### Conservation laws

A generic linear conservation law is defined by a constant vector $\vec{c}$ such that

\begin{equation}
    \vec{c}^{\ T}\cdot\frac{d\vec{x}}{dt}=0
\end{equation}

Substituting equation (eq:odes-reazioni-chimiche-generali-forma-compatta):

\begin{equation*}
    \vec{c}^{\ T}\cdot S\vec{r}\left(\vec{x}\right)=0
\end{equation*}

If the conservation law is valid, the relationship written above must be valid regardless of the choice of $\vec{r}\left(\vec{x}\right)$, so we will have to set $\vec{c}^{\ T}S$ to zero, or equivalently:

\begin{equation}
    \boxed{S^T\vec{c}=\vec{0}}
\end{equation}

Let's remember that the set of vectors $\vec{x}_i$ such that $A\vec{x}_i=\vec{0}$ constitutes the *kernel* of the matrix $A$. Similarly, the vectors $\vec{c}$ such that $S^T\vec{c}=\vec{0}$ constitute the kernel of $S^T$. We can therefore state that

::: {.definition}
    The **coefficients** relative to the **linear conservation laws** belong to the **kernel** of the **transposed stoichiometric matrix**

:::

From this it also follows that the number of *independent* linear conservation laws is equal to the dimension of the kernel of $S^T$.

To understand what has been written, we can start from a very simple example, such as the trivial irreversible relation

\begin{equation*}
    X+Y \ \longrightarrow \ Z
\end{equation*}

The stoichiometric matrix is given by

\begin{equation*}
    S=
    \begin{pmatrix}
        0-1\\
        0-1\\
        1-0
    \end{pmatrix}
    =
    \begin{pmatrix}
        -1\\
        -1\\
        1
    \end{pmatrix}
\end{equation*}

The kernel of $S^T$ is

\begin{equation*}
    S^T\vec{c}=
    \begin{pmatrix}
        -1 & -1 & 1
    \end{pmatrix}
    \begin{pmatrix}
        c_1\\
        c_2\\
        c_3
    \end{pmatrix}
    =-c_1-c_2+c_3=0
\end{equation*}

One possibility is that

\begin{equation*}
    c_3=c_1+c_2 \ \Longrightarrow \ \vec{c}=
    \begin{pmatrix}
        c_1\\
        c_2\\
        c_1+c_2
    \end{pmatrix}
    =
    c_1
    \begin{pmatrix}
        1\\
        0\\
        1
    \end{pmatrix}
    +c_2
    \begin{pmatrix}
        0\\
        1\\
        1
    \end{pmatrix}
\end{equation*}

By arbitrarily setting $c_1=c_2=1$ we will find the conservation laws

\begin{equation*}
    \begin{cases}
        x+z=0\\
        y+z=0
    \end{cases}
\end{equation*}

Another possibility is the following:

\begin{equation*}
    c_1=-c_2+c_3 \ \Longrightarrow \ \vec{c}=
    \begin{pmatrix}
        -c_2+c_3\\
        c_2\\
        c_3
    \end{pmatrix}
    =c_2
    \begin{pmatrix}
        -1\\
        1\\
        0
    \end{pmatrix}
    +c_3
    \begin{pmatrix}
        1\\
        0\\
        1
    \end{pmatrix}
\end{equation*}

In this case the conservation laws are

\begin{equation*}
    \begin{cases}
        -x+y=0\\
        x+z=0
    \end{cases}
\end{equation*}

which are the same ones we used in paragraph (sec:evoluzione-delle-concentrazioni).

## Known models

As anticipated, all the reasoning done can be extended to dynamics completely different from that of actual chemical reactions, such as biological ecosystems, epidemic models, physiological mechanisms and so on. Below we will see some practical examples in which systems of ordinary differential equations come into play.\footnote{All the various examples proposed were formally assigned by the professor as exercises, so they were only introduced together with the relative reactions; for this reason I do not assure any certainty about the veracity of the results obtained.}

### Lotka-Volterra model

A first system to study is the one related to the **predator-prey** dynamics. Indicating with $X$ the prey and with $Y$ the predator, we can consider three dynamics:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item **Reproduction of a prey**, which allows the multiplication of this species
    \item **Encounter between prey and predator**, which involves the death of the prey and the consequent reproduction of the predator who survives precisely thanks to the nourishment obtained from the prey
    \item **Death of the predator**, whether due to hardship, disease or simple old age
\end{enumerate}

The system of reactions is therefore the following:

\begin{equation}
    \begin{cases}
        X \ \xrightarrow{K_1} \ 2X\\
        X+Y \ \xrightarrow{K_2} \ 2Y\\
        Y \ \xrightarrow{K_3} \ \varnothing
    \end{cases}
\end{equation}

It is important to understand that in this case these are not real *reactions* as they are understood in chemistry, since there is not necessarily an interaction between two different species as we observe in the first and third cases. We could in some way speak of an \q{interaction} between the individual and the surrounding environment, such as the prey and the food useful for its survival and therefore its subsequent reproduction, but the food in question is not a real element involved in the dynamics of evolution of the species, but only something that is intrinsically part of the system and allows it to exist in the way we observe it.

In any case, the law of mass action can also be extended to cases like these; the differential equations that indicate the variations of the species, in fact, will be given by

\begin{equation}
    \begin{cases}
        x'(t)=K_1x(t)-K_2x(t)y(t)\\
        y'(t)=K_2x(t)y(t)-K_3y(t)
    \end{cases}
    \Longrightarrow
    \boxed{\begin{cases}
        x'(t)=\left[K_1-K_2y(t)\right]x(t)\\
        y'(t)=\left[K_2x(t)-K_3\right]y(t)
    \end{cases}}
\end{equation}

These equations are known in the literature as the **Lotka-Volterra equations**.

At steady state, excluding the trivial solution $\left(\bar{x},\bar{y}\right)=(0,0)$, it results:

\begin{equation*}
    \begin{cases}
        x'(t)=0\\
        y'(t)=0
    \end{cases}
    \iff
    \begin{cases}
        K_1-K_2\bar{y}=0\\
        K_2\bar{x}-K_3=0
    \end{cases}
    \Longrightarrow
    \begin{cases}
        \bar{y}=\frac{K_1}{K_2}\\
        \bar{x}=\frac{K_3}{K_2}
    \end{cases}
\end{equation*}

For the dynamics of a physical system of this type, it is useful to calculate the value assumed by the eigenvalue relative to the Jacobian at the stationary point; the possibilities are shown to be five:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item **Real and positive** eigenvalue: every deviation is amplified over time, leading to an **instability**
    \item **Real and negative** eigenvalue: the system converges towards equilibrium, reaching an **asymptotic stability**
    \item **Complex eigenvalue with positive real part**: the system proceeds to a departure from equilibrium undergoing oscillations, thus following an **unstable spiral**
    \item **Complex eigenvalue with negative real part**: the system approaches equilibrium with damped oscillations, forming a **stable spiral**
    \item **Pure imaginary** eigenvalue: a stable closed orbit is formed around the equilibrium point without convergence or divergence, thus reaching **neutral stability**
\end{enumerate}

Let's try to evaluate the case of the predator-prey system by calculating the Jacobian of the system:

\begin{equation*}
    J=
    \begin{pmatrix}
        \frac{\partial x'}{\partial x} & \frac{\partial x'}{\partial y}\\
        \frac{\partial y'}{\partial x} & \frac{\partial y'}{\partial y}
    \end{pmatrix}
    =
    \begin{pmatrix}
        K_1-K_2y & -K_2x\\
        K_2y & K_2x-K_3
    \end{pmatrix}
\end{equation*}

At the stationary point the Jacobian becomes

\begin{equation*}
    J\left(\bar{x},\bar{y}\right)=
    \begin{pmatrix}
        K_1-K_2\cdot\frac{K_1}{K_2} & -K_2\cdot\frac{K_3}{K_2}\\
        K_2\cdot\frac{K_1}{K_2} & K_2\cdot\frac{K_3}{K_2}-K_3
    \end{pmatrix}
    =
    \begin{pmatrix}
        0 & -K_3\\
        K_1 & 0
    \end{pmatrix}
\end{equation*}

The eigenvalues of the matrix are

\begin{equation*}
    \det\left(J\left(\bar{x},\bar{y}\right)-\lambda I\right)=
    \begin{pmatrix}
        -\lambda & -K_3\\
        K_1 & -\lambda
    \end{pmatrix}
    =\lambda^2+K_1K_3=0 \ \Longrightarrow \ \lambda=\pm i\sqrt{K_1K_3}
\end{equation*}

We are therefore in the last case, in which the system neither converges nor diverges but tends to stabilize in an orbit around the point defined by the kinetic constants $K_1$, $K_2$ and $K_3$.

In this case, calculating the conservation law is not trivial; the quantity that is shown to be conserved is the **Lyapunov function**:

\begin{equation*}
    V(x,y)=K_2(x+y)-K_3\ln x-K_1\ln y
\end{equation*}

By deriving, in fact, we obtain

\begin{align*}
    \frac{dV}{dt}&=\frac{\partial V}{\partial x}\frac{\partial x}{\partial t}+\frac{\partial V}{\partial y}\frac{\partial y}{\partial t}=\\
    &=\left(K_2-\frac{K_3}{x}\right)\left(K_1-K_2y\right)x+\left(K_2-\frac{K_1}{y}\right)\left(K_2x-K_3\right)y=\\
    &=\left(K_2x-K_3\right)\left(K_1-K_2y\right)+\left(K_2y-K_1\right)\left(K_2x-K_3\right)=\\
    &=\left(K_2x-K_3\right)\left(\cancel{K_1}-\cancel{K_2y}+\cancel{K_2y}-\cancel{K_1}\right)=0
\end{align*}

Let's always keep in mind that this model can hardly be applied in real contexts, because it ignores many aspects that are very important in an ecosystem that we know to be - in general - very delicate. A trivial example is the number of individuals of a species: according to this model, in fact, in principle a species can have a population of any size, but in reality we know that below a certain threshold a species becomes at serious risk of extinction. The Lotka-Volterra model is however useful as a starting point for developing more elaborate models that can describe real systems.

### Brusselator

Another system associated with chaotic behaviors with limit cycles is the **brusselator** model, described by the following reactions:

\begin{equation}
    \begin{cases}
        A \ \xrightarrow{K_1} \ X\\
        B+X \ \xrightarrow{K_2} \ Y+D\\
        2X+Y \ \xrightarrow{K_3} \ 3X\\
        X \ \xrightarrow{K_4} \ E\\
    \end{cases}
\end{equation}

This model was introduced to study **autocatalytic reactions**, that is, those reactions that have as a product a catalyst capable of accelerating the same. It was conceived by the Nobel Prize winner for chemistry Ilya Prigogine, and was later studied in depth due to its chaotic dynamic characteristics and oscillatory behaviors.

The quantities $A$, $B$, $D$ and $E$ are considered constant. The remaining concentrations will follow the system of differential equations constructed as we know:\footnote{With an abuse of notation we will indicate with $A$ and $B$ both the substances and their respective concentrations.}

\begin{equation*}
    \begin{cases}
        x'(t)=K_1A-K_2Bx(t)+K_3x(t)^2y(t)-K_4x(t)\\
        y'(t)=K_2Bx(t)-K_3x(t)^2y(t)
    \end{cases}
\end{equation*}

which, by ordering the terms, becomes

\begin{equation}
    \boxed{\begin{cases}
        x'(t)=K_1A-\left[K_2B-K_3x(t)y(t)+K_4\right]x(t)\\
        y'(t)=\left[K_2B-K_3x(t)y(t)\right]x(t)
    \end{cases}}
\end{equation}

Excluding the obvious solution for the equilibrium that would be had with $x(t)=0$, as the only other possibility remains

\begin{equation*}
    K_2B-K_3\bar{x}\bar{y}=0 \ \iff \ \bar{y}=\frac{K_2B}{K_3\bar{x}}
\end{equation*}

in order to cancel $y'(t)$. Substituting in the differential equation relative to $x(t)$ results:

\begin{equation*}
    K_1A-\left(K_2B-\cancel{K_3}\bar{x}\frac{K_2B}{\cancel{K_3}\bar{x}}+K_4\right)\bar{x}=0 \ \iff \ K_1A\cancel{-K_2B\bar{x}}\cancel{+K_2B\bar{x}}-K_3\bar{x}=0
\end{equation*}

The stationary point will therefore be

\begin{equation}
    \begin{cases}
        \bar{x}=\frac{K_1}{K_4}A\\
        \bar{y}=\frac{K_2K_4}{K_1K_3}\frac{B}{A}
    \end{cases}
\end{equation}

### Michaelis-Menten kinetics

Moving to the biological field, we can consider an enzymatic reaction, in which an enzyme $E$ binds to a substrate $S$ forming what is called an *intermediate complex* $ES$, which can subsequently release the same enzyme together with the synthesized protein $P$:\footnote{Actually, from what I have found in the literature, the reaction $C_-$ should not exist. I report it anyway as the complete reaction was written this way by the professor.}

\begin{equation}
    E+S \ \genfrac{}{}{-3pt}{}{\xrightarrow{K_+}}{\xleftarrow[K_-]{}} \ ES \ \genfrac{}{}{-3pt}{}{\xrightarrow{C_+}}{\xleftarrow[C_-]{}} \ E+P
\end{equation}

This model is called **Michaelis-Menten kinetics** because of the two chemists who first proposed it in 1913. We can rewrite it for simplicity as

\begin{equation*}
    X+Y \ \genfrac{}{}{-3pt}{}{\xrightarrow{K_+}}{\xleftarrow[K_-]{}} \ Z \ \genfrac{}{}{-3pt}{}{\xrightarrow{C_+}}{\xleftarrow[C_-]{}} \ X+W
\end{equation*}

We thus find the following equations:

\begin{equation*}
    \begin{cases}
        x'(t)=-K_+xy+K_-z+C_+z-C_-xw\\
        y'(t)=-K_+xy+K_-z\\
        z'(t)=K_+xy-K_-z-C_+z+C_-xw\\
        w'(t)=C_+z-C_-xw
    \end{cases}
\end{equation*}

which ordered become

\begin{equation}
    \boxed{\begin{cases}
        x'(t)=-\left(K_+y+C_-w\right)x+\left(K_-+C_+\right)z\\
        y'(t)=-K_+xy+K_-z\\
        z'(t)=\left(K_+y+C_-w\right)x-\left(K_-+C_+\right)z\\
        w'(t)=-C_-xw+C_+z
    \end{cases}}
\end{equation}

At equilibrium, the rate of formation of the enzyme-substrate complex must equal the rate of its decomposition, so $z$ must not change:

\begin{equation*}
    z'(t)=0 \ \iff \ z=\frac{K_+y+C_-w}{K_-+C_+}x
\end{equation*}

Furthermore, from the equations we note that

\begin{equation*}
    x'(t)-z'(t)=0 \ \Longrightarrow \ x-z=x_0-z_0 \ \iff \ x=z+x_0-z_0
\end{equation*}

Let's substitute:

\begin{equation*}
    z=\frac{K_+y+C_-w}{K_-+C_+}\left(z+x_0-z_0\right)
\end{equation*}

Let's calculate $z$:

\begin{equation*}
    \left(1-\frac{K_+y+C_-w}{K_-+C_+}\right)z=\frac{K_+y+C_-w}{K_-+C_+}\left(x_0-z_0\right)
\end{equation*}

\begin{equation*}
    \frac{K_-+C_+-K_+y-C_-w}{K_-+C_+}z=\frac{K_+y+C_-w}{K_-+C_+}\left(x_0-z_0\right)
\end{equation*}

\begin{equation*}
    z=\frac{K_+y+C_-w}{K_-+C_+-K_+y-C_-w}\left(x_0-z_0\right)
\end{equation*}

Dividing the numerator and denominator by $K_+y+C_-w$:

\begin{equation*}
    z=\frac{1}{\frac{K_-+C_+}{K_+y+C_-w}-1}\left(x_0-z_0\right)
\end{equation*}

Indicating the fraction in the denominator with $K(y,w)$ we can write:

\begin{equation}
    \boxed{z=\frac{1}{K(y,w)-1}\left(x_0-z_0\right)}
\end{equation}

We have thus managed to find a relationship that links the complex to the concentrations of the substrate and the protein.\footnote{As anticipated, the solution is different from that obtained in the literature, since in that case $C_-$ is absent and this greatly simplifies the formula. In the literature, a constant is also defined, called the *Michaelis-Menten constant*, which expresses an affinity between the enzyme and the substrate, which I was not able to derive for the same reason.}

### Kinetics of gene expression

Still in the biological field, we find another model constructed through reaction: gene expression. This occurs through the *transcription* of genes into mRNA, which is then *translated* into a protein; both the mRNA and the protein then decay.

\begin{equation}
    \begin{cases}
        G \ \xrightarrow{K_c} \ mRNA\\
        mRNA \ \xrightarrow{K_d} \ mRNA + P\\
        mRNA \ \xrightarrow{\delta_m} \ \varnothing\\
        P \ \xrightarrow{\delta_p} \ \varnothing
    \end{cases}
\end{equation}

The equations will be\footnote{Again, with an abuse of notation, we indicate with $G$ both the substance (the genes, in this case) and the concentration.}

\begin{equation}
    \begin{cases}
        m'(t)=K_cG\cancel{-K_dm}\cancel{+K_dm}-\delta_mm\\
        p'(t)=K_dm-\delta_pp
    \end{cases}
    \Longrightarrow
    \boxed{\begin{cases}
        m'(t)=K_cG-\delta_mm\\
        p'(t)=K_dm-\delta_pp
    \end{cases}}
\end{equation}

From this system it is easy to calculate the stationary point:

\begin{equation}
    \bar{m}=\frac{K_cG}{\delta_m} \ \Longrightarrow \ K_d\frac{K_cG}{\delta_m}-\delta_p\bar{p}=0 \ \Longrightarrow \
    \begin{cases}
        \bar{m}=\frac{K_cG}{\delta_m}\\
        \bar{p}=\frac{K_cK_dG}{\delta_m\delta_p}
    \end{cases}
\end{equation}

### SEIR model

There are various epidemiological models capable of adapting to dynamics involving viral infections. One of the best known and most used is the **SEIR**, which considers four types of individuals:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item **Susceptible**: subjects potentially at risk
    \item **Exposed**: individuals recently in contact with infected people
    \item **Infected**: vehicles of the infection
    \item **Recovered**: individuals recently free from the infection
\end{enumerate}

The dynamics that are considered in total are three:

\begin{itemize}
    \item A susceptible person is exposed to the infection
    \item An exposed person becomes infected
    \item An infected person recovers
\end{itemize}

which translated into formulas becomes

\begin{equation}
    \begin{cases}
        S+I \ \xrightarrow{\beta} \ E+I\\
        E \ \xrightarrow{\sigma} \ I\\
        I \ \xrightarrow{\gamma} \ R
    \end{cases}
\end{equation}

The differential equations that describe the system are

\begin{equation}
    \boxed{\begin{cases}
        s'(t)=-\beta s(t)i(t)\\
        e'(t)=\beta s(t)i(t)-\sigma e(t)\\
        i'(t)=\sigma e(t)-\gamma i(t)\\
        r'(t)=\gamma i(t)
    \end{cases}}
\end{equation}

The equilibrium point will trivially be given by $i=e=0$, that is, when there are no more infected or exposed people, in which case the disease has been eradicated and all individuals are therefore susceptible or recovered.

## Fluctuations

### Deterministic limit

Molecules (or individuals) are discrete entities that collide at random; when the required colliding set meets, the reaction occurs at some rate. The discrete nature of reacting entities therefore generates random events and hence stochasticity in the dynamics. The deterministic mass–action ODEs derived earlier are recovered only in the limit of large system size (many molecules), when fluctuations become negligible. In what follows we write the chemical Master Equation (CME) for a general well‑mixed system, state the combinatorial propensities, and perform the standard system‑size (Kramers–Moyal) expansion that connects the CME to the deterministic mass‑action ODEs and to a mesoscopic diffusion approximation.

Let $\vec{n}=(n_1,n_2,\dots,n_S)^\top$ be the vector of molecule counts for species $X_1,\dots,X_S$. Consider $R$ reactions. For reaction $r$ denote the reactant and product stoichiometric coefficients by $N_{ir}$ and $M_{ir}$ respectively, and define the stoichiometric change vector
$$
\vec{s}_r=(M_{1r}-N_{1r},\;M_{2r}-N_{2r},\;\dots,\;M_{Sr}-N_{Sr})^\top.
$$

If reaction $r$ fires in the forward direction it maps
$$
\vec{n}\xrightarrow{T_r^+(\vec{n})}\vec{n}+\vec{s}_r,
$$
whereas a backward (reverse) firing maps
$$
\vec{n}\xrightarrow{T_r^-(\vec{n})}\vec{n}-\vec{s}_r.
$$

For a well‑mixed system the combinatorial propensities (transition rates) take the form
\begin{align}
T_r^+(\vec{n}) &= k_r^+ \prod_{i=1}^S \binom{n_i}{N_{ir}}, \\
T_r^-(\vec{n}) &= k_r^- \prod_{i=1}^S \binom{n_i}{M_{ir}},
\end{align}
because for reaction $r$ to occur one must choose, for each species $i$, an unordered set of $N_{ir}$ reactant molecules out of the $n_i$ present. These $T_r^\pm$ are also called propensities.

The chemical Master Equation for the probability $p(\vec{n},t)$ of being in state $\vec{n}$ at time $t$ is
$$
\partial_t p(\vec{n},t)=\sum_{r=1}^R\Big[\,T_r^+(\vec{n}-\vec{s}_r)\,p(\vec{n}-\vec{s}_r,t)-T_r^+(\vec{n})\,p(\vec{n},t)
+T_r^-(\vec{n}+\vec{s}_r)\,p(\vec{n}+\vec{s}_r,t)-T_r^-(\vec{n})\,p(\vec{n},t)\Big].
$$
This CME is an exact description of the stochastic well‑mixed model and can be simulated exactly using Gillespie's stochastic simulation algorithm.

To obtain the deterministic limit introduce a system‑size parameter $\Omega$ (for example the physical reaction volume). Define concentrations
$$
\vec{x}=\frac{\vec{n}}{\Omega},
$$
and assume molecule counts scale with $\Omega$ so that typical $n_i=O(\Omega)$ and the net reaction fluxes are extensive. For large $\Omega$ the combinatorial factors satisfy the leading‑order approximation
$$
\binom{\Omega x_i}{N_{ir}}\simeq\frac{(\Omega x_i)^{N_{ir}}}{N_{ir}!}\qquad(\Omega\to\infty),
$$
hence, with suitably scaled rate constants,
$$
T_r^\pm(\Omega\vec{x})\simeq \Omega\,v_r^\pm(\vec{x}),
$$
where the macroscopic per‑unit‑volume reaction rates are
\begin{align}
v_r^+(\vec{x}) &= k_r^+\prod_{i=1}^S\frac{x_i^{N_{ir}}}{N_{ir}!},\\
v_r^-(\vec{x}) &= k_r^-\prod_{i=1}^S\frac{x_i^{M_{ir}}}{M_{ir}!},
\end{align}
and the net macroscopic rate per unit volume is $v_r(\vec{x})\equiv v_r^+(\vec{x})-v_r^-(\vec{x})$.

Multiplying the CME by $\vec{n}$ and summing over all states gives the exact evolution for the mean counts:
$$
\frac{d}{dt}\langle\vec{n}\rangle=\sum_{r=1}^R\vec{s}_r\Big\langle T_r^+(\vec{n})-T_r^-(\vec{n})\Big\rangle.
$$
In concentrations this reads
$$
\frac{d}{dt}\langle\vec{x}\rangle=\sum_{r=1}^R\vec{s}_r\left[\frac{\langle T_r^+(\Omega\vec{x})\rangle}{\Omega}-\frac{\langle T_r^-(\Omega\vec{x})\rangle}{\Omega}\right].
$$

The right‑hand side above must be $O(1)$ as $\Omega\to\infty$ for a nontrivial macroscopic limit. Under the scaling stated earlier one finds
$$
\frac{\langle T_r^\pm(\Omega\vec{x})\rangle}{\Omega}\simeq v_r^\pm(\langle\vec{x}\rangle)+o(1),
$$
provided fluctuations are negligible. If correlations and higher cumulants vanish in the large‑$\Omega$ limit (mean‑field or moment‑closure approximation),
$$
\Big\langle\prod_i x_i^{N_{ir}}\Big\rangle \approx \prod_i\langle x_i\rangle^{N_{ir}},
$$
and we recover the deterministic mass–action ODEs (the law of large numbers limit):
$$
\frac{d}{dt}x_i=\sum_{r=1}^R s_{ir}\,v_r(\vec{x}),\qquad i=1,\dots,S.
$$
These are the same mass–action ODEs written earlier. The approximation is valid when molecule numbers are large, the system is well mixed, and correlations are negligible.

A more refined approximation is obtained by the Kramers–Moyal (system‑size) expansion of the CME to second order. This yields a chemical Fokker–Planck equation (diffusive approximation) for the probability density of $\vec{x}$:
$$
\partial_t p(\vec{x},t)=-\sum_{i=1}^S\frac{\partial}{\partial x_i}\big[A_i(\vec{x})\,p\big]+\frac{1}{2}\sum_{i,j=1}^S\frac{\partial^2}{\partial x_i\partial x_j}\big[B_{ij}(\vec{x})\,p\big],
$$
with drift and diffusion coefficients
\begin{align}
A_i(\vec{x}) &= \sum_{r=1}^R s_{ir}\,v_r(\vec{x}), \\
B_{ij}(\vec{x}) &= \frac{1}{\Omega}\sum_{r=1}^R s_{ir}s_{jr}\,\big(v_r^+(\vec{x})+v_r^-(\vec{x})\big).
\end{align}
The drift equals the deterministic mass‑action velocity and the diffusion is of order $1/\Omega$.

The Fokker–Planck equation corresponds (not uniquely) to the Chemical Langevin Equation (CLE), which provides a mesoscopic stochastic differential equation approximation:
$$
dx_i=\sum_{r=1}^R s_{ir}\,v_r(\vec{x})\,dt+\frac{1}{\sqrt{\Omega}}\sum_{r=1}^R s_{ir}\sqrt{v_r^+(\vec{x})+v_r^-(\vec{x})}\;dW_r(t),
$$
where the $dW_r$ are independent standard Wiener increments (one noise per reaction channel). The CLE shows that fluctuations around the deterministic trajectory scale as $\Omega^{-1/2}$.

Remarks and conditions of validity:
\begin{itemize}
\item The deterministic ODEs are the law‑of‑large‑numbers limit and hold when molecule numbers are large, the system is well mixed, and correlations are negligible.
\item The CLE / Fokker–Planck (diffusive) approximation requires that reaction jump sizes $\vec{s}_r$ are small compared with typical molecule numbers and that propensities are sufficiently large so the central‑limit scaling applies.
\item If some species have small counts (rare species) or if some reactions are very infrequent, the diffusion approximation breaks down and the full CME (simulated e.g. by Gillespie) should be used.
\end{itemize}

In summary, starting from the discrete stochastic description (CME) with combinatorial propensities one recovers the deterministic mass‑action ODEs by (i) introducing concentrations, (ii) imposing the appropriate scaling of rate constants with system size, and (iii) applying a mean‑field closure. The leading stochastic corrections are captured by the Fokker–Planck equation and the CLE, which quantify fluctuations of order $\Omega^{-1/2}$ about the deterministic trajectory.

\section*{Examples}

\begin{enumerate}
\item **Single species birth–coagulation:** $A \xrightleftharpoons[k^-]{k^+} 2A$.

Microscopic transition rates for count $n$ (system size $\Omega$) are
\begin{align*}
T^+(n)&=k^+(\Omega)\binom{n}{1}=k^+(\Omega)n, \\
T^-(n)&=k^-(\Omega)\binom{n}{2}=k^-(\Omega)\tfrac{n(n-1)}{2}.
\end{align*}
With the usual scaling $x=n/\Omega$ and choosing scalings so that the macroscopic per-volume rates are finite, one obtains the macroscopic rates
\begin{align*}
v^+(x)&=k^+ x, \\
v^-(x)&=k^- x^2.
\end{align*}

Mean-field (deterministic) equation (mean‑field closure $\langle x^2\rangle\simeq\langle x\rangle^2$):
$$
\dot x = v^+(x)-v^-(x)=k^+ x - k^- x^2.
$$
Chemical master equation for $p(n,t)$:
\begin{align*}
\dot p(n)&=T^+(n-1)p(n-1)-T^+(n)p(n)\\
&\quad+T^-(n+1)p(n+1)-T^-(n)p(n).
\end{align*}
Diffusive (Fokker–Planck / CLE) approximation: the scalar diffusion coefficient is
$$
B(x)=\frac{v^+(x)+v^-(x)}{\Omega}=\frac{k^+ x + k^- x^2}{\Omega}.
$$
The corresponding Chemical Langevin Equation (CLE) is
$$
dx =\bigl(v^+(x)-v^-(x)\bigr)\,dt + \frac{1}{\sqrt{\Omega}}\bigl(\sqrt{v^+(x)}\,dW_1 - \sqrt{v^-(x)}\,dW_2\bigr),
$$
where $W_1,W_2$ are independent standard Wiener processes (one noise per reaction channel). This CLE is a good approximation when molecule numbers are large and propensities are not too small.

\item **Three‑species multi‑molecule reaction:** $A + 3B \xrightleftharpoons[k^-]{k^+} 2C$.

Stoichiometric vectors (reactants, products) and net change:
$$
N=(1,3,0),\quad M=(0,0,2),\quad J=M-N=(-1,-3,2)^\top.
$$
Microscopic propensities (combinatorial form):
\begin{align*}
T^+(\mathbf{n})&=k^+(\Omega)\binom{n_A}{1}\binom{n_B}{3}=k^+(\Omega)\tfrac{n_A n_B(n_B-1)(n_B-2)}{6}, \\
T^-(\mathbf{n})&=k^-(\Omega)\binom{n_C}{2}=k^-(\Omega)\tfrac{n_C(n_C-1)}{2}.
\end{align*}
With standard scaling the macroscopic per‑volume rates take the form (after absorbing factorials into the macroscopic rate constants):
\begin{align*}
v^+(\mathbf{x})&=k^+ x_A x_B^3, \\
v^-(\mathbf{x})&=k^- x_C^2.
\end{align*}
Deterministic mass–action ODEs:
$$
\dot{\mathbf{x}} = J\bigl(v^+(\mathbf{x})-v^-(\mathbf{x})\bigr)=(-1,-3,2)^\top\bigl(v^+-v^-\bigr),
$$
explicitly
\begin{align*}
\dot x_A&= -\bigl(k^+ x_A x_B^3 - k^- x_C^2\bigr),\\
\dot x_B&= -3\bigl(k^+ x_A x_B^3 - k^- x_C^2\bigr),\\
\dot x_C&= 2\bigl(k^+ x_A x_B^3 - k^- x_C^2\bigr).
\end{align*}

Diffusion matrix (Kramers–Moyal / CLE pre‑factor):
$$
B(\mathbf{x})=\frac{v^+(\mathbf{x})+v^-(\mathbf{x})}{\Omega}\;JJ^\top
=\frac{v^++v^-}{\Omega}\begin{pmatrix}1&3&-2\\3&9&-6\\-2&-6&4\end{pmatrix}.
$$
This matrix enters the Fokker–Planck equation and the CLE (one noise per reaction channel).

\item **Two‑species example (autocatalysis and conversion):**
Reactions
\begin{align*}
(1)\quad &X \xrightarrow{k_1} 2X, \\
(2)\quad &X+Y \xrightarrow{k_2} 2Y, \\
(3)\quad &Y \xrightarrow{k_3} \varnothing.
\end{align*}

Stoichiometric change vectors:
\begin{align*}
s_1&=(1,0)^\top,\\
s_2&=(-1,1)^\top,\\
s_3&=(0,-1)^\top.
\end{align*}
Macroscopic rates (mean‑field limit):
\[v_1=k_1 x, \quad v_2=k_2 x y, \quad v_3=k_3 y.\]
Mean‑field ODEs (mass–action):
$$
\begin{pmatrix}\dot x\\\dot y\end{pmatrix}= s_1 v_1 + s_2 v_2 + s_3 v_3 = \begin{pmatrix}k_1 x - k_2 x y\\ k_2 x y - k_3 y\end{pmatrix}.
$$
Diffusion (noise) matrix:
$$
B(\mathbf{x})=\frac{1}{\Omega}\Bigl[s_1s_1^\top v_1 + s_2s_2^\top v_2 + s_3s_3^\top v_3\Bigr]
=\frac{1}{\Omega}\begin{pmatrix}k_1 x + k_2 x y & -k_2 x y \\
-k_2 x y & k_2 x y + k_3 y\end{pmatrix}.
$$
The corresponding CLE approximation is
$$
\begin{pmatrix}dx\\dy\end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix}k_1 x - k_2 x y\\ k_2 x y - k_3 y\end{pmatrix}dt + \frac{1}{\sqrt{\Omega}}\sum_{r=1}^3 s_r\sqrt{v_r(\mathbf{x})}\,dW_r(t),
$$
i.e.
$$
\begin{pmatrix}dx\\dy\end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix}k_1 x - k_2 x y\\ k_2 x y - k_3 y\end{pmatrix}dt + \frac{1}{\sqrt{\Omega}}\begin{pmatrix}\sqrt{k_1 x}\,dW_1 - \sqrt{k_2 x y}\,dW_2\\ \sqrt{k_2 x y}\,dW_2 - \sqrt{k_3 y}\,dW_3\end{pmatrix}.
$$
\end{enumerate}
