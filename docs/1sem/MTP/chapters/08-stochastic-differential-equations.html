10 Stochastic differential equations

We have already written a first stochastic differential equation in (eq:processi-stocastici-prima-equazione-differenziale-stocastica) when talking about the simulation of Brownian motion through a computer. The purpose now is to deepen this type of equation, trying to give a mathematical formalism that is not only linked to the simulation processes obtained through finite difference approximations.

10.1 Gaussian white noise

Let’s take up (eq:processi-stocastici-prima-equazione-differenziale-stocastica) again and try to write it in the form of a differential equation by dividing both sides by \(dt\):

\[\begin{equation} \frac{dx}{dt}=\mu+\sigma\frac{dB}{dt} \end{equation}\]

having replaced \(\sqrt{D}\) with a generic function of the diffusion coefficient, indicated with \(\sigma\).

The question at this point is: what does the derivative of \(B(t)\) correspond to? As we have already stated in section (sec:integrale-sui-cammini-di-wiener), in reference to the formal expression written in equation (eq:integrale-sui-cammini-espressione-formale-differenziale-dP), a stochastic realization of Brownian motion is not differentiable at any point. Nevertheless, we can try to give a form to the derivative of \(B(t)\) starting from its statistical properties.

Let’s start from the finite expression, defining the following quantity:

\[\begin{equation} \xi_{\Delta,i}=\frac{\Delta B_i}{\Delta t} \end{equation}\]

where \(\Delta B_i=B\left(t_i+\Delta t\right)-B\left(t_i\right)\).

Let’s remember that this difference follows a Gaussian distribution centered at zero, so the average value of the quantity just defined is:

\[\begin{equation} \left\langle\xi_{\Delta,i}\right\rangle=\left\langle\frac{\Delta B_i}{\Delta t}\right\rangle=\frac{\left\langle\Delta B_i\right\rangle}{\Delta t}=0 \end{equation}\]

Being identically equal to zero, this result will also hold in the limit for \(\Delta t\to0\).

Another fundamental aspect, to understand how successive instants of time are correlated, is the covariance. To calculate it, it is useful to consider a generic function \(f(t)\), which when discretized will assume the value \(f\left(t_i\right)\), and to make the following sum explicit:

\[\begin{equation*} \sum_if\left(t_i\right)\left\langle\xi_{\Delta,i},\xi_{\Delta,j}\right\rangle\Delta t=\sum_if\left(t_i\right)\left\langle\frac{\Delta B_i}{\Delta t},\frac{\Delta B_j}{\Delta t}\right\rangle\Delta t=\sum_if\left(t_i\right)\frac{\left\langle \Delta B_i,\Delta B_j\right\rangle}{(\Delta t)^{\cancel{2}}}\cancel{\Delta t} \end{equation*}\]

Since the increments are independent of each other, the covariance \(\left\langle \Delta B_i,\Delta B_j\right\rangle\), in general, is zero; the only exception is when \(i=j\), since in that case we are dealing with the variance which we know to be equal to \(t_i+\Delta t-t_i=\Delta t\).

We will have:

\[\begin{equation*} \sum_if\left(t_i\right)\left\langle\xi_{\Delta,i},\xi_{\Delta,j}\right\rangle\Delta t=\sum_if\left(t_i\right)\frac{\delta_{ij}\cancel{\Delta t}}{\cancel{\Delta t}}=f\left(t_j\right) \end{equation*}\]

Passing to the limit for \(\Delta t\to0\), therefore:

\[\begin{equation*} \int f(t)\langle\xi(t)\xi(t')\rangle dt=f(t') \end{equation*}\]

From which it follows that

\[\begin{equation*} \langle\xi(t)\xi(t')\rangle=\delta(t-t') \end{equation*}\]

Although we cannot give an analytical form to \(\xi(t)\) - except as a limit of \(\xi_{\Delta,i}\) for \(\Delta t\to0\), which however has no real solution from a rigorous point of view - we were still able to find two characteristic aspects:

\[\begin{equation} \begin{cases} \langle\xi(t)\rangle=0\\ \langle\xi(t)\xi(t')\rangle=\delta(t-t') \end{cases} \end{equation}\]

A function that follows these two properties is called Gaussian white noise, or also \(\boldsymbol{\delta}\)-correlated noise. Having defined this function, we can rewrite the starting differential equation in the following form:

\[\begin{equation} \boxed{\frac{dx}{dt}=\mu+\sigma\xi(t)} \end{equation}\]

10.2 Langevin equation

Let’s try to generalize what has been written so far. Let’s start from an ordinary differential equation, which we know has the form

\[\begin{equation*} dx=f(x,t)dt \end{equation*}\]

To introduce the stochastic component we could proceed in the most general way, that is, by making the function also depend on the Brownian motion expressed by the function \(B\):

\[\begin{equation*} dx=f(x,t,B)dt \end{equation*}\]

The problem with an equation of this type is that it is, in general, very difficult to approach. By introducing the noise in an additive form, we find an equation called the Langevin equation:

\[\begin{equation} \boxed{dx=\mu(x,t)dt+\sigma dB} \end{equation}\]

having rewritten the function \(f(x,t)\) as \(\mu(x,t)\) for consistency with the previous section, and being \(\sigma\) a constant coefficient.

Although the additive form is simpler to treat, it still hides aspects that are not identifiable through standard mathematical analysis. To understand in what sense, let’s consider the simple case in which \(\mu(x,t)\equiv-\mu x\):

\[\begin{equation} dx=-\mu xdt+\sigma dB \end{equation}\]

Let’s consider the average value on the first and second members:

\[\begin{equation*} d\cancel{\langle x\rangle}=-\mu\langle x\rangle dt+\sigma d\cancel{\langle B\rangle} \ \Longrightarrow \ \frac{d}{dt}\langle x\rangle=-\mu\langle x\rangle \end{equation*}\]

This differential equation is known to give rise to the following solution:

\[\begin{equation*} \langle x(t)\rangle=x_0e^{-\mu t} \end{equation*}\]

To calculate the variance, we will have to multiply both members of (eq:sde-equazione-di-langevin-caso-lineare) by \(2x\) and proceed in a similar way:

\[\begin{equation*} 2xdx=-2\mu x^2dt+2\sigma xdB \ \Longrightarrow \ 2\langle xdx\rangle=-2\mu\left\langle x^2\right\rangle dt+2\sigma\langle xdB\rangle \end{equation*}\]

The reason why we multiplied by \(2x\) is that the derivative of \(x^2\) is

\[\begin{equation*} \frac{d\left(x^2\right)}{dt}=\frac{d\left(x^2\right)}{dx}\frac{dx}{dt}=2x\frac{dx}{dt} \ \Longrightarrow \ d\left(x^2\right)=2xdx \ \Longrightarrow \ d\left\langle x^2\right\rangle=2\langle xdx\rangle \end{equation*}\]

Returning to the previous expression, we will therefore have that

\[\begin{equation} d\left\langle x^2\right\rangle=-2\mu\left\langle x^2\right\rangle dt+2\sigma\langle xdB\rangle \end{equation}\]

We can reasonably assume that the increments \(dB\) are uncorrelated with the coordinate \(x\), in the sense that they do not depend on it; it follows that:

\[\begin{equation*} \langle xdB\rangle=\langle x\rangle d\cancel{\langle B\rangle}=0 \ \Longrightarrow \ d\left\langle x^2\right\rangle=-2\mu\left\langle x^2\right\rangle dt \ \Longrightarrow \ \frac{d}{dt}\left\langle x^2\right\rangle=-2\mu\left\langle x^2\right\rangle \end{equation*}\]

The solution of this differential equation is

\[\begin{equation*} \left\langle x(t)^2\right\rangle=x_0^2e^{-2\mu t} \end{equation*}\]

A variance follows:

\[\begin{equation*} Var[x(t)]=\left\langle x^2\right\rangle-\langle x\rangle^2=x_0^2e^{-2\mu t}-\left(x_0e^{-\mu t}\right)^2=0 \end{equation*}\]

In essence, we would have shown that, despite the introduction of noise, the fluctuations are completely absent. This is obviously an absurdity, and depends on the fact that we cannot apply standard analysis for equations that contemplate stochastic terms such as those relating to the trajectories of Brownian motion.

10.3 Multiplicative noise

We can further generalize (eq:sde-equazione-di-langevin) by making \(\sigma\) no longer constant, but dependent on the coordinates \(x\) and \(t\):

\[\begin{equation} dx=\mu(x,t)dt+\sigma(x,t)dB \end{equation}\]

A general solution of this equation has the following formula:

\[\begin{equation} \boxed{x(t)=x_0+\int_0^t\mu(x(t'),t')dt'+\int_0^t\sigma(x(t'),t')dB(t')} \end{equation}\]

If the trajectory \(B(t)\) is given, an explicit function of \(B(t)\) that satisfies the relation (eq:sde-soluzione-generale), together with the initial condition \(x(0)=x_0\), is called a strong solution:

\[\begin{equation*} x(t)\equiv\Phi\left(B(t') \ \text{with} \ t'<t,x_0\right) \end{equation*}\]

From now on, our attention shifts to the last term on the right-hand side of (eq:sde-soluzione-generale): being the contribution of a stochastic nature, we will have to understand how to interpret this integral.

First of all, let’s understand why it is not a normal integral as we understand it in analysis; let’s consider for simplicity \(\mu(x,t)=0\) and \(\sigma(x,t)=B(t)\):

\[\begin{equation} x(t)=x_0+\int_0^tB(t')dB(t') \end{equation}\]

What we expect is that \(\langle x(t)\rangle=x_0\), since Brownian motion introduces noise distributed symmetrically with respect to \(0\), and therefore that the average value of the integral written above is zero. By applying standard analysis, however, this average value results:

\[\begin{equation*} \left\langle \int_0^tB(t')dB(t')\right\rangle=\left\langle\frac{B(t')^2}{2}\bigg|_0^t\right\rangle=\frac{\left\langle B(t)^2\right\rangle}{2}=\frac{t}{2}\neq0 \end{equation*}\]

The reason for this inconsistency will become clearer thanks to a theorem that we will see below; to prove this theorem, however, we first need to verify a lemma.

10.3.1 Lemma on finite quadratic variation

For this lemma we need to introduce the mean square limit, defined as follows:

\[\begin{equation} \mslim_{n\to\infty}x_n=x \ \iff \ \lim_{n\to\infty}\left\langle\left(x_n-x\right)^2\right\rangle=0 \end{equation}\]

We now have all the tools to state the lemma.

Given $t>0$, let's partition the interval $[0,t]$ into $n$ sub-intervals:

\begin{equation*}
    P_n=\left\{t_0=0,t_1,t_2,\dots,t_{n-1},t_n=t\right\}
\end{equation*}

Let's use the following notation:

\begin{equation*}
    \abs{P_n}=\max_i\abs{t_i-t_{i-1}}
\end{equation*}

Let's assume that, in the limit for $n\to\infty$, it results $\abs{P_n}\to0$. If this is valid, setting

\begin{equation*}
    Q_n=\sum_{i=1}^n\left[B\left(t_i\right)-B\left(t_{i-1}\right)\right]^2
\end{equation*}

it results:

\begin{equation}
    \boxed{\mslim_{n\to\infty}Q_n=t}
\end{equation}

We will limit ourselves to proving this result, but it should be borne in mind that it is possible to prove something even stronger, namely the almost certain convergence of \(Q_n\) itself:

\[\begin{equation*} \lim_{n\to\infty}Q_n=t \end{equation*}\]

This implies that, although \(Q_n\) is random for finite \(n\), it is no longer so in the limit for \(n\to\infty\).

Let’s proceed below with the proof.

As we have partitioned the set, the time $t$ can be rewritten as

\begin{equation*}
    t=\sum_{i=1}^nt_i-t_{i-1}
\end{equation*}

since the summation simplifies all the terms $t_i$ except for $t_n-t_0=t-0$.

It follows that

\begin{equation*}
    Q_n-t=\sum_{i=1}^n\left\{\left[B\left(t_i\right)-B\left(t_{i-1}\right)\right]^2-\left(t_i-t_{i-1}\right)\right\}
\end{equation*}

What we must prove is that

\begin{equation*}
    \left\langle\left(Q_n-t\right)^2\right\rangle\xrightarrow{n\to\infty}0
\end{equation*}

All that remains to be done, therefore, is to make this quantity explicit. Being

\begin{equation*}
    \left\langle\left(Q_n-t\right)^2\right\rangle=\left\langle\left(Q_n-t\right)\left(Q_n-t\right)\right\rangle
\end{equation*}

then the average value can be written as:

\footnotesize

\begin{equation*}
    \left\langle\sum_{i=1}^n\left\{\left[B\left(t_i\right)-B\left(t_{i-1}\right)\right]^2-\left(t_i-t_{i-1}\right)\right\}\sum_{j=1}^n\left\{\left[B\left(t_j\right)-B\left(t_{j-1}\right)\right]^2-\left(t_j-t_{j-1}\right)\right\}\right\rangle
\end{equation*}

\normalsize

By the linearity of the average value with respect to the sum:

\footnotesize

\begin{equation*}
    \sum_{i=1}^n\sum_{j=1}^n\left\langle\left\{\left[B\left(t_i\right)-B\left(t_{i-1}\right)\right]^2-\left(t_i-t_{i-1}\right)\right\}\left\{\left[B\left(t_j\right)-B\left(t_{j-1}\right)\right]^2-\left(t_j-t_{j-1}\right)\right\}\right\rangle
\end{equation*}

\normalsize

In the case where $i\neq j$, since the increments of Brownian motion are independent by definition, the average value of the product will be equal to the product of the average values; the generic term of the summations will be equal to

\footnotesize

\begin{align*}
    &\left\langle\left\{\left[B\left(t_i\right)-B\left(t_{i-1}\right)\right]^2-\left(t_i-t_{i-1}\right)\right\}\left\{\left[B\left(t_j\right)-B\left(t_{j-1}\right)\right]^2-\left(t_j-t_{j-1}\right)\right\}\right\rangle=\\
    &=\left\langle\left[B\left(t_i\right)-B\left(t_{i-1}\right)\right]^2-\left(t_i-t_{i-1}\right)\right\rangle\left\langle\left[B\left(t_j\right)-B\left(t_{j-1}\right)\right]^2-\left(t_j-t_{j-1}\right)\right\rangle=\\
    &=\left\{\left\langle\left[B\left(t_i\right)-B\left(t_{i-1}\right)\right]^2\right\rangle-\left(t_i-t_{i-1}\right)\right\}\left\{\left\langle\left[B\left(t_j\right)-B\left(t_{j-1}\right)\right]^2\right\rangle-\left(t_j-t_{j-1}\right)\right\}=\\
    &=\left\{\cancel{\left(t_i-t_{i-1}\right)}\cancel{-\left(t_i-t_{i-1}\right)}\right\}\left\{\cancel{\left(t_j-t_{j-1}\right)}\cancel{-\left(t_j-t_{j-1}\right)}\right\}=0
\end{align*}

\normalsize

Of the two summations, only the contributions in which $i=j$ remain:

\begin{equation*}
    \sum_{i=1}^n\left\langle\left\{\left[B\left(t_i\right)-B\left(t_{i-1}\right)\right]^2-\left(t_i-t_{i-1}\right)\right\}^2\right\rangle
\end{equation*}

Let's expand the square:

\begin{equation*}
    \sum_{i=1}^n\left\langle\left[B\left(t_i\right)-B\left(t_{i-1}\right)\right]^4-2\left[B\left(t_i\right)-B\left(t_{i-1}\right)\right]^2\left(t_i-t_{i-1}\right)+\left(t_i-t_{i-1}\right)^2\right\rangle
\end{equation*}

Dividing the average value among the three terms:

\small

\begin{equation*}
    \sum_{i=1}^n\left\{\left\langle\left[B\left(t_i\right)-B\left(t_{i-1}\right)\right]^4\right\rangle-2\left\langle\left[B\left(t_i\right)-B\left(t_{i-1}\right)\right]^2\right\rangle\left(t_i-t_{i-1}\right)+\left(t_i-t_{i-1}\right)^2\right\}
\end{equation*}

\normalsize

The first term is the fourth-order moment relative to a Gaussian centered at zero and with variance $t_i-t_{i-1}$. The formula for finding the generic (even) order moment is (eq:integrali-gaussiani-formula-momento-n), from which we know that the result is equal to $3\left(t_i-t_{i-1}\right)^2$.

The final result will be:

\begin{equation*}
    \sum_{i=1}^n\left\{3\left(t_i-t_{i-1}\right)^2-2\left(t_i-t_{i-1}\right)^2+\left(t_i-t_{i-1}\right)^2\right\}=2\sum_{i=1}^n\left(t_i-t_{i-1}\right)^2
\end{equation*}

Let's remember that this is none other than a rewriting of $\left\langle\left(Q_n-t\right)^2\right\rangle$. To obtain the thesis, let's try to majorize with respect to $\abs{P_n}$:

\begin{equation*}
    \left\langle\left(Q_n-t\right)^2\right\rangle=2\sum_{i=1}^n\left(t_i-t_{i-1}\right)^2\leqslant2\max_i\abs{t_i-t_{i-1}}\sum_{i=1}^n\left(t_i-t_{i-1}\right)=2\abs{P_n}t
\end{equation*}

Since in the limit for $n\to\infty$ we have $\abs{P_n}\to0$, the thesis follows.

10.3.2 Theorem on the stochastic Riemann sum

Through the lemma just proved, we can prove the following theorem, which will allow us to understand the reason why the passage from discrete quantities to infinitesimal quantities in the limit is not as immediate as it is according to standard mathematical analysis.

Given $t>0$, let's partition the interval $[0,t]$ into $n$ sub-intervals:

\begin{equation*}
    P_n\left\{t_0=0,t_1,t_2,\dots,t_{n-1},t_n=t\right\}
\end{equation*}

Let's use the following notation:

\begin{equation*}
    \abs{P_n}=\max_i\abs{t_i-t_{i-1}}
\end{equation*}

Let's assume that, in the limit for $n\to\infty$, it results $\abs{P_n}\to0$. If this is valid, given $\lambda\in[0,1]\subseteq\mathbb{R}$ and setting

\begin{equation*}
    X_n=\sum_{i=1}^nB\left(\lambda t_i+(1-\lambda)t_{i-1}\right)\left[B\left(t_i\right)-B\left(t_{i-1}\right)\right]
\end{equation*}

it results:

\begin{equation}
    \boxed{\mslim_{n\to\infty}X_n=\frac{B(t)^2}{2}+(\lambda-\frac{1}{2})t}
\end{equation}

Before proving the theorem, it is useful to interpret the thesis right away. We note that the quantity \(\lambda t_i+(1-\lambda)t_{i-1}\), being \(\lambda\in[0,1]\), is intermediate between \(t_i\) and \(t_{i-1}\): in fact, for \(\lambda=0\) it is equal to \(t_{i-1}\), while for \(\lambda=1\) it is equal to \(t_i\), so for all intermediate values it will be between the two.

In the \(X_n\) defined in the theorem, we therefore recognize the approximate form of the integral, which should return the exact integral in the limit according to the Riemann sum illustrated in figure (fig:somma-di-riemann). From what we know from mathematical analysis, this sum should not depend on the intermediate point chosen in the interval of the domain, but equation (eq:sde-teorema-somma-di-riemann-risultato) shows us a result that is dependent on \(\lambda\), and therefore on the choice of the intermediate point. This peculiar property is what mainly characterizes stochastic integrals and what substantially differentiates them from classical integrals.

Let’s proceed below with the proof of the theorem.

To simplify the writing, let's adopt the following notations:

\begin{align*}
    &B_i=B\left(t_i\right)\\
    &B_i^\lambda=B\left(\lambda t_i+(1-\lambda)t_{i-1}\right)
\end{align*}

$X_n$ will therefore be equal to

\begin{equation*}
    X_n=\sum_{i=1}^nB_i^\lambda\left(B_i-B_{i-1}\right)
\end{equation*}

Working in the context of Brownian motion, it is always convenient to reduce to squares and squares of differences. For this reason, let's add and subtract $B_{i-1}$ to the first term and $B_i^\lambda$ to the second term:

\begin{equation*}
    B_i^\lambda\left(B_i-B_{i-1}\right)=\left(B_i^\lambda-B_{i-1}+B_{i-1}\right)\left(B_i-B_i^\lambda+B_i^\lambda-B_{i-1}\right)
\end{equation*}

Let's multiply the two parentheses as follows:

\footnotesize

\begin{align*}
    &\left(\orange{B_i^\lambda-B_{i-1}}+\green{B_{i-1}}\right)\left(\red{B_i-B_i^\lambda}+\orange{B_i^\lambda-B_{i-1}}\right)=\\
    &=\left(\orange{B_i^\lambda-B_{i-1}}\right)\left(\red{B_i-B_i^\lambda}\right)+\left(\orange{B_i^\lambda-B_{i-1}}\right)^2+\green{B_{i-1}}\left(\red{B_i-B_i^\lambda}\right)+\green{B_{i-1}}\left(\orange{B_i^\lambda-B_{i-1}}\right)
\end{align*}

\normalsize

Let's focus on the last two terms:

\begin{equation*}
    B_{i-1}\left(B_i-B_i^\lambda\right)+B_{i-1}\left(B_i^\lambda-B_{i-1}\right)=B_{i-1}B_i\cancel{-B_{i-1}B_i^\lambda}\cancel{+B_{i-1}B_i^\lambda}-B_{i-1}^2
\end{equation*}

Returning to the starting expression, we find:

\begin{equation*}
    B_i^\lambda\left(B_i-B_{i-1}\right)=\left(B_i^\lambda-B_{i-1}\right)\left(B_i-B_i^\lambda\right)+\left(B_i^\lambda-B_{i-1}\right)^2+B_{i-1}B_i-B_{i-1}^2
\end{equation*}

We note that they are actually all squares or products of differences except for $B_{i-1}B_i$. To also write the latter in this form, let's try to reduce it to the double product of the square of a binomial:

\begin{align*}
    B_{i-1}B_i&=\frac{1}{2}\cdot2B_{i-1}B_i=-\frac{1}{2}\cdot(-2B_{i-1}B_i)=\\
    &=-\frac{1}{2}\left(B_i^2-2B_iB_{i-1}+B_{i-1}^2\right)+\frac{1}{2}B_i^2+\frac{1}{2}B_{i-1}^2=\\
    &=-\frac{1}{2}\left(B_i-B_{i-1}\right)^2+\frac{1}{2}B_i^2+\frac{1}{2}B_{i-1}^2
\end{align*}

We obtain the following expression for $B_i^\lambda\left(B_i-B_{i-1}\right)$:

\begin{equation*}
    \red{\left(B_i^\lambda-B_{i-1}\right)\left(B_i-B_i^\lambda\right)}+\orange{\left(B_i^\lambda-B_{i-1}\right)^2}\green{-\frac{1}{2}\left(B_i-B_{i-1}\right)^2}+\blue{\frac{1}{2}\left(B_i^2-B_{i-1}^2\right)}
\end{equation*}

Let's now calculate the individual contributions of the four highlighted terms.

\begin{enumerate}
    \item $\red{\left(B_i^\lambda-B_{i-1}\right)\left(B_i-B_i^\lambda\right)}$

    Considering the average value of the square, we can write:

    \small

    \begin{equation*}
        \left\langle\left[\sum_{i=1}^n\left(B_i^\lambda-B_{i-1}\right)\left(B_i-B_i^\lambda\right)\right]^2\right\rangle=\sum_{i=1}^n\left\langle\left(B_i^\lambda-B_{i-1}\right)^2\left(B_i-B_i^\lambda\right)^2\right\rangle
    \end{equation*}

    \normalsize

    In this step we have ignored the double products resulting from the expansion of the square, as the increments are independent and therefore, by distributing the average value over the individual differences, the expected values would have been zero, since these differences are distributed as Gaussians centered at the origin.

    Always for the same reason, that is, the independence of the increments in Brownian motion, we can distribute the average value in the remaining term:

    \begin{equation*}
        \sum_{i=1}^n\left\langle\left(B_i^\lambda-B_{i-1}\right)^2\right\rangle\left\langle\left(B_i-B_i^\lambda\right)^2\right\rangle
    \end{equation*}

    Remembering that $B_i^\lambda=B\left(\lambda t_i+(1-\lambda)t_{i-1}\right)$ it then follows that

    \begin{align*}
        &\sum_{i=1}^n\left[\lambda t_i+(1-\lambda)t_{i-1}-t_{i-1}\right]\left[t_i-\lambda t_i-(1-\lambda)t_{i-1}\right]=\\
        &=\sum_{i=1}^n\left[\lambda t_i\cancel{+t_{i-1}}-\lambda t_{i-1}\cancel{-t_{i-1}}\right]\left[(1-\lambda)t_i-(1-\lambda)t_{i-1}\right]=\\
        &=\sum_{i=1}^n\lambda\left(t_i-t_{i-1}\right)\cdot(1-\lambda)\left(t_i-t_{i-1}\right)=\lambda(1-\lambda)\sum_{i=1}^n\left(t_i-t_{i-1}\right)^2
    \end{align*}

    Let's majorize exactly as we did in the proof of the lemma on finite quadratic variation:

    \footnotesize

    \begin{equation*}
        \lambda(1-\lambda)\sum_{i=1}^n\left(t_i-t_{i-1}\right)^2\leqslant\lambda(1-\lambda)\max_i\abs{t_i-t_{i-1}}\sum_{i=1}^n\left(t_i-t_{i-1}\right)=\lambda(1-\lambda)\abs{P_n}t
    \end{equation*}

    \normalsize

    In the limit for $n\to\infty$ we have that $\abs{P_n}\to0$, so this contribution is zero.

    \item $\orange{\left(B_i^\lambda-B_{i-1}\right)^2}$

    Let's make both $B_i^\lambda$ and $B_{i-1}$ explicit:

    \begin{equation*}
        \sum_{i=1}^n\left[B\left(\lambda t_i+(1-\lambda)t_{i-1}\right)-B\left(t_{i-1}\right)\right]^2
    \end{equation*}

    By the stationarity of the increments $B(t)-B(t')=B(t+\epsilon)-B(t'+\epsilon)$, so we can add the same quantity inside both functions while keeping the difference the same.

    That said, let's add $-(1-\lambda)t_{i-1}$:

    \begin{align*}
        &\sum_{i=1}^n\left[B\left(\lambda t_i\cancel{+(1-\lambda)t_{i-1}}\cancel{-(1-\lambda)t_{i-1}}\right)-B\left(t_{i-1}-(1-\lambda)t_{i-1}\right)\right]^2=\\
        &=\sum_{i=1}^n\left[B\left(\lambda t_i\right)-B\left(\cancel{t_{i-1}}\cancel{-t_{i-1}}+\lambda t_{i-1}\right)\right]^2=\sum_{i=1}^n\left[B\left(\lambda t_i\right)-B\left(\lambda t_{i-1}\right)\right]^2
    \end{align*}

    Let's set $\bar{t}_i=\lambda t_i$ obtaining the following limit from the lemma proved before:

    \begin{equation*}
        \mslim_{n\to\infty}\sum_{i=1}^n\left[B\left(\bar{t}_i\right)-B\left(\bar{t}_{i-1}\right)\right]^2=\bar{t}=\lambda t
    \end{equation*}

    \item $\green{-\frac{1}{2}\left(B_i-B_{i-1}\right)^2}$

    The result of the limit is immediate from the lemma on finite quadratic variation:

    \begin{equation*}
        -\frac{1}{2}\mslim_{n\to\infty}\left[B_i-B_{i-1}\right]^2=-\frac{1}{2}\mslim_{n\to\infty}\left[B\left(t_i\right)-B\left(t_{i-1}\right)\right]^2=-\frac{t}{2}
    \end{equation*}

    \item $\blue{\frac{1}{2}\left(B_i^2-B_{i-1}^2\right)}$

    In the summation all the terms will be simplified except for the first and the last:

    \begin{align*}
        \frac{1}{2}\sum_{i=1}^n\left(B_i^2-B_{i-1}^2\right)&=\frac{1}{2}\left(B_n^2-B_0^2\right)=\frac{1}{2}\left[B\left(t_n\right)^2-B\left(t_0\right)^2\right]=\\
        &=\frac{1}{2}\left[B(t)^2-\cancel{B(0)}^2\right]
    \end{align*}

    Being a finite quantity, $\frac{B(t)^2}{2}$ is the final result also in the limit for $n\to\infty$.
\end{enumerate}

Putting the four pieces together, it results:

\begin{equation*}
    \mslim_{n\to\infty}X_n=0+\lambda t-\frac{t}{2}+\frac{B(t)^2}{2}=\frac{B(t)^2}{2}+(\lambda-\frac{1}{2})t
\end{equation*}

just as predicted by the thesis.

10.4 Solution of the stochastic integral

The \(X_n\) defined in the theorem, in the limit, is none other than the stochastic integral written in equation (eq:sde-rumore-moltiplicativo-integrale-stocastico-primo-accenno). In essence, we have shown that

\[\begin{equation} \boxed{\int_0^tB(t')dB(t')=\frac{B(t)^2}{2}+(\lambda-\frac{1}{2})t} \end{equation}\]

The result of the integral therefore depends on the choice of \(\lambda\), which we remember is between zero and one. We will see below two possible choices, which involve different interpretations of the stochastic contribution.

10.4.1 Itô integral (\(\lambda=0\))

Following the Itô convention, let’s set \(\lambda=0\), obtaining:

\[\begin{equation} \boxed{\int_0^tB(t')dB(t')=\frac{B(t)^2}{2}-\frac{t}{2}} \end{equation}\]

This choice is the most widespread because it brings several advantages with it, all due to an important property: by choosing \(\lambda=0\), in fact, the intermediate point \(\lambda t_i+(1-\lambda)t_{i-1}\) is equal to \(t_{i-1}\). This means that the integrand function is evaluated at the initial point of each interval of the partition: in other words, the integrand does not depend in any way on a time but only on past and present values. Functions of this type are called non-anticipative functions, and are the basis of mathematical analysis in the statistical field declined according to the Itô convention, called Itô calculus, which we will deepen later.

A first advantage of this convention is that the function \(\sigma(t)\) and the differential \(dB(t)\) are independent of each other, since the latter is relative to a future interval while the non-anticipative function depends exclusively on the initial point. In a non-rigorous way, we can therefore write:

\[\begin{equation} \left\langle\int_0^t\sigma(t')dB(t')\right\rangle=\int_0^t\langle\sigma(t')\rangle\langle dB(t')\rangle \end{equation}\]

Since the differences of \(B(t)\) are distributed according to a Gaussian centered at zero, the average value of \(dB(t')\) will be zero, therefore

\[\begin{equation*} \left\langle\int_0^t\sigma(t')dB(t')\right\rangle=\int_0^t\langle\sigma(t')\rangle\cancel{\langle dB(t')\rangle}=0 \end{equation*}\]

This property is also confirmed by the particular case in which \(\sigma(t)=B(t)\), as is evident from equation (eq:sde-integrale-di-ito):

\[\begin{equation*} \left\langle\int_0^tB(t')dB(t')\right\rangle=\frac{\left\langle B(t)^2\right\rangle}{2}-\frac{t}{2}=\frac{t}{2}-\frac{t}{2}=0 \end{equation*}\]

In general, the Itô integral is linked to the discrete form by the following relation:

\[\begin{equation} \boxed{\int_0^t\sigma(t')dB(t')=\mslim_{n\to\infty}\sum_{i=1}^n\sigma\left(t_{i-1}\right)\left[B\left(t_i\right)-B\left(t_{i-1}\right)\right]} \end{equation}\]

being \(\sigma(t)\) a non-anticipative function, and therefore depending exclusively on \(t_{i-1}\).

The link between Itô calculus and standard analysis is given by the following relation:

\[\begin{equation} \int_0^t\sigma(t')dB(t')^2=\int_0^t\sigma(t')dt' \end{equation}\]

This, in fact, connects the two fields through the equality of the two differentials: on the one hand we have the infinitesimal square of Brownian motion, while on the other the differential of time. The first cannot exist in standard analysis, because, as we have repeated several times, the function \(B(t)\) is not differentiable; nevertheless, in integral calculus, the two differentials are actually equal, thus creating a point of connection between the two .

For the proof we must consider the following limit:

\[\begin{equation} \lim_{n\to\infty}\left\langle\left[\sum_{i=1}^n\sigma_{i-1}\left(\Delta B_i^2-\Delta t_i\right)\right]^2\right\rangle \end{equation}\]

having written for simplicity \(\sigma_{i-1}=\sigma\left(t_{i-1}\right)\), \(\Delta B_i^2=\left[B\left(t_i\right)-B\left(t_{i-1}\right)\right]^2\) and \(\Delta t_i=t_i-t_{i-1}\).

Let’s expand the square, also considering the double products:

\[\begin{equation*} \lim_{n\to\infty}\left\langle\sum_{i=1}^n\sigma_{i-1}^2\left(\Delta B_i^2-\Delta t_i\right)^2+2\sum_{i>j}\sigma_{i-1}\sigma_{j-1}\left(\Delta B_i^2-\Delta t_i\right)\left(\Delta B_j^2-\Delta t_j\right)\right\rangle \end{equation*}\]

The term \(\sigma_{i-1}\) is independent of the increments \(\Delta B_i\) due to the non-anticipative property of the Itô convention. We can therefore distribute the average value among all the individual terms:

\[\begin{equation*} \lim_{n\to\infty}\left[\sum_{i=1}^n\left\langle\sigma_{i-1}^2\right\rangle\left\langle\left(\Delta B_i^2-\Delta t_i\right)^2\right\rangle+2\sum_{i>j}\left\langle\sigma_{i-1}\sigma_{j-1}\right\rangle\left(\left\langle\Delta B_i^2\right\rangle-\Delta t_i\right)\left(\left\langle\Delta B_j^2\right\rangle-\Delta t_j\right)\right] \end{equation*}\]

In the mixed terms, the contributions in parentheses are zero, since

\[\begin{equation*} \left\langle\Delta B_i^2\right\rangle-\Delta t_i=\Delta t_i-\Delta t_i=0 \end{equation*}\]

In the surviving term we can expand the square:

\[\begin{equation*} \left\langle\left(\Delta B_i^2-\Delta t_i\right)^2\right\rangle=\left\langle\Delta B_i^4\right\rangle-2\left\langle\Delta B_i^2\right\rangle\Delta t_i+\Delta t_i^2=3\Delta t_i^2-2\Delta t_i^2+\Delta t_i^2=2\Delta t_i^2 \end{equation*}\]

Substituting in the starting limit:

\[\begin{equation*} 2\lim_{n\to\infty}\sum_{i=1}^n\left\langle\sigma_{i-1}^2\right\rangle\Delta t_i^2 \end{equation*}\]

By majorizing as we have already done previously as a function of \(\abs{P_n}\), we find that the limit converges to zero:

\[\begin{align*} 2\lim_{n\to\infty}\sum_{i=1}^n\left\langle\sigma_{i-1}^2\right\rangle\Delta t_i^2&\leqslant2\lim_{n\to\infty}\left(\max_i\abs{t_i-t_{i-1}}\sum_{i=1}^n\left\langle\sigma_{i-1}^2\right\rangle\Delta t_i\right)=\\ &=2\lim_{n\to\infty}\left(\abs{P_n}\sum_{i=1}^n\left\langle\sigma_{i-1}^2\right\rangle\Delta t_i\right)=0 \end{align*}\]

Returning to the limit of (eq:sde-ito-differenziali-uguali-limite-iniziale), setting \(x_n\) equal to the summation and \(x=0\), we can write

\[\begin{equation*} \lim_{n\to\infty}\left\langle\left(x_n-x\right)^2\right\rangle=0 \end{equation*}\]

This expression defines the mean squared limit for \(x_n\), that is, for the summation:

\[\begin{equation*} \mslim_{n\to\infty}\sum_{i=1}^n\sigma_{i-1}\left(\Delta B_i^2-\Delta t_i\right)=0 \end{equation*}\]

which can also be rewritten as

\[\begin{equation*} \mslim_{n\to\infty}\left(\sum_{i=1}^n\sigma_{i-1}\Delta B_i^2-\sum_{i=1}^n\sigma_{i-1}\Delta t_i\right)=0 \end{equation*}\]

Isolating the two terms:

\[\begin{equation} \mslim_{n\to\infty}\sum_{i=1}^n\sigma_{i-1}\Delta B_i^2=\mslim_{n\to\infty}\sum_{i=1}^n\sigma_{i-1}\Delta t_i \end{equation}\]

According to the Itô convention, the two mean squared limits are none other than the integrals written in (eq:sde-ito-relazione-collante), thus proving the written relation.

10.4.2 Stratonovich integral (\(\lambda=\frac{1}{2}\))

Following the Stratonovich convention, let’s set \(\lambda=\frac{1}{2}\), obtaining:

\[\begin{equation} \boxed{\int_0^tB(t')dB(t')=\frac{B(t)^2}{2}} \end{equation}\]

In this case the average value of the integral is not zero, since

\[\begin{equation*} \left\langle\int_0^tB(t')dB(t')\right\rangle=\frac{\left\langle B(t)^2\right\rangle}{2}=\frac{t}{2} \end{equation*}\]

Intuitively, this means that the Brownian motion \(B(t)\) and its increment \(dB(t)\) are not uncorrelated with each other, and this depends on the fact that the point considered in which the function is evaluated at the limit of the Riemann sum is the intermediate point between \(t_{i-1}\) and \(t_i\):

\[\begin{equation*} \lambda t_i+(1-\lambda)t_{i-1} \ \xrightarrow{\lambda=\frac{1}{2}} \ \frac{1}{2}t_i+\left(1-\frac{1}{2}\right)t_{i-1}=\frac{1}{2}t_i+\frac{1}{2}t_{i-1}=\frac{1}{2}\left(t_i+t_{i-1}\right) \end{equation*}\]

This means that intrinsically, in the calculation, we partially consider a time in each interval, which precisely constrains the correlation between the two quantities.

The Stratonovich convention is mostly used in the theoretical field, especially in the physical one, where the choice of the intermediate point corresponds to the most behavior in the evolution of a system in which dissipative effects and stochastic fluctuations intervene. To this is added the fact that in the choice of Stratonovich the chain rule of standard mathematical analysis is preserved, and this allows one to easily perform substitutions of variables as is common in calculations in physics.

10.5 Itô calculus

What we will derive below are two important laws, which define the main properties of infinitesimal analysis: the differentiation rule and the chain rule, the latter also called the Itô formula in the context in which we find ourselves.

In both cases we will refer to a fundamental relation that we have already expressed in the past, both in equation (eq:processi-stocastici-wiener-andamento-modulo-delta-B) and in equation (eq:sde-ito-relazione-collante), according to which

\[\begin{equation} \Delta B^2=\Delta t \end{equation}\]

This relation, in a non-rigorous way, we can also write it in infinitesimal form through the differential:

\[\begin{equation} dB^2=dt \end{equation}\]

10.5.1 Differentiation rule

Let’s consider a function \(u(B(t),t)\). The differential \(du\) will therefore be a function of \(dt\) and of \(dB\). Furthermore, since \(dB^2=dt\), the quadratic term \(dB^2\) of the expansion is not negligible, but we must include it in the expansion:

\[\begin{equation*} du=\frac{\partial u}{\partial t}dt+\frac{\partial u}{\partial B}dB+\frac{1}{2}\frac{\partial^2u}{\partial B^2}dB^2=\frac{\partial u}{\partial t}dt+\frac{\partial u}{\partial B}dB+\frac{1}{2}\frac{\partial^2u}{\partial B^2}dt \end{equation*}\]

Ultimately, the differential \(du\) is equal to

\[\begin{equation} \boxed{du=\left(\frac{\partial u}{\partial t}+\frac{1}{2}\frac{\partial^2u}{\partial B^2}\right)dt+\frac{\partial u}{\partial B}dB} \end{equation}\]

Let’s take for example \(u=B^2\); not depending explicitly on \(t\), then \(\frac{\partial u}{\partial t}=0\), and what remains is:

\[\begin{equation*} du\equiv dB^2=\frac{1}{2}\frac{\partial^2\left(B^2\right)}{\partial B^2}dt+\frac{\partial\left(B^2\right)}{\partial B}dB=\frac{1}{\cancel{2}}\cdot\cancel{2}dt+2BdB \end{equation*}\]

Integrating this relation we obtain:

\[\begin{equation*} \int_0^tdB(t')^2=\int_0^tdt'+2\int_0^tB(t')dB(t') \ \Longrightarrow \ B(t)^2=t+2\int_0^tB(t')dB(t') \end{equation*}\]

It follows that

\[\begin{equation*} \int_0^tB(t')dB(t')=\frac{B(t)^2}{2}-\frac{t}{2} \end{equation*}\]

which is precisely the Itô integral reported in equation (eq:sde-integrale-di-ito).

# Stochastic differential equations

We have already written a first stochastic differential equation in (eq:processi-stocastici-prima-equazione-differenziale-stocastica) when talking about the simulation of Brownian motion through a computer. The purpose now is to deepen this type of equation, trying to give a mathematical formalism that is not only linked to the simulation processes obtained through finite difference approximations.

## Gaussian white noise

Let's take up (eq:processi-stocastici-prima-equazione-differenziale-stocastica) again and try to write it in the form of a differential equation by dividing both sides by $dt$:

\begin{equation}
    \frac{dx}{dt}=\mu+\sigma\frac{dB}{dt}
\end{equation}

having replaced $\sqrt{D}$ with a generic function of the diffusion coefficient, indicated with $\sigma$.

The question at this point is: what does the derivative of $B(t)$ correspond to? As we have already stated in section (sec:integrale-sui-cammini-di-wiener), in reference to the formal expression written in equation (eq:integrale-sui-cammini-espressione-formale-differenziale-dP), a stochastic realization of Brownian motion is not differentiable at any point. Nevertheless, we can try to give a \q{functional} form to the derivative of $B(t)$ starting from its statistical properties.

Let's start from the finite expression, defining the following quantity:

\begin{equation}
    \xi_{\Delta,i}=\frac{\Delta B_i}{\Delta t}
\end{equation}

where $\Delta B_i=B\left(t_i+\Delta t\right)-B\left(t_i\right)$.

Let's remember that this difference follows a Gaussian distribution centered at zero, so the average value of the quantity just defined is:

\begin{equation}
    \left\langle\xi_{\Delta,i}\right\rangle=\left\langle\frac{\Delta B_i}{\Delta t}\right\rangle=\frac{\left\langle\Delta B_i\right\rangle}{\Delta t}=0
\end{equation}

Being identically equal to zero, this result will also hold in the limit for $\Delta t\to0$.

Another fundamental aspect, to understand how successive instants of time are correlated, is the *covariance*. To calculate it, it is useful to consider a generic function $f(t)$, which when discretized will assume the value $f\left(t_i\right)$, and to make the following sum explicit:

\begin{equation*}
    \sum_if\left(t_i\right)\left\langle\xi_{\Delta,i},\xi_{\Delta,j}\right\rangle\Delta t=\sum_if\left(t_i\right)\left\langle\frac{\Delta B_i}{\Delta t},\frac{\Delta B_j}{\Delta t}\right\rangle\Delta t=\sum_if\left(t_i\right)\frac{\left\langle \Delta B_i,\Delta B_j\right\rangle}{(\Delta t)^{\cancel{2}}}\cancel{\Delta t}
\end{equation*}

Since the increments are independent of each other, the covariance $\left\langle \Delta B_i,\Delta B_j\right\rangle$, in general, is zero; the only exception is when $i=j$, since in that case we are dealing with the variance which we know to be equal to $t_i+\Delta t-t_i=\Delta t$.

We will have:

\begin{equation*}
    \sum_if\left(t_i\right)\left\langle\xi_{\Delta,i},\xi_{\Delta,j}\right\rangle\Delta t=\sum_if\left(t_i\right)\frac{\delta_{ij}\cancel{\Delta t}}{\cancel{\Delta t}}=f\left(t_j\right)
\end{equation*}

Passing to the limit for $\Delta t\to0$, therefore:

\begin{equation*}
    \int f(t)\langle\xi(t)\xi(t')\rangle dt=f(t')
\end{equation*}

From which it follows that

\begin{equation*}
    \langle\xi(t)\xi(t')\rangle=\delta(t-t')
\end{equation*}

Although we cannot give an analytical form to $\xi(t)$ - except as a limit of $\xi_{\Delta,i}$ for $\Delta t\to0$, which however has no real solution from a rigorous point of view - we were still able to find two characteristic aspects:

\begin{equation}
    \begin{cases}
        \langle\xi(t)\rangle=0\\
        \langle\xi(t)\xi(t')\rangle=\delta(t-t')
    \end{cases}
\end{equation}

A function that follows these two properties is called **Gaussian white noise**, or also $\boldsymbol{\delta}$**-correlated noise**. Having defined this function, we can rewrite the starting differential equation in the following form:

\begin{equation}
    \boxed{\frac{dx}{dt}=\mu+\sigma\xi(t)}
\end{equation}

## Langevin equation

Let's try to generalize what has been written so far. Let's start from an ordinary differential equation, which we know has the form

\begin{equation*}
    dx=f(x,t)dt
\end{equation*}

To introduce the stochastic component we could proceed in the most general way, that is, by making the function also depend on the Brownian motion expressed by the function $B$:

\begin{equation*}
    dx=f(x,t,B)dt
\end{equation*}

The problem with an equation of this type is that it is, in general, very difficult to approach. By introducing the noise in an *additive* form, we find an equation called the **Langevin equation**:\footnote{It is required that the function $\mu(x,t)$ be a *smooth* function.}

\begin{equation}
    \boxed{dx=\mu(x,t)dt+\sigma dB}
\end{equation}

having rewritten the function $f(x,t)$ as $\mu(x,t)$ for consistency with the previous section, and being $\sigma$ a constant coefficient.

Although the additive form is simpler to treat, it still hides aspects that are not identifiable through standard mathematical analysis. To understand in what sense, let's consider the simple case in which $\mu(x,t)\equiv-\mu x$: \footnote{There is a bit of confusion regarding the various names of the equations: the one below is reported by the professor as an example of a Langevin equation, but according to Wikipedia (\url{https://it.wikipedia.org/wiki/Equazione_di_Langevin}) it is the real Langevin equation, not a particular case. Furthermore, the same Langevin equation written in (eq:sde-equazione-di-langevin) is specified by the professor as being *with additive noise*, suggesting that there is a more general form that is not mentioned later.}

\begin{equation}
    dx=-\mu xdt+\sigma dB
\end{equation}

Let's consider the average value on the first and second members:\footnote{There is also confusion regarding the notation of the differential with the average value: for consistency of writing, I have here reported the differential *outside* the expected value, but later it will also be written $\langle dB\rangle$. Due to the independence of the increments, the two writings should however be equivalent.}

\begin{equation*}
    d\cancel{\langle x\rangle}=-\mu\langle x\rangle dt+\sigma d\cancel{\langle B\rangle} \ \Longrightarrow \ \frac{d}{dt}\langle x\rangle=-\mu\langle x\rangle
\end{equation*}

This differential equation is known to give rise to the following solution:

\begin{equation*}
    \langle x(t)\rangle=x_0e^{-\mu t}
\end{equation*}

To calculate the variance, we will have to multiply both members of (eq:sde-equazione-di-langevin-caso-lineare) by $2x$ and proceed in a similar way:

\begin{equation*}
    2xdx=-2\mu x^2dt+2\sigma xdB \ \Longrightarrow \ 2\langle xdx\rangle=-2\mu\left\langle x^2\right\rangle dt+2\sigma\langle xdB\rangle
\end{equation*}

The reason why we multiplied by $2x$ is that the derivative of $x^2$ is

\begin{equation*}
    \frac{d\left(x^2\right)}{dt}=\frac{d\left(x^2\right)}{dx}\frac{dx}{dt}=2x\frac{dx}{dt} \ \Longrightarrow \ d\left(x^2\right)=2xdx \ \Longrightarrow \ d\left\langle x^2\right\rangle=2\langle xdx\rangle
\end{equation*}

Returning to the previous expression, we will therefore have that

\begin{equation}
    d\left\langle x^2\right\rangle=-2\mu\left\langle x^2\right\rangle dt+2\sigma\langle xdB\rangle
\end{equation}

We can reasonably assume that the increments $dB$ are uncorrelated with the coordinate $x$, in the sense that they do not depend on it; it follows that:

\begin{equation*}
    \langle xdB\rangle=\langle x\rangle d\cancel{\langle B\rangle}=0 \ \Longrightarrow \ d\left\langle x^2\right\rangle=-2\mu\left\langle x^2\right\rangle dt \ \Longrightarrow \ \frac{d}{dt}\left\langle x^2\right\rangle=-2\mu\left\langle x^2\right\rangle
\end{equation*}

The solution of this differential equation is

\begin{equation*}
    \left\langle x(t)^2\right\rangle=x_0^2e^{-2\mu t}
\end{equation*}

A variance follows:

\begin{equation*}
    Var[x(t)]=\left\langle x^2\right\rangle-\langle x\rangle^2=x_0^2e^{-2\mu t}-\left(x_0e^{-\mu t}\right)^2=0
\end{equation*}

In essence, we would have shown that, despite the introduction of noise, the fluctuations are completely absent. This is obviously an absurdity, and depends on the fact that we cannot apply standard analysis for equations that contemplate stochastic terms such as those relating to the trajectories of Brownian motion.

## Multiplicative noise

We can further generalize (eq:sde-equazione-di-langevin) by making $\sigma$ no longer constant, but dependent on the coordinates $x$ and $t$:

\begin{equation}
    dx=\mu(x,t)dt+\sigma(x,t)dB
\end{equation}

A general solution of this equation has the following formula:

\begin{equation}
    \boxed{x(t)=x_0+\int_0^t\mu(x(t'),t')dt'+\int_0^t\sigma(x(t'),t')dB(t')}
\end{equation}

If the trajectory $B(t)$ is *given*, an explicit function of $B(t)$ that satisfies the relation (eq:sde-soluzione-generale), together with the initial condition $x(0)=x_0$, is called a **strong solution**:

\begin{equation*}
    x(t)\equiv\Phi\left(B(t') \ \text{with} \ t'<t,x_0\right)
\end{equation*}

From now on, our attention shifts to the last term on the right-hand side of (eq:sde-soluzione-generale): being the contribution of a stochastic nature, we will have to understand how to interpret this integral.

First of all, let's understand why it is not a normal integral as we understand it in analysis; let's consider for simplicity $\mu(x,t)=0$ and $\sigma(x,t)=B(t)$:

\begin{equation}
    x(t)=x_0+\int_0^tB(t')dB(t')
\end{equation}

What we expect is that $\langle x(t)\rangle=x_0$, since Brownian motion introduces noise distributed symmetrically with respect to $0$, and therefore that the average value of the integral written above is zero. By applying standard analysis, however, this average value results:

\begin{equation*}
    \left\langle \int_0^tB(t')dB(t')\right\rangle=\left\langle\frac{B(t')^2}{2}\bigg|_0^t\right\rangle=\frac{\left\langle B(t)^2\right\rangle}{2}=\frac{t}{2}\neq0
\end{equation*}

The reason for this inconsistency will become clearer thanks to a theorem that we will see below; to prove this theorem, however, we first need to verify a lemma.

### Lemma on finite quadratic variation

For this lemma we need to introduce the *mean square limit*, defined as follows:

\begin{equation}
    \mslim_{n\to\infty}x_n=x \ \iff \ \lim_{n\to\infty}\left\langle\left(x_n-x\right)^2\right\rangle=0
\end{equation}

We now have all the tools to state the lemma.

::: {.lemma name="on finite quadratic variation"}
    Given $t>0$, let's partition the interval $[0,t]$ into $n$ sub-intervals:

    \begin{equation*}
        P_n=\left\{t_0=0,t_1,t_2,\dots,t_{n-1},t_n=t\right\}
    \end{equation*}

    Let's use the following notation:

    \begin{equation*}
        \abs{P_n}=\max_i\abs{t_i-t_{i-1}}
    \end{equation*}

    Let's assume that, in the limit for $n\to\infty$, it results $\abs{P_n}\to0$. If this is valid, setting

    \begin{equation*}
        Q_n=\sum_{i=1}^n\left[B\left(t_i\right)-B\left(t_{i-1}\right)\right]^2
    \end{equation*}

    it results:

    \begin{equation}
        \boxed{\mslim_{n\to\infty}Q_n=t}
    \end{equation}

:::

We will limit ourselves to proving this result, but it should be borne in mind that it is possible to prove something even stronger, namely the almost certain convergence of $Q_n$ itself:

\begin{equation*}
    \lim_{n\to\infty}Q_n=t
\end{equation*}

This implies that, although $Q_n$ is random for finite $n$, it is no longer so in the limit for $n\to\infty$.

Let's proceed below with the proof.

::: {.theorem}
    As we have partitioned the set, the time $t$ can be rewritten as

    \begin{equation*}
        t=\sum_{i=1}^nt_i-t_{i-1}
    \end{equation*}

    since the summation simplifies all the terms $t_i$ except for $t_n-t_0=t-0$.

    It follows that

    \begin{equation*}
        Q_n-t=\sum_{i=1}^n\left\{\left[B\left(t_i\right)-B\left(t_{i-1}\right)\right]^2-\left(t_i-t_{i-1}\right)\right\}
    \end{equation*}

    What we must prove is that

    \begin{equation*}
        \left\langle\left(Q_n-t\right)^2\right\rangle\xrightarrow{n\to\infty}0
    \end{equation*}

    All that remains to be done, therefore, is to make this quantity explicit. Being

    \begin{equation*}
        \left\langle\left(Q_n-t\right)^2\right\rangle=\left\langle\left(Q_n-t\right)\left(Q_n-t\right)\right\rangle
    \end{equation*}

    then the average value can be written as:

    \footnotesize

    \begin{equation*}
        \left\langle\sum_{i=1}^n\left\{\left[B\left(t_i\right)-B\left(t_{i-1}\right)\right]^2-\left(t_i-t_{i-1}\right)\right\}\sum_{j=1}^n\left\{\left[B\left(t_j\right)-B\left(t_{j-1}\right)\right]^2-\left(t_j-t_{j-1}\right)\right\}\right\rangle
    \end{equation*}

    \normalsize

    By the linearity of the average value with respect to the sum:

    \footnotesize

    \begin{equation*}
        \sum_{i=1}^n\sum_{j=1}^n\left\langle\left\{\left[B\left(t_i\right)-B\left(t_{i-1}\right)\right]^2-\left(t_i-t_{i-1}\right)\right\}\left\{\left[B\left(t_j\right)-B\left(t_{j-1}\right)\right]^2-\left(t_j-t_{j-1}\right)\right\}\right\rangle
    \end{equation*}

    \normalsize

    In the case where $i\neq j$, since the increments of Brownian motion are independent by definition, the average value of the product will be equal to the product of the average values; the generic term of the summations will be equal to

    \footnotesize

    \begin{align*}
        &\left\langle\left\{\left[B\left(t_i\right)-B\left(t_{i-1}\right)\right]^2-\left(t_i-t_{i-1}\right)\right\}\left\{\left[B\left(t_j\right)-B\left(t_{j-1}\right)\right]^2-\left(t_j-t_{j-1}\right)\right\}\right\rangle=\\
        &=\left\langle\left[B\left(t_i\right)-B\left(t_{i-1}\right)\right]^2-\left(t_i-t_{i-1}\right)\right\rangle\left\langle\left[B\left(t_j\right)-B\left(t_{j-1}\right)\right]^2-\left(t_j-t_{j-1}\right)\right\rangle=\\
        &=\left\{\left\langle\left[B\left(t_i\right)-B\left(t_{i-1}\right)\right]^2\right\rangle-\left(t_i-t_{i-1}\right)\right\}\left\{\left\langle\left[B\left(t_j\right)-B\left(t_{j-1}\right)\right]^2\right\rangle-\left(t_j-t_{j-1}\right)\right\}=\\
        &=\left\{\cancel{\left(t_i-t_{i-1}\right)}\cancel{-\left(t_i-t_{i-1}\right)}\right\}\left\{\cancel{\left(t_j-t_{j-1}\right)}\cancel{-\left(t_j-t_{j-1}\right)}\right\}=0
    \end{align*}

    \normalsize

    Of the two summations, only the contributions in which $i=j$ remain:

    \begin{equation*}
        \sum_{i=1}^n\left\langle\left\{\left[B\left(t_i\right)-B\left(t_{i-1}\right)\right]^2-\left(t_i-t_{i-1}\right)\right\}^2\right\rangle
    \end{equation*}

    Let's expand the square:

    \begin{equation*}
        \sum_{i=1}^n\left\langle\left[B\left(t_i\right)-B\left(t_{i-1}\right)\right]^4-2\left[B\left(t_i\right)-B\left(t_{i-1}\right)\right]^2\left(t_i-t_{i-1}\right)+\left(t_i-t_{i-1}\right)^2\right\rangle
    \end{equation*}

    Dividing the average value among the three terms:

    \small

    \begin{equation*}
        \sum_{i=1}^n\left\{\left\langle\left[B\left(t_i\right)-B\left(t_{i-1}\right)\right]^4\right\rangle-2\left\langle\left[B\left(t_i\right)-B\left(t_{i-1}\right)\right]^2\right\rangle\left(t_i-t_{i-1}\right)+\left(t_i-t_{i-1}\right)^2\right\}
    \end{equation*}

    \normalsize

    The first term is the fourth-order moment relative to a Gaussian centered at zero and with variance $t_i-t_{i-1}$. The formula for finding the generic (even) order moment is (eq:integrali-gaussiani-formula-momento-n), from which we know that the result is equal to $3\left(t_i-t_{i-1}\right)^2$.

    The final result will be:

    \begin{equation*}
        \sum_{i=1}^n\left\{3\left(t_i-t_{i-1}\right)^2-2\left(t_i-t_{i-1}\right)^2+\left(t_i-t_{i-1}\right)^2\right\}=2\sum_{i=1}^n\left(t_i-t_{i-1}\right)^2
    \end{equation*}

    Let's remember that this is none other than a rewriting of $\left\langle\left(Q_n-t\right)^2\right\rangle$. To obtain the thesis, let's try to majorize with respect to $\abs{P_n}$:

    \begin{equation*}
        \left\langle\left(Q_n-t\right)^2\right\rangle=2\sum_{i=1}^n\left(t_i-t_{i-1}\right)^2\leqslant2\max_i\abs{t_i-t_{i-1}}\sum_{i=1}^n\left(t_i-t_{i-1}\right)=2\abs{P_n}t
    \end{equation*}

    Since in the limit for $n\to\infty$ we have $\abs{P_n}\to0$, the thesis follows.

:::

### Theorem on the stochastic Riemann sum

Through the lemma just proved, we can prove the following theorem, which will allow us to understand the reason why the passage from discrete quantities to infinitesimal quantities in the limit is not as immediate as it is according to standard mathematical analysis.

::: {.theorem name="Theorem on the stochastic Riemann sum"}
    Given $t>0$, let's partition the interval $[0,t]$ into $n$ sub-intervals:

    \begin{equation*}
        P_n\left\{t_0=0,t_1,t_2,\dots,t_{n-1},t_n=t\right\}
    \end{equation*}

    Let's use the following notation:

    \begin{equation*}
        \abs{P_n}=\max_i\abs{t_i-t_{i-1}}
    \end{equation*}

    Let's assume that, in the limit for $n\to\infty$, it results $\abs{P_n}\to0$. If this is valid, given $\lambda\in[0,1]\subseteq\mathbb{R}$ and setting

    \begin{equation*}
        X_n=\sum_{i=1}^nB\left(\lambda t_i+(1-\lambda)t_{i-1}\right)\left[B\left(t_i\right)-B\left(t_{i-1}\right)\right]
    \end{equation*}

    it results:

    \begin{equation}
        \boxed{\mslim_{n\to\infty}X_n=\frac{B(t)^2}{2}+(\lambda-\frac{1}{2})t}
    \end{equation}

:::

Before proving the theorem, it is useful to interpret the thesis right away. We note that the quantity $\lambda t_i+(1-\lambda)t_{i-1}$, being $\lambda\in[0,1]$, is intermediate between $t_i$ and $t_{i-1}$: in fact, for $\lambda=0$ it is equal to $t_{i-1}$, while for $\lambda=1$ it is equal to $t_i$, so for all intermediate values it will be between the two.

In the $X_n$ defined in the theorem, we therefore recognize the approximate form of the integral, which should return the exact integral in the limit according to the *Riemann sum* illustrated in figure (fig:somma-di-riemann). From what we know from mathematical analysis, this sum should not depend on the intermediate point chosen in the interval of the domain, but equation (eq:sde-teorema-somma-di-riemann-risultato) shows us a result that is *dependent* on $\lambda$, and therefore on the choice of the intermediate point. This peculiar property is what mainly characterizes stochastic integrals and what substantially differentiates them from classical integrals.

Let's proceed below with the proof of the theorem.

::: {.theorem}
    To simplify the writing, let's adopt the following notations:

    \begin{align*}
        &B_i=B\left(t_i\right)\\
        &B_i^\lambda=B\left(\lambda t_i+(1-\lambda)t_{i-1}\right)
    \end{align*}

    $X_n$ will therefore be equal to

    \begin{equation*}
        X_n=\sum_{i=1}^nB_i^\lambda\left(B_i-B_{i-1}\right)
    \end{equation*}

    Working in the context of Brownian motion, it is always convenient to reduce to squares and squares of differences. For this reason, let's add and subtract $B_{i-1}$ to the first term and $B_i^\lambda$ to the second term:

    \begin{equation*}
        B_i^\lambda\left(B_i-B_{i-1}\right)=\left(B_i^\lambda-B_{i-1}+B_{i-1}\right)\left(B_i-B_i^\lambda+B_i^\lambda-B_{i-1}\right)
    \end{equation*}

    Let's multiply the two parentheses as follows:

    \footnotesize

    \begin{align*}
        &\left(\orange{B_i^\lambda-B_{i-1}}+\green{B_{i-1}}\right)\left(\red{B_i-B_i^\lambda}+\orange{B_i^\lambda-B_{i-1}}\right)=\\
        &=\left(\orange{B_i^\lambda-B_{i-1}}\right)\left(\red{B_i-B_i^\lambda}\right)+\left(\orange{B_i^\lambda-B_{i-1}}\right)^2+\green{B_{i-1}}\left(\red{B_i-B_i^\lambda}\right)+\green{B_{i-1}}\left(\orange{B_i^\lambda-B_{i-1}}\right)
    \end{align*}

    \normalsize

    Let's focus on the last two terms:

    \begin{equation*}
        B_{i-1}\left(B_i-B_i^\lambda\right)+B_{i-1}\left(B_i^\lambda-B_{i-1}\right)=B_{i-1}B_i\cancel{-B_{i-1}B_i^\lambda}\cancel{+B_{i-1}B_i^\lambda}-B_{i-1}^2
    \end{equation*}

    Returning to the starting expression, we find:

    \begin{equation*}
        B_i^\lambda\left(B_i-B_{i-1}\right)=\left(B_i^\lambda-B_{i-1}\right)\left(B_i-B_i^\lambda\right)+\left(B_i^\lambda-B_{i-1}\right)^2+B_{i-1}B_i-B_{i-1}^2
    \end{equation*}

    We note that they are actually all squares or products of differences except for $B_{i-1}B_i$. To also write the latter in this form, let's try to reduce it to the double product of the square of a binomial:

    \begin{align*}
        B_{i-1}B_i&=\frac{1}{2}\cdot2B_{i-1}B_i=-\frac{1}{2}\cdot(-2B_{i-1}B_i)=\\
        &=-\frac{1}{2}\left(B_i^2-2B_iB_{i-1}+B_{i-1}^2\right)+\frac{1}{2}B_i^2+\frac{1}{2}B_{i-1}^2=\\
        &=-\frac{1}{2}\left(B_i-B_{i-1}\right)^2+\frac{1}{2}B_i^2+\frac{1}{2}B_{i-1}^2
    \end{align*}

    We obtain the following expression for $B_i^\lambda\left(B_i-B_{i-1}\right)$:

    \begin{equation*}
        \red{\left(B_i^\lambda-B_{i-1}\right)\left(B_i-B_i^\lambda\right)}+\orange{\left(B_i^\lambda-B_{i-1}\right)^2}\green{-\frac{1}{2}\left(B_i-B_{i-1}\right)^2}+\blue{\frac{1}{2}\left(B_i^2-B_{i-1}^2\right)}
    \end{equation*}

    Let's now calculate the individual contributions of the four highlighted terms.

    \begin{enumerate}
        \item $\red{\left(B_i^\lambda-B_{i-1}\right)\left(B_i-B_i^\lambda\right)}$

        Considering the average value of the square, we can write:

        \small

        \begin{equation*}
            \left\langle\left[\sum_{i=1}^n\left(B_i^\lambda-B_{i-1}\right)\left(B_i-B_i^\lambda\right)\right]^2\right\rangle=\sum_{i=1}^n\left\langle\left(B_i^\lambda-B_{i-1}\right)^2\left(B_i-B_i^\lambda\right)^2\right\rangle
        \end{equation*}

        \normalsize

        In this step we have ignored the double products resulting from the expansion of the square, as the increments are independent and therefore, by distributing the average value over the individual differences, the expected values would have been zero, since these differences are distributed as Gaussians centered at the origin.

        Always for the same reason, that is, the independence of the increments in Brownian motion, we can distribute the average value in the remaining term:

        \begin{equation*}
            \sum_{i=1}^n\left\langle\left(B_i^\lambda-B_{i-1}\right)^2\right\rangle\left\langle\left(B_i-B_i^\lambda\right)^2\right\rangle
        \end{equation*}

        Remembering that $B_i^\lambda=B\left(\lambda t_i+(1-\lambda)t_{i-1}\right)$ it then follows that

        \begin{align*}
            &\sum_{i=1}^n\left[\lambda t_i+(1-\lambda)t_{i-1}-t_{i-1}\right]\left[t_i-\lambda t_i-(1-\lambda)t_{i-1}\right]=\\
            &=\sum_{i=1}^n\left[\lambda t_i\cancel{+t_{i-1}}-\lambda t_{i-1}\cancel{-t_{i-1}}\right]\left[(1-\lambda)t_i-(1-\lambda)t_{i-1}\right]=\\
            &=\sum_{i=1}^n\lambda\left(t_i-t_{i-1}\right)\cdot(1-\lambda)\left(t_i-t_{i-1}\right)=\lambda(1-\lambda)\sum_{i=1}^n\left(t_i-t_{i-1}\right)^2
        \end{align*}

        Let's majorize exactly as we did in the proof of the lemma on finite quadratic variation:

        \footnotesize

        \begin{equation*}
            \lambda(1-\lambda)\sum_{i=1}^n\left(t_i-t_{i-1}\right)^2\leqslant\lambda(1-\lambda)\max_i\abs{t_i-t_{i-1}}\sum_{i=1}^n\left(t_i-t_{i-1}\right)=\lambda(1-\lambda)\abs{P_n}t
        \end{equation*}

        \normalsize

        In the limit for $n\to\infty$ we have that $\abs{P_n}\to0$, so this contribution is zero.

        \item $\orange{\left(B_i^\lambda-B_{i-1}\right)^2}$

        Let's make both $B_i^\lambda$ and $B_{i-1}$ explicit:

        \begin{equation*}
            \sum_{i=1}^n\left[B\left(\lambda t_i+(1-\lambda)t_{i-1}\right)-B\left(t_{i-1}\right)\right]^2
        \end{equation*}

        By the stationarity of the increments $B(t)-B(t')=B(t+\epsilon)-B(t'+\epsilon)$, so we can add the same quantity inside both functions while keeping the difference the same.

        That said, let's add $-(1-\lambda)t_{i-1}$:

        \begin{align*}
            &\sum_{i=1}^n\left[B\left(\lambda t_i\cancel{+(1-\lambda)t_{i-1}}\cancel{-(1-\lambda)t_{i-1}}\right)-B\left(t_{i-1}-(1-\lambda)t_{i-1}\right)\right]^2=\\
            &=\sum_{i=1}^n\left[B\left(\lambda t_i\right)-B\left(\cancel{t_{i-1}}\cancel{-t_{i-1}}+\lambda t_{i-1}\right)\right]^2=\sum_{i=1}^n\left[B\left(\lambda t_i\right)-B\left(\lambda t_{i-1}\right)\right]^2
        \end{align*}

        Let's set $\bar{t}_i=\lambda t_i$ obtaining the following limit from the lemma proved before:

        \begin{equation*}
            \mslim_{n\to\infty}\sum_{i=1}^n\left[B\left(\bar{t}_i\right)-B\left(\bar{t}_{i-1}\right)\right]^2=\bar{t}=\lambda t
        \end{equation*}

        \item $\green{-\frac{1}{2}\left(B_i-B_{i-1}\right)^2}$

        The result of the limit is immediate from the lemma on finite quadratic variation:

        \begin{equation*}
            -\frac{1}{2}\mslim_{n\to\infty}\left[B_i-B_{i-1}\right]^2=-\frac{1}{2}\mslim_{n\to\infty}\left[B\left(t_i\right)-B\left(t_{i-1}\right)\right]^2=-\frac{t}{2}
        \end{equation*}

        \item $\blue{\frac{1}{2}\left(B_i^2-B_{i-1}^2\right)}$

        In the summation all the terms will be simplified except for the first and the last:

        \begin{align*}
            \frac{1}{2}\sum_{i=1}^n\left(B_i^2-B_{i-1}^2\right)&=\frac{1}{2}\left(B_n^2-B_0^2\right)=\frac{1}{2}\left[B\left(t_n\right)^2-B\left(t_0\right)^2\right]=\\
            &=\frac{1}{2}\left[B(t)^2-\cancel{B(0)}^2\right]
        \end{align*}

        Being a finite quantity, $\frac{B(t)^2}{2}$ is the final result also in the limit for $n\to\infty$.
    \end{enumerate}

    Putting the four pieces together, it results:

    \begin{equation*}
        \mslim_{n\to\infty}X_n=0+\lambda t-\frac{t}{2}+\frac{B(t)^2}{2}=\frac{B(t)^2}{2}+(\lambda-\frac{1}{2})t
    \end{equation*}

    just as predicted by the thesis.

## Solution of the stochastic integral

The $X_n$ defined in the theorem, in the limit, is none other than the stochastic integral written in equation (eq:sde-rumore-moltiplicativo-integrale-stocastico-primo-accenno). In essence, we have shown that

\begin{equation}
    \boxed{\int_0^tB(t')dB(t')=\frac{B(t)^2}{2}+(\lambda-\frac{1}{2})t}
\end{equation}

The result of the integral therefore depends on the choice of $\lambda$, which we remember is between zero and one. We will see below two possible choices, which involve different interpretations of the stochastic contribution.

### Itô integral ($\lambda=0$)

Following the Itô convention, let's set $\lambda=0$, obtaining:

\begin{equation}
    \boxed{\int_0^tB(t')dB(t')=\frac{B(t)^2}{2}-\frac{t}{2}}
\end{equation}

This choice is the most widespread because it brings several advantages with it, all due to an important property: by choosing $\lambda=0$, in fact, the intermediate point $\lambda t_i+(1-\lambda)t_{i-1}$ is equal to $t_{i-1}$. This means that the integrand function is evaluated at the **initial point** of each interval of the partition: in other words, the integrand does not depend in any way on a \q{future} time but only on past and present values. Functions of this type are called **non-anticipative functions**, and are the basis of mathematical analysis in the statistical field declined according to the Itô convention, called *Itô calculus*, which we will deepen later.

A first advantage of this convention is that the function $\sigma(t)$ and the differential $dB(t)$ are *independent* of each other, since the latter is relative to a future interval while the non-anticipative function depends exclusively on the initial point. In a non-rigorous way, we can therefore write:

\begin{equation}
    \left\langle\int_0^t\sigma(t')dB(t')\right\rangle=\int_0^t\langle\sigma(t')\rangle\langle dB(t')\rangle
\end{equation}

Since the differences of $B(t)$ are distributed according to a Gaussian centered at zero, the average value of $dB(t')$ will be zero, therefore

\begin{equation*}
    \left\langle\int_0^t\sigma(t')dB(t')\right\rangle=\int_0^t\langle\sigma(t')\rangle\cancel{\langle dB(t')\rangle}=0
\end{equation*}

This property is also confirmed by the particular case in which $\sigma(t)=B(t)$, as is evident from equation (eq:sde-integrale-di-ito):

\begin{equation*}
    \left\langle\int_0^tB(t')dB(t')\right\rangle=\frac{\left\langle B(t)^2\right\rangle}{2}-\frac{t}{2}=\frac{t}{2}-\frac{t}{2}=0
\end{equation*}

In general, the Itô integral is linked to the discrete form by the following relation:

\begin{equation}
    \boxed{\int_0^t\sigma(t')dB(t')=\mslim_{n\to\infty}\sum_{i=1}^n\sigma\left(t_{i-1}\right)\left[B\left(t_i\right)-B\left(t_{i-1}\right)\right]}
\end{equation}

being $\sigma(t)$ a non-anticipative function, and therefore depending exclusively on $t_{i-1}$.

\subsubsection{Proof that $dB(t)^2=dt$}

The link between Itô calculus and standard analysis is given by the following relation:

\begin{equation}
    \int_0^t\sigma(t')dB(t')^2=\int_0^t\sigma(t')dt'
\end{equation}

This, in fact, connects the two fields through the equality of the two differentials: on the one hand we have the infinitesimal square of Brownian motion, while on the other the differential of \q{deterministic} time. The first cannot exist in standard analysis, because, as we have repeated several times, the function $B(t)$ is not differentiable; nevertheless, in integral calculus, the two differentials are actually equal, thus creating a point of connection between the two \q{worlds}.\footnote{The following proof was not reported by the professor, but I interpreted it starting from *Gardiner* (second edition, paragraph $4.2.5$).}

For the proof we must consider the following limit:

\begin{equation}
    \lim_{n\to\infty}\left\langle\left[\sum_{i=1}^n\sigma_{i-1}\left(\Delta B_i^2-\Delta t_i\right)\right]^2\right\rangle
\end{equation}

having written for simplicity $\sigma_{i-1}=\sigma\left(t_{i-1}\right)$, $\Delta B_i^2=\left[B\left(t_i\right)-B\left(t_{i-1}\right)\right]^2$ and $\Delta t_i=t_i-t_{i-1}$.

Let's expand the square, also considering the double products:

\begin{equation*}
    \lim_{n\to\infty}\left\langle\sum_{i=1}^n\sigma_{i-1}^2\left(\Delta B_i^2-\Delta t_i\right)^2+2\sum_{i>j}\sigma_{i-1}\sigma_{j-1}\left(\Delta B_i^2-\Delta t_i\right)\left(\Delta B_j^2-\Delta t_j\right)\right\rangle
\end{equation*}

The term $\sigma_{i-1}$ is independent of the increments $\Delta B_i$ due to the non-anticipative property of the Itô convention. We can therefore distribute the average value among all the individual terms:

\small

\begin{equation*}
    \lim_{n\to\infty}\left[\sum_{i=1}^n\left\langle\sigma_{i-1}^2\right\rangle\left\langle\left(\Delta B_i^2-\Delta t_i\right)^2\right\rangle+2\sum_{i>j}\left\langle\sigma_{i-1}\sigma_{j-1}\right\rangle\left(\left\langle\Delta B_i^2\right\rangle-\Delta t_i\right)\left(\left\langle\Delta B_j^2\right\rangle-\Delta t_j\right)\right]
\end{equation*}

\normalsize

In the mixed terms, the contributions in parentheses are zero, since

\begin{equation*}
    \left\langle\Delta B_i^2\right\rangle-\Delta t_i=\Delta t_i-\Delta t_i=0
\end{equation*}

In the surviving term we can expand the square:

\begin{equation*}
    \left\langle\left(\Delta B_i^2-\Delta t_i\right)^2\right\rangle=\left\langle\Delta B_i^4\right\rangle-2\left\langle\Delta B_i^2\right\rangle\Delta t_i+\Delta t_i^2=3\Delta t_i^2-2\Delta t_i^2+\Delta t_i^2=2\Delta t_i^2
\end{equation*}

Substituting in the starting limit:

\begin{equation*}
    2\lim_{n\to\infty}\sum_{i=1}^n\left\langle\sigma_{i-1}^2\right\rangle\Delta t_i^2
\end{equation*}

By majorizing as we have already done previously as a function of $\abs{P_n}$, we find that the limit converges to zero:

\begin{align*}
    2\lim_{n\to\infty}\sum_{i=1}^n\left\langle\sigma_{i-1}^2\right\rangle\Delta t_i^2&\leqslant2\lim_{n\to\infty}\left(\max_i\abs{t_i-t_{i-1}}\sum_{i=1}^n\left\langle\sigma_{i-1}^2\right\rangle\Delta t_i\right)=\\
    &=2\lim_{n\to\infty}\left(\abs{P_n}\sum_{i=1}^n\left\langle\sigma_{i-1}^2\right\rangle\Delta t_i\right)=0
\end{align*}

Returning to the limit of (eq:sde-ito-differenziali-uguali-limite-iniziale), setting $x_n$ equal to the summation and $x=0$, we can write

\begin{equation*}
    \lim_{n\to\infty}\left\langle\left(x_n-x\right)^2\right\rangle=0
\end{equation*}

This expression defines the mean squared limit for $x_n$, that is, for the summation:

\begin{equation*}
    \mslim_{n\to\infty}\sum_{i=1}^n\sigma_{i-1}\left(\Delta B_i^2-\Delta t_i\right)=0
\end{equation*}

which can also be rewritten as

\begin{equation*}
    \mslim_{n\to\infty}\left(\sum_{i=1}^n\sigma_{i-1}\Delta B_i^2-\sum_{i=1}^n\sigma_{i-1}\Delta t_i\right)=0
\end{equation*}

Isolating the two terms:

\begin{equation}
    \mslim_{n\to\infty}\sum_{i=1}^n\sigma_{i-1}\Delta B_i^2=\mslim_{n\to\infty}\sum_{i=1}^n\sigma_{i-1}\Delta t_i
\end{equation}

According to the Itô convention, the two mean squared limits are none other than the integrals written in (eq:sde-ito-relazione-collante), thus proving the written relation.

### Stratonovich integral ($\lambda=\frac{1}{2}$)

Following the Stratonovich convention, let's set $\lambda=\frac{1}{2}$, obtaining:

\begin{equation}
    \boxed{\int_0^tB(t')dB(t')=\frac{B(t)^2}{2}}
\end{equation}

In this case the average value of the integral is not zero, since

\begin{equation*}
    \left\langle\int_0^tB(t')dB(t')\right\rangle=\frac{\left\langle B(t)^2\right\rangle}{2}=\frac{t}{2}
\end{equation*}

Intuitively, this means that the Brownian motion $B(t)$ and its increment $dB(t)$ are not uncorrelated with each other, and this depends on the fact that the point considered in which the function is evaluated at the limit of the Riemann sum is the *intermediate* point between $t_{i-1}$ and $t_i$:

\begin{equation*}
    \lambda t_i+(1-\lambda)t_{i-1} \ \xrightarrow{\lambda=\frac{1}{2}} \ \frac{1}{2}t_i+\left(1-\frac{1}{2}\right)t_{i-1}=\frac{1}{2}t_i+\frac{1}{2}t_{i-1}=\frac{1}{2}\left(t_i+t_{i-1}\right)
\end{equation*}

This means that intrinsically, in the calculation, we partially consider a \q{future} time in each interval, which precisely constrains the correlation between the two quantities.

The Stratonovich convention is mostly used in the theoretical field, especially in the physical one, where the choice of the intermediate point corresponds to the most \q{natural} behavior in the evolution of a system in which dissipative effects and stochastic fluctuations intervene. To this is added the fact that in the choice of Stratonovich the **chain rule** of standard mathematical analysis is preserved, and this allows one to easily perform substitutions of variables as is common in calculations in physics.

In this sense, it is often said that choosing the Stratonovich convention means \q{using standard calculus}; nevertheless, below we will exclusively deepen the Itô calculus, which finds more practical applications, especially in the field of finance, where all calculations are carried out taking into consideration only the knowledge at the current time, thus not having to consider future events.

## Itô calculus

What we will derive below are two important laws, which define the main properties of infinitesimal analysis: the *differentiation rule* and the *chain rule*, the latter also called the *Itô formula* in the context in which we find ourselves.

In both cases we will refer to a fundamental relation that we have already expressed in the past, both in equation (eq:processi-stocastici-wiener-andamento-modulo-delta-B) and in equation (eq:sde-ito-relazione-collante), according to which

\begin{equation}
    \Delta B^2=\Delta t
\end{equation}

This relation, in a non-rigorous way, we can also write it in infinitesimal form through the differential:

\begin{equation}
    dB^2=dt
\end{equation}

### Differentiation rule

Let's consider a function $u(B(t),t)$. The differential $du$ will therefore be a function of $dt$ and of $dB$. Furthermore, since $dB^2=dt$, the quadratic term $dB^2$ of the expansion is not negligible, but we must include it in the expansion:

\begin{equation*}
    du=\frac{\partial u}{\partial t}dt+\frac{\partial u}{\partial B}dB+\frac{1}{2}\frac{\partial^2u}{\partial B^2}dB^2=\frac{\partial u}{\partial t}dt+\frac{\partial u}{\partial B}dB+\frac{1}{2}\frac{\partial^2u}{\partial B^2}dt
\end{equation*}

Ultimately, the differential $du$ is equal to

\begin{equation}
    \boxed{du=\left(\frac{\partial u}{\partial t}+\frac{1}{2}\frac{\partial^2u}{\partial B^2}\right)dt+\frac{\partial u}{\partial B}dB}
\end{equation}

Let's take for example $u=B^2$; not depending explicitly on $t$, then $\frac{\partial u}{\partial t}=0$, and what remains is:

\begin{equation*}
    du\equiv dB^2=\frac{1}{2}\frac{\partial^2\left(B^2\right)}{\partial B^2}dt+\frac{\partial\left(B^2\right)}{\partial B}dB=\frac{1}{\cancel{2}}\cdot\cancel{2}dt+2BdB
\end{equation*}

Integrating this relation we obtain:

\begin{equation*}
    \int_0^tdB(t')^2=\int_0^tdt'+2\int_0^tB(t')dB(t') \ \Longrightarrow \ B(t)^2=t+2\int_0^tB(t')dB(t')
\end{equation*}

It follows that

\begin{equation*}
    \int_0^tB(t')dB(t')=\frac{B(t)^2}{2}-\frac{t}{2}
\end{equation*}

which is precisely the Itô integral reported in equation (eq:sde-integrale-di-ito).

### Chain rule

Let's consider a function $x(t)$ that satisfies the following stochastic differential equation:

\begin{equation*}
    dx=\mu(x,t)dt+\sigma(x,t)dB
\end{equation*}

Let's now consider a function $u(x(t),t)$. Due to the dependence of $dx$ on $dB$, we will have to expand the differential of $u$ up to the second order of $x$:

\begin{equation*}
    du=\frac{\partial u}{\partial t}dt+\frac{\partial u}{\partial x}dx+\frac{1}{2}\frac{\partial^2u}{\partial x^2}dx^2=\frac{\partial u}{\partial t}dt+\frac{\partial u}{\partial x}(\mu dt+\sigma dB)+\frac{1}{2}\frac{\partial^2u}{\partial x^2}(\mu dt+\sigma dB)^2
\end{equation*}

Obviously, to do this, we must explicitly assume that $u\in C(t)^1$ and that $u\in C(x)^2$.

In the expansion of the square, we only obtain terms of order higher than $dt$, with the exception of $dB^2$ which, as we know, corresponds to $dt$:

\begin{equation*}
    du=\frac{\partial u}{\partial t}dt+\frac{\partial u}{\partial x}(\mu dt+\sigma dB)+\frac{1}{2}\frac{\partial^2u}{\partial x^2}\sigma^2dt
\end{equation*}

By collecting with respect to $dt$ and $dB$ we obtain the chain rule, also called the **Itô formula**:

\begin{equation}
    \boxed{du=\left(\frac{\partial u}{\partial t}+\frac{\partial u}{\partial x}\mu+\frac{1}{2}\frac{\partial^2u}{\partial x^2}\sigma^2\right)dt+\frac{\partial u}{\partial x}\sigma dB}
\end{equation}
