6 Ordinary differential equation systems

6.1 Review of dynamical systems

Many applications (physics, biology, chemistry, economics, …) lead to nonlinear systems of ordinary differential equations (ODEs) \[\begin{equation} \begin{cases} \dot{\mathbf{x}}(t)=\mathbf{f}(\mathbf{x}(t)),\\[4pt] \mathbf{x}(0)=\mathbf{x}_0, \end{cases} \end{equation}\] where \(\mathbf{x}(t)=(x_1(t),\dots,x_N(t))\in U\subseteq\mathbb{R}^N\), with \(U\) an open connected set. The map \(\mathbf{f}:U\to\mathbb{R}^N\) is called a and \(U\) is the . A solution \(\mathbf{x}(t;\mathbf{x}_0)\) is a trajectory; trajectories are smooth curves in phase space parametrised by time.

At each point \(\mathbf{x}\in U\) the vector \(\mathbf{f}(\mathbf{x})\) indicates the instantaneous direction of motion. By ``flowing’’ along the vector field a point traces a trajectory.

Definition (trajectory). A curve \(t\mapsto\mathbf{x}(t)\) satisfying \(\eqref{eq:autonomous}\) for \(t\) in some interval is a trajectory (or orbit) of the system.

Remark. For autonomous systems the vector field does not depend explicitly on time; time-translations of trajectories are again trajectories.

6.1.1 Nullclines and phase portraits

Nullclines are subsets of phase space where a component of the vector field vanishes. For a two-dimensional system \[ \begin{cases} \dot{x}_1=f_1(x_1,x_2),\\[3pt] \dot{x}_2=f_2(x_1,x_2), \end{cases} \] the \(x_1\)-nullcline is \(\{(x_1,x_2)\mid f_1(x_1,x_2)=0\}\) and the \(x_2\)-nullcline is \(\{f_2=0\}\). Intersections of nullclines are fixed points (equilibria). A phase portrait is the collection of trajectories with arrows showing the direction of motion.

6.2 Simple examples

Example 1 (one dimension). \[ \dot{x}=\sin x. \] The vector field is scalar; equilibria occur where \(\sin x=0\), i.e. \(x=k\pi\), \(k\in\mathbb{Z}\). The sign of \(\sin x\) determines motion between equilibria.

Example 2 (two dimensions). \[ \begin{cases} \dot{x}=1,\\[2pt] \dot{y}=x^2+y^2. \end{cases} \]

6.3 Existence and uniqueness: Picard–Lindelöf theorem

Theorem (Picard–Lindelöf / Picard). Suppose \(\mathbf{f}:U\subset\mathbb{R}^N\to\mathbb{R}^N\) is continuous on an open set \(U\) and is Lipschitz in \(\mathbf{x}\) on a neighbourhood of \(\mathbf{x}_0\) (for instance if \(\mathbf{f}\) and all first partial derivatives \(\partial f_i/\partial x_j\) are continuous). Then there exists \(\delta>0\) and a unique solution \(\mathbf{x}(t)\) of \(\eqref{eq:autonomous}\) defined for \(t\in(-\delta,\delta)\) with \(\mathbf{x}(0)=\mathbf{x}_0\).

Corollary. Under the hypotheses above trajectories do not intersect in phase space.

Remarks. - Lipschitz continuity is the key hypothesis for uniqueness. Continuity alone guarantees existence but not uniqueness. - Solutions may blow up in finite time (maximal interval of existence may be bounded).

6.4 Fixed points and linearization

Definition (fixed point / equilibrium). A point \(\mathbf{x}^*\in U\) is a fixed point if \[ \mathbf{f}(\mathbf{x}^*)=\mathbf{0}. \] To study local behaviour near a fixed point we set \(\mathbf{u}(t)=\mathbf{x}(t)-\mathbf{x}^*\) and Taylor-expand: \[ \dot{\mathbf{u}}=A\,\mathbf{u}+ \mathbf{R}(\mathbf{u}), \] where the Jacobian matrix \[ A\;=\;\left.\left(\frac{\partial f_i}{\partial x_j}\right)\right|_{\mathbf{x}^*} \] and the remainder satisfies \(\mathbf{R}(\mathbf{u})=o(\|\mathbf{u}\|)\) as \(\mathbf{u}\to\mathbf{0}\). The is the linear system \[ \dot{\mathbf{u}}=A\mathbf{u}. \] Question: when does the linearization correctly describe the nonlinear system? The next section gives a sufficient condition.

6.5 Hyperbolic fixed points and the Hartman–Grobman theorem

Definition. A fixed point \(\mathbf{x}^*\) is if no eigenvalue \(\lambda\) of the Jacobian \(A\) has zero real part, i.e. \(\operatorname{Re}\lambda\neq0\) for all eigenvalues.

Theorem (Hartman–Grobman). If \(\mathbf{x}^*\) is a hyperbolic fixed point of \(\eqref{eq:autonomous}\), then there exists a homeomorphism mapping a neighbourhood of \(\mathbf{x}^*\) onto a neighbourhood of the origin that conjugates the nonlinear flow to the linear flow generated by \(A\). In other words, the phase portrait near a hyperbolic fixed point is topologically equivalent to that of the linearization.

Consequence. For a hyperbolic equilibrium the linearized system determines stability type (node, saddle, focus, etc.).

Linearization is inconclusive when eigenvalues lie on the imaginary axis. Two instructive one-dimensional examples illustrate non-uniqueness and finite-time blow-up.

Example (finite-time blow-up). \[ \dot{y}=y^2,\qquad y(0)=1. \] Separation gives \(\int y^{-2}\,dy=t+c\), so \(-y^{-1}=t+c\) and \[ y(t)=\frac{1}{-t-c}=\frac{1}{1-t} \] (using initial condition to get \(c=-1\)). This solution blows up at \(t=1\), so it exists only on the maximal interval \((-\infty,1)\).

Example (non-uniqueness). \[ \dot{y}=\sqrt{y},\qquad y(0)=0. \] Separation yields \(2\sqrt{y}=t+C\). One solution is \(y(t)=\frac{t^2}{4}\) for \(t\ge0\). However \(y(t)\equiv0\) is also a solution, and more generally the family \[ y_T(t)=\begin{cases} 0, & 0\le t\le T,\\[4pt] \dfrac{(t-T)^2}{4}, & t>T, \end{cases}\qquad T\ge0, \] provides infinitely many solutions with the same initial condition. Non-uniqueness arises because the vector field \(\sqrt{y}\) fails to be Lipschitz at \(y=0\).

6.6 Stable, unstable and center manifolds (brief)

For a hyperbolic fixed point, the linear system decomposes the phase space into stable and unstable eigenspaces (associated with eigenvalues of negative or positive real part). For the nonlinear system there exist invariant stable and unstable manifolds tangent to those eigenspaces at the fixed point. (The center manifold theory treats eigenvalues with zero real part and requires finer analysis.)

# Ordinary differential equation systems

## Review of dynamical systems

Many applications (physics, biology, chemistry, economics, ...) lead to nonlinear systems of ordinary differential equations (ODEs)
\begin{equation}
\begin{cases}
\dot{\mathbf{x}}(t)=\mathbf{f}(\mathbf{x}(t)),\\[4pt]
\mathbf{x}(0)=\mathbf{x}_0,
\end{cases}
\end{equation}
where $\mathbf{x}(t)=(x_1(t),\dots,x_N(t))\in U\subseteq\mathbb{R}^N$, with $U$ an open connected set. The map $\mathbf{f}:U\to\mathbb{R}^N$ is called a \emph{vector field} and $U$ is the \emph{phase space}. A solution $\mathbf{x}(t;\mathbf{x}_0)$ is a trajectory; trajectories are smooth curves in phase space parametrised by time.

At each point $\mathbf{x}\in U$ the vector $\mathbf{f}(\mathbf{x})$ indicates the instantaneous direction of motion. By ``flowing'' along the vector field a point traces a trajectory.

Definition (trajectory). A curve $t\mapsto\mathbf{x}(t)$ satisfying \eqref{eq:autonomous} for $t$ in some interval is a trajectory (or orbit) of the system.

Remark. For autonomous systems the vector field does not depend explicitly on time; time-translations of trajectories are again trajectories.

### Nullclines and phase portraits

Nullclines are subsets of phase space where a component of the vector field vanishes. For a two-dimensional system
$$
\begin{cases}
\dot{x}_1=f_1(x_1,x_2),\\[3pt]
\dot{x}_2=f_2(x_1,x_2),
\end{cases}
$$
the $x_1$-nullcline is $\{(x_1,x_2)\mid f_1(x_1,x_2)=0\}$ and the $x_2$-nullcline is $\{f_2=0\}$. Intersections of nullclines are fixed points (equilibria). A phase portrait is the collection of trajectories with arrows showing the direction of motion.

## Existence and uniqueness: Picard--Lindelöf theorem

Theorem (Picard--Lindelöf / Picard). Suppose $\mathbf{f}:U\subset\mathbb{R}^N\to\mathbb{R}^N$ is continuous on an open set $U$ and is Lipschitz in $\mathbf{x}$ on a neighbourhood of $\mathbf{x}_0$ (for instance if $\mathbf{f}$ and all first partial derivatives $\partial f_i/\partial x_j$ are continuous). Then there exists $\delta>0$ and a unique solution $\mathbf{x}(t)$ of \eqref{eq:autonomous} defined for $t\in(-\delta,\delta)$ with $\mathbf{x}(0)=\mathbf{x}_0$.

Corollary. Under the hypotheses above trajectories do not intersect in phase space.

Remarks.
- Lipschitz continuity is the key hypothesis for uniqueness. Continuity alone guarantees existence but not uniqueness.
- Solutions may blow up in finite time (maximal interval of existence may be bounded).

## Fixed points and linearization

Definition (fixed point / equilibrium). A point $\mathbf{x}^*\in U$ is a fixed point if
$$
\mathbf{f}(\mathbf{x}^*)=\mathbf{0}.
$$
To study local behaviour near a fixed point we set $\mathbf{u}(t)=\mathbf{x}(t)-\mathbf{x}^*$ and Taylor-expand:
$$
\dot{\mathbf{u}}=A\,\mathbf{u}+ \mathbf{R}(\mathbf{u}),
$$
where the Jacobian matrix
$$
A\;=\;\left.\left(\frac{\partial f_i}{\partial x_j}\right)\right|_{\mathbf{x}^*}
$$
and the remainder satisfies $\mathbf{R}(\mathbf{u})=o(\|\mathbf{u}\|)$ as $\mathbf{u}\to\mathbf{0}$. The \emph{linearization} is the linear system
$$
\dot{\mathbf{u}}=A\mathbf{u}.
$$
Question: when does the linearization correctly describe the nonlinear system? The next section gives a sufficient condition.

## Hyperbolic fixed points and the Hartman--Grobman theorem

Definition. A fixed point $\mathbf{x}^*$ is \emph{hyperbolic} if no eigenvalue $\lambda$ of the Jacobian $A$ has zero real part, i.e.\ $\operatorname{Re}\lambda\neq0$ for all eigenvalues.

Theorem (Hartman--Grobman). If $\mathbf{x}^*$ is a hyperbolic fixed point of \eqref{eq:autonomous}, then there exists a homeomorphism mapping a neighbourhood of $\mathbf{x}^*$ onto a neighbourhood of the origin that conjugates the nonlinear flow to the linear flow generated by $A$. In other words, the phase portrait near a hyperbolic fixed point is topologically equivalent to that of the linearization.

Consequence. For a hyperbolic equilibrium the linearized system determines stability type (node, saddle, focus, etc.).

### Explicit classification of $2\times2$ linear systems

Consider the linear system $\dot{\mathbf{u}}=A\mathbf{u}$ with
$$
A=\begin{pmatrix} a & b\\[2pt] c & d\end{pmatrix},\qquad
T=\operatorname{tr}A=a+d,\qquad D=\det A=ad-bc,
$$
and discriminant
$$
\Delta \;=\; T^2-4D.
$$
The eigenvalues are $\lambda_{1,2}=(T\pm\sqrt{\Delta})/2$. The local phase portrait type at the origin is determined by the signs of $T$, $D$ and the value of $\Delta$ as follows.

\begin{enumerate}
\item **Saddle (unstable).**
  \begin{itemize}
  \item Condition: $D<0$ (equivalently eigenvalues real with opposite signs).
  \item Geometry: one stable and one unstable direction; all nontrivial trajectories approach along the stable eigendirection and depart along the unstable eigendirection.
  \item Canonical linear form: $\dot u=\lambda_1 u,\ \dot v=\lambda_2 v$ with $\lambda_1<0<\lambda_2$.
  \end{itemize}

\item **Node (real, same sign eigenvalues, $\Delta>0$, $D>0$).**
  \begin{itemize}
  \item Stable node: $D>0$, $\Delta>0$, and $T<0$ (both eigenvalues real and negative).
  \item Unstable node: $D>0$, $\Delta>0$, and $T>0$ (both eigenvalues real and positive).
  \item If $A$ is diagonalizable the node is proper (star-like); if defective (one eigenvector) the node is an improper or degenerate node (Jordan form) showing tangent flow lines leading to the repeated direction.
  \end{itemize}

\item **Spiral (focus) — complex conjugates ($\Delta<0$, $D>0$).**
  \begin{itemize}
  \item Eigenvalues: $\lambda=\alpha\pm i\beta$ with $\alpha=T/2$, $\beta=\sqrt{4D-T^2}/2$.
  \item Stable spiral (spiral sink): $T<0$ ($\alpha<0$) — trajectories spiral into the origin.
  \item Unstable spiral (spiral source): $T>0$ ($\alpha>0$) — trajectories spiral out from the origin.
  \item Center (linear): $T=0$ and $\Delta<0$ ($\alpha=0$) gives purely imaginary eigenvalues; linear system produces closed orbits (center), but nonlinear terms determine actual stability.
  \end{itemize}

\item **Repeated eigenvalue ($\Delta=0$).**
  \begin{itemize}
  \item The eigenvalue is $\lambda=T/2$ with algebraic multiplicity two and $D=T^2/4$.
  \item If $\lambda<0$ the equilibrium is (linearly) asymptotically stable; if $\lambda>0$ it is unstable; if $\lambda=0$ it is non-hyperbolic.
  \item Distinguish diagonalizable (two independent eigenvectors, ``proper star node'') versus defective (one eigenvector, Jordan block; solutions contain $t e^{\lambda t}$ terms producing tangential flow).
  \end{itemize}
\end{enumerate}

Summary decision flow (use these tests in order):
\begin{enumerate}
\item If $D<0$ $\Rightarrow$ saddle (unstable).
\item Else ($D>0$): compute $\Delta=T^2-4D$.
  \begin{itemize}
  \item If $\Delta>0$ $\Rightarrow$ real eigenvalues (node): sign of $T$ gives stability.
  \item If $\Delta<0$ $\Rightarrow$ complex conjugates (spiral or center): sign of $T$ gives stability; $T=0$ is the linear center.
  \item If $\Delta=0$ $\Rightarrow$ repeated eigenvalue (investigate diagonalizability).
  \end{itemize}
\end{enumerate}

Remarks and practical checks:
\begin{itemize}
\item Hyperbolicity test: equilibrium is hyperbolic iff $D\neq0$ and $T^2\neq4D$ (equivalently no eigenvalue with zero real part).
\item A simple numeric check in code: compute $T$ and $D$ and branch using the flow above to choose a canonical phase portrait to draw.
\item For nonlinear systems linearized at $\mathbf{x}^*$, if the Jacobian satisfies the hyperbolicity condition above, Hartman--Grobman guarantees topological equivalence between the nonlinear and linear phase portraits in a neighbourhood of $\mathbf{x}^*$.
\end{itemize}

## When linearization fails: non-hyperbolic cases and examples

Linearization is inconclusive when eigenvalues lie on the imaginary axis. Two instructive one-dimensional examples illustrate non-uniqueness and finite-time blow-up.

Example (finite-time blow-up).
$$
\dot{y}=y^2,\qquad y(0)=1.
$$
Separation gives $\int y^{-2}\,dy=t+c$, so $-y^{-1}=t+c$ and
$$
y(t)=\frac{1}{-t-c}=\frac{1}{1-t}
$$
(using initial condition to get $c=-1$). This solution blows up at $t=1$, so it exists only on the maximal interval $(-\infty,1)$.

Example (non-uniqueness).
$$
\dot{y}=\sqrt{y},\qquad y(0)=0.
$$
Separation yields $2\sqrt{y}=t+C$. One solution is $y(t)=\frac{t^2}{4}$ for $t\ge0$. However $y(t)\equiv0$ is also a solution, and more generally the family
$$
y_T(t)=\begin{cases}
0, & 0\le t\le T,\\[4pt]
\dfrac{(t-T)^2}{4}, & t>T,
\end{cases}\qquad T\ge0,
$$
provides infinitely many solutions with the same initial condition. Non-uniqueness arises because the vector field $\sqrt{y}$ fails to be Lipschitz at $y=0$.
