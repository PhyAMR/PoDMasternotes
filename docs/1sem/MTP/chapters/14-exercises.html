*Write the characteristic function* $\phi(k)$ *for the pdf*

\begin{equation*}

    \begin{cases}

\end{equation*}

*Then use the moment generating property of* $\phi(k)$ *to calculate the mean* $\langle x\rangle$ *of the distribution p.*

Let's calculate the characteristic function as defined (equation (eq:integrali-gaussiani-funzioni-caratteristiche-definizione)):

\begin{align*}
    \phi(k)&=\cancel{\int_{-\infty}^0e^{ikx}\cdot0dx}+\int_0^{a}e^{ikx}\cdot\frac{1}{a}dx+\cancel{\int_a^{+\infty}e^{ikx}\cdot0dx}=\
    &=\frac{1}{iak}e^{ikx}\bigg|_0^a=\frac{1}{iak}\left(e^{iak}-1\right)
\end{align*}

The mean relative to the distribution $p$ will be given by

\begin{equation*}
    \langle x\rangle=-i\frac{d\phi}{dk}\bigg|_{k=0}
\end{equation*}

so let's first calculate the derivative of $\phi$:

\begin{align*}
    \frac{d\phi}{dk}&=\frac{d}{dk}\left[\frac{1}{iak}\left(e^{iak}-1\right)\right]=-\frac{1}{iak^2}\left(e^{iak}-1\right)+\frac{1}{iak}\cdot iae^{iak}=\
    &=\frac{1-e^{iak}}{iak^2}+\frac{e^{iak}}{k}=\frac{1-e^{iak}+iake^{iak}}{iak^2}=\frac{1+\left(iak-1\right)e^{iak}}{iak^2}
\end{align*}

The substitution $k=0$ leads us to the indeterminate form $\frac{0}{0}$, so we are forced to calculate the limit for $k\to0$, which can be obtained using De L'Hôpital's rule.

\begin{align*}
    \lim_{k\to0}\frac{d\phi}{dk}&=\lim_{k\to0}\frac{iae^{iak}+ia(iak-1)e^{iak}}{2iak}=\
    &=\lim_{k\to0}\frac{iae^{iak}+i^2a^2ke^{iak}-iae^{iak}}{2iak}=\lim_{k\to0}\frac{-a^2ke^{iak}}{2iak}=-\frac{a}{2i}
\end{align*}

From this it follows that

\begin{equation*}
    \langle x\rangle=-i\lim_{k\to0}\frac{d\phi}{dk}=-i\left(-\frac{a}{2i}\right)=\frac{a}{2}

\begin{equation*}
    I(s)=\int_0^\infty xe^{sx-\frac{e^{2x}}{2}}dx
\end{equation*}

*in the limit for* $s$ *large.*

Let's factor out $s$ in the exponent:

\begin{equation*}
    I(s)=\int_0^\infty xe^{s\left(x-\frac{e^{2x}}{2s}\right)}dx
\end{equation*}

Let's consider the function

\begin{equation*}
    f(x)=x-\frac{e^{2x}}{2s}
\end{equation*}

and let's calculate its first and second derivatives:

\begin{equation*}
    f'(x)=1-\frac{e^{2x}}{s} \ \Longrightarrow \ f''(x)=-\frac{2e^{2x}}{s}
\end{equation*}

From the second derivative we understand that it is a concave function in the whole interval, since the numerator and denominator are both positive quantities, so with a minus sign in front the whole function is negative.

Let's now calculate the maximum point:

\begin{equation*}
    f'(x)=0 \ \iff \ e^{2x_0}=s \ \Longrightarrow \ x_0=\frac{1}{2}\ln s
\end{equation*}

At this point the second derivative is equal to

\begin{equation*}
    f''\left(x_0\right)=-\frac{2e^{\ln s}}{s}=-\frac{2\cancel{s}}{\cancel{s}}=-2
\end{equation*}

We therefore understand that although the maximum point is not fixed, growing as $s$ grows, the concavity at the point instead remains constant. Let's remember that the Laplace method is based on the approximation to a Gaussian of the exponential function, whose exponent is given by

\begin{equation*}
    s f(x_0) + \frac{s f''(x_0)}{2}(x-x_0)^2
\end{equation*}

This means that the center of the Gaussian would shift to the right, thus remaining within the interval $[0,\infty)$, and that the variance - linked to the concavity - would instead always remain the same. This reasoning allows us to conclude that it is possible to apply the Laplace method as we know it, although there is a dependence of $f(x)$ on the parameter $s$.

Let's therefore calculate the value of $f(x)$ at $x_0$:

\begin{equation*}
    f\left(x_0\right)=\frac{1}{2}\ln s-\frac{s}{2s}=\frac{1}{2}\left(\ln s-1\right)
\end{equation*}

The final result will therefore be

\begin{equation*}
    I(s)\simeq g\left(x_0\right)e^{sf\left(x_0\right)}\sqrt{\frac{2\pi}{s\abs{f''\left(x_0\right)}}}=\frac{\ln s}{2}e^{\frac{s}{2}\left(\ln s-1\right)}\sqrt{\frac{2\pi}{2s}}=\sqrt{\frac{\pi}{s}}\frac{\ln s}{2}e^{\frac{s}{2}(\ln s-1)}
\end{equation*}

First of all, the variables in this case can only assume integer values, so what is an integral in the general formula becomes a summation here; moreover, being a Poisson process, the summed variable $n_1$ cannot assume all possible values in $\mathbb{N}$: being associated with the number of events that occurred at time $t_1$, it will be limited below by $n_0$ (number of events that occurred at time $t_0<t_1$) and above by $n_2$ (number of events that occurred at time $t_2>t_1$).

\end{align*}

To obtain a summation that starts from zero, let's change the index: $k=n_1-n_0$. When $n_1=n_0, k=0$. When $n_1=n_2, k=n_2-n_0$.
$n_1 = k+n_0$. $n_2-n_1 = n_2-k-n_0$.

\begin{align*}

\end{align*}

It now remains to prove the equation (eq:processi-stocastici-relazione-distribuzione-iniziale-propagatore); this time, since the initial distribution is $p_1(n,t_1)$, we have:

\begin{align*}
    p_1(n_2,t_2) &= \sum_{n_1=0}^{n_2} p_{1|1}(n_2,t_2|n_1,t_1) p_1(n_1,t_1) \\
    &=\sum_{n_1=0}^{n_2}\frac{\left(t_2-t_1\right)^{n_2-n_1}}{\left(n_2-n_1\right)!}e^{-\left(t_2-t_1\right)}\cdot\frac{t_1^{n_1}}{n_1!}e^{-t_1}=\\
    &=e^{-t_2}\sum_{n_1=0}^{n_2}\frac{\left(t_2-t_1\right)^{n_2-n_1}t_1^{n_1}}{\left(n_2-n_1\right)!n_1!} \\
    &=\frac{e^{-t_2}}{n_2!}\sum_{n_1=0}^{n_2}\binom{n_2}{n_1}\left(t_2-t_1\right)^{n_2-n_1}t_1^{n_1}=\frac{e^{-t_2}}{n_2!}\left(t_2-t_1+t_1\right)^{n_2} = \frac{t_2^{n_2}}{n_2!}e^{-t_2}
\end{align*}

This shows that the Poisson process thus defined is a Markov process. Since $p_1(n,t)$ explicitly depends on time, it is a non-stationary Markov process.

\end{equation*}

::: {.exercise name="Assigned in class - 29/11/2024"}
    *Show that the Poisson process defined by*

    \begin{equation*}
        p_{1|1}\left(n_2,t_2|n_1,t_1\right)=\frac{\left(\lambda(t_2-t_1)\right)^{n_2-n_1}}{\left(n_2-n_1\right)!}e^{-\lambda\left(t_2-t_1\right)}   t_2>t_1
    \end{equation*}

    *and by*

    \begin{equation*}
        p_1(n,0)=\delta_{n,0} \ \Longrightarrow \ p_1(n,t)=\frac{(\lambda t)^n}{n!}e^{-\lambda t}
    \end{equation*}

    *is a non-stationary Markov process. (Assuming rate $\lambda=1$ as in the original text).*

    First, let's prove the Chapman-Kolmogorov equation ((eq:processi-stocastici-equazione-di-chapman-kolmogorov)). First of all, the variables in this case can only assume integer values, so what is an integral in the general formula becomes a summation here; moreover, being a Poisson process, the summed variable $n_1$ cannot assume all possible values in $\mathbb{N}$: being associated with the number of events that occurred at time $t_1$, it will be limited below by $n_0$ (number of events that occurred at time $t_0<t_1$) and above by $n_2$ (number of events that occurred at time $t_2>t_1$).

    We will therefore have to calculate the following quantity:

    \begin{align*}
        &\sum_{n_1=n_0}^{n_2} p_{1|1}(n_2,t_2|n_1,t_1) p_{1|1}(n_1,t_1|n_0,t_0) \\
        &= \sum_{n_1=n_0}^{n_2}\frac{\left(t_2-t_1\right)^{n_2-n_1}}{\left(n_2-n_1\right)!}e^{-\left(t_2-t_1\right)}\cdot\frac{\left(t_1-t_0\right)^{n_1-n_0}}{\left(n_1-n_0\right)!}e^{-\left(t_1-t_0\right)}\\
        &=e^{-\left(t_2-t_0\right)}\sum_{n_1=n_0}^{n_2}\frac{\left(t_2-t_1\right)^{n_2-n_1}\left(t_1-t_0\right)^{n_1-n_0}}{\left(n_2-n_1\right)!\left(n_1-n_0\right)!}
    \end{align*}

    To obtain a summation that starts from zero, let's change the index: $k=n_1-n_0$. When $n_1=n_0, k=0$. When $n_1=n_2, k=n_2-n_0$.
    $n_1 = k+n_0$. $n_2-n_1 = n_2-k-n_0$.

    \begin{align*}
        &=e^{-\left(t_2-t_0\right)}\sum_{k=0}^{n_2-n_0}\frac{\left(t_2-t_1\right)^{n_2-n_0-k}\left(t_1-t_0\right)^{k}}{\left(n_2-n_0-k\right)!k!} \\
        &=\frac{e^{-\left(t_2-t_0\right)}}{(n_2-n_0)!} \sum_{k=0}^{n_2-n_0}\frac{(n_2-n_0)!}{\left(n_2-n_0-k\right)!k!} \left(t_2-t_1\right)^{n_2-n_0-k}\left(t_1-t_0\right)^{k} \\
        &=\frac{e^{-\left(t_2-t_0\right)}}{(n_2-n_0)!} (t_2-t_1+t_1-t_0)^{n_2-n_0} \\
        &=\frac{(t_2-t_0)^{n_2-n_0}}{(n_2-n_0)!}e^{-(t_2-t_0)} = p_{1|1}(n_2,t_2|n_0,t_0)
    \end{align*}

    It now remains to prove the equation (eq:processi-stocastici-relazione-distribuzione-iniziale-propagatore); this time, since the initial distribution is $p_1(n,t_1)$, we have:

    \begin{align*}
        p_1(n_2,t_2) &= \sum_{n_1=0}^{n_2} p_{1|1}(n_2,t_2|n_1,t_1) p_1(n_1,t_1) \\
        &=\sum_{n_1=0}^{n_2}\frac{\left(t_2-t_1\right)^{n_2-n_1}}{\left(n_2-n_1\right)!}e^{-\left(t_2-t_1\right)}\cdot\frac{t_1^{n_1}}{n_1!}e^{-t_1}=\\
        &=e^{-t_2}\sum_{n_1=0}^{n_2}\frac{\left(t_2-t_1\right)^{n_2-n_1}t_1^{n_1}}{\left(n_2-n_1\right)!n_1!} \\
        &=\frac{e^{-t_2}}{n_2!}\sum_{n_1=0}^{n_2}\binom{n_2}{n_1}\left(t_2-t_1\right)^{n_2-n_1}t_1^{n_1}=\frac{e^{-t_2}}{n_2!}\left(t_2-t_1+t_1\right)^{n_2} = \frac{t_2^{n_2}}{n_2!}e^{-t_2}
    \end{align*}

    This shows that the Poisson process thus defined is a Markov process. Since $p_1(n,t)$ explicitly depends on time, it is a non-stationary Markov process.

    *which is called the log-normal distribution.*

    $dY = \frac{\partial Y}{\partial t} dt + \frac{\partial Y}{\partial B} dB + \frac{1}{2}\frac{\partial^2 Y}{\partial B^2} (dB)^2$.
    $\frac{\partial Y}{\partial t} = -\frac{\lambda^2}{2} Y$.
    $\frac{\partial Y}{\partial B} = \lambda Y$.
    $\frac{\partial^2 Y}{\partial B^2} = \lambda^2 Y$.
    $dY = -\frac{\lambda^2}{2} Y dt + \lambda Y dB + \frac{1}{2} \lambda^2 Y dt = \lambda Y dB$.

    It is also evident that

    \begin{equation*}
        Y(0)=e^{\lambda B(0)-\frac{\lambda^2}{2}\cdot0}=e^{0}=1
    \end{equation*}

    therefore $Y(t)$ solves the SDE written in the assignment.

    Let's now try to calculate the average value:

    \begin{equation*}
        \langle Y(t)\rangle=\left\langle e^{\lambda B(t)-\frac{\lambda^2}{2}t}\right\rangle=e^{-\frac{\lambda^2}{2}t}\left\langle e^{\lambda B(t)}\right\rangle
    \end{equation*}

    We know that $B(t)$ follows a Gaussian distribution centered at zero and with variance $t$. The expectation $\langle e^{\lambda B(t)} \rangle$ is the moment generating function of a $N(0,t)$ variable, evaluated at $\lambda$.
    $M_{B(t)}(k) = e^{\frac{t k^2}{2}}$. So $\langle e^{\lambda B(t)} \rangle = e^{\frac{t\lambda^2}{2}}$.

    \begin{align*}
        \langle Y(t)\rangle&=e^{-\frac{\lambda^2}{2}t} e^{\frac{\lambda^2 t}{2}} = 1
    \end{align*}

    Finally, to calculate the distribution, let's start from the logarithm of $Y(t)$:

    \begin{equation*}
        \ln Y(t)=\lambda B(t)-\frac{\lambda^2}{2}t
    \end{equation*}
    Since $B(t) \sim \mathcal{N}(0,t)$, then $\lambda B(t) \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \lambda^2 t)$.
    So, $\ln Y(t)$ is a normally distributed random variable with mean $-\frac{\lambda^2}{2}t$ and variance $\lambda^2 t$.
    Let $X = \ln Y(t)$. The pdf of $X$ is
    \begin{equation*}
        p_X(x,t)=\frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi\lambda^2t}}e^{-\frac{\left(x+\frac{\lambda^2}{2}t\right)^2}{2\lambda^2t}}
    \end{equation*}

    To pass to the distribution relative to $Y(t)$ we must perform a change of variable, from $x$ to $y=e^x$, so $x=\ln y$. We must multiply by the Jacobian $\abs{\frac{dx}{dy}} = \frac{1}{y}$.

    \begin{align*}
        p_{Y}(y,t)&=p_{X}(\ln y,t)\abs{\frac{d x}{d y}}=\frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi\lambda^2t}}e^{-\frac{\left(\ln y+\frac{\lambda^2}{2}t\right)^2}{2\lambda^2t}}\cdot\frac{1}{y}
    \end{align*}

    *The stochastic process* $x(t)$ *that defines the following Itô stochastic differential equation is called  extbf{geometric Brownian motion*:}

    \begin{equation*}
        \begin{cases}
            dx=\mu x dt+\sigma x dB\\
            x(0)=x_0
        \end{cases}
    \end{equation*}
    (The problem in the text has $\mu=1/2, \sigma=1, x_0=1$)

    *Show that the stochastic process* $y=\ln x$ *satisfies the following Itô stochastic differential equation:*

    \begin{equation*}
        \begin{cases}
            dy=dB\\
            y(0)=0
        \end{cases}
    \end{equation*}

    *and that therefore the solution of the original differential equation is* $x(t)=e^{B(t)}$*.*

    Let's make the differential of $y=\ln x$ explicit using Itô's lemma:

    \begin{equation*}
        dy=\frac{\partial(\ln x)}{\partial x}dx+\frac{1}{2}\frac{\partial^2(\ln x)}{\partial x^2}(dx)^2=\frac{1}{x}dx-\frac{1}{2x^2}(dx)^2
    \end{equation*}

    Let's substitute $dx = \frac{x}{2}dt + xdB$. We only need the $dB$ part for $(dx)^2$, so $(dx)^2 \approx (xdB)^2 = x^2 dt$.

    \begin{align*}
        dy&=\frac{1}{x}\left(\frac{x}{2}dt+xdB\right)-\frac{1}{2x^2}\left(x^2 dt\right) \\
        &=\frac{1}{2}dt+dB-\frac{1}{2}dt = dB
    \end{align*}

    Furthermore, since $x(0)=1$:

    \begin{equation*}
        y(0)=\ln(x(0))=\ln 1=0
    \end{equation*}

    Since $dy=dB$, by integrating we will obtain

    \begin{equation*}
        y(t)-y(0) = B(t)-B(0) \implies y(t) = B(t)
    \end{equation*}

    and from the relationship with $x(t)$ we obtain:

    \begin{equation*}
        \ln x(t) = B(t) \ \iff \ x(t)=e^{B(t)}
    \end{equation*}

    just as predicted by the text.
