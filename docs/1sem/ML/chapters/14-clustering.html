15  Clustering

15.1 Concept and Problem Setup

Clustering is the task of partitioning a dataset into groups (clusters) such that points within a cluster are similar to each other, and points in different clusters are dissimilar. Unlike supervised learning, clustering has no labels—we must define what “similarity” and “good clustering” mean ourselves.

15.1.1 Hard vs. Soft Clustering

  • Hard clustering: Each point belongs to exactly one cluster. Output is a partition \(C = (C_1, \ldots, C_k)\) with \(\bigcup_i C_i = X\) and \(C_i \cap C_j = \emptyset\) for \(i \neq j\).
  • Soft clustering: Each point has a probabilistic assignment to clusters, represented as a vector of membership probabilities \((p_1(x), \ldots, p_k(x))\) with \(\sum_i p_i(x) = 1\).

15.1.2 Distance and Similarity

Clustering assumes we have a distance metric \(d: X \times X \to \mathbb{R}_+\) satisfying: - Symmetry: \(d(x, y) = d(y, x)\), - Identity: \(d(x, x) = 0\), - (Often) Triangle inequality: \(d(x, z) \leq d(x, y) + d(y, z)\).

Alternatively, a similarity function \(s: X \times X \to [0, 1]\) with \(s(x, x) = 1\).

15.1.3 The Transitivity Problem

A fundamental challenge: similarity is not transitive. Consider a chain of points \(x_1, x_2, \ldots, x_m\) where consecutive points are very similar but \(x_1\) and \(x_m\) are dissimilar. If we cluster to keep similar points together, we must place the entire chain in one cluster—yet this groups dissimilar points. Different clustering objectives prioritize different aspects of similarity vs. separation.

15.2 Objective Functions and Quality Measures

15.2.1 k-means Objective

The most popular clustering objective is k-means. We partition data into \(k\) clusters \(C_1, \ldots, C_k\), each represented by a centroid \(\mu_j\) (typically the mean of points in \(C_j\)). The objective is:

\[G_{\text{k-means}}(X, d, C_1, \ldots, C_k) = \sum_{i=1}^{k} \sum_{x \in C_i} \|x - \mu_i\|^2,\]

where \(\mu_i = \arg\min_{\mu} \sum_{x \in C_i} \|x - \mu\|^2\) is the optimal centroid for cluster \(i\).

Intuition: Minimize total squared distance from each point to its cluster center. This is related to quantization in signal processing—representing many signals with few codewords.

15.2.2 Silhouette Index

For a point \(x \in C_j\), define:

  • Intra-cluster distance: \(a(x) = \frac{1}{|C_j|} \sum_{y \in C_j} d(x, y)\) (average distance to points in its cluster).
  • Inter-cluster distance: \(b(x) = \min_{i \neq j} \frac{1}{|C_i|} \sum_{y \in C_i} d(x, y)\) (minimum average distance to another cluster).

The silhouette index for point \(x\) is:

\[s(x) = \frac{b(x) - a(x)}{\max(a(x), b(x))}.\]

It ranges in \([-1, 1]\): - \(s(x) \approx 1\): \(x\) is well-separated (far from neighbors, close to its cluster). - \(s(x) \approx 0\): \(x\) is on the boundary. - \(s(x) \approx -1\): \(x\) might belong to the wrong cluster.

The average silhouette over all points gauges overall clustering quality.

15.2.3 Dunn Index

\[\text{Dunn} = \frac{\min_{i\,\neq\,j} d(C_i, C_j)}{\max_{\ell} \text{diam}(C_\ell)},\]

where \(d(C_i, C_j) = \min_{x \in C_i, y \in C_j} d(x, y)\) is the minimum inter-cluster distance, and \(\text{diam}(C_\ell) = \max_{x, y \in C_\ell} d(x, y)\) is the cluster diameter.

Higher is better: Large Dunn index means clusters are compact and far apart.

15.2.4 Davies-Bouldin Index

For cluster \(C_i\), define its dispersion as:

\[S_i = \left(\frac{1}{|C_i|} \sum_{x \in C_i} d(x, \mu_i)^2\right)^{1/2}.\]

The Davies-Bouldin index is:

\[\text{DB} = \frac{1}{k} \sum_{i=1}^{k} \max_{j\,\neq\,i} \frac{S_i + S_j}{d(\mu_i, \mu_j)}.\]

Lower is better: Clusters should be tight (small \(S_i\)) and well-separated (large \(d(\mu_i, \mu_j)\)).

15.3 k-means Algorithm (Lloyd’s Algorithm)

The k-means objective is NP-hard to minimize exactly. Lloyd’s algorithm is a simple, widely-used heuristic:

Input: Data \(X\), number of clusters \(k\).

Initialize: Randomly choose initial centroids \(\mu_1, \ldots, \mu_k\).

Repeat until convergence: 1. Assignment: For each point \(x \in X\), assign it to the nearest centroid: \(C_i \gets \{x : i = \arg\min_j \|x - \mu_j\|\}\). 2. Update: Recompute each centroid as the mean of points in its cluster: \(\mu_i \gets \frac{1}{|C_i|} \sum_{x \in C_i} x\).

Output: Clusters \(C_1, \ldots, C_k\).

Each iteration of Lloyd’s algorithm does not increase the k-means objective.

Intuition: Assignment step reduces cost by choosing the best cluster for each point. Update step finds the centroid that minimizes within-cluster variance. Combined, the objective can only decrease or stay the same.

15.3.1 k-means++ Initialization

Random initialization can lead to poor local minima. k-means++ initializes centroids sequentially:

  1. Pick the first centroid uniformly at random from \(X\).
  2. For \(j = 2, \ldots, k\): Pick the \(j\)-th centroid with probability proportional to \(d(x, C_{<j})^2\) (distance squared to the nearest existing centroid).

This favors spreading centroids apart initially. k-means++ achieves an \(O(\log k)\) approximation ratio in expectation.

15.4 Hierarchical / Linkage-Based Clustering

These algorithms build a dendrogram—a tree representing hierarchical clustering at all levels.

Algorithm: 1. Start: Each point is a singleton cluster. 2. Repeat: Merge the two closest clusters (by some definition of distance). 3. Output: A dendrogram; cut at desired height to get a partition.

15.4.1 Linkage Definitions

Given clusters \(A\) and \(B\):

  • Single linkage: \(d(A, B) = \min_{x \in A, y \in B} d(x, y)\) (closest pair).
    • Pro: Can find elongated clusters.
    • Con: Prone to “chaining” (long bridges connecting clusters).
  • Average linkage: \(d(A, B) = \frac{1}{|A| |B|} \sum_{x \in A, y \in B} d(x, y)\) (average distance).
    • Balanced trade-off.
  • Complete linkage (max linkage): \(d(A, B) = \max_{x \in A, y \in B} d(x, y)\) (farthest pair).
    • Pro: Produces compact, round clusters.
    • Con: Sensitive to outliers.

Hierarchical methods do not require specifying \(k\) in advance; instead, we cut the dendrogram at a threshold or desired number of clusters.

15.5 DBSCAN: Density-Based Clustering

DBSCAN (Density-Based Spatial Clustering of Applications with Noise) discovers clusters of arbitrary shape by grouping dense regions.

Given parameters \(\epsilon\) (radius) and \(\text{MinPts}\) (minimum neighbors):

  • Core point: A point \(x\) with at least \(\text{MinPts}\) points in its \(\epsilon\)-neighborhood \(N_\epsilon(x) = \{y : d(x, y) \leq \epsilon\}\).
  • Border point: A non-core point in the neighborhood of a core point.
  • Noise point: A point that is neither core nor border.
  • Density-connected: Two points are connected if there is a chain of core points between them, each within \(\epsilon\) of the next.

Algorithm: 1. Label all core points. 2. Form clusters by connecting density-reachable points (non-recursive). 3. Noise points form a separate “cluster” or are left unassigned.

Advantages: - Discovers arbitrary-shaped clusters. - Automatically identifies outliers (noise). - No need to specify \(k\) in advance.

Disadvantages: - Sensitive to \(\epsilon\) and \(\text{MinPts}\) parameters. - Struggles with varying density.

15.6 Spectral Clustering

Spectral clustering uses the graph Laplacian to relax the cluster-balancing problem.

Given a similarity matrix \(W\) (with \(W_{ij} = s(x_i, x_j) \geq 0\)), define:

\[L = D - W,\]

where \(D\) is the degree matrix: \(D_{ii} = \sum_j W_{ij}\) and \(D_{ij} = 0\) for \(i \neq j\).

The RatioCut objective balances cluster size:

\[\text{RatioCut}(C_1, \ldots, C_k) = \sum_{i=1}^{k} \frac{\text{cut}(C_i, \overline{C_i})}{|C_i|},\]

where \(\text{cut}(C_i, \overline{C_i}) = \sum_{x \in C_i, y \notin C_i} W_{xy}\) is the sum of edge weights crossing the boundary.

Minimizing RatioCut directly is NP-hard, but spectral clustering relaxes it:

  1. Compute the \(k\) eigenvectors \(v_1, \ldots, v_k\) of \(L\) corresponding to the \(k\) smallest eigenvalues.
  2. Form a matrix \(V \in \mathbb{R}^{m \times k}\) with these eigenvectors as columns.
  3. Treat the rows of \(V\) as points in \(\mathbb{R}^k\) and apply k-means.

Intuition: The spectral representation transforms the clustering problem into a form where k-means works well. Spectral clustering often finds balanced, well-separated clusters even when k-means fails on the original data.

15.7 Kleinberg Impossibility Theorem

A fundamental result shows that no clustering algorithm can simultaneously satisfy three desirable properties:

There is no clustering function \(f\) that takes any finite set \(X\) with a distance metric and outputs a partition, such that \(f\) satisfies all three of the following:

  1. Scale Invariance (SI): \(f(X, d) = f(X, \alpha d)\) for any \(\alpha > 0\) (scaling distances doesn’t change clustering).
  2. Richness (Ri): For any partition \(C\) of \(X\), there exists a distance metric such that \(f(X, d) = C\) (any partition is achievable).
  3. Consistency (C): If we decrease intra-cluster distances and increase inter-cluster distances, the clustering doesn’t change.

Moreover, every pair of these properties can be satisfied by some algorithm.

Implication: We must trade off which properties matter most. Different algorithms—k-means, hierarchical, DBSCAN—implicitly prioritize different properties, explaining why they produce different results on the same data.

15.8 Worked Examples

Consider points in \(\mathbb{R}^2\): \(A=(0,0), B=(1,1), C=(10,10), D=(11,11)\) with \(k=2\).

Iteration 1: - Initialize: \(\mu_1 = (0, 0), \mu_2 = (10, 10)\). - Assign: \(\|A - \mu_1\|^2 = 0 < 200 = \|A - \mu_2\|^2 \Rightarrow A \in C_1\). Similarly, \(B \in C_1, C \in C_2, D \in C_2\). - Update: \(\mu_1 = (0.5, 0.5), \mu_2 = (10.5, 10.5)\).

Iteration 2: - Assign: No change (same clusters). - Convergence!

Cost: \(\|A - \mu_1\|^2 + \|B - \mu_1\|^2 + \|C - \mu_2\|^2 + \|D - \mu_2\|^2 = 0.5 + 0.5 + 0.5 + 0.5 = 2\).

Given \(X = \{a, b, c, d, e\}\) with pairwise distances, single linkage merges closest clusters: - Start: \(\{\{a\}, \{b\}, \{c\}, \{d\}, \{e\}\}\). - Merge \(\{b, c\}\) (distance 2). - Merge \(\{b, c, d\}\) (add \(d\); distance 3). - Merge \(\{a, e\}\) (distance 4). - Merge \(\{a, e, b, c, d\}\) (distance 5).

The resulting dendrogram shows the hierarchical structure. Cutting at height 3 gives clusters \(\{a, e\}\) and \(\{b, c, d\}\).

15.9 Summary

Clustering lacks a universal “ground truth,” so practitioners choose methods and metrics based on domain knowledge:

  • k-means: Fast, scalable; assumes roughly spherical, equally-sized clusters; requires specifying \(k\).
  • Hierarchical: Flexible; produces a dendrogram; no need to pre-specify \(k\); can be slow.
  • DBSCAN: Discovers arbitrary-shaped clusters and outliers; sensitive to parameters.
  • Spectral: Graph-based; handles non-convex shapes; powerful but computationally heavier.

Quality metrics (silhouette, Dunn, Davies-Bouldin) help validate and compare clusterings, though they also reflect implicit assumptions.

## Worked Examples

:::{.example name="k-means on a 4-point dataset"}
Consider points in $\mathbb{R}^2$: $A=(0,0), B=(1,1), C=(10,10), D=(11,11)$ with $k=2$.

**Iteration 1**:
- Initialize: $\mu_1 = (0, 0), \mu_2 = (10, 10)$.
- Assign: $\|A - \mu_1\|^2 = 0 < 200 = \|A - \mu_2\|^2 \Rightarrow A \in C_1$. Similarly, $B \in C_1, C \in C_2, D \in C_2$.
- Update: $\mu_1 = (0.5, 0.5), \mu_2 = (10.5, 10.5)$.

**Iteration 2**:
- Assign: No change (same clusters).
- Convergence!

**Cost**: $\|A - \mu_1\|^2 + \|B - \mu_1\|^2 + \|C - \mu_2\|^2 + \|D - \mu_2\|^2 = 0.5 + 0.5 + 0.5 + 0.5 = 2$.
:::

:::{.example name="Hierarchical clustering dendrogram"}
Given $X = \{a, b, c, d, e\}$ with pairwise distances, single linkage merges closest clusters:
- Start: $\{\{a\}, \{b\}, \{c\}, \{d\}, \{e\}\}$.
- Merge $\{b, c\}$ (distance 2).
- Merge $\{b, c, d\}$ (add $d$; distance 3).
- Merge $\{a, e\}$ (distance 4).
- Merge $\{a, e, b, c, d\}$ (distance 5).

The resulting dendrogram shows the hierarchical structure. Cutting at height 3 gives clusters $\{a, e\}$ and $\{b, c, d\}$.
:::
