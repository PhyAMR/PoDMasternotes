18.1 1. VC Dimension

18.1.1 1.1 VC Dimension Calculation — Exercise 1

Consider the hypothesis class \(H = \{h_a : a \in \mathbb{R}\}\) where \(h_a : \mathbb{R} \to \{0,1\}\) is: \[ h_a(x) = \begin{cases} 1 & \text{if } x \leq a \text{ or } x \geq a+1 \\ 0 & \text{otherwise} \end{cases} \]

What is \(\text{VCdim}(H)\)? Provide a proof of your claim.

Solution. The VC dimension is 2.

Lower bound (shatterable): Choose \(x_1 = 0\) and \(x_2 = 0.5\). We can achieve all 4 labelings: - (0, 0): \(a = -0.25\) (both points inside \([-0.25, 0.75]\)) - (0, 1): \(a = -0.75\) (both inside \([-0.75, 0.25]\)) - (1, 0): \(a = 0.25\) (both outside \([0.25, 1.25]\)) - (1, 1): \(a = 0.75\) (both outside \([0.75, 1.75]\))

Upper bound (not shatterable): For any 3 points \(x_1 < x_2 < x_3\), the labeling (1, 0, 1) is impossible. If the outer points are labeled 1 (outside the interval), they must be outside \([a, a+1)\). But then any point between them is inside the interval, so it must be labeled 0. Thus, the middle point cannot be labeled 1 when the outer points are labeled 1.

Therefore, \(\text{VCdim}(H) = 2\).

18.1.2 1.2 VC Dimension Calculation — Exercise 2

Consider the hypothesis class $H where a circle with radius 1 and arbitrary center \(c \in \mathbb{R}^2\) classifies points inside as class 1 and outside as class -1. What is \(\text{VCdim}(H)\)? Provide a proof.

Solution. The VC dimension is 3.

Lower bound (shatterable): We can shatter an equilateral triangle with side 0.5. Let \(A = (-0.25, 0)\), \(B = (0.25, 0)\), \(C = (0, \sqrt{2}/4)\).

For each of 8 labelings, we can find a center \(c\): - (0, 0, 0): Center far away, e.g., \((10, 10)\) - (0, 0, 1): Center at \((0, 1 + \sqrt{2}/8)\) (only \(C\) inside) - (0, 1, 0): Center at \((1.1, 0)\) (only \(B\) inside) - (0, 1, 1): Center at \((0.9, \sqrt{2}/4)\) (B and C inside) - (1, 0, 0): Center at \((-1.1, 0)\) (only A inside) - (1, 0, 1): Center at \((-0.9, \sqrt{2}/4)\) (A and C inside) - (1, 1, 0): Center at \((0, -2/3)\) (A and B inside) - (1, 1, 1): Center at \((0, \sqrt{2}/8)\) (all three inside)

Upper bound (not shatterable): We cannot shatter any 4 points. The key insight: if 4 points form a convex quadrilateral and we mark 3 as class 1 (inside circle) and 1 as class 0, there are geometric constraints. For instance, labeling (1, 1, 1, 0) becomes impossible for certain point configurations because a circle cannot isolate one point from three others without violating the radius constraint.

Therefore, \(\text{VCdim}(H) = 3\).

18.2.4 2.4 SVM Parameter Matching — Exercise 2

Match the four plots to: - (a) Linear, \(C=100\) - (b) Linear, \(C=10\) - (c) RBF, \(\sigma=1, C=10\) - (d) RBF, \(\sigma=1, C=100\)

Justify and discuss RBF pros/cons.

Solution. Kernel type: Plots 1, 4 have linear boundaries; plots 2, 3 have curved.

Regularization: \(C = 1/\lambda\). Higher \(C\) \(\to\) lower regularization \(\to\) narrow margin (fit outliers). Lower \(C\) \(\to\) higher regularization \(\to\) wide margin.

Matching: - (a) Linear, \(C=100\): Low regularization, narrow margin. \(\to\) Plot 4 - (b) Linear, \(C=10\): Higher regularization, wider margin. \(\to\) Plot 1 - (c) RBF, \(C=10\): Curved boundary, moderate regularization. \(\to\) Plot 2 - (d) RBF, \(C=100\): Curved, tight fit. \(\to\) Plot 3

RBF Pros: - Captures non-linear patterns flexibly. - Expressive for complex decision boundaries.

RBF Cons: - Higher overfitting risk (especially small \(\sigma\) or large \(C\)). - Computationally expensive (dense kernel matrix). - Hyperparameter \(\sigma\) requires careful tuning. - Less interpretable than linear classifiers.

18.2.5 2.5 SVM Drawing — Exercise 1

Figure shows 2D points (orange and blue classes). Draw the soft SVM boundary, identify outliers and support vectors, and discuss margin and slack values. You may choose any \(\lambda > 0\).

Solution. Typical SVM solution:

The boundary is a red line maximizing the margin (distance to nearest points). Two parallel dashed lines show the margin width.

Support vectors: Points on or inside the margin (green circles). Typically 2-3 orange support vectors and 1-2 blue ones.

Outliers: Points on the wrong side of the margin (light blue boxes). One outlier is severely misclassified (\(\xi \approx 2\)); others have \(0 < \xi < 1\) (inside margin but correct class).

Slack interpretation: - \(\xi_i = 0\): Point on correct side of margin. - \(0 < \xi_i < 1\): Point inside margin but correct side. - \(\xi_i > 1\): Point misclassified.

Margin width: Depends on \(\lambda\). High \(\lambda\) (strong regularization) yields wide margin and tolerates outliers. Low \(\lambda\) yields narrow margin and fewer misclassifications.

Discussion: Since one orange outlier makes the data non-linearly separable, hard SVM fails. Soft SVM allows slack with cost proportional to violation magnitude, balancing fitting and generalization.

18.5 5. Unsupervised Learning

18.5.1 5.1 Principal Component Analysis — Exercise 1

For three 2D datasets (Figure UNS.1), draw the two principal components. When is reducing from 2D to 1D via PC1 appropriate?

Solution. For all three datasets: The first PC is at 45° (direction \(\sqrt{2}/2 [1, 1]\)); the second PC is at 135° (direction \(\sqrt{2}/2 [-1, 1]\)).

Case 1: Elongated cloud along a diagonal. PCA is appropriate. The data has clear directional structure; PC1 captures the main variance; projecting preserves the essential structure.

Case 2: Isotropic ball (variance similar in all directions). PCA is not appropriate. No preferred direction; PC1 captures only ~50% of variance. Projecting loses nearly half the information without insight.

Case 3: Two well-separated clusters perpendicular to PC1. PCA is inappropriate. The two clusters have similar variance along PC1 (they average out). Projecting to 1D flattens them together, destroying the meaningful structure.

Conclusion: PCA works best when data has clear directional structure aligned with the PC. It fails when meaningful variation is orthogonal to the PCs (e.g., clustered data).

18.5.4 5.4 DBSCAN — Exercise 1

Apply DBSCAN with \(\epsilon = 0.1\), min_neighbors = 4 to Figure UNS.3 data (10 points per color). Explain the steps. What if \(\epsilon = 1.75\), min_neighbors = 15?

Solution. With \(\epsilon = 0.1\), min_neighbors = 4:

Start from a red point. Its 9 neighbors (other red points within \(0.1\)) form a core cluster. Expand to include all red points (all are core).

Repeat for blue, purple, yellow. Each group is dense internally (\(\geq 10\) points per color) and far from others (\(\epsilon = 0.1\) too small to bridge).

Result: 4 clusters (one per color).

With \(\epsilon = 1.75\), min_neighbors = 15:

Red point has 9 neighbors (< 15), so it’s not core \(\to\) marked noise.

Purple point has 10 purple + 10 blue = 20 neighbors (core). Expand: blue points also have enough neighbors within 1.75, so they merge with purple. Yellow points are nearby and added too.

## 1. VC Dimension

### 1.1 VC Dimension Calculation --- Exercise 1

:::{.exercise name="1.1 --- VC dimension of interval-based classifier"}
*Consider the hypothesis class $H = \{h_a : a \in \mathbb{R}\}$ where $h_a : \mathbb{R} \to \{0,1\}$ is:*
$$
h_a(x) = \begin{cases} 1 & \text{if } x \leq a \text{ or } x \geq a+1 \\ 0 & \text{otherwise} \end{cases}
$$

*What is $\text{VCdim}(H)$? Provide a proof of your claim.*
:::

:::{.solution}
The VC dimension is 2.

**Lower bound (shatterable):** Choose $x_1 = 0$ and $x_2 = 0.5$. We can achieve all 4 labelings:
- **(0, 0):** $a = -0.25$ (both points inside $[-0.25, 0.75]$)
- **(0, 1):** $a = -0.75$ (both inside $[-0.75, 0.25]$)
- **(1, 0):** $a = 0.25$ (both outside $[0.25, 1.25]$)
- **(1, 1):** $a = 0.75$ (both outside $[0.75, 1.75]$)

**Upper bound (not shatterable):** For any 3 points $x_1 < x_2 < x_3$, the labeling **(1, 0, 1)** is impossible. If the outer points are labeled 1 (outside the interval), they must be outside $[a, a+1)$. But then any point between them is inside the interval, so it must be labeled 0. Thus, the middle point cannot be labeled 1 when the outer points are labeled 1.

Therefore, $\text{VCdim}(H) = 2$.
:::

### 1.2 VC Dimension Calculation --- Exercise 2

:::{.exercise name="1.2 --- VC dimension of unit circle classifier"}
*Consider the hypothesis class $H  where a circle with radius 1 and arbitrary center $c \in \mathbb{R}^2$ classifies points inside as class 1 and outside as class -1. What is $\text{VCdim}(H)$? Provide a proof.*
:::

:::{.solution}
The VC dimension is 3.

**Lower bound (shatterable):** We can shatter an equilateral triangle with side 0.5. Let $A = (-0.25, 0)$, $B = (0.25, 0)$, $C = (0, \sqrt{2}/4)$.

For each of 8 labelings, we can find a center $c$:
- **(0, 0, 0):** Center far away, e.g., $(10, 10)$
- **(0, 0, 1):** Center at $(0, 1 + \sqrt{2}/8)$ (only $C$ inside)
- **(0, 1, 0):** Center at $(1.1, 0)$ (only $B$ inside)

\alpha_2 = \frac{1}{2}\log\frac{5/6}{1/6} = \frac{1}{2}\log 5 \approx 0.8

### 6.3 SARSA vs. Q-learning --- Exercise 1

:::{.exercise name="6.3 --- SARSA and Q-learning update comparison"}
*Experience: $(s, a, r, s', a') = (1, 2, 1, 1, 1)$; $\gamma=0.9, \alpha=0.1$. Current $Q_t(s,a)=9, Q_t(s',a')=10$. If $a$ and $a'$ are both greedy:*

**(a)** *SARSA update change?*
**(b)** *Q-learning update change?*
**(c)** *If $a$ is exploratory, does this change?*
:::

:::{.solution}
**(a) SARSA:**
Q_{t+1}(s,a) = 9 + 0.1[1 + 0.9 \times 10 - 9] = 9 + 0.1[1] = 9.1
**Change: $\Delta Q = 0.1$**

**(b) Q-learning:**
Q_{t+1}(s,a) = 9 + 0.1[1 + 0.9 \times \max(Q(s',\cdot)) - 9]
Since $a' = 1$ is greedy, $\max_a Q(s', a) = Q(s', 1) = 10$.
Q_{t+1}(s,a) = 9.1
**Change: $\Delta Q = 0.1$ (same as SARSA)**

**(c)** Whether $a$ is greedy or exploratory doesn't matter. The update depends only on $r, Q(s',\cdot), Q(s,a)$, not on how $a$ was selected.

**Conclusion:** When $a'$ is greedy, SARSA and Q-learning are **equivalent**. They differ only when $a'$ is exploratory.
:::

### 6.4 SARSA vs. Q-learning --- Exercise 2

:::{.exercise name="6.4 --- Expected difference between SARSA and Q-learning"}
*Epsilon-greedy policy ($\epsilon=0.1$); $\gamma=0.8, \alpha=0.05$. $Q(s',1)=10, Q(s',2)=8$. What is the expected difference $Q_{\text{QL}} - Q_{\text{SARSA}}$ when updating at state $s'$?*
:::

:::{.solution}
**Q-learning:** Always picks greedy action 1.
Q_{\text{QL}} = Q_t(s,a) + \alpha[r + 0.8 \times 10 - Q_t(s,a)]

**SARSA:** Under $\epsilon$-greedy, action 1 is chosen with probability $1-\epsilon+\epsilon/2 = 0.95$; action 2 with probability $\epsilon/2 = 0.05$.

Expected next-state value:
\mathbb{E}[Q(s',a')] = 0.95 \times 10 + 0.05 \times 8 = 9.5 + 0.4 = 9.9

Q_{\text{SARSA}} = Q_t(s,a) + \alpha[r + 0.8 \times 9.9 - Q_t(s,a)]

**Difference:**
Q_{\text{QL}} - Q_{\text{SARSA}} = \alpha \gamma[Q(s',1) - \mathbb{E}[Q(s',a')]]
= 0.05 \times 0.8 \times (10 - 9.9) = 0.04 \times 0.1 = 0.004

**Result:** Q-learning updates **0.004 units higher** on average. This reflects Q-learning's off-policy optimism: it assumes future greedy action, while SARSA averages over the exploratory distribution.
:::
