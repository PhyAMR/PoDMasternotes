3  Fundamentals of Boolean Algebra

In Boolean algebra, variables can only take on two values, generally indicated as \(0\) and \(1\) or as false and true. In the context of circuitry (logic or digital), it is of fundamental importance as it makes use of quantized signals, that is, a series of signals that alternate between two states: absence and presence (also called LOW and HIGH).

3.1 Postulates

Formally, the algebra is formed by a \(M\) and by the two operators of (\(+\), called OR) and (\(\cdot\), called AND).

We will therefore be dealing with a triplet \((M,+, cdot)\) that must satisfy the following properties:

The bar above a variable (\(\bar{A}\)) indicates the operation of negation, also called NOT, and expresses the complementary value of the variable, so if \(A=0\) then \(\bar{A}=1\) and vice versa.

We note that all the properties listed are valid for both the sum operator and the product operator; this is actually a valid characteristic for all the theorems and statements that are given in Boolean algebra: in particular, starting from a certain expression, by exchanging the operators and replacing the complements of the variables, the expression remains valid. This is known as the , and the expression obtained through the substitutions is called the of the initial expression.

3.1.1 Proof of non-contradiction

In order for the postulates to express a coherent algebra, it is necessary that they do not contradict each other; let’s verify, therefore, that the results obtained from all the various combinations are compatible with the properties introduced.

We will use the so-called truth tables, which we will study in more detail later when talking about Boolean functions: these are tables in which the variables considered are reported along the columns, while the values that these variables can assume are indicated along the rows, ordered in such a way as to obtain all possible combinations.

  1. Commutativity is evident from the central rows, in which the result is the same even if the values are inverted with respect to the variables.

  2. The neutral element is expressed in the first two rows of the OR, in which the sum of \(0\) does not modify the variable \(A\) whether it is \(A=0\) or \(A=1\); the same can also be said of the last two rows of the AND.

  3. The complement is given in the central rows of both tables, in which the result is \(1\) in the case of the OR and \(0\) in the case of the AND.

For the last property it is necessary to write two new truth tables built from the two in table [tab:fondamenti-di-algebra-booleana-tabelle-and-or].

The columns in red represent the two members of the distributive property, and since they are equal, the two expressions will also be equal.

We have thus shown that all the postulated properties are useful for defining a coherent algebra.

3.2.1 De Morgan’s Laws

De Morgan’s laws allow us to express the negation of a sum and a product of variables.

They state that:

\[ \boxed{\orange{\overbar{A+B}=\bar{A}\cdot\bar{B}}} \hspace{1 cm} \green{\overbar{A\cdot B}=\bar{A}+\bar{B}} \]

Let’s proceed first with the proof of the negation of the sum, remembering that by the principle of duality the proof of the negation of the product will then also be immediate.

The idea is to verify what both the sum and the product of the second member with the first non-negated member give rise to:

\[ \begin{aligned} &A+B+\bar{A}\cdot\bar{B}\overset{\boldred{P4}}{=}\left(A+B+\bar{A}\right)\left(A+B+\bar{B}\right)\overset{\boldred{P3}}{=}(B+1)(A+1)\overset{\boldred{T2}}{=}1\cdot1=1\\ &(A+B)\cdot\bar{A}\cdot\bar{B}\overset{\boldred{P4}}{=}A\cdot\bar{A}\cdot\bar{B}+B\cdot\bar{A}\cdot\bar{B}\overset{\boldred{P3}}{=}\bar{B}\cdot0+\bar{A}\cdot0\overset{\boldred{T2}}{=}0+0=0 \end{aligned} \]

According to property it follows that \(A+B\) is the complement of \(\bar{A}\cdot\bar{B}\), hence the thesis.

As already mentioned, by the principle of duality the proof of the second equality follows from the first in an immediate way; only for completeness we also report it here below:

\[ \begin{aligned} &A\cdot B+\bar{A}+\bar{B}\overset{\boldred{P4}}{=}\left(\bar{A}+\bar{B}+A\right)\left(\bar{A}+\bar{B}+B\right)\overset{\boldred{P3}}{=}(\bar{B}+1)(\bar{A}+1)\overset{\boldred{T2}}{=}1\cdot1=1\\ &A\cdot B\cdot(\bar{A}+\bar{B})\overset{\boldred{P4}}{=}A\cdot B\cdot\bar{A}+A\cdot B\cdot\bar{B}\overset{\boldred{P3}}{=}B\cdot0+A\cdot0\overset{\boldred{T2}}{=}0+0=0 \end{aligned} \]

Prove that

\[ (A+B)\left(\bar{A}+C\right)=\bar{A}B+AC \]

Let’s apply the distributive property:

\[ (A+B)\left(\bar{A}+C\right)=\cancel{A\bar{A}}+AC+\bar{A}B+BC \]

Let’s multiply the last term on the right side by \(1\), which, by property , will be equal to \(A+\bar{A}\):

\[ (A+B)\left(\bar{A}+C\right)=AC+\bar{A}B+BC\cdot1=AC+\bar{A}B+BC\cdot\left(A+\bar{A}\right) \]

Let’s apply the distributive property again:

\[ (A+B)\left(\bar{A}+C\right)=AC+\bar{A}B+ABC+\bar{A}BC \]

Let’s collect the terms in \(\bar{A}B\) and in \(AC\):

\[ (A+B)\left(\bar{A}+C\right)=\bar{A}B(1+C)+AC(1+B)=\bar{A}B+AC \]

Prove that

\[ f(A,B)=\bar{A}\bar{B}+AB=\bar{f}\left(\bar{A}B\right) \]

Let’s make \(\bar{f}\left(\bar{A}B\right)\) explicit:

\[ \bar{f}\left(\bar{A}B\right)=\overbar{A\bar{B}+\bar{A}B} \]

Let’s apply De Morgan’s law to the sum:

\[ \bar{f}\left(\bar{A}B\right)=\overbar{A\bar{B}}\cdot\overbar{\bar{A}B} \]

Let’s apply De Morgan’s law again, but this time to the two products:

\[ \bar{f}\left(\bar{A}B\right)=\left(\bar{A}+B\right)\cdot\left(A+\bar{B}\right) \]

Finally, let’s use the distributive property:

\[ \bar{f}\left(\bar{A}B\right)=\cancel{\bar{A}A}+\bar{A}\bar{B}+AB+\cancel{B\bar{B}}\equiv f(A,B) \]

3.3 Logic functions

A , also called a Boolean function, 1, is a function that takes \(n\) Boolean variables as input and returns a Boolean value, therefore \(0\) or \(1\).

It can be expressed in algebraic form, reporting the Boolean variables and the logical operators that link them, such as for example

\[ f(A,B)=\bar{A}+\bar{B} \]

Another very useful expression is that by means of a truth table, which we introduced in paragraph 1.1.1, whose number of rows is equal to \(2^n\), this being the number of possible combinations considering the \(n\) variables and the two values that each can assume.

3.3.1 Canonical forms

Starting from the same truth table it is possible to derive different algebraic expressions. The following functions, for example, all have the same truth table written above:

\[ \overbar{A\cdot B} \hspace{1.2 cm} \bar{A}\bar{B}+A\bar{B}+\bar{A}B \hspace{1.2 cm} \bar{A}B+\bar{B} \]

and are therefore perfectly equivalent alternative expressions to \(\bar{A}+\bar{B}\).

One of these, however, is considered a canonical form through which to express Boolean functions starting from the truth table. In addition to this, by the principle of duality, there is another one; we will see below that of these forms there is the one that makes use of minterms and the one that makes use of maxterms.

The (\(m_i\)) are given by the product of all the Boolean variables of a function, both in negated and non-negated form. For this reason the input of the function will determine only one minterm equal to \(1\) when all the others will instead be \(0\); it follows that to each combination of values of the variables corresponds a single non-zero minterm: in the case in which the input is \(000\), for example, the associated minterm is \(\bar{A}\bar{B}\bar{C}\), while if the input is \(0101\) the minterm will be \(\bar{A}B\bar{C}D\), and so on; we immediately understand, therefore, that the rule is to write in order the variable associated with the input \(0\) as negated and the one associated with the input \(1\) as non-negated.

For clarity, we report below the table with three variables and the minterms associated with each input; in the last column is reported the notation associated with the minterms.

The (\(M_i\)), on the other hand, are given by the sum of all the Boolean variables, also both in negated and non-negated form. The input of the function, this time, will determine a single maxterm equal to \(0\) when all the others will be equal to \(1\). It follows that to each combination of values of the Boolean variables corresponds a single null maxterm, in which - it is easy to guess - each variable will be the complement with respect to the minterm associated with the same input.

Starting from minterms and maxterms it is possible to express a Boolean function in the following ways:

  • Logical sum of minterms

  • Logical product of maxterms

In particular, the procedure is as follows: one observes in the truth table which inputs give rise to an output \(1\) and the minterms associated with these inputs are added together. To a given input, in fact, one and only one minterm will be equal to \(1\), and if this is present among the minterms of the sum, an output \(1\) will also result; vice versa, if this minterm is not present in the sum, then this will give as a result \(0\), which is what we expect from the truth table.

Let’s consider, for example, the function \(f(A,B)=\bar{A}+\bar{B}\) seen at the beginning of the section; from the truth table we understand that the output equal to \(1\) is associated with three different inputs, so it will be sufficient to put their three minterms in sum to obtain the canonical form of the function:

\[ f(A,B)=\bar{A}\bar{B}+A\bar{B}+\bar{A}B \]

The canonical form with the maxterms is, in a certain sense, the of the one just seen: the inputs that produce \(0\) as output are considered and the relative maxterms are multiplied through logical product; since, given an input, there will be only one maxterm equal to \(0\), if this is present in the product then it will cancel the entire function, as provided by the truth table, while if it is not present the product will necessarily be \(1\). Consequently, the function \(f(A,B)\) has already been written in canonical form through the product of maxterms, since the only maxterm is precisely \(\bar{A}+\bar{B}\).

3.3.2 Functions with two variables

From the reasoning just made we understand that the number of Boolean functions is more limited than one might initially think, since the real of a function is its truth table and these are limited by a certain number of combinations.

Let’s study, for example, the functions that contemplate only two Boolean variables, \(A\) and \(B\). We know that for them the number of possible inputs, that is of rows in the truth table, is \(2^2=4\); the question now is: how many combinations in input can we get?

To answer, let’s imagine placing the outputs \(f_i\) (that is, the rows of the truth table) in order as columns of a new table; it is clear that the possible combinations will be a total of \(2^4=16\), and that therefore, more generally, the number of possible functions given \(n\) variables is equal to \(2^{2^n}\).

### De Morgan's Laws

De Morgan's laws allow us to express the negation of a sum and a product of variables.

They state that:

$$
\boxed{\orange{\overbar{A+B}=\bar{A}\cdot\bar{B}}} \hspace{1 cm} \green{\overbar{A\cdot B}=\bar{A}+\bar{B}}
$$

The idea is to verify what both the sum and the product of the second member with the first non-negated member give rise to:

$$
\begin{aligned}
    &A+B+\bar{A}\cdot\bar{B}\overset{\boldred{P4}}{=}\left(A+B+\bar{A}\right)\left(A+B+\bar{B}\right)\overset{\boldred{P3}}{=}(B+1)(A+1)\overset{\boldred{T2}}{=}1\cdot1=1\\

\end{aligned}

According to property it follows that $A+B$ is the complement of $\bar{A}\cdot\bar{B}$, hence the thesis.

$$

\end{aligned}
