4  Superconducting circuits: from the LC oscillator to qubits

4.1 Roadmap

The goal of this chapter is to build, from first principles, the quantum theory of the simplest possible electrical circuit — a capacitor in parallel with an inductor — and to understand it as the electromagnetic analogue of the mechanical harmonic oscillator. The harmonic LC circuit has a uniform spectrum that prevents addressing individual transitions; in Ch. 4 we introduce the Josephson junction as a non-linear inductor that lifts this degeneracy and turns the oscillator into a genuine qubit.

We proceed in five steps:

  • §3.1 — Why superconducting circuits: artificial vs. natural qubits, and the role of dissipation.
  • §3.2 — The LC oscillator and the node-flux / node-charge variables.
  • §3.3 — Energy stored in the inductor and the capacitor.
  • §3.4 — Classical Lagrangian and Hamiltonian via the Legendre transform.
  • §3.5 — Canonical quantisation: promote \((\Phi,q) \to (\hat\Phi,\hat q)\), define ladder operators, recover the harmonic-oscillator spectrum, and identify the temperature and frequency windows in which superconducting circuits live.

4.2 3.1 Artificial vs natural qubits

As seen in the first part of the course, qubits can be implemented using natural atoms, i.e. systems with given, immutable intrinsic properties (level spacings set by the Coulomb interaction, transition matrix elements set by quantum numbers). In the superconducting platform, qubits are instead engineered objects: their circuit parameters — inductance \(L\), capacitance \(C\), junction critical current \(I_c\), anharmonicity — are design knobs that the experimenter chooses by lithography.

Definition — artificial atom / superconducting qubit

An artificial atom is a macroscopic quantum system whose discrete energy spectrum is designed by engineering its constituents rather than inherited from a microscopic potential. A superconducting qubit is an artificial atom built from a non-linear, low-loss electrical circuit whose two lowest energy levels form the computational basis \(\{\ket{0},\ket{1}\}\).

Two practical constraints shape every choice we make in this chapter:

  • Dissipation kills coherence. In conventional circuits, resistors convert ordered electromagnetic energy into heat (\(\Rightarrow\) energy loss and decoherence). We therefore build the circuits from superconducting materials (zero DC resistance below \(T_c\)) deposited on low-loss dielectric substrates (sapphire, high-resistivity Si). Every resistive element is a decoherence channel and is, ideally, eliminated.
  • Non-linearity is mandatory. A purely linear LCR circuit has equally-spaced energy levels (see §3.5), so a microwave drive resonant with \(\ket{0}\to\ket{1}\) is also resonant with every \(\ket{n}\to\ket{n+1}\). The system leaks out of the computational subspace. The cure is to introduce a non-linear element. In superconducting circuits that element is the Josephson junction, which we will treat as a non-linear inductance in Ch. 4.

This chapter focuses on the linear part of the story — the LC oscillator — because it fixes the variables, the Hamiltonian structure and the quantisation procedure that the non-linear case will simply inherit.

4.3 3.2 The LC circuit: starting point

We start from the simplest non-trivial circuit: a capacitor \(C\) in parallel with an inductor \(L\), sharing two nodes (Fig. \(\ref{fig:lc-circuit}\)). One of the nodes is taken as ground; the other carries a single dynamical degree of freedom.

Constitutive relations. From elementary circuit theory:

  • Inductor: \(V(t) = L\,dI/dt\).
  • Capacitor: \(q = CV\), so \(I = dq/dt = C\,dV/dt\).

Node flux. It is convenient to work not with currents and voltages but with the time-integrated voltage at the active node, \[ \Phi(t) \;=\; \int_{-\infty}^{t} V(t')\,dt' , \] so that \(V = \dot\Phi\). We call \(\Phi\) the node flux. Despite the name, it is no longer necessarily the magnetic flux through a loop — it is a generalised coordinate attached to a circuit node.

Node charge. Its partner is the node charge \(q\), equal to the charge stored on the capacitor plate connected to that node.

Definition — node variables

On a lumped-element circuit, every node \(n\) (other than ground) carries two scalar variables:

  • the node flux \(\Phi_n(t) = \int_{-\infty}^{t} V_n(t')\,dt'\), the time-integrated voltage at the node;
  • the node charge \(q_n(t) = C_n \dot\Phi_n(t)\), the charge stored on the capacitances incident at the node.

For the LC oscillator there is only one such node, and \((\Phi,q)\) play the role of canonical variables.

We will choose \((\Phi,q)\) as canonical pair, in analogy with \((x,p)\) for a mechanical harmonic oscillator.

4.4 3.3 Energy of the circuit

The instantaneous power delivered to an element is \(P = VI\). Integrating in time gives the energy stored in each element as a function of the canonical variables.

Inductive energy. Using \(V = L\,dI/dt\) and the node-flux relation \(V = \dot\Phi\) (so \(L\,I = \Phi\)): \[ E_L \;=\; \int_{-\infty}^{t} V\, I\, dt' \;=\; \int_{-\infty}^{t} L\,\dot I\, I\, dt' \;=\; \tfrac{1}{2} L\, I^2 \;=\; \frac{\Phi^2}{2L}. \]

Capacitive energy. Using \(I = \dot q\) and \(V = q/C\): \[ E_C \;=\; \int_{-\infty}^{t} V\, I\, dt' \;=\; \int_{-\infty}^{t} \frac{q\,\dot q}{C}\, dt' \;=\; \frac{q^2}{2C}. \]

Total energy. \[ \boxed{\quad E \;=\; \frac{q^2}{2C} \;+\; \frac{\Phi^2}{2L} \quad} \]

Energy oscillates back and forth between the capacitor and the inductor. The first term, quadratic in \(q\) and divided by \(2C\), plays the role of kinetic energy; the second term, quadratic in \(\Phi\) and divided by \(2L\), plays the role of potential energy. We therefore identify \(\Phi \leftrightarrow\) position and \(q \leftrightarrow\) momentum.

Mechanical–electrical analogy

A particle of mass \(m\) on a spring of stiffness \(k\) has \(E = p^2/(2m) + \tfrac{1}{2} k x^2\). Comparing term by term:

mechanical electrical (LC)
position \(x\) node flux \(\Phi\)
momentum \(p\) node charge \(q\)
mass \(m\) capacitance \(C\)
spring constant \(k\) inverse inductance \(1/L\)
frequency \(\omega_0 = \sqrt{k/m}\) \(\omega_0 = 1/\sqrt{LC}\)
kinetic \(p^2/2m\) capacitive \(q^2/2C\)
potential \(kx^2/2\) inductive \(\Phi^2/2L\)

The dictionary is exact: every formula derived for the mechanical oscillator transfers to the LC oscillator by substitution.

4.5 3.4 Lagrangian and Hamiltonian formulation

To find the classical Hamiltonian we follow the standard recipe of analytical mechanics: pick a generalised coordinate, write the Lagrangian, identify the canonical momentum, and Legendre-transform.

Generalised coordinate. We choose the node flux \(\Phi\) as the generalised coordinate. Its velocity is \(\dot\Phi = V\).

Lagrangian. With kinetic energy \(T = \tfrac{1}{2} C \dot\Phi^2\) (capacitive) and potential energy \(V_{\text{pot}} = \Phi^2/(2L)\) (inductive), \[ \mathcal{L}(\Phi,\dot\Phi) \;=\; T - V_{\text{pot}} \;=\; \frac{1}{2}\,C\,\dot\Phi^2 \;-\; \frac{1}{2L}\,\Phi^2 . \]

Euler–Lagrange equation. A quick check: \(\frac{d}{dt}\!\left(\partial\mathcal{L}/\partial\dot\Phi\right) - \partial\mathcal{L}/\partial\Phi = 0\) gives \(C\ddot\Phi + \Phi/L = 0\), i.e. simple harmonic motion at \(\omega_0 = 1/\sqrt{LC}\). Good — this is the expected classical LC dynamics.

Canonical momentum. Conjugate to \(\Phi\) is \[ q \;=\; \frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial \dot\Phi} \;=\; C\,\dot\Phi . \] So the node charge is literally the canonical momentum of the node flux. This is a beautiful internal consistency check: the variable we called “charge” on physical grounds turns out to be the Hamiltonian conjugate of the variable we called “flux”.

Hamiltonian (Legendre transform). Invert \(q = C\dot\Phi\) to get \(\dot\Phi = q/C\), then \[ H \;=\; q\,\dot\Phi - \mathcal{L} \;=\; \frac{q^2}{C} - \frac{1}{2}\,C\!\left(\frac{q}{C}\right)^{\!2} + \frac{\Phi^2}{2L} \;=\; \frac{q^2}{2C} + \frac{\Phi^2}{2L} . \]

\[ \boxed{\quad H \;=\; \frac{q^2}{2C} \;+\; \frac{\Phi^2}{2L} \;=\; \frac{q^2}{2C} \;+\; \frac{1}{2}\, C\,\omega_0^2\, \Phi^2 ,\qquad \omega_0 \;=\; \frac{1}{\sqrt{LC}} . \quad} \]

This is the Hamiltonian of the classical LC oscillator. Energy sloshes between capacitor and inductor at angular frequency \(\omega_0\).

4.6 3.5 Canonical quantisation

We now quantise the classical Hamiltonian via the canonical recipe: promote the canonical pair to operators with the canonical commutator. \[ \Phi \;\to\; \hat\Phi ,\qquad q \;\to\; \hat q ,\qquad [\hat\Phi,\hat q] \;=\; i\hbar . \]

The quantum Hamiltonian is \[ \hat H \;=\; \frac{\hat q^2}{2C} \;+\; \frac{\hat\Phi^2}{2L} . \]

This is exactly the quantum harmonic oscillator with the dictionary \(m \leftrightarrow C\), \(k \leftrightarrow 1/L\), \(x \leftrightarrow \Phi\), \(p \leftrightarrow q\).

Ladder operators. Following the textbook construction, define \[ \hat\Phi \;=\; \Phi_{\text{zpf}}\,(a + a^\dagger) ,\qquad \hat q \;=\; i\,q_{\text{zpf}}\,(a^\dagger - a) , \] with the zero-point fluctuations \[ \Phi_{\text{zpf}} \;=\; \sqrt{\frac{\hbar Z}{2}} ,\qquad q_{\text{zpf}} \;=\; \sqrt{\frac{\hbar}{2Z}} ,\qquad Z \;=\; \sqrt{\frac{L}{C}} . \] Here \(Z\) is the characteristic impedance of the oscillator (it has units of Ohms). The product \(\Phi_{\text{zpf}}\, q_{\text{zpf}} = \hbar/2\) is fixed by the canonical commutator and is independent of \(Z\); the impedance only controls how the \(\hbar/2\) uncertainty is split between flux and charge.

Diagonal form. Substituting into \(\hat H\) gives \[ \boxed{\quad \hat H \;=\; \hbar\,\omega_0\!\left(a^\dagger a + \tfrac{1}{2}\right) ,\qquad \omega_0 \;=\; \frac{1}{\sqrt{LC}} . \quad} \] The number operator \(\hat n = a^\dagger a\) counts excitations (microwave photons) stored in the LC mode; eigenstates \(\ket{n}\) have energy \(E_n = \hbar\omega_0(n+\tfrac{1}{2})\).

What we just did. This procedure quantises one mode of the electromagnetic field, macroscopically, in a circuit big enough to see and wire-bond. Its validity is experimentally verified to high precision (and recognised by the 2025 Nobel Prize in Physics to J. Martinis, M. Devoret and J. Clarke for the demonstration of macroscopic quantum tunnelling and energy quantisation in superconducting circuits).

Frequency window. Two practical observations bracket the usable range of \(\omega_0\):

  • Lower bound — thermal noise. To resolve individual quanta we need \(\hbar\omega_0 \gg k_B T\). A useful rule of thumb: \(1\) MHz \(\sim 480\) pK, \(10\) GHz \(\sim 480\) mK. Dilution refrigerators reach \(T \sim 10\) mK on solid-state samples, so \[ \nu_0 \;=\; \frac{\omega_0}{2\pi} \;\gtrsim\; \frac{k_B T}{h} \;\sim\; \frac{1.38\times 10^{-23} \times 0.01}{6.6\times 10^{-34}} \;\sim\; 0.2\,\text{GHz} \;\;\Longrightarrow\;\; \nu_0 \;\gtrsim\; 1\,\text{GHz} . \]
  • Upper bound — pair breaking. We must not exceed the BCS gap, otherwise a photon breaks a Cooper pair and the superconducting description collapses: \[ \nu_0 \;\ll\; \Delta_{\text{BCS}}/h . \] In Al, \(\Delta_{\text{BCS}}/h \sim 50\) GHz; in Nb it is larger. Even apart from this, in the interval \(0.1 - 10\) THz sources and detectors for single photons are inefficient. The “sweet spot” for superconducting qubits is therefore \(\nu_0 \sim 1-10\) GHz.
Definition — LC oscillator as harmonic-oscillator analogue

The LC oscillator is a lumped-element electrical circuit with Hamiltonian \[ \hat H_{LC} \;=\; \frac{\hat q^2}{2C} + \frac{\hat\Phi^2}{2L} \;=\; \hbar\omega_0\!\left(a^\dagger a + \tfrac{1}{2}\right), \] with \(\omega_0 = 1/\sqrt{LC}\). It is the electromagnetic analogue of the quantum mechanical harmonic oscillator, with capacitance \(C\) playing the role of mass, \(1/L\) that of spring constant, flux \(\hat\Phi\) that of position, and charge \(\hat q\) that of momentum.

4.6.1 Why this is not yet a qubit

The eigenenergies \(E_n = \hbar\omega_0(n+\tfrac{1}{2})\) form a uniform ladder: the transition \(\ket{0}\to\ket{1}\) has the same frequency as \(\ket{1}\to\ket{2}\), \(\ket{2}\to\ket{3}\), and so on. A microwave drive tuned to \(\omega_0\) therefore does not address the two-level subspace \(\{\ket{0},\ket{1}\}\) cleanly — population leaks into \(\ket{2}\) and higher. The LC circuit is the wrong shape for a qubit.

4.6.2 Bridge to Ch. 4

The non-linearity needed to turn this LC oscillator into a qubit is supplied by the Josephson junction, treated in Ch. 4. We will see that replacing the linear inductor with a JJ gives an inductive energy of the form \(-E_J\cos(\hat\varphi)\) instead of \(\hat\Phi^2/(2L)\). Its Taylor expansion contributes a \(\hat\Phi^4\) term to the Hamiltonian, which lifts the harmonic-ladder degeneracy and produces a genuinely anharmonic spectrum — the prerequisite for a qubit.

## 3.4 Lagrangian and Hamiltonian formulation

To find the *classical* Hamiltonian we follow the standard recipe of analytical mechanics: pick a generalised coordinate, write the Lagrangian, identify the canonical momentum, and Legendre-transform.

**Generalised coordinate.** We choose the node flux $\Phi$ as the generalised coordinate. Its velocity is $\dot\Phi = V$.

**Lagrangian.** With *kinetic* energy $T = \tfrac{1}{2} C \dot\Phi^2$ (capacitive) and *potential* energy $V_{\text{pot}} = \Phi^2/(2L)$ (inductive),
$$
\mathcal{L}(\Phi,\dot\Phi) \;=\; T - V_{\text{pot}} \;=\; \frac{1}{2}\,C\,\dot\Phi^2 \;-\; \frac{1}{2L}\,\Phi^2 .
$$

**Euler–Lagrange equation.** A quick check: $\frac{d}{dt}\!\left(\partial\mathcal{L}/\partial\dot\Phi\right) - \partial\mathcal{L}/\partial\Phi = 0$ gives $C\ddot\Phi + \Phi/L = 0$, i.e. simple harmonic motion at $\omega_0 = 1/\sqrt{LC}$. Good — this is the expected classical LC dynamics.

**Canonical momentum.** Conjugate to $\Phi$ is
$$
q \;=\; \frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial \dot\Phi} \;=\; C\,\dot\Phi .
$$
So the node charge is *literally* the canonical momentum of the node flux. This is a beautiful internal consistency check: the variable we called "charge" on physical grounds turns out to be the Hamiltonian conjugate of the variable we called "flux".

**Hamiltonian (Legendre transform).** Invert $q = C\dot\Phi$ to get $\dot\Phi = q/C$, then
$$
H \;=\; q\,\dot\Phi - \mathcal{L} \;=\; \frac{q^2}{C} - \frac{1}{2}\,C\!\left(\frac{q}{C}\right)^{\!2} + \frac{\Phi^2}{2L} \;=\; \frac{q^2}{2C} + \frac{\Phi^2}{2L} .
$$

$$
\boxed{\quad H \;=\; \frac{q^2}{2C} \;+\; \frac{\Phi^2}{2L} \;=\; \frac{q^2}{2C} \;+\; \frac{1}{2}\, C\,\omega_0^2\, \Phi^2 ,\qquad \omega_0 \;=\; \frac{1}{\sqrt{LC}} . \quad}
$$

This is the Hamiltonian of the classical LC oscillator. Energy sloshes between capacitor and inductor at angular frequency $\omega_0$.

## 3.5 Canonical quantisation

We now quantise the classical Hamiltonian via the canonical recipe: promote the canonical pair to operators with the canonical commutator.
$$
\Phi \;\to\; \hat\Phi ,\qquad q \;\to\; \hat q ,\qquad [\hat\Phi,\hat q] \;=\; i\hbar .
$$

The quantum Hamiltonian is
$$
\hat H \;=\; \frac{\hat q^2}{2C} \;+\; \frac{\hat\Phi^2}{2L} .
$$

This is *exactly* the quantum harmonic oscillator with the dictionary $m \leftrightarrow C$, $k \leftrightarrow 1/L$, $x \leftrightarrow \Phi$, $p \leftrightarrow q$.

**Ladder operators.** Following the textbook construction, define
$$
\hat\Phi \;=\; \Phi_{\text{zpf}}\,(a + a^\dagger) ,\qquad
\hat q \;=\; i\,q_{\text{zpf}}\,(a^\dagger - a) ,
$$
with the *zero-point fluctuations*
$$
\Phi_{\text{zpf}} \;=\; \sqrt{\frac{\hbar Z}{2}} ,\qquad
q_{\text{zpf}} \;=\; \sqrt{\frac{\hbar}{2Z}} ,\qquad
Z \;=\; \sqrt{\frac{L}{C}} .
$$
Here $Z$ is the **characteristic impedance** of the oscillator (it has units of Ohms). The product $\Phi_{\text{zpf}}\, q_{\text{zpf}} = \hbar/2$ is fixed by the canonical commutator and is independent of $Z$; the impedance only controls how the $\hbar/2$ uncertainty is split between flux and charge.

**Diagonal form.** Substituting into $\hat H$ gives
$$
\boxed{\quad \hat H \;=\; \hbar\,\omega_0\!\left(a^\dagger a + \tfrac{1}{2}\right) ,\qquad \omega_0 \;=\; \frac{1}{\sqrt{LC}} . \quad}
$$
The number operator $\hat n = a^\dagger a$ counts excitations (microwave photons) stored in the LC mode; eigenstates $\ket{n}$ have energy $E_n = \hbar\omega_0(n+\tfrac{1}{2})$.

**What we just did.** This procedure quantises one mode of the electromagnetic field, *macroscopically*, in a circuit big enough to see and wire-bond. Its validity is experimentally verified to high precision (and recognised by the 2025 Nobel Prize in Physics to J. Martinis, M. Devoret and J. Clarke for the demonstration of macroscopic quantum tunnelling and energy quantisation in superconducting circuits).

**Frequency window.** Two practical observations bracket the usable range of $\omega_0$:

- **Lower bound — thermal noise.** To resolve individual quanta we need $\hbar\omega_0 \gg k_B T$. A useful rule of thumb: $1$ MHz $\sim 480$ pK, $10$ GHz $\sim 480$ mK. Dilution refrigerators reach $T \sim 10$ mK on solid-state samples, so
$$
\nu_0 \;=\; \frac{\omega_0}{2\pi} \;\gtrsim\; \frac{k_B T}{h} \;\sim\; \frac{1.38\times 10^{-23} \times 0.01}{6.6\times 10^{-34}} \;\sim\; 0.2\,\text{GHz} \;\;\Longrightarrow\;\; \nu_0 \;\gtrsim\; 1\,\text{GHz} .
$$
- **Upper bound — pair breaking.** We must not exceed the BCS gap, otherwise a photon breaks a Cooper pair and the superconducting description collapses:
$$
\nu_0 \;\ll\; \Delta_{\text{BCS}}/h .
$$
In Al, $\Delta_{\text{BCS}}/h \sim 50$ GHz; in Nb it is larger. Even apart from this, in the interval $0.1 - 10$ THz sources and detectors for single photons are inefficient. The "sweet spot" for superconducting qubits is therefore $\nu_0 \sim 1-10$ GHz.
