11.1 Introduction: The multimodality trap

Standard Metropolis–Hastings and Hamiltonian Monte Carlo samplers work well when the target posterior is unimodal or has modes close together. However, when the posterior has several well-separated modes, a single MCMC chain becomes trapped near the mode it encounters first. It cannot escape because crossing the energy barrier between modes occurs with probability decaying exponentially in the barrier height.

Consider two modes separated by a barrier of height \(\Delta E\). The probability of escaping is approximately \[p \approx \exp\left(-\frac{\Delta E}{T}\right)\] by the Arrhenius formula, where we write the posterior as \(\pi(\theta) = \exp[-E(\theta)]\). When \(\Delta E \gg 1\), this probability is negligibly small, and the chain explores only a tiny fraction of the parameter space.

Simulated annealing and parallel tempering both tackle this problem by introducing a temperature parameter. They exploit a key observation: at high temperature \(T\), the posterior is flattened, and energy barriers become easier to cross. The two methods differ in how they use this idea.

11.2 Tempered distributions and temperatures

For any target posterior \(\pi(\theta)\) with energy representation \(\pi(\theta) = \exp[-E(\theta)]\), we define the tempered posterior at inverse temperature \(\beta \in (0, 1]\) by \[\pi_\beta(\theta) \propto \pi(\theta)^\beta = \exp[-\beta E(\theta)].\]

Equivalently, using temperature \(T = 1/\beta\), \[\pi_T(\theta) \propto \pi(\theta)^{1/T} = \exp\left[-\frac{E(\theta)}{T}\right].\]

The two limits play distinct roles:

  • Cold limit (\(\beta \to 1\) or \(T \to 1\)). \(\pi_\beta(\theta) \to \pi(\theta)\), the original posterior.
  • Hot limit (\(\beta \to 0\) or \(T \to \infty\)). \(\pi_\beta(\theta)\) becomes nearly flat, and the chain explores the entire space freely.
  • Concentrated limit (\(T \to 0\), used in optimization). As the temperature decreases to zero, \(\pi_T(\theta)\) concentrates its mass on the global minima of \(E(\theta)\).

At high temperature, the contrast between modes vanishes, making it easy for the chain to move between them. As the temperature decreases, the modes become increasingly separated again.

11.3 Simulated annealing

Simulated annealing is an optimization method that cools a single chain to find the maximum a posteriori (MAP) estimator \(\hat{\theta}_{\text{MAP}} = \arg\max_\theta \pi(\theta)\). It generates a sequence of increasingly concentrated distributions by running a cooling schedule.

Simulated annealing algorithm:

  1. Initialize \(\theta_0\) and temperature \(T_0 > 0\).
  2. For \(k = 0, 1, \ldots, K\):
    • Propose \(\theta' \sim q(\theta' \mid \theta_k)\).
    • Accept with probability \(\alpha_T(\theta_k \to \theta') = \min\{1, \exp[-\beta_k(E(\theta') - E(\theta_k))]\}\) where \(\beta_k = 1/T_k\).
    • Update \(T_{k+1} = \gamma T_k\) for some \(0 < \gamma < 1\) (e.g., geometric cooling) or use \(T_k = c / \log k\) (logarithmic cooling).
  3. Return the state \(\theta\) with highest \(\pi(\theta)\) encountered.

The choice of cooling schedule controls both correctness and cost:

  • Logarithmic cooling and barrier crossing. With \(T_k = c / \log k\), the chain crosses any fixed energy barrier infinitely often (with probability 1) as \(k \to \infty\), because the cumulative escape probability \[\sum_{k=1}^\infty p_k = \sum_{k=1}^\infty \exp\left(-\frac{\Delta E \cdot \log k}{c}\right) = \sum_{k=1}^\infty k^{-\Delta E/c}\] diverges if and only if \(\Delta E/c \leq 1\), i.e., \(c \geq \Delta E\). Simulated annealing is therefore theoretically guaranteed to find the global optimum almost surely with sufficiently slow cooling.
  • Geometric cooling in practice. Slower cooling requires more iterations, so the geometric schedule \(T_{k+1} = \gamma T_k\) is usually preferred for computational speed, accepting that the optimality guarantee becomes heuristic.
  • What is returned. The output is the state with the highest \(\pi(\theta)\) encountered (or the final low-temperature state), not a sample from \(\pi\) — annealing answers an optimization question, not a sampling one.

11.4 Parallel tempering (replica exchange)

Parallel tempering runs \(K\) chains in parallel, each at a different inverse temperature \(\beta_1 = 1 > \beta_2 > \cdots > \beta_K > 0\). The chain at \(\beta_1 = 1\) is the “cold chain” that targets the posterior of interest. Chains at smaller \(\beta\) are “hot chains” that explore more freely. The key innovation is swaps: occasionally, we exchange the states of neighbouring chains. This allows the cold chain to benefit from discoveries made by hot chains, enabling it to escape local modes without the cold chain ever needing to climb the barrier alone.

The algorithm is built from two pieces — a joint target and two move types:

  • Joint target. Let \(\theta_l\) denote the state of chain \(l\), write \(\Theta = (\theta_1, \ldots, \theta_K)\), and define the product target \[\Pi(\Theta) = \prod_{l=1}^K \pi_{\beta_l}(\theta_l) \propto \prod_{l=1}^K \pi(\theta_l)^{\beta_l}.\]
  • Within-temperature moves. Each chain runs a standard Metropolis–Hastings update with the tempered acceptance probability \[\alpha_l(\theta_l \to \theta_l') = \min\left\{1, \left(\frac{\pi(\theta_l')}{\pi(\theta_l)}\right)^{\beta_l} \frac{q(\theta_l \mid \theta_l')}{q(\theta_l' \mid \theta_l)}\right\}.\] Hot chains (small \(\beta_l\)) accept uphill moves more readily, allowing them to traverse the landscape freely.

The marginal distribution of \(\theta_1\) (the cold chain) is exactly \[\pi_{\beta_1}(\theta_1) = \pi(\theta_1),\] since \(\beta_1 = 1\). Samples from the cold chain are valid posterior samples, and expectations computed from the cold chain are unbiased estimates of posterior expectations.

11.4.1 Swap moves and detailed balance

The second type of move exchanges states between neighbouring chains \(l\) and \(l+1\): \[(\theta_l, \theta_{l+1}) \to (\theta_{l+1}, \theta_l).\]

The Metropolis acceptance probability for this move is \[\alpha_{\text{swap}}(\theta_l, \theta_{l+1}) = \min\left\{1, \frac{\pi(\theta_{l+1})^{\beta_l} \pi(\theta_l)^{\beta_{l+1}}}{\pi(\theta_l)^{\beta_l} \pi(\theta_{l+1})^{\beta_{l+1}}}\right\}.\]

In log form (preferred numerically): \[\log \alpha_{\text{swap}} = \min\{0, (\beta_l - \beta_{l+1})[E(\theta_l) - E(\theta_{l+1})]\}.\]

The normalizing constants cancel, making the swap probability independent of the partition function.

The combined algorithm of within-temperature MH updates and swap moves satisfies detailed balance with respect to the product target \(\Pi(\Theta)\). Therefore, the cold chain’s marginal converges to \(\pi\).

Proof. Within-temperature moves leave \(\pi_{\beta_l}(\theta_l)\) invariant at each temperature. Swap moves satisfy detailed balance for the product density: the probability of being in state \((\theta_l, \theta_{l+1})\) and swapping to \((\theta_{l+1}, \theta_l)\) equals the probability of being in \((\theta_{l+1}, \theta_l)\) and swapping back (by the Metropolis ratio). The product of these two reversible operations preserves \(\Pi(\Theta)\).

The mechanism that makes the cold chain mix can now be read off the construction:

  • Hot chains explore freely. Because \(\beta_l\) is small for hot chains, they traverse the landscape easily, finding new modes.
  • Swaps transport information. When a hot chain discovers a mode, a swap can move it to a colder chain, bringing the discovery closer to the target.
  • Cold chain remains on target. The cold chain never leaves the target distribution \(\pi\), yet it benefits from the hot chains’ discoveries via successful swaps.

This is the crucial difference from simulated annealing: the temperature is fixed, not decreasing. The algorithm is homogeneous, run indefinitely, and all cold-chain samples are valid posterior samples.

11.5 Temperature ladder design

The spacing of temperatures is critical. If \(\beta\) values are too far apart, swap acceptance rates become vanishingly small, and information cannot flow between chains. If they are too close, many chains are needed. Three design choices control the trade-off:

  • Geometric spacing. A simple and effective choice is \[\beta_l = \beta_1 r^{l-1}, \quad 0 < r < 1,\] which keeps temperatures evenly spaced in a logarithmic sense. Under the standard assumption that the energy variance scales linearly with the dimension, geometric spacing approximately equalises swap acceptance between every adjacent pair.
  • Target swap acceptance. A practical rule of thumb is to set the ladder so that the swap acceptance probability between adjacent chains is in the range 20–40%. Below this range information stops flowing along the ladder; above it the ladder is denser than necessary and one is paying for redundant chains.
  • All-pairs vs. neighbour-only swaps. The basic algorithm swaps only neighbouring pairs. Allowing swaps between arbitrary chains (“all-pairs”) can improve mixing but at higher computational cost. For most problems, neighbour-only swaps are sufficient if the ladder is carefully tuned.

11.6 Worked example: bimodal 1D mixture

Consider a bimodal posterior that is a mixture of two Gaussians: \[\pi(\theta) \propto 0.35 \, N(\theta; -4, 1) + 0.65 \, N(\theta; 3, 0.7^2).\]

The right mode is higher. We will show that a single-chain MH sampler gets trapped, while parallel tempering explores both modes.

# Log-posterior for bimodal mixture (minus normalization)
log_pi <- function(x) {
  a <- log(0.35) + dnorm(x, -4, 1, log = TRUE)
  b <- log(0.65) + dnorm(x, 3, 0.7, log = TRUE)
  m <- pmax(a, b)
  m + log(exp(a - m) + exp(b - m))
}

# Target density (for plotting)
pi_density <- function(x) {
  0.35 * dnorm(x, -4, 1) + 0.65 * dnorm(x, 3, 0.7)
}

# Single MH step at temperature beta
tempered_step <- function(x, beta, step = 1.0) {
  x_prop <- x + rnorm(1, 0, step)
  loga <- beta * (log_pi(x_prop) - log_pi(x))
  if (log(runif(1)) < loga) x_prop else x
}
Show code 
single_chain_mh <- function(n_iter = 20000, x0 = -4, step = 1.0) {
  x <- x0
  out <- numeric(n_iter)
  for (i in 1:n_iter) {
    x <- tempered_step(x, beta = 1.0, step = step)
    out[i] <- x
  }
  out
}

set.seed(1)
trace_single <- single_chain_mh(n_iter = 20000, x0 = -4, step = 1.2)

parallel_tempering <- function(n_iter = 20000,
                               betas = c(1.0, 0.65, 0.4, 0.2, 0.08),
                               step = 1.2) {
  L <- length(betas)
  x <- seq(-4, 4, length.out = L)  # Initialize chains at different locations

  x_by_temp <- matrix(NA, nrow = n_iter, ncol = L)

  for (i in 1:n_iter) {
    # Within-temperature MH updates
    for (l in 1:L) {
      x[l] <- tempered_step(x[l], betas[l], step = step)
    }

    # Swap moves between neighbouring chains
    for (l in sample(1:(L - 1))) {
      loga <- (betas[l] - betas[l + 1]) * (log_pi(x[l + 1]) - log_pi(x[l]))
      if (log(runif(1)) < loga) {
        tmp <- x[l]
        x[l] <- x[l + 1]
        x[l + 1] <- tmp
      }
    }

    # Store state of cold chain (beta = 1)
    x_by_temp[i, ] <- x
  }

  list(x_by_temp = x_by_temp, betas = betas)
}

set.seed(1)
res_pt <- parallel_tempering(n_iter = 20000,
                             betas = c(1.0, 0.65, 0.4, 0.2, 0.08),
                             step = 1.2)

11.7 Practical considerations

Choosing whether to deploy parallel tempering — and what to compare it against — comes down to the question being asked and the resources available:

  • Use parallel tempering when the posterior is multimodal with well-separated modes, standard MCMC chains get trapped, the interest is in accurate posterior sampling and uncertainty quantification, or the problem involves phase transitions or critical phenomena (common in physics).
  • Use simulated annealing when the goal is to find the MAP estimate or a point estimate, optimization (not sampling) is the primary objective, and you need fast identification of the best fit, not uncertainty.
  • Prefer HMC/NUTS when the posterior is unimodal and high-dimensional, gradient information is available, or computational resources are limited and you cannot afford many chains. HMC and NUTS excel in high dimensions because they use gradient information to make large, informed moves, but they fail on multimodal targets because a single trajectory cannot jump between modes.
  • Computational cost. Running \(K\) chains costs roughly \(K\) times as much as a single chain. However, this is often money well spent: the mixing time of the cold chain typically decreases dramatically when the temperature ladder is well-designed.

11.8 Summary

Tempered distributions scale the exponent of the posterior: \(\pi_\beta(\theta) \propto \pi(\theta)^\beta\). At \(\beta = 1\) we recover the target; at \(\beta \to 0\) the distribution flattens.

Simulated annealing is a single-chain method with a time-varying cooling schedule; it targets optimization (MAP estimation) and asymptotically concentrates on the global maximum.

Parallel tempering (replica exchange MCMC) runs \(K\) chains at fixed inverse temperatures \(1 = \beta_1 > \beta_2 > \cdots > \beta_K\) and periodically swaps states between neighbours. The cold chain remains on the target distribution throughout and escapes local modes via information shared from hotter chains.

Detailed balance is satisfied: within-temperature moves are standard MH, and swap moves satisfy detailed balance with respect to the product target \(\Pi(\Theta) = \prod_{l=1}^K \pi_{\beta_l}(\theta_l)\). Thus the cold chain’s marginal is exactly \(\pi\).

Temperature ladder design is critical: geometric spacing in inverse temperature and target swap acceptance rates of 20–40% are practical guidelines.

The worked example shows that parallel tempering succeeds where single-chain MH fails on a bimodal target, recovering the true posterior over both modes.

11.9 Exercises

Define the tempered distribution \(\pi_\beta(x) \propto \pi(x)^\beta\) for \(\beta \in [0, 1]\). Identify the two limiting cases \(\beta = 0\) and \(\beta = 1\).

Solution. At \(\beta = 1\) we recover the target, \[\pi_1(x) \propto \pi(x),\] while at \(\beta = 0\) we have \(\pi(x)^0 = 1\), so \[\pi_0(x) \propto 1,\] i.e. the uniform (or prior, in a Bayesian setting) reference distribution on the support of \(\pi\).

Let \(\pi(x) \propto \exp(-U(x))\). Show that \(\pi_\beta(x) \propto \exp(-\beta U(x))\). Explain why lowering \(\beta\) flattens the landscape and lowers energy barriers.

Solution. Raising the density to the power \(\beta\) gives \[\pi_\beta(x) \propto \pi(x)^\beta = \big[\exp(-U(x))\big]^\beta = \exp(-\beta U(x)).\] The effective energy becomes \(\beta U(x)\), so all energy differences (including barrier heights \(\Delta U\)) are rescaled by \(\beta\). For \(\beta < 1\) this shrinks the contrast between modes and valleys, so the chain crosses barriers with probability \(\sim e^{-\beta\Delta U}\) instead of \(e^{-\Delta U}\).

Bimodal target. Consider \(\pi(x) = \tfrac{1}{2}\mathcal{N}(-3, 1) + \tfrac{1}{2}\mathcal{N}(3, 1)\). Sketch (qualitatively) the tempered density \(\pi_\beta\) for \(\beta = 1, 0.5, 0.1\) and explain why a random-walk Metropolis chain on \(\pi_1\) has trouble crossing the mode gap.

Solution. At \(\beta = 1\) the density has two sharp peaks at \(\pm 3\) separated by a deep valley near \(0\); at \(\beta = 0.5\) the peaks broaden and the valley between them rises; at \(\beta = 0.1\) the density is nearly flat across \([-5,5]\). Random-walk MH must traverse the low-density gap by a sequence of small steps, each accepted with probability \(\sim e^{-\Delta U}\) where \(\Delta U \approx U(0) - U(\pm 3) \approx \tfrac{9}{2}\), giving an exponentially small per-step crossing probability and effective trapping in one mode.

Swap acceptance probability. For replicas at inverse temperatures \(\beta_i\) and \(\beta_j\) holding states \(x_i, x_j\), derive the Metropolis–Hastings acceptance probability \[\alpha_{ij} = \min\!\left\{1,\ \exp\!\big[(\beta_i - \beta_j)(U(x_i) - U(x_j))\big]\right\}.\]

Solution. The product target is \(\Pi(x_i, x_j) \propto e^{-\beta_i U(x_i) - \beta_j U(x_j)}\), and the swap proposal \((x_i, x_j) \to (x_j, x_i)\) is deterministic and symmetric. The MH ratio is therefore \[\frac{\Pi(x_j, x_i)}{\Pi(x_i, x_j)} = \frac{e^{-\beta_i U(x_j) - \beta_j U(x_i)}}{e^{-\beta_i U(x_i) - \beta_j U(x_j)}} = \exp\!\big[(\beta_i - \beta_j)(U(x_i) - U(x_j))\big],\] so \(\alpha_{ij} = \min\{1, \exp[(\beta_i - \beta_j)(U(x_i) - U(x_j))]\}\).

Show that the swap move described above satisfies detailed balance with respect to the product target \(\Pi(x_1, \ldots, x_K) = \prod_l \pi_{\beta_l}(x_l)\). Use this to argue that the marginal of the cold chain is exactly \(\pi\).

Solution. For the swap of replicas \(i, j\) with symmetric proposal probability \(q\), the MH rule gives \[\Pi(\ldots, x_i, \ldots, x_j, \ldots)\, q\, \alpha_{ij} = \Pi(\ldots, x_j, \ldots, x_i, \ldots)\, q\, \alpha_{ji},\] which is exactly the standard MH detailed balance identity using \(\alpha_{ij} = \min\{1, \Pi'/\Pi\}\) (all factors at other replicas cancel). Since both within-temperature moves and swaps preserve \(\Pi\), the stationary distribution is \(\Pi\), and the marginal over \(x_1\) at \(\beta_1 = 1\) is \(\pi_{\beta_1} = \pi\).

Geometric ladder. Show that with a geometric temperature schedule \(\beta_{l+1} / \beta_l = r\) (constant), the expected swap acceptance between adjacent replicas becomes approximately independent of \(l\) when the energy variance scales linearly with the dimension.

Solution. The log swap ratio between replicas \(l, l+1\) is \(\Delta_l = (\beta_l - \beta_{l+1})(U_l - U_{l+1}) = \beta_l(1-r)\,(U_l - U_{l+1})\). Standard scaling arguments give \(\mathrm{Var}_{\pi_\beta}[U] \approx d\, c(\beta)/\beta^2\) for some smooth \(c(\beta)\), so \[\mathrm{Var}(\Delta_l) \approx \beta_l^2 (1-r)^2 \cdot 2 \cdot \frac{d\, c(\beta_l)}{\beta_l^2} = 2 d\, c(\beta_l)\,(1-r)^2,\] which is independent of \(l\) up to the slow variation of \(c(\beta)\). The expected acceptance, governed by the distribution of \(\Delta_l\), is therefore nearly constant along the ladder.

Number of replicas. For a target in \(d\) dimensions with average energy gap \(\Delta U\) between cold and hot ends, estimate the number of replicas \(K\) needed to keep adjacent-pair swap acceptance around \(25\%\). Express your answer in terms of \(\Delta U\) and the desired swap rate.

Solution. For a target acceptance \(a\), each adjacent pair should produce a typical log-ratio of order \(|\log a|\), i.e. \((\beta_l - \beta_{l+1})\,\Delta U_{\text{adj}} \sim |\log a|\), where the per-pair energy gap on a geometric ladder is \(\Delta U_{\text{adj}} \approx \Delta U / (K-1)\). Setting \((1)(\Delta U / (K-1)) \approx |\log a|\) gives \[K \approx 1 + \frac{\Delta U}{|\log a|},\] so for \(a \approx 0.25\), \(|\log a| \approx 1.4\) and \(K \sim 1 + \Delta U / 1.4\). (For a target whose energy variance grows like \(d\), the same argument with \(\sigma_U \sim \sqrt{d}\) rescales this to \(K \sim 1 + \sqrt{d}\,\Delta U / |\log a|\).)

Simulated annealing vs parallel tempering. Compare the two algorithms in two sentences each: what each one tries to achieve, and why PT generates valid posterior samples while annealing typically does not.

Solution. Simulated annealing runs a single chain whose temperature \(T_k \to 0\) on a schedule; it is an optimisation method that concentrates on \(\arg\max \pi\), and the resulting trajectory has a non-stationary, time-inhomogeneous law so its iterates are not samples from any fixed distribution. Parallel tempering, by contrast, runs \(K\) chains at fixed inverse temperatures with \(\beta_1 = 1\) and swaps states between neighbours; the joint stationary distribution is \(\Pi = \prod_l \pi_{\beta_l}\), so the cold chain marginal is exactly \(\pi\) and its iterates are valid posterior samples.

Thermodynamic integration. Show that \[\log Z = -\int_0^1 E_{\pi_\beta}[U]\,d\beta,\] and explain how this identity allows the marginal likelihood (evidence) to be estimated by sampling at multiple temperatures.

Solution. Define \(Z(\beta) = \int e^{-\beta U(x)}\,dx\), with \(Z(0) = |\mathcal{X}|\) (or \(1\) for a normalised reference) and \(Z(1) = Z\). Differentiating under the integral, \[\frac{d \log Z(\beta)}{d\beta} = \frac{-\int U(x) e^{-\beta U(x)}\,dx}{Z(\beta)} = -E_{\pi_\beta}[U],\] so integrating from \(0\) to \(1\) gives \(\log Z = \log Z(1) - \log Z(0) = -\int_0^1 E_{\pi_\beta}[U]\,d\beta\) (taking \(Z(0)=1\)). In practice one estimates \(\hat U(\beta_l) \approx E_{\pi_{\beta_l}}[U]\) from the replica at \(\beta_l\) and applies a quadrature rule (trapezoid/Simpson) over the temperature ladder.

Optimal acceptance rate for swap moves. Discuss why empirical guidance places the optimal swap acceptance in the range \(20\)–\(40\%\). What goes wrong below and above this range?

Solution. The swap rate measures the rate at which information (replicas) flows along the ladder. Below \(\sim 20\%\) the energy histograms of adjacent replicas barely overlap, swaps are rare and the cold chain receives very few hot discoveries (information bottleneck). Above \(\sim 40\%\) adjacent temperatures are nearly identical: each accepted swap conveys little new information per chain and one is paying for redundant replicas, so the wall-clock cost per ESS rises. The empirical sweet spot \(20\)–\(40\%\) balances “swaps happen often enough” against “each swap actually changes the energy”.

Effective sample size after a PT run. With \(K\) replicas, only the cold chain produces posterior samples. Explain why the total wall-clock cost scales with \(K\) but the ESS only grows by the swap mixing improvement, and why the trade-off is still typically favourable for multimodal targets.

Solution. Each PT iteration costs \(\sim K\) likelihood evaluations (one per replica) but only the \(\beta_1\) chain contributes to the posterior estimate, so cost-per-cold-iterate scales as \(K\). The benefit is a reduction of the cold-chain integrated autocorrelation \(\tau\): roughly \(\mathrm{ESS} \sim N / \tau_{\text{PT}}\) vs. \(N / \tau_{\text{single}}\). For unimodal targets \(\tau_{\text{PT}} \approx \tau_{\text{single}}\) and the factor \(K\) is pure overhead, but for multimodal targets a single chain has \(\tau_{\text{single}} \sim e^{\Delta U}\) (exponential in barrier height) while PT gives \(\tau_{\text{PT}} \sim K^2\) (polynomial round-trip time), so the speedup \(\tau_{\text{single}}/(K\,\tau_{\text{PT}}) \sim e^{\Delta U}/K^3\) is enormous.

Energy histogram overlap. Show qualitatively that adjacent replicas must have overlapping energy distributions for swaps to be accepted with reasonable probability. Sketch the case of (a) too few replicas and (b) too many.

Solution.

The swap log-ratio is \((\beta_l - \beta_{l+1})(U_l - U_{l+1})\), which is appreciable only when \(U_l\) and \(U_{l+1}\) are drawn from overlapping distributions; if the histograms of \(U\) at \(\beta_l\) and \(\beta_{l+1}\) are disjoint then \(U_l - U_{l+1}\) is systematically large and negative, making \(\alpha \approx 0\).

Good design has adjacent histograms whose tails overlap noticeably but whose means differ.

Replica exchange for a discrete target. A finite chain has stationary distribution \(\pi\) on \(\{0, 1, \ldots, K-1\}\). Construct the tempered transition kernel at inverse temperature \(\beta\) and write the swap acceptance with a neighbour at \(\beta'\).

Solution. Write \(\pi(x) \propto e^{-U(x)}\) with \(U(x) = -\log \pi(x)\), and define the tempered target on the same state space by \[\pi_\beta(x) \propto \pi(x)^\beta = e^{-\beta U(x)}.\] A simple within-temperature kernel is Metropolis with a symmetric neighbour proposal \(q(x \to y)\) accepted with probability \[\alpha_\beta(x \to y) = \min\!\left\{1, \left(\frac{\pi(y)}{\pi(x)}\right)^{\beta}\right\}.\] The swap with a neighbour at \(\beta'\) holding state \(x'\) uses \[\alpha_{\text{swap}}(x, x') = \min\!\left\{1, \exp\!\big[(\beta - \beta')(U(x) - U(x'))\big]\right\}.\]

Power-posterior interpretation. In Bayesian inference \(\pi(\theta \mid D) \propto L(\theta)^1 p(\theta)\). State the corresponding tempered version \(\pi_\beta(\theta \mid D)\) and identify the prior, the maximum-likelihood-dominated posterior, and the standard posterior as special cases.

Solution. The power posterior is obtained by raising the likelihood to \(\beta\): \[\pi_\beta(\theta \mid D) \propto L(\theta)^\beta\, p(\theta).\] Then \(\beta = 0\) gives \(\pi_0 \propto p(\theta)\) (the prior); \(\beta = 1\) gives \(\pi_1 \propto L(\theta) p(\theta)\) (the standard posterior); and \(\beta \to \infty\) concentrates on \(\arg\max_\theta L(\theta)\), the MLE/likelihood-dominated limit. Intermediate \(\beta \in (0, 1)\) interpolates between prior and posterior and underlies the thermodynamic-integration estimate of the evidence \(\log Z = \int_0^1 E_{\pi_\beta}[\log L]\,d\beta\).

When parallel tempering is overkill. Give two situations where PT is not worth the extra computational cost (i.e. a single-chain HMC or random-walk Metropolis is preferable).

Solution.

  1. Unimodal, well-conditioned targets in moderate-to-high dimension. Here a single HMC/NUTS chain already mixes in \(O(1)\) effective steps; the \(K\times\) overhead of PT buys essentially no extra ESS. (ii) Tight computational budget for a quick exploratory fit, especially when the posterior is unimodal or only mildly multimodal — running \(K\) replicas multiplies the per-iteration cost while a single tuned chain (perhaps with multiple random restarts) gives faster posterior estimates with adequate diagnostics.

## Introduction: The multimodality trap

Standard Metropolis--Hastings and Hamiltonian Monte Carlo samplers work well when the target posterior is unimodal or has modes close together. However, when the posterior has several well-separated modes, a single MCMC chain becomes trapped near the mode it encounters first. It cannot escape because crossing the energy barrier between modes occurs with probability decaying exponentially in the barrier height.

Consider two modes separated by a barrier of height $\Delta E$. The probability of escaping is approximately
$$p \approx \exp\left(-\frac{\Delta E}{T}\right)$$
by the Arrhenius formula, where we write the posterior as $\pi(\theta) = \exp[-E(\theta)]$. When $\Delta E \gg 1$, this probability is negligibly small, and the chain explores only a tiny fraction of the parameter space.

Simulated annealing and parallel tempering both tackle this problem by introducing a temperature parameter. They exploit a key observation: at high temperature $T$, the posterior is flattened, and energy barriers become easier to cross. The two methods differ in how they use this idea.

## Tempered distributions and temperatures

For any target posterior $\pi(\theta)$ with energy representation $\pi(\theta) = \exp[-E(\theta)]$, we define the **tempered posterior** at inverse temperature $\beta \in (0, 1]$ by
$$\pi_\beta(\theta) \propto \pi(\theta)^\beta = \exp[-\beta E(\theta)].$$

Equivalently, using temperature $T = 1/\beta$,
$$\pi_T(\theta) \propto \pi(\theta)^{1/T} = \exp\left[-\frac{E(\theta)}{T}\right].$$

The two limits play distinct roles:

- **Cold limit** ($\beta \to 1$ or $T \to 1$). $\pi_\beta(\theta) \to \pi(\theta)$, the original posterior.
- **Hot limit** ($\beta \to 0$ or $T \to \infty$). $\pi_\beta(\theta)$ becomes nearly flat, and the chain explores the entire space freely.
- **Concentrated limit** ($T \to 0$, used in optimization). As the temperature decreases to zero, $\pi_T(\theta)$ concentrates its mass on the global minima of $E(\theta)$.

At high temperature, the contrast between modes vanishes, making it easy for the chain to move between them. As the temperature decreases, the modes become increasingly separated again.

## Simulated annealing

Simulated annealing is an optimization method that cools a single chain to find the maximum a posteriori (MAP) estimator $\hat{\theta}_{\text{MAP}} = \arg\max_\theta \pi(\theta)$. It generates a sequence of increasingly concentrated distributions by running a cooling schedule.

**Simulated annealing algorithm:**

1. Initialize $\theta_0$ and temperature $T_0 > 0$.
2. For $k = 0, 1, \ldots, K$:
   - Propose $\theta' \sim q(\theta' \mid \theta_k)$.
   - Accept with probability $\alpha_T(\theta_k \to \theta') = \min\{1, \exp[-\beta_k(E(\theta') - E(\theta_k))]\}$ where $\beta_k = 1/T_k$.
   - Update $T_{k+1} = \gamma T_k$ for some $0 < \gamma < 1$ (e.g., geometric cooling) or use $T_k = c / \log k$ (logarithmic cooling).
3. Return the state $\theta$ with highest $\pi(\theta)$ encountered.

The choice of cooling schedule controls both correctness and cost:

- **Logarithmic cooling and barrier crossing.** With $T_k = c / \log k$, the chain crosses any fixed energy barrier infinitely often (with probability 1) as $k \to \infty$, because the cumulative escape probability
$$\sum_{k=1}^\infty p_k = \sum_{k=1}^\infty \exp\left(-\frac{\Delta E \cdot \log k}{c}\right) = \sum_{k=1}^\infty k^{-\Delta E/c}$$
diverges if and only if $\Delta E/c \leq 1$, i.e., $c \geq \Delta E$. Simulated annealing is therefore theoretically guaranteed to find the global optimum almost surely with sufficiently slow cooling.
- **Geometric cooling in practice.** Slower cooling requires more iterations, so the geometric schedule $T_{k+1} = \gamma T_k$ is usually preferred for computational speed, accepting that the optimality guarantee becomes heuristic.
- **What is returned.** The output is the state with the highest $\pi(\theta)$ encountered (or the final low-temperature state), not a sample from $\pi$ — annealing answers an optimization question, not a sampling one.

## Parallel tempering (replica exchange)

Parallel tempering runs **$K$ chains in parallel**, each at a different inverse temperature $\beta_1 = 1 > \beta_2 > \cdots > \beta_K > 0$. The chain at $\beta_1 = 1$ is the "cold chain" that targets the posterior of interest. Chains at smaller $\beta$ are "hot chains" that explore more freely. The key innovation is **swaps**: occasionally, we exchange the states of neighbouring chains. This allows the cold chain to benefit from discoveries made by hot chains, enabling it to escape local modes without the cold chain ever needing to climb the barrier alone.

The algorithm is built from two pieces — a joint target and two move types:

- **Joint target.** Let $\theta_l$ denote the state of chain $l$, write $\Theta = (\theta_1, \ldots, \theta_K)$, and define the **product target**
$$\Pi(\Theta) = \prod_{l=1}^K \pi_{\beta_l}(\theta_l) \propto \prod_{l=1}^K \pi(\theta_l)^{\beta_l}.$$
- **Within-temperature moves.** Each chain runs a standard Metropolis--Hastings update with the tempered acceptance probability
$$\alpha_l(\theta_l \to \theta_l') = \min\left\{1, \left(\frac{\pi(\theta_l')}{\pi(\theta_l)}\right)^{\beta_l} \frac{q(\theta_l \mid \theta_l')}{q(\theta_l' \mid \theta_l)}\right\}.$$
Hot chains (small $\beta_l$) accept uphill moves more readily, allowing them to traverse the landscape freely.

:::{.note}
The marginal distribution of $\theta_1$ (the cold chain) is exactly
$$\pi_{\beta_1}(\theta_1) = \pi(\theta_1),$$
since $\beta_1 = 1$. Samples from the cold chain are valid posterior samples, and expectations computed from the cold chain are unbiased estimates of posterior expectations.
:::

### Swap moves and detailed balance

The second type of move exchanges states between neighbouring chains $l$ and $l+1$:
$$(\theta_l, \theta_{l+1}) \to (\theta_{l+1}, \theta_l).$$

The Metropolis acceptance probability for this move is
$$\alpha_{\text{swap}}(\theta_l, \theta_{l+1}) = \min\left\{1, \frac{\pi(\theta_{l+1})^{\beta_l} \pi(\theta_l)^{\beta_{l+1}}}{\pi(\theta_l)^{\beta_l} \pi(\theta_{l+1})^{\beta_{l+1}}}\right\}.$$

In log form (preferred numerically):
$$\log \alpha_{\text{swap}} = \min\{0, (\beta_l - \beta_{l+1})[E(\theta_l) - E(\theta_{l+1})]\}.$$

The normalizing constants cancel, making the swap probability independent of the partition function.

:::{.theorem}
The combined algorithm of within-temperature MH updates and swap moves satisfies detailed balance with respect to the product target $\Pi(\Theta)$. Therefore, the cold chain's marginal converges to $\pi$.
:::

:::{.proof}
Within-temperature moves leave $\pi_{\beta_l}(\theta_l)$ invariant at each temperature. Swap moves satisfy detailed balance for the product density: the probability of being in state $(\theta_l, \theta_{l+1})$ and swapping to $(\theta_{l+1}, \theta_l)$ equals the probability of being in $(\theta_{l+1}, \theta_l)$ and swapping back (by the Metropolis ratio). The product of these two reversible operations preserves $\Pi(\Theta)$.
:::

The mechanism that makes the cold chain mix can now be read off the construction:

- **Hot chains explore freely.** Because $\beta_l$ is small for hot chains, they traverse the landscape easily, finding new modes.
- **Swaps transport information.** When a hot chain discovers a mode, a swap can move it to a colder chain, bringing the discovery closer to the target.
- **Cold chain remains on target.** The cold chain never leaves the target distribution $\pi$, yet it benefits from the hot chains' discoveries via successful swaps.

This is the crucial difference from simulated annealing: the temperature is fixed, not decreasing. The algorithm is homogeneous, run indefinitely, and all cold-chain samples are valid posterior samples.

## Temperature ladder design

The spacing of temperatures is critical. If $\beta$ values are too far apart, swap acceptance rates become vanishingly small, and information cannot flow between chains. If they are too close, many chains are needed. Three design choices control the trade-off:

- **Geometric spacing.** A simple and effective choice is
$$\beta_l = \beta_1 r^{l-1}, \quad 0 < r < 1,$$
which keeps temperatures evenly spaced in a logarithmic sense. Under the standard assumption that the energy variance scales linearly with the dimension, geometric spacing approximately equalises swap acceptance between every adjacent pair.
- **Target swap acceptance.** A practical rule of thumb is to set the ladder so that the swap acceptance probability between adjacent chains is in the range 20--40%. Below this range information stops flowing along the ladder; above it the ladder is denser than necessary and one is paying for redundant chains.
- **All-pairs vs.\ neighbour-only swaps.** The basic algorithm swaps only neighbouring pairs. Allowing swaps between arbitrary chains ("all-pairs") can improve mixing but at higher computational cost. For most problems, neighbour-only swaps are sufficient if the ladder is carefully tuned.

## Worked example: bimodal 1D mixture

:::{.example}
Consider a bimodal posterior that is a mixture of two Gaussians:
$$\pi(\theta) \propto 0.35 \, N(\theta; -4, 1) + 0.65 \, N(\theta; 3, 0.7^2).$$

The right mode is higher. We will show that a single-chain MH sampler gets trapped, while parallel tempering explores both modes.
:::

```{r}
#| label: lst-tempered-update
#| lst-cap: "Metropolis update at tempered temperature."

# Log-posterior for bimodal mixture (minus normalization)
log_pi <- function(x) {
  a <- log(0.35) + dnorm(x, -4, 1, log = TRUE)
  b <- log(0.65) + dnorm(x, 3, 0.7, log = TRUE)
  m <- pmax(a, b)
  m + log(exp(a - m) + exp(b - m))
}

# Target density (for plotting)
pi_density <- function(x) {
  0.35 * dnorm(x, -4, 1) + 0.65 * dnorm(x, 3, 0.7)
}

# Single MH step at temperature beta
tempered_step <- function(x, beta, step = 1.0) {
  x_prop <- x + rnorm(1, 0, step)
  loga <- beta * (log_pi(x_prop) - log_pi(x))
  if (log(runif(1)) < loga) x_prop else x
}
```

#| label: lst-single-chain
#| lst-cap: "Single-chain MH sampler on bimodal target."

single_chain_mh <- function(n_iter = 20000, x0 = -4, step = 1.0) {
  x <- x0
  out <- numeric(n_iter)
  for (i in 1:n_iter) {
    x <- tempered_step(x, beta = 1.0, step = step)
    out[i] <- x
  }
  out
}

```{r}
#| label: lst-parallel-tempering
#| lst-cap: "Parallel tempering algorithm with swaps."

parallel_tempering <- function(n_iter = 20000,
                               betas = c(1.0, 0.65, 0.4, 0.2, 0.08),
                               step = 1.2) {
  L <- length(betas)
  x <- seq(-4, 4, length.out = L)  # Initialize chains at different locations

  x_by_temp <- matrix(NA, nrow = n_iter, ncol = L)

  for (i in 1:n_iter) {
    # Within-temperature MH updates
    for (l in 1:L) {
      x[l] <- tempered_step(x[l], betas[l], step = step)
    }

    # Swap moves between neighbouring chains
    for (l in sample(1:(L - 1))) {
      loga <- (betas[l] - betas[l + 1]) * (log_pi(x[l + 1]) - log_pi(x[l]))
      if (log(runif(1)) < loga) {
        tmp <- x[l]
        x[l] <- x[l + 1]
        x[l + 1] <- tmp
      }
    }

    # Store state of cold chain (beta = 1)
    x_by_temp[i, ] <- x
  }

  list(x_by_temp = x_by_temp, betas = betas)
}

set.seed(1)
res_pt <- parallel_tempering(n_iter = 20000,
                             betas = c(1.0, 0.65, 0.4, 0.2, 0.08),
                             step = 1.2)
```

## Practical considerations

Choosing whether to deploy parallel tempering — and what to compare it against — comes down to the question being asked and the resources available:

- **Use parallel tempering when** the posterior is multimodal with well-separated modes, standard MCMC chains get trapped, the interest is in accurate posterior sampling and uncertainty quantification, or the problem involves phase transitions or critical phenomena (common in physics).
- **Use simulated annealing when** the goal is to find the MAP estimate or a point estimate, optimization (not sampling) is the primary objective, and you need fast identification of the best fit, not uncertainty.
- **Prefer HMC/NUTS when** the posterior is unimodal and high-dimensional, gradient information is available, or computational resources are limited and you cannot afford many chains. HMC and NUTS excel in high dimensions because they use gradient information to make large, informed moves, but they fail on multimodal targets because a single trajectory cannot jump between modes.
- **Computational cost.** Running $K$ chains costs roughly $K$ times as much as a single chain. However, this is often money well spent: the mixing time of the cold chain typically decreases dramatically when the temperature ladder is well-designed.

## Summary

:::{.definition}
**Tempered distributions** scale the exponent of the posterior: $\pi_\beta(\theta) \propto \pi(\theta)^\beta$. At $\beta = 1$ we recover the target; at $\beta \to 0$ the distribution flattens.
:::

**Simulated annealing** is a single-chain method with a time-varying cooling schedule; it targets optimization (MAP estimation) and asymptotically concentrates on the global maximum.

**Parallel tempering (replica exchange MCMC)** runs $K$ chains at fixed inverse temperatures $1 = \beta_1 > \beta_2 > \cdots > \beta_K$ and periodically swaps states between neighbours. The cold chain remains on the target distribution throughout and escapes local modes via information shared from hotter chains.

**Detailed balance** is satisfied: within-temperature moves are standard MH, and swap moves satisfy detailed balance with respect to the product target $\Pi(\Theta) = \prod_{l=1}^K \pi_{\beta_l}(\theta_l)$. Thus the cold chain's marginal is exactly $\pi$.

**Temperature ladder design** is critical: geometric spacing in inverse temperature and target swap acceptance rates of 20--40% are practical guidelines.

The worked example shows that parallel tempering succeeds where single-chain MH fails on a bimodal target, recovering the true posterior over both modes.

## Exercises

::: {.exercise}

Define the tempered distribution $\pi_\beta(x) \propto \pi(x)^\beta$ for $\beta \in [0, 1]$. Identify the two limiting cases $\beta = 0$ and $\beta = 1$.
:::

::: {.solution}
At $\beta = 1$ we recover the target,
$$\pi_1(x) \propto \pi(x),$$
while at $\beta = 0$ we have $\pi(x)^0 = 1$, so
$$\pi_0(x) \propto 1,$$
i.e. the uniform (or prior, in a Bayesian setting) reference distribution on the support of $\pi$.
:::

::: {.exercise}

Let $\pi(x) \propto \exp(-U(x))$. Show that $\pi_\beta(x) \propto \exp(-\beta U(x))$. Explain why lowering $\beta$ flattens the landscape and lowers energy barriers.
:::

::: {.solution}
Raising the density to the power $\beta$ gives
$$\pi_\beta(x) \propto \pi(x)^\beta = \big[\exp(-U(x))\big]^\beta = \exp(-\beta U(x)).$$
The effective energy becomes $\beta U(x)$, so all energy differences (including barrier heights $\Delta U$) are rescaled by $\beta$. For $\beta < 1$ this shrinks the contrast between modes and valleys, so the chain crosses barriers with probability $\sim e^{-\beta\Delta U}$ instead of $e^{-\Delta U}$.
:::

::: {.exercise}

Bimodal target. Consider $\pi(x) = \tfrac{1}{2}\mathcal{N}(-3, 1) + \tfrac{1}{2}\mathcal{N}(3, 1)$. Sketch (qualitatively) the tempered density $\pi_\beta$ for $\beta = 1, 0.5, 0.1$ and explain why a random-walk Metropolis chain on $\pi_1$ has trouble crossing the mode gap.
:::

::: {.solution}
At $\beta = 1$ the density has two sharp peaks at $\pm 3$ separated by a deep valley near $0$; at $\beta = 0.5$ the peaks broaden and the valley between them rises; at $\beta = 0.1$ the density is nearly flat across $[-5,5]$. Random-walk MH must traverse the low-density gap by a sequence of small steps, each accepted with probability $\sim e^{-\Delta U}$ where $\Delta U \approx U(0) - U(\pm 3) \approx \tfrac{9}{2}$, giving an exponentially small per-step crossing probability and effective trapping in one mode.
:::

::: {.exercise}

Swap acceptance probability. For replicas at inverse temperatures $\beta_i$ and $\beta_j$ holding states $x_i, x_j$, derive the Metropolis–Hastings acceptance probability
$$\alpha_{ij} = \min\!\left\{1,\ \exp\!\big[(\beta_i - \beta_j)(U(x_i) - U(x_j))\big]\right\}.$$
:::

::: {.solution}
The product target is $\Pi(x_i, x_j) \propto e^{-\beta_i U(x_i) - \beta_j U(x_j)}$, and the swap proposal $(x_i, x_j) \to (x_j, x_i)$ is deterministic and symmetric. The MH ratio is therefore
$$\frac{\Pi(x_j, x_i)}{\Pi(x_i, x_j)} = \frac{e^{-\beta_i U(x_j) - \beta_j U(x_i)}}{e^{-\beta_i U(x_i) - \beta_j U(x_j)}} = \exp\!\big[(\beta_i - \beta_j)(U(x_i) - U(x_j))\big],$$
so $\alpha_{ij} = \min\{1, \exp[(\beta_i - \beta_j)(U(x_i) - U(x_j))]\}$.
:::

::: {.exercise}

Show that the swap move described above satisfies detailed balance with respect to the product target $\Pi(x_1, \ldots, x_K) = \prod_l \pi_{\beta_l}(x_l)$. Use this to argue that the marginal of the cold chain is exactly $\pi$.
:::

::: {.solution}
For the swap of replicas $i, j$ with symmetric proposal probability $q$, the MH rule gives
$$\Pi(\ldots, x_i, \ldots, x_j, \ldots)\, q\, \alpha_{ij} = \Pi(\ldots, x_j, \ldots, x_i, \ldots)\, q\, \alpha_{ji},$$
which is exactly the standard MH detailed balance identity using $\alpha_{ij} = \min\{1, \Pi'/\Pi\}$ (all factors at other replicas cancel). Since both within-temperature moves and swaps preserve $\Pi$, the stationary distribution is $\Pi$, and the marginal over $x_1$ at $\beta_1 = 1$ is $\pi_{\beta_1} = \pi$.
:::

::: {.exercise}

Geometric ladder. Show that with a geometric temperature schedule $\beta_{l+1} / \beta_l = r$ (constant), the expected swap acceptance between adjacent replicas becomes approximately independent of $l$ when the energy variance scales linearly with the dimension.
:::

::: {.solution}
The log swap ratio between replicas $l, l+1$ is $\Delta_l = (\beta_l - \beta_{l+1})(U_l - U_{l+1}) = \beta_l(1-r)\,(U_l - U_{l+1})$. Standard scaling arguments give $\mathrm{Var}_{\pi_\beta}[U] \approx d\, c(\beta)/\beta^2$ for some smooth $c(\beta)$, so
$$\mathrm{Var}(\Delta_l) \approx \beta_l^2 (1-r)^2 \cdot 2 \cdot \frac{d\, c(\beta_l)}{\beta_l^2} = 2 d\, c(\beta_l)\,(1-r)^2,$$
which is independent of $l$ up to the slow variation of $c(\beta)$. The expected acceptance, governed by the distribution of $\Delta_l$, is therefore nearly constant along the ladder.
:::

::: {.exercise}

Number of replicas. For a target in $d$ dimensions with average energy gap $\Delta U$ between cold and hot ends, estimate the number of replicas $K$ needed to keep adjacent-pair swap acceptance around $25\%$. Express your answer in terms of $\Delta U$ and the desired swap rate.
:::

::: {.solution}
For a target acceptance $a$, each adjacent pair should produce a typical log-ratio of order $|\log a|$, i.e.\ $(\beta_l - \beta_{l+1})\,\Delta U_{\text{adj}} \sim |\log a|$, where the per-pair energy gap on a geometric ladder is $\Delta U_{\text{adj}} \approx \Delta U / (K-1)$. Setting $(1)(\Delta U / (K-1)) \approx |\log a|$ gives
$$K \approx 1 + \frac{\Delta U}{|\log a|},$$
so for $a \approx 0.25$, $|\log a| \approx 1.4$ and $K \sim 1 + \Delta U / 1.4$. (For a target whose energy variance grows like $d$, the same argument with $\sigma_U \sim \sqrt{d}$ rescales this to $K \sim 1 + \sqrt{d}\,\Delta U / |\log a|$.)
:::

::: {.exercise}

Simulated annealing vs parallel tempering. Compare the two algorithms in two sentences each: what each one tries to achieve, and why PT generates valid posterior samples while annealing typically does not.
:::

::: {.solution}
Simulated annealing runs a single chain whose temperature $T_k \to 0$ on a schedule; it is an *optimisation* method that concentrates on $\arg\max \pi$, and the resulting trajectory has a non-stationary, time-inhomogeneous law so its iterates are not samples from any fixed distribution. Parallel tempering, by contrast, runs $K$ chains at *fixed* inverse temperatures with $\beta_1 = 1$ and swaps states between neighbours; the joint stationary distribution is $\Pi = \prod_l \pi_{\beta_l}$, so the cold chain marginal is exactly $\pi$ and its iterates are valid posterior samples.
:::

::: {.exercise}

Thermodynamic integration. Show that
$$\log Z = -\int_0^1 E_{\pi_\beta}[U]\,d\beta,$$
and explain how this identity allows the marginal likelihood (evidence) to be estimated by sampling at multiple temperatures.
:::

::: {.solution}
Define $Z(\beta) = \int e^{-\beta U(x)}\,dx$, with $Z(0) = |\mathcal{X}|$ (or $1$ for a normalised reference) and $Z(1) = Z$. Differentiating under the integral,
$$\frac{d \log Z(\beta)}{d\beta} = \frac{-\int U(x) e^{-\beta U(x)}\,dx}{Z(\beta)} = -E_{\pi_\beta}[U],$$
so integrating from $0$ to $1$ gives $\log Z = \log Z(1) - \log Z(0) = -\int_0^1 E_{\pi_\beta}[U]\,d\beta$ (taking $Z(0)=1$). In practice one estimates $\hat U(\beta_l) \approx E_{\pi_{\beta_l}}[U]$ from the replica at $\beta_l$ and applies a quadrature rule (trapezoid/Simpson) over the temperature ladder.
:::

::: {.exercise}

Optimal acceptance rate for swap moves. Discuss why empirical guidance places the optimal swap acceptance in the range $20$–$40\%$. What goes wrong below and above this range?
:::

::: {.solution}
The swap rate measures the rate at which information (replicas) flows along the ladder. Below $\sim 20\%$ the energy histograms of adjacent replicas barely overlap, swaps are rare and the cold chain receives very few hot discoveries (information bottleneck). Above $\sim 40\%$ adjacent temperatures are nearly identical: each accepted swap conveys little new information per chain and one is paying for redundant replicas, so the wall-clock cost per ESS rises. The empirical sweet spot $20$–$40\%$ balances "swaps happen often enough" against "each swap actually changes the energy".
:::

::: {.exercise}

Effective sample size after a PT run. With $K$ replicas, only the cold chain produces posterior samples. Explain why the total wall-clock cost scales with $K$ but the ESS only grows by the swap mixing improvement, and why the trade-off is still typically favourable for multimodal targets.
:::

::: {.solution}
Each PT iteration costs $\sim K$ likelihood evaluations (one per replica) but only the $\beta_1$ chain contributes to the posterior estimate, so cost-per-cold-iterate scales as $K$. The benefit is a reduction of the cold-chain integrated autocorrelation $\tau$: roughly $\mathrm{ESS} \sim N / \tau_{\text{PT}}$ vs.\ $N / \tau_{\text{single}}$. For unimodal targets $\tau_{\text{PT}} \approx \tau_{\text{single}}$ and the factor $K$ is pure overhead, but for multimodal targets a single chain has $\tau_{\text{single}} \sim e^{\Delta U}$ (exponential in barrier height) while PT gives $\tau_{\text{PT}} \sim K^2$ (polynomial round-trip time), so the speedup $\tau_{\text{single}}/(K\,\tau_{\text{PT}}) \sim e^{\Delta U}/K^3$ is enormous.
:::

::: {.exercise}

Energy histogram overlap. Show qualitatively that adjacent replicas must have overlapping energy distributions for swaps to be accepted with reasonable probability. Sketch the case of (a) too few replicas and (b) too many.
:::

::: {.solution}
The swap log-ratio is $(\beta_l - \beta_{l+1})(U_l - U_{l+1})$, which is appreciable only when $U_l$ and $U_{l+1}$ are drawn from overlapping distributions; if the histograms of $U$ at $\beta_l$ and $\beta_{l+1}$ are disjoint then $U_l - U_{l+1}$ is systematically large and negative, making $\alpha \approx 0$.
\begin{itemize}
\item[(a)] **Too few replicas:** the histograms at adjacent $\beta_l$ are widely separated bell curves with negligible overlap, so almost every swap is rejected.
\item[(b)] **Too many replicas:** the histograms are nearly identical and almost fully overlap; swaps are accepted essentially every time but exchange very little energy, so information barely propagates per chain.
\end{itemize}
Good design has adjacent histograms whose tails overlap noticeably but whose means differ.
:::

::: {.exercise}

Replica exchange for a discrete target. A finite chain has stationary distribution $\pi$ on $\{0, 1, \ldots, K-1\}$. Construct the tempered transition kernel at inverse temperature $\beta$ and write the swap acceptance with a neighbour at $\beta'$.
:::

::: {.solution}
Write $\pi(x) \propto e^{-U(x)}$ with $U(x) = -\log \pi(x)$, and define the tempered target on the same state space by
$$\pi_\beta(x) \propto \pi(x)^\beta = e^{-\beta U(x)}.$$
A simple within-temperature kernel is Metropolis with a symmetric neighbour proposal $q(x \to y)$ accepted with probability
$$\alpha_\beta(x \to y) = \min\!\left\{1, \left(\frac{\pi(y)}{\pi(x)}\right)^{\beta}\right\}.$$
The swap with a neighbour at $\beta'$ holding state $x'$ uses
$$\alpha_{\text{swap}}(x, x') = \min\!\left\{1, \exp\!\big[(\beta - \beta')(U(x) - U(x'))\big]\right\}.$$
:::

::: {.exercise}

Power-posterior interpretation. In Bayesian inference $\pi(\theta \mid D) \propto L(\theta)^1 p(\theta)$. State the corresponding tempered version $\pi_\beta(\theta \mid D)$ and identify the prior, the maximum-likelihood-dominated posterior, and the standard posterior as special cases.
:::

::: {.solution}
The power posterior is obtained by raising the likelihood to $\beta$:
$$\pi_\beta(\theta \mid D) \propto L(\theta)^\beta\, p(\theta).$$
Then $\beta = 0$ gives $\pi_0 \propto p(\theta)$ (the **prior**); $\beta = 1$ gives $\pi_1 \propto L(\theta) p(\theta)$ (the **standard posterior**); and $\beta \to \infty$ concentrates on $\arg\max_\theta L(\theta)$, the **MLE/likelihood-dominated** limit. Intermediate $\beta \in (0, 1)$ interpolates between prior and posterior and underlies the thermodynamic-integration estimate of the evidence $\log Z = \int_0^1 E_{\pi_\beta}[\log L]\,d\beta$.
:::

::: {.exercise}

When parallel tempering is overkill. Give two situations where PT is *not* worth the extra computational cost (i.e.\ a single-chain HMC or random-walk Metropolis is preferable).
:::

::: {.solution}
(i) **Unimodal, well-conditioned targets in moderate-to-high dimension.** Here a single HMC/NUTS chain already mixes in $O(1)$ effective steps; the $K\times$ overhead of PT buys essentially no extra ESS. (ii) **Tight computational budget for a quick exploratory fit**, especially when the posterior is unimodal or only mildly multimodal — running $K$ replicas multiplies the per-iteration cost while a single tuned chain (perhaps with multiple random restarts) gives faster posterior estimates with adequate diagnostics.
:::
