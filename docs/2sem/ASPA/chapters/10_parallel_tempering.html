11.1 Introduction: The multimodality trap

Standard Metropolis–Hastings and Hamiltonian Monte Carlo samplers work well when the target posterior is unimodal or has modes close together. However, when the posterior has several well-separated modes, a single MCMC chain becomes trapped near the mode it encounters first. It cannot escape because crossing the energy barrier between modes occurs with probability decaying exponentially in the barrier height.

Consider two modes separated by a barrier of height \(\Delta E\). The probability of escaping is approximately \[p \approx \exp\left(-\frac{\Delta E}{T}\right)\] by the Arrhenius formula, where we write the posterior as \(\pi(\theta) = \exp[-E(\theta)]\). When \(\Delta E \gg 1\), this probability is negligibly small, and the chain explores only a tiny fraction of the parameter space.

Simulated annealing and parallel tempering both tackle this problem by introducing a temperature parameter. They exploit a key observation: at high temperature \(T\), the posterior is flattened, and energy barriers become easier to cross. The two methods differ in how they use this idea.

11.2 Tempered distributions and temperatures

11.2.1 Definition

For any target posterior \(\pi(\theta)\) with energy representation \(\pi(\theta) = \exp[-E(\theta)]\), we define the tempered posterior at inverse temperature \(\beta \in (0, 1]\) by \[\pi_\beta(\theta) \propto \pi(\theta)^\beta = \exp[-\beta E(\theta)].\]

Equivalently, using temperature \(T = 1/\beta\), \[\pi_T(\theta) \propto \pi(\theta)^{1/T} = \exp\left[-\frac{E(\theta)}{T}\right].\]

11.2.2 Limiting behaviour

  • Cold limit (\(\beta \to 1\) or \(T \to 1\)): \(\pi_\beta(\theta) \to \pi(\theta)\), the original posterior.
  • Hot limit (\(\beta \to 0\) or \(T \to \infty\)): \(\pi_\beta(\theta)\) becomes nearly flat, and the chain explores the entire space freely.
  • Concentrated limits (\(\beta \to 0\) in the context of optimization): As the temperature decreases to zero, \(\pi_T(\theta)\) concentrates its mass on the global minima of \(E(\theta)\).

At high temperature, the contrast between modes vanishes, making it easy for the chain to move between them. As the temperature decreases, the modes become increasingly separated again.

11.3 Simulated annealing

11.3.1 Purpose and algorithm

Simulated annealing is an optimization method that cools a single chain to find the maximum a posteriori (MAP) estimator \(\hat{\theta}_{\text{MAP}} = \arg\max_\theta \pi(\theta)\). It generates a sequence of increasingly concentrated distributions by running a cooling schedule.

Simulated annealing algorithm: 1. Initialize \(\theta_0\) and temperature \(T_0 > 0\). 2. For \(k = 0, 1, \ldots, K\): - Propose \(\theta' \sim q(\theta' \mid \theta_k)\). - Accept with probability \(\alpha_T(\theta_k \to \theta') = \min\{1, \exp[-\beta_k(E(\theta') - E(\theta_k))]\}\) where \(\beta_k = 1/T_k\). - Update \(T_{k+1} = \gamma T_k\) for some \(0 < \gamma < 1\) (e.g., geometric cooling) or use \(T_k = c / \log k\) (logarithmic cooling). 3. Return the state \(\theta\) with highest \(\pi(\theta)\) encountered.

11.3.2 Cooling schedule and barrier crossing

A crucial result: if we use a logarithmic cooling schedule \(T_k = c / \log k\), then the chain crosses any fixed energy barrier infinitely often (with probability 1) as \(k \to \infty\). This is because the cumulative escape probability \[\sum_{k=1}^\infty p_k = \sum_{k=1}^\infty \exp\left(-\frac{\Delta E \cdot \log k}{c}\right) = \sum_{k=1}^\infty k^{-\Delta E/c}\] diverges if and only if \(\Delta E/c \leq 1\), i.e., \(c \geq \Delta E\). Thus simulated annealing is theoretically guaranteed to find the global optimum (almost surely) with sufficiently slow cooling. In practice, slower cooling requires more iterations, and geometric schedules are often preferred for computational speed.

11.4 Parallel tempering (replica exchange)

11.4.1 The parallel tempering framework

Parallel tempering runs \(K\) chains in parallel, each at a different inverse temperature \(\beta_1 = 1 > \beta_2 > \cdots > \beta_K > 0\). The chain at \(\beta_1 = 1\) is the “cold chain” that targets the posterior of interest. Chains at smaller \(\beta\) are “hot chains” that explore more freely.

The key innovation is swaps: occasionally, we exchange the states of neighbouring chains. This allows the cold chain to benefit from discoveries made by hot chains, enabling it to escape local modes without the cold chain ever needing to climb the barrier alone.

11.4.2 Joint target

Let \(\theta_l\) denote the state of chain \(l\). The joint state is \(\Theta = (\theta_1, \ldots, \theta_K)\), and the product target is \[\Pi(\Theta) = \prod_{l=1}^K \pi_{\beta_l}(\theta_l) \propto \prod_{l=1}^K \pi(\theta_l)^{\beta_l}.\]

The marginal distribution of \(\theta_1\) (the cold chain) is exactly \[\pi_{\beta_1}(\theta_1) = \pi(\theta_1),\] since \(\beta_1 = 1\). Samples from the cold chain are valid posterior samples, and expectations computed from the cold chain are unbiased estimates of posterior expectations.

11.4.3 Within-temperature moves

At each temperature, we perform a standard Metropolis–Hastings move with the tempered acceptance probability. For chain \(l\): \[\alpha_l(\theta_l \to \theta_l') = \min\left\{1, \left(\frac{\pi(\theta_l')}{\pi(\theta_l)}\right)^{\beta_l} \frac{q(\theta_l \mid \theta_l')}{q(\theta_l' \mid \theta_l)}\right\}.\]

Hot chains (small \(\beta_l\)) accept uphill moves more readily, allowing them to traverse the landscape freely.

11.4.4 Swap moves and detailed balance

The second type of move exchanges states between neighbouring chains \(l\) and \(l+1\): \[(\theta_l, \theta_{l+1}) \to (\theta_{l+1}, \theta_l).\]

The Metropolis acceptance probability for this move is \[\alpha_{\text{swap}}(\theta_l, \theta_{l+1}) = \min\left\{1, \frac{\pi(\theta_{l+1})^{\beta_l} \pi(\theta_l)^{\beta_{l+1}}}{\pi(\theta_l)^{\beta_l} \pi(\theta_{l+1})^{\beta_{l+1}}}\right\}.\]

In log form (preferred numerically): \[\log \alpha_{\text{swap}} = \min\{0, (\beta_l - \beta_{l+1})[E(\theta_l) - E(\theta_{l+1})]\}.\]

The normalizing constants cancel, making the swap probability independent of the partition function.

The combined algorithm of within-temperature MH updates and swap moves satisfies detailed balance with respect to the product target \(\Pi(\Theta)\). Therefore, the cold chain’s marginal converges to \(\pi\).

Proof. Within-temperature moves leave \(\pi_{\beta_l}(\theta_l)\) invariant at each temperature. Swap moves satisfy detailed balance for the product density: the probability of being in state \((\theta_l, \theta_{l+1})\) and swapping to \((\theta_{l+1}, \theta_l)\) equals the probability of being in \((\theta_{l+1}, \theta_l)\) and swapping back (by the Metropolis ratio). The product of these two reversible operations preserves \(\Pi(\Theta)\).

11.4.5 How parallel tempering works

  1. Hot chains explore freely: Because \(\beta_l\) is small for hot chains, they traverse the landscape easily, finding new modes.
  2. Swaps transport information: When a hot chain discovers a mode, a swap can move it to a colder chain, bringing the discovery closer to the target.
  3. Cold chain remains on target: The cold chain never leaves the target distribution \(\pi\), yet it benefits from the hot chains’ discoveries via successful swaps.

This is the crucial difference from simulated annealing: the temperature is fixed, not decreasing. The algorithm is homogeneous, run indefinitely, and all cold-chain samples are valid posterior samples.

11.5 Temperature ladder design

11.5.1 Geometric spacing

The spacing of temperatures is critical. If \(\beta\) values are too far apart, swap acceptance rates become vanishingly small, and information cannot flow between chains. If they are too close, many chains are needed.

A simple and effective choice is geometric spacing: \[\beta_l = \beta_1 r^{l-1}, \quad 0 < r < 1.\]

This keeps temperatures evenly spaced in a logarithmic sense.

11.5.2 Target swap acceptance

A practical rule of thumb is to set the temperature ladder such that the swap acceptance probability between adjacent chains is in the range 20–40%. This balances exploration and computational efficiency.

11.5.3 All-pairs vs. neighbour-only swaps

The basic algorithm swaps only neighbouring pairs. Allowing swaps between arbitrary chains (“all-pairs”) can improve mixing but at higher computational cost. For most problems, neighbour-only swaps are sufficient if the ladder is carefully tuned.

11.6 Worked example: bimodal 1D mixture

Consider a bimodal posterior that is a mixture of two Gaussians: \[\pi(\theta) \propto 0.35 \, N(\theta; -4, 1) + 0.65 \, N(\theta; 3, 0.7^2).\]

The right mode is higher. We will show that a single-chain MH sampler gets trapped, while parallel tempering explores both modes.

# Log-posterior for bimodal mixture (minus normalization)
log_pi <- function(x) {
  a <- log(0.35) + dnorm(x, -4, 1, log = TRUE)
  b <- log(0.65) + dnorm(x, 3, 0.7, log = TRUE)
  m <- pmax(a, b)
  m + log(exp(a - m) + exp(b - m))
}

# Target density (for plotting)
pi_density <- function(x) {
  0.35 * dnorm(x, -4, 1) + 0.65 * dnorm(x, 3, 0.7)
}

# Single MH step at temperature beta
tempered_step <- function(x, beta, step = 1.0) {
  x_prop <- x + rnorm(1, 0, step)
  loga <- beta * (log_pi(x_prop) - log_pi(x))
  if (log(runif(1)) < loga) x_prop else x
}

single_chain_mh <- function(n_iter = 20000, x0 = -4, step = 1.0) {
  x <- x0
  out <- numeric(n_iter)
  for (i in 1:n_iter) {
    x <- tempered_step(x, beta = 1.0, step = step)
    out[i] <- x
  }
  out
}

set.seed(1)
trace_single <- single_chain_mh(n_iter = 20000, x0 = -4, step = 1.2)

parallel_tempering <- function(n_iter = 20000,
                               betas = c(1.0, 0.65, 0.4, 0.2, 0.08),
                               step = 1.2) {
  L <- length(betas)
  x <- seq(-4, 4, length.out = L)  # Initialize chains at different locations

  x_by_temp <- matrix(NA, nrow = n_iter, ncol = L)

  for (i in 1:n_iter) {
    # Within-temperature MH updates
    for (l in 1:L) {
      x[l] <- tempered_step(x[l], betas[l], step = step)
    }

    # Swap moves between neighbouring chains
    for (l in sample(1:(L - 1))) {
      loga <- (betas[l] - betas[l + 1]) * (log_pi(x[l + 1]) - log_pi(x[l]))
      if (log(runif(1)) < loga) {
        tmp <- x[l]
        x[l] <- x[l + 1]
        x[l + 1] <- tmp
      }
    }

    # Store state of cold chain (beta = 1)
    x_by_temp[i, ] <- x
  }

  list(x_by_temp = x_by_temp, betas = betas)
}

set.seed(1)

                             betas = c(1.0, 0.65, 0.4, 0.2, 0.08),
                             step = 1.2)

11.7 Practical considerations

11.7.1 When to use parallel tempering

Use parallel tempering when: - The posterior is multimodal with well-separated modes. - Standard MCMC chains get trapped. - Interest is in accurate posterior sampling and uncertainty quantification. - The problem involves phase transitions or critical phenomena (common in physics).

Use simulated annealing when: - The goal is to find the MAP estimate or a point estimate. - Optimization (not sampling) is the primary objective. - You need fast identification of the best fit, not uncertainty.

Avoid parallel tempering (prefer HMC/NUTS) when: - The posterior is unimodal and high-dimensional. - Gradient information is available. - Computational resources are limited and you cannot afford many chains.

HMC and NUTS excel in high dimensions because they use gradient information to make large, informed moves. However, they fail on multimodal targets because a single trajectory cannot jump between modes.

11.7.2 Computational cost

Running \(K\) chains costs roughly \(K\) times as much as a single chain. However, this is often money well spent: the mixing time of the cold chain typically decreases dramatically when the temperature ladder is well-designed.

11.8 Summary

Tempered distributions scale the exponent of the posterior: \(\pi_\beta(\theta) \propto \pi(\theta)^\beta\). At \(\beta = 1\) we recover the target; at \(\beta \to 0\) the distribution flattens.

Simulated annealing is a single-chain method with a time-varying cooling schedule; it targets optimization (MAP estimation) and asymptotically concentrates on the global maximum.

Parallel tempering (replica exchange MCMC) runs \(K\) chains at fixed inverse temperatures \(1 = \beta_1 > \beta_2 > \cdots > \beta_K\) and periodically swaps states between neighbours. The cold chain remains on the target distribution throughout and escapes local modes via information shared from hotter chains.

Detailed balance is satisfied: within-temperature moves are standard MH, and swap moves satisfy detailed balance with respect to the product target \(\Pi(\Theta) = \prod_{l=1}^K \pi_{\beta_l}(\theta_l)\). Thus the cold chain’s marginal is exactly \(\pi\).

Temperature ladder design is critical: geometric spacing in inverse temperature and target swap acceptance rates of 20–40% are practical guidelines.

The worked example shows that parallel tempering succeeds where single-chain MH fails on a bimodal target, recovering the true posterior over both modes.

## Introduction: The multimodality trap

Standard Metropolis--Hastings and Hamiltonian Monte Carlo samplers work well when the target posterior is unimodal or has modes close together. However, when the posterior has several well-separated modes, a single MCMC chain becomes trapped near the mode it encounters first. It cannot escape because crossing the energy barrier between modes occurs with probability decaying exponentially in the barrier height.

Consider two modes separated by a barrier of height $\Delta E$. The probability of escaping is approximately
$$p \approx \exp\left(-\frac{\Delta E}{T}\right)$$
by the Arrhenius formula, where we write the posterior as $\pi(\theta) = \exp[-E(\theta)]$. When $\Delta E \gg 1$, this probability is negligibly small, and the chain explores only a tiny fraction of the parameter space.

Simulated annealing and parallel tempering both tackle this problem by introducing a temperature parameter. They exploit a key observation: at high temperature $T$, the posterior is flattened, and energy barriers become easier to cross. The two methods differ in how they use this idea.

## Tempered distributions and temperatures

### Definition

For any target posterior $\pi(\theta)$ with energy representation $\pi(\theta) = \exp[-E(\theta)]$, we define the **tempered posterior** at inverse temperature $\beta \in (0, 1]$ by
$$\pi_\beta(\theta) \propto \pi(\theta)^\beta = \exp[-\beta E(\theta)].$$

Equivalently, using temperature $T = 1/\beta$,
$$\pi_T(\theta) \propto \pi(\theta)^{1/T} = \exp\left[-\frac{E(\theta)}{T}\right].$$

### Limiting behaviour

- **Cold limit** ($\beta \to 1$ or $T \to 1$): $\pi_\beta(\theta) \to \pi(\theta)$, the original posterior.
- **Hot limit** ($\beta \to 0$ or $T \to \infty$): $\pi_\beta(\theta)$ becomes nearly flat, and the chain explores the entire space freely.
- **Concentrated limits** ($\beta \to 0$ in the context of optimization): As the temperature decreases to zero, $\pi_T(\theta)$ concentrates its mass on the global minima of $E(\theta)$.

At high temperature, the contrast between modes vanishes, making it easy for the chain to move between them. As the temperature decreases, the modes become increasingly separated again.

## Simulated annealing

### Purpose and algorithm

Simulated annealing is an optimization method that cools a single chain to find the maximum a posteriori (MAP) estimator $\hat{\theta}_{\text{MAP}} = \arg\max_\theta \pi(\theta)$. It generates a sequence of increasingly concentrated distributions by running a cooling schedule.

**Simulated annealing algorithm:**
1. Initialize $\theta_0$ and temperature $T_0 > 0$.
2. For $k = 0, 1, \ldots, K$:
   - Propose $\theta' \sim q(\theta' \mid \theta_k)$.
   - Accept with probability $\alpha_T(\theta_k \to \theta') = \min\{1, \exp[-\beta_k(E(\theta') - E(\theta_k))]\}$ where $\beta_k = 1/T_k$.
   - Update $T_{k+1} = \gamma T_k$ for some $0 < \gamma < 1$ (e.g., geometric cooling) or use $T_k = c / \log k$ (logarithmic cooling).
3. Return the state $\theta$ with highest $\pi(\theta)$ encountered.

### Cooling schedule and barrier crossing

A crucial result: if we use a logarithmic cooling schedule $T_k = c / \log k$, then the chain crosses any fixed energy barrier infinitely often (with probability 1) as $k \to \infty$. This is because the cumulative escape probability
$$\sum_{k=1}^\infty p_k = \sum_{k=1}^\infty \exp\left(-\frac{\Delta E \cdot \log k}{c}\right) = \sum_{k=1}^\infty k^{-\Delta E/c}$$
diverges if and only if $\Delta E/c \leq 1$, i.e., $c \geq \Delta E$. Thus simulated annealing is theoretically guaranteed to find the global optimum (almost surely) with sufficiently slow cooling. In practice, slower cooling requires more iterations, and geometric schedules are often preferred for computational speed.

## Parallel tempering (replica exchange)

### The parallel tempering framework

Parallel tempering runs **$K$ chains in parallel**, each at a different inverse temperature $\beta_1 = 1 > \beta_2 > \cdots > \beta_K > 0$. The chain at $\beta_1 = 1$ is the "cold chain" that targets the posterior of interest. Chains at smaller $\beta$ are "hot chains" that explore more freely.

The key innovation is **swaps**: occasionally, we exchange the states of neighbouring chains. This allows the cold chain to benefit from discoveries made by hot chains, enabling it to escape local modes without the cold chain ever needing to climb the barrier alone.

### Joint target

Let $\theta_l$ denote the state of chain $l$. The joint state is $\Theta = (\theta_1, \ldots, \theta_K)$, and the **product target** is
$$\Pi(\Theta) = \prod_{l=1}^K \pi_{\beta_l}(\theta_l) \propto \prod_{l=1}^K \pi(\theta_l)^{\beta_l}.$$

:::{.note}
The marginal distribution of $\theta_1$ (the cold chain) is exactly
$$\pi_{\beta_1}(\theta_1) = \pi(\theta_1),$$
since $\beta_1 = 1$. Samples from the cold chain are valid posterior samples, and expectations computed from the cold chain are unbiased estimates of posterior expectations.
:::

### Within-temperature moves

At each temperature, we perform a standard Metropolis--Hastings move with the tempered acceptance probability. For chain $l$:
$$\alpha_l(\theta_l \to \theta_l') = \min\left\{1, \left(\frac{\pi(\theta_l')}{\pi(\theta_l)}\right)^{\beta_l} \frac{q(\theta_l \mid \theta_l')}{q(\theta_l' \mid \theta_l)}\right\}.$$

Hot chains (small $\beta_l$) accept uphill moves more readily, allowing them to traverse the landscape freely.

### Swap moves and detailed balance

The second type of move exchanges states between neighbouring chains $l$ and $l+1$:
$$(\theta_l, \theta_{l+1}) \to (\theta_{l+1}, \theta_l).$$

The Metropolis acceptance probability for this move is
$$\alpha_{\text{swap}}(\theta_l, \theta_{l+1}) = \min\left\{1, \frac{\pi(\theta_{l+1})^{\beta_l} \pi(\theta_l)^{\beta_{l+1}}}{\pi(\theta_l)^{\beta_l} \pi(\theta_{l+1})^{\beta_{l+1}}}\right\}.$$

In log form (preferred numerically):
$$\log \alpha_{\text{swap}} = \min\{0, (\beta_l - \beta_{l+1})[E(\theta_l) - E(\theta_{l+1})]\}.$$

The normalizing constants cancel, making the swap probability independent of the partition function.

:::{.theorem}
The combined algorithm of within-temperature MH updates and swap moves satisfies detailed balance with respect to the product target $\Pi(\Theta)$. Therefore, the cold chain's marginal converges to $\pi$.
:::

:::{.proof}
Within-temperature moves leave $\pi_{\beta_l}(\theta_l)$ invariant at each temperature. Swap moves satisfy detailed balance for the product density: the probability of being in state $(\theta_l, \theta_{l+1})$ and swapping to $(\theta_{l+1}, \theta_l)$ equals the probability of being in $(\theta_{l+1}, \theta_l)$ and swapping back (by the Metropolis ratio). The product of these two reversible operations preserves $\Pi(\Theta)$.
:::

### How parallel tempering works

1. **Hot chains explore freely**: Because $\beta_l$ is small for hot chains, they traverse the landscape easily, finding new modes.
2. **Swaps transport information**: When a hot chain discovers a mode, a swap can move it to a colder chain, bringing the discovery closer to the target.
3. **Cold chain remains on target**: The cold chain never leaves the target distribution $\pi$, yet it benefits from the hot chains' discoveries via successful swaps.

This is the crucial difference from simulated annealing: the temperature is fixed, not decreasing. The algorithm is homogeneous, run indefinitely, and all cold-chain samples are valid posterior samples.

## Temperature ladder design

### Geometric spacing

The spacing of temperatures is critical. If $\beta$ values are too far apart, swap acceptance rates become vanishingly small, and information cannot flow between chains. If they are too close, many chains are needed.

A simple and effective choice is **geometric spacing**:
$$\beta_l = \beta_1 r^{l-1}, \quad 0 < r < 1.$$

This keeps temperatures evenly spaced in a logarithmic sense.

### Target swap acceptance

A practical rule of thumb is to set the temperature ladder such that the swap acceptance probability between adjacent chains is in the range 20--40%. This balances exploration and computational efficiency.

### All-pairs vs. neighbour-only swaps

The basic algorithm swaps only neighbouring pairs. Allowing swaps between arbitrary chains ("all-pairs") can improve mixing but at higher computational cost. For most problems, neighbour-only swaps are sufficient if the ladder is carefully tuned.

```{r}
#| lst-cap: "Metropolis update at tempered temperature."

# Log-posterior for bimodal mixture (minus normalization)
log_pi <- function(x) {
  a <- log(0.35) + dnorm(x, -4, 1, log = TRUE)
  b <- log(0.65) + dnorm(x, 3, 0.7, log = TRUE)
  m <- pmax(a, b)
  m + log(exp(a - m) + exp(b - m))
}

# Target density (for plotting)
pi_density <- function(x) {
  0.35 * dnorm(x, -4, 1) + 0.65 * dnorm(x, 3, 0.7)
}

# Single MH step at temperature beta
tempered_step <- function(x, beta, step = 1.0) {
  x_prop <- x + rnorm(1, 0, step)
  loga <- beta * (log_pi(x_prop) - log_pi(x))
  if (log(runif(1)) < loga) x_prop else x
}
```

```{r}
#| label: lst-single-chain
#| lst-cap: "Single-chain MH sampler on bimodal target."

single_chain_mh <- function(n_iter = 20000, x0 = -4, step = 1.0) {
  x <- x0
  out <- numeric(n_iter)
  for (i in 1:n_iter) {
    x <- tempered_step(x, beta = 1.0, step = step)
    out[i] <- x
  }
  out
}

set.seed(1)
trace_single <- single_chain_mh(n_iter = 20000, x0 = -4, step = 1.2)
```

```{r}
#| label: lst-parallel-tempering
#| lst-cap: "Parallel tempering algorithm with swaps."

parallel_tempering <- function(n_iter = 20000,
                               betas = c(1.0, 0.65, 0.4, 0.2, 0.08),
                               step = 1.2) {
  L <- length(betas)
  x <- seq(-4, 4, length.out = L)  # Initialize chains at different locations

  x_by_temp <- matrix(NA, nrow = n_iter, ncol = L)

  for (i in 1:n_iter) {
    # Within-temperature MH updates
    for (l in 1:L) {
      x[l] <- tempered_step(x[l], betas[l], step = step)
    }

    # Swap moves between neighbouring chains
    for (l in sample(1:(L - 1))) {
      loga <- (betas[l] - betas[l + 1]) * (log_pi(x[l + 1]) - log_pi(x[l]))
      if (log(runif(1)) < loga) {
        tmp <- x[l]
        x[l] <- x[l + 1]
        x[l + 1] <- tmp
      }
    }

    # Store state of cold chain (beta = 1)
    x_by_temp[i, ] <- x
  }

  list(x_by_temp = x_by_temp, betas = betas)
}

set.seed(1)
res_pt <- parallel_tempering(n_iter = 20000,
                             betas = c(1.0, 0.65, 0.4, 0.2, 0.08),
                             step = 1.2)
```

## Practical considerations

### When to use parallel tempering

**Use parallel tempering when:**
- The posterior is multimodal with well-separated modes.
- Standard MCMC chains get trapped.
- Interest is in accurate posterior sampling and uncertainty quantification.
- The problem involves phase transitions or critical phenomena (common in physics).

**Use simulated annealing when:**
- The goal is to find the MAP estimate or a point estimate.
- Optimization (not sampling) is the primary objective.
- You need fast identification of the best fit, not uncertainty.

**Avoid parallel tempering (prefer HMC/NUTS) when:**
- The posterior is unimodal and high-dimensional.
- Gradient information is available.
- Computational resources are limited and you cannot afford many chains.

HMC and NUTS excel in high dimensions because they use gradient information to make large, informed moves. However, they fail on multimodal targets because a single trajectory cannot jump between modes.

### Computational cost

Running $K$ chains costs roughly $K$ times as much as a single chain. However, this is often money well spent: the mixing time of the cold chain typically decreases dramatically when the temperature ladder is well-designed.

## Summary

:::{.definition}
**Tempered distributions** scale the exponent of the posterior: $\pi_\beta(\theta) \propto \pi(\theta)^\beta$. At $\beta = 1$ we recover the target; at $\beta \to 0$ the distribution flattens.
:::

**Simulated annealing** is a single-chain method with a time-varying cooling schedule; it targets optimization (MAP estimation) and asymptotically concentrates on the global maximum.

**Parallel tempering (replica exchange MCMC)** runs $K$ chains at fixed inverse temperatures $1 = \beta_1 > \beta_2 > \cdots > \beta_K$ and periodically swaps states between neighbours. The cold chain remains on the target distribution throughout and escapes local modes via information shared from hotter chains.

**Detailed balance** is satisfied: within-temperature moves are standard MH, and swap moves satisfy detailed balance with respect to the product target $\Pi(\Theta) = \prod_{l=1}^K \pi_{\beta_l}(\theta_l)$. Thus the cold chain's marginal is exactly $\pi$.

**Temperature ladder design** is critical: geometric spacing in inverse temperature and target swap acceptance rates of 20--40% are practical guidelines.

The worked example shows that parallel tempering succeeds where single-chain MH fails on a bimodal target, recovering the true posterior over both modes.
