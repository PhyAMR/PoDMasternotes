8.1 Motivation and the curse of dimensionality

In Bayesian inference, the central computational challenge is to evaluate expectations of the form \[\mathbb{E}_\pi[f(\theta)] = \int f(\theta) \pi(\theta \mid D) \, d\theta,\] where \(\pi(\theta \mid D)\) is the posterior distribution. These integrals appear everywhere: computing the evidence, credible intervals, point estimates, and predictions. Most realistic models lack analytical solutions, especially in high dimensions.

Classical numerical integration methods fail catastrophically in high dimensions. Consider a grid-based quadrature with \(n\) points per dimension in a \(d\)-dimensional space. The total number of grid points grows as \(N = n^d\)—exponential in dimension. This is the curse of dimensionality for deterministic methods.

Consider the integral over a unit hypercube \([0,1]^d\) with an indicator function that counts volume inside a ball of radius \(r = 1/2\) centered at the origin: \[I_d = \int_{[0,1]^d} \mathbb{1}_{\|x\| < 1/2} \, dx.\]

The volume of the region is proportional to \(r^d\) while the hypercube has volume 1. As \(d\) grows, nearly all of the hypercube’s volume lies outside the ball (since \(V_{\text{ball}} / V_{\text{cube}} \sim (1/2)^d \to 0\)). A uniform grid wastes almost all points. Even with \(N = 10^6\) samples in dimension \(d = 20\), we expect essentially zero hits, making the variance unbounded and estimation impossible.

Monte Carlo to the rescue. Monte Carlo methods replace high-dimensional integration with averaging over random samples. The fundamental principle is

\[\mathbb{E}_p[f(X)] \approx \frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^{N} f(x_i), \quad x_i \sim p(x).\]

By the law of large numbers, this average converges to the true expectation as \(N \to \infty\). Crucially, the error does not depend explicitly on dimension.

Let \(x_1, \ldots, x_N \sim p(x)\) be i.i.d. samples. The Monte Carlo estimator \[\widehat{I}_N = \frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^{N} f(x_i)\] satisfies: - Unbiasedness: \(\mathbb{E}[\widehat{I}_N] = \mathbb{E}_p[f(X)]\) - Variance: \(\text{Var}(\widehat{I}_N) = \frac{1}{N} \text{Var}_p(f)\) - Convergence: \(\widehat{I}_N - \mathbb{E}_p[f] = \mathcal{O}(N^{-1/2})\)

The error scales universally as \(N^{-1/2}\) regardless of dimension \(d\).

However, there is a catch: while the error rate is independent of dimension, the variance \(\text{Var}_p(f)\) itself can grow exponentially with \(d\). For the hypercube example, the variance approaches zero as \(d \to \infty\) (since \(I_d \to 0\)), but the relative error \[\frac{\sqrt{\text{Var}(\widehat{I}_N)}}{\mathbb{E}[\widehat{I}_N]} \sim \frac{1}{\sqrt{N I_d}} \to \infty\] explodes exponentially. We need \(N \sim 1/I_d\) to maintain constant relative error—which is infeasible for rare events. Techniques like rejection sampling and importance sampling exist precisely to mitigate this degeneracy by concentrating samples where the integrand is large.

Explain why Monte Carlo methods are central in Bayesian inference. What is the difference between using independent posterior samples and samples generated by a Markov chain?

Solution. Bayesian inference is built on posterior expectations, \[E[f(\theta) \mid D] = \int f(\theta)\,p(\theta \mid D)\,d\theta.\] In moderate to high dimensions, deterministic grid integration becomes impractical because the number of grid points grows exponentially with the parameter dimension (curse of dimensionality). Monte Carlo replaces the integral by an average over samples: \[E[f(\theta) \mid D] \approx \frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^N f(\theta_i), \qquad \theta_i \sim p(\theta \mid D).\] If the samples \(\theta_i\) are independent, the variance of the estimator scales as \(\text{Var}(f)/N\). With MCMC the samples are correlated by construction, so the variance is inflated by the integrated autocorrelation time \(\tau_{\text{int}}\): \[\text{Var}(\widehat I_N) \approx \frac{\tau_{\text{int}}}{N}\,\text{Var}_\pi(f).\] The relevant sample size is therefore the effective sample size \(N_{\text{eff}} \approx N/\tau_{\text{int}}\), not the raw chain length. MCMC remains valid after burn-in and convergence to the stationary distribution, but reporting \(N_{\text{eff}}\) — not \(N\) — is essential.

8.2 Direct sampling: inverse transform method

The most straightforward sampling approach is the inverse transform method (or inverse CDF method). It is exact and works whenever the CDF can be computed and inverted analytically.

Let \(F(x) = P(X \le x) = \int_{-\infty}^x p(x') \, dx'\) be the cumulative distribution function of a continuous random variable \(X \sim p(x)\). The inverse transform method generates samples by: 1. Compute \(F(x)\) and its inverse \(F^{-1}(u)\) (the quantile function). 2. Sample \(U \sim \text{Uniform}(0,1)\). 3. Set \(X = F^{-1}(U)\).

Then \(X \sim p(x)\).

Proof: By the probability integral transform, \[P(X \le x) = P(F^{-1}(U) \le x) = P(U \le F(x)) = F(x),\] so the distribution of \(X\) is \(p(x)\) as required.

Example: exponential distribution. For \(p(x) = \lambda e^{-\lambda x}\) with \(x \ge 0\), the CDF is \(F(x) = 1 - e^{-\lambda x}\). Inverting \(u = 1 - e^{-\lambda x}\) gives \[x = -\frac{1}{\lambda} \log(1-u).\] Since \(1 - U \sim \text{Uniform}(0,1)\) when \(U \sim \text{Uniform}(0,1)\), we have \[X = -\frac{1}{\lambda} \log(U).\]

The inverse transform method is exact, simple, and efficient in one dimension. However, it requires analytical inversion of \(F\), which is rarely available for complex distributions (e.g., posteriors) or in high dimensions.

Let \(U \sim \text{Uniform}(0, 1)\) and define \(X = -\lambda^{-1}\log(1 - U)\) with \(\lambda > 0\). Find the density of \(X\) and explain the Monte Carlo interpretation.

Solution. For \(x \geq 0\), \[P(X \leq x) = P\!\left(-\lambda^{-1}\log(1 - U) \leq x\right) = P\!\left(U \leq 1 - e^{-\lambda x}\right) = 1 - e^{-\lambda x}.\] Differentiating gives \(f_X(x) = \lambda e^{-\lambda x}\) for \(x \geq 0\). Thus the inverse-CDF construction transforms uniform random numbers into exponential waiting times with rate \(\lambda\). This is the simplest non-trivial Monte Carlo recipe and underlies almost every random-number generator in Poisson process simulation.

8.3 Rejection sampling

When the target density \(\pi(x)\) is difficult to sample from directly, rejection sampling uses a proposal distribution \(q(x)\) from which sampling is easy, and accepts or rejects each proposal to asymptotically recover the target.

Suppose we want to sample from a target \(\pi(x) = \tilde{\pi}(x) / Z\) where \(\tilde{\pi}(x)\) is the unnormalized density and \(Z\) is unknown. Choose a proposal density \(q(x)\) with the same support. If there exists \(M > 0\) such that \[\tilde{\pi}(x) \le M \, q(x) \quad \forall x,\] then the following algorithm produces samples from \(\pi(x)\):

Rejection Sampling Algorithm: 1. Sample \(x \sim q(x)\). 2. Sample \(u \sim \text{Uniform}(0,1)\). 3. Accept \(x\) if \(u \le \tilde{\pi}(x) / (M q(x))\); else reject and repeat.

The accepted samples are i.i.d. from \(\pi(x)\).

Correctness: The joint density of proposing and accepting is \[p(x, \text{accept}) = q(x) \cdot \frac{\tilde{\pi}(x)}{M q(x)} = \frac{\tilde{\pi}(x)}{M}.\] The marginal acceptance probability is \[P(\text{accept}) = \int \frac{\tilde{\pi}(x)}{M} \, dx = \frac{Z}{M},\] and conditioning on acceptance yields \[p(x \mid \text{accept}) = \frac{\tilde{\pi}(x)/M}{Z/M} = \frac{\tilde{\pi}(x)}{Z} = \pi(x).\]

Acceptance ratio and efficiency. The acceptance probability is \(P(\text{accept}) = Z / M\). For efficiency, we want \(M\) as small as possible (ideally \(M^* = \max_x [\tilde{\pi}(x) / q(x)]\)). When acceptance probability is low, most computational effort is wasted on rejected proposals.

In high dimensions, rejection sampling becomes pathologically inefficient. Consider sampling from a Gaussian target in dimension \(d\). A reasonably-scaled proposal will have acceptance probability that typically decays as \(e^{-\alpha d}\) for some constant \(\alpha > 0\). The algorithm wastes an exponentially growing fraction of samples, making it impractical for large \(d\).

8.4 Importance sampling

Importance sampling avoids the rejection step by using weighted samples. Every sample is retained, but weighted by how much the proposal underestimates the target.

To compute \(\mathbb{E}_\pi[f(\theta)] = \int f(\theta) \pi(\theta) \, d\theta\), rewrite the integral in terms of a proposal \(q(\theta)\) with the same support: \[\mathbb{E}_\pi[f] = \int f(\theta) \pi(\theta) \, d\theta = \int f(\theta) \frac{\pi(\theta)}{q(\theta)} q(\theta) \, d\theta = \mathbb{E}_q\left[ f(\theta) w(\theta) \right],\] where the importance weight is \[w(\theta) = \frac{\pi(\theta)}{q(\theta)}.\]

Sample \(\theta_i \sim q(\theta)\) i.i.d. and estimate \[\widehat{I} = \frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^{N} f(\theta_i) w(\theta_i).\] This is unbiased when \(\pi\) is known up to a normalizing constant.

Self-normalized importance sampling. In practice, the target \(\pi(\theta)\) is often known only up to normalization: \(\pi(\theta) = \tilde{\pi}(\theta) / Z\) where \(Z\) is unknown (as in Bayesian posteriors). Use unnormalized weights \(\tilde{w}_i = \tilde{\pi}(\theta_i) / q(\theta_i)\) and estimate via the ratio \[\widehat{I} = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^N f(\theta_i) \tilde{w}_i}{\sum_{i=1}^N \tilde{w}_i}.\] This estimator is biased but consistent (converges to the true value as \(N \to \infty\)).

The efficiency of importance sampling depends critically on how well the weights match the target distribution. If the proposal \(q\) is far from the target \(\pi\), most weight concentrates on a few samples, and the effective sample size collapses.

The effective sample size (ESS) quantifies this degeneracy: \[\text{ESS} = \frac{(\sum_i \tilde{w}_i)^2}{\sum_i \tilde{w}_i^2}.\]

Intuitively, if all weights are equal (\(\tilde{w}_i = \tilde{w}^*\)), then \(\text{ESS} = N\). If one weight dominates, \(\text{ESS} \ll N\), signaling weight degeneracy. As a rule of thumb, \(\text{ESS} < N / 10\) indicates serious efficiency loss.

Optimal proposal. The variance of the importance-sampling estimator is proportional to \(\mathbb{E}_q[(f(X) w(X))^2]\). By calculus of variations, the variance-minimizing proposal is \[q^*(x) \propto |f(x)| \pi(x).\] However, this optimal proposal is itself as difficult to sample from as \(\pi\), so it is rarely useful in practice. Practical guidance: match the shape and tail behavior of \(\pi\), ensure heavy tails to avoid undersampling the tail regions, and capture correlations.

High-dimensional limitations. In very high dimensions, any suboptimal proposal leads to catastrophic weight degeneracy. The mass of \(\pi\) concentrates in thin, high-curvature regions (e.g., the typical set of a high-dimensional Gaussian). Unless \(q\) places comparable mass in those regions, the ESS shrinks exponentially with dimension. This is a fundamental curse of dimensionality for importance sampling, which motivates the turn to Markov chain methods.

8.5 Variance reduction

Before moving to MCMC, two classical variance-reduction tricks deserve mention.

Antithetic sampling: Use correlated pairs to reduce variance. If \(U \sim \text{Uniform}(0,1)\), sample both \(U\) and \(1-U\) (which is also uniform). Since \(f(F^{-1}(U))\) and \(f(F^{-1}(1-U))\) are negatively correlated for many smooth functions, averaging them reduces variance.

Control variates: If you know the expectation of a related function \(g(x)\) under \(\pi\), use \[\widehat{I}_{\text{cv}} = \frac{1}{N} \sum_i [f(x_i) - c(g(x_i) - \mathbb{E}[g])]\] for a suitably chosen \(c\). The term \(c(g(x_i) - \mathbb{E}[g])\) has zero mean but can have large negative correlation with \(f(x_i) - \mathbb{E}[f]\), reducing variance.

8.6 Markov chains and stationarity

When neither inverse sampling, rejection, nor importance sampling are practical, we turn to Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC). The key idea is to construct a stochastic dynamics whose long-time distribution equals the target \(\pi(x)\).

A Markov chain is a discrete-time stochastic process \(\{X_t\}_{t \ge 0}\) where the next state depends only on the current state (the Markov property): \[P(X_{t+1} = x' \mid X_t = x, X_{t-1}, \ldots, X_0) = P(X_{t+1} = x' \mid X_t = x) \equiv T(x \to x').\]

The transition operator \(T(x \to x')\) is the core object; it specifies how the chain evolves in a single step.

Stationarity: A distribution \(\pi(x)\) is stationary with respect to \(T\) if \[\pi(x) = \sum_{x'} T(x' \to x) \pi(x'),\] or in integral form, \[\pi(x) = \int T(x' \to x) \pi(x') \, dx'.\] Under one transition step, the distribution remains unchanged.

Convergence: For the chain to be useful, we need it to converge to \(\pi\) starting from any initial distribution \(p_0\). This requires: - Irreducibility: all states (or regions) are mutually reachable with positive probability. - Aperiodicity: the chain does not cycle with a fixed period.

Under these conditions, the distribution evolves as \[p_t(x) = T^t p_0(x) \xrightarrow{t \to \infty} \pi(x).\]

8.7 Detailed balance and reversibility

A sufficient condition for stationarity is detailed balance: \[\pi(x) T(x \to x') = \pi(x') T(x' \to x) \quad \forall x, x'.\]

This says that the probability current from \(x\) to \(x'\) balances the current from \(x'\) to \(x\) at equilibrium. While not necessary for stationarity (some stationary distributions have non-zero net currents), detailed balance is mathematically convenient and physically intuitive—it ensures the chain is reversible.

A Markov chain on states \(\{0, 1\}\) has transition probabilities \(P(0 \to 1) = a\) and \(P(1 \to 0) = b\), with \(a, b \in (0, 1)\). Find the stationary distribution and verify detailed balance.

Solution. Let the stationary distribution be \((\pi_0, \pi_1)\). Stationarity requires the probability flow out of \(0\) to equal the flow in: \[\pi_0\,a = \pi_1\,b, \qquad \pi_0 + \pi_1 = 1.\] Solving, \[\pi_0 = \frac{b}{a + b}, \qquad \pi_1 = \frac{a}{a + b}.\] Detailed balance asks \(\pi_0\,P(0 \to 1) = \pi_1\,P(1 \to 0)\). Substituting, \[\frac{b}{a + b}\,a = \frac{a}{a + b}\,b,\] which holds trivially. In two-state chains stationarity and detailed balance are equivalent — any stationary distribution is automatically reversible.

8.8 Metropolis–Hastings algorithm

The Metropolis–Hastings (MH) algorithm constructs a transition operator satisfying detailed balance by combining a proposal \(q(x' \mid x)\) with an accept/reject step.

Given a target \(\pi(x)\) (known up to normalization), the MH transition is \[T(x \to x') = q(x' \mid x) \, \alpha(x \to x'),\] where the acceptance probability is \[\alpha(x \to x') = \min\left(1, \frac{\pi(x') q(x \mid x')}{\pi(x) q(x' \mid x)}\right).\]

This is the MH ratio or Hastings ratio.

Proof of detailed balance: Define the ratio \(r(x, x') = \frac{\pi(x') q(x \mid x')}{\pi(x) q(x' \mid x)}\).

Case 1: \(r \ge 1\). Then \(\alpha(x \to x') = 1\) and \(\alpha(x' \to x) = 1/r\), so \[\pi(x) q(x' \mid x) \alpha(x \to x') = \pi(x) q(x' \mid x) = \pi(x') q(x \mid x') \cdot \frac{1}{r} = \pi(x') q(x \mid x') \alpha(x' \to x).\]

Case 2: \(r < 1\). Then \(\alpha(x \to x') = r\) and \(\alpha(x' \to x) = 1\), giving the same identity.

Thus detailed balance holds, and \(\pi\) is stationary. \(\square\)

Practical MH algorithm. One step from the current state \(x_t\):

  1. Propose \(x' \sim q(x' \mid x_t)\).
  2. Compute the ratio \(r = \pi(x') q(x_t \mid x') / [\pi(x_t) q(x' \mid x_t)]\).
  3. Draw \(u \sim \text{Uniform}(0,1)\).
  4. Accept if \(u < \min(1, r)\) and set \(x_{t+1} = x'\); reject otherwise and set \(x_{t+1} = x_t\).

For a symmetric proposal \(q(x' \mid x) = q(x \mid x')\) (e.g., a Gaussian random walk), the acceptance probability simplifies to \[\alpha(x \to x') = \min\left(1, \frac{\pi(x')}{\pi(x)}\right).\]

8.9 Convergence and ergodicity

After an initial burn-in period during which the chain forgets its starting point, the samples \(x_t\) are approximately distributed as \(\pi(x)\). Estimates are formed as time averages: \[\widehat{I} = \frac{1}{N} \sum_{t=1}^N f(x_t) \approx \mathbb{E}_\pi[f].\]

However, successive samples are correlated. The integrated autocorrelation time \[\tau_{\text{int}} = 1 + 2 \sum_{k=1}^\infty \rho_f(k)\] quantifies this correlation, where \(\rho_f(k)\) is the normalized autocorrelation at lag \(k\). The effective number of independent samples is \[N_{\text{eff}} \approx \frac{N}{2 \tau_{\text{int}}},\] which can be much smaller than the total number of draws.

The decay of autocorrelation is controlled by the spectral gap of the transition operator—the inverse of the largest non-zero eigenvalue. A small gap indicates slow mixing and long memory, forcing either longer chains or better proposals.

A single MCMC chain has \(N = 20\,000\) stored samples. The estimated normalised autocorrelation is approximately \(\rho(\tau) = 0.8^\tau\) for \(\tau \geq 1\). Estimate \(\tau_{\text{int}}\) and \(N_{\text{eff}}\) using \[\tau_{\text{int}} = 1 + 2\sum_{\tau=1}^\infty \rho(\tau).\]

Solution. The tail is a geometric series: \[\sum_{\tau=1}^\infty 0.8^\tau = \frac{0.8}{1 - 0.8} = 4.\] Hence \[\tau_{\text{int}} = 1 + 2\cdot 4 = 9, \qquad N_{\text{eff}} \approx \frac{N}{\tau_{\text{int}}} = \frac{20\,000}{9} \approx 2\,222.\] The chain contains far less information than its raw length suggests — long-memory autocorrelation discounts every additional sample.

For a stationary Markov chain \(\{x_t\}_{t=1}^N\) and an observable \(A(x)\), define the autocorrelation function, the normalised autocorrelation function, and the integrated autocorrelation time. If \(M = 5\) independent chains each have length \(N = 10^4\) and the integrated autocorrelation time is \(\tau_{\text{int}} = 250\), estimate the total effective sample size. Is it acceptable or should the proposal be improved?

Solution. The autocorrelation function of \(A\) is \[C_A(\tau) = E[A(x_t)\,A(x_{t+\tau})] - E[A]^2,\] the normalised version is \(\rho_A(\tau) = C_A(\tau)/C_A(0)\), and \[\tau_{\text{int}} = 1 + 2\sum_{\tau=1}^\infty \rho_A(\tau).\] With \(M = 5\) chains of length \(N = 10^4\), the total number of stored samples is \(N_{\text{tot}} = M N = 5\cdot 10^4\). Inflating the variance by \(\tau_{\text{int}}\), \[N_{\text{eff}} \approx \frac{N_{\text{tot}}}{\tau_{\text{int}}} = \frac{5\cdot 10^4}{250} = 200.\] Five correlated chains carry the same information as roughly \(2\cdot 10^2\) independent draws. The ratio \(N/\tau_{\text{int}} = 40\) per chain shows that most moves are wasteful — the proposal is poorly matched to the target geometry, and either a better proposal (HMC, adapted scale) or a reparametrisation is needed.

target_density <- function(x) {
  0.5 * dnorm(x, mean = -3, sd = 1) + 0.5 * dnorm(x, mean = 3, sd = 1)
}

log_target <- function(x) log(target_density(x))

# MH sampler
mh_sample <- function(n_steps, x0, proposal_sd) {
  x <- numeric(n_steps)
  x[1] <- x0
  n_accept <- 0
  for (t in 2:n_steps) {
    x_current <- x[t - 1]
    x_prop <- rnorm(1, mean = x_current, sd = proposal_sd)
    log_r <- log_target(x_prop) - log_target(x_current)
    if (log(runif(1)) < log_r) {
      x[t] <- x_prop
      n_accept <- n_accept + 1
    } else {
      x[t] <- x_current
    }
  }
  list(samples = x, accept_rate = n_accept / (n_steps - 1))
}

8.10 Summary and roadmap

This chapter has covered the foundational sampling methods:

  • Direct sampling (inverse transform) is exact when the CDF is invertible, but unavailable for complex posteriors.
  • Rejection sampling corrects for sampling from the wrong density but wastes samples in high dimensions.
  • Importance sampling avoids rejection but suffers from weight degeneracy when the proposal is misspecified.
  • Markov chain Monte Carlo constructs a dynamics whose stationary distribution is the target, enabling asymptotically exact sampling even in high dimensions.

The next chapters develop practical refinements and extensions:

  • Chapter 8 explores advanced MCMC algorithms: Hamiltonian Monte Carlo (which uses gradient information to reduce autocorrelation), adaptive proposal tuning, and parallel tempering for multimodal distributions.
  • Chapter 9 covers variational inference—a fast, approximate alternative that trades accuracy for speed via optimization.
  • Chapter 10 introduces particle methods: sequential Monte Carlo and resampling strategies for filtering and online inference.

Solution. For \(x \geq 0\), using that \(U\) and \(1-U\) are both uniform on \((0,1)\), \[P(X \leq x) = P(-\log U / \lambda \leq x) = P(U \geq e^{-\lambda x}) = 1 - e^{-\lambda x}.\] Differentiating yields the density \(f_X(x) = \lambda e^{-\lambda x}\) for \(x \geq 0\), i.e. \(X \sim \text{Exp}(\lambda)\).

Importance sampling for a tail probability. Estimate \(\mathbb{P}_p(X > 3)\) for \(p = \mathcal{N}(0, 1)\) using the shifted proposal \(q = \mathcal{N}(3, 1)\). Write the estimator and explain why the proposal beats naïve Monte Carlo.

Solution. With \(f(x) = \mathbb{1}_{x > 3}\) and weights \[w(x) = \frac{p(x)}{q(x)} = \exp\!\left(-\tfrac{1}{2}x^2 + \tfrac{1}{2}(x-3)^2\right) = e^{-3x + 9/2},\] the estimator with \(x_i \sim \mathcal N(3, 1)\) is \[\widehat{P} = \frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^N \mathbb{1}_{x_i > 3}\,e^{-3 x_i + 9/2}.\] Naïve sampling from \(p\) produces a hit only with probability \(\Phi^c(3) \approx 1.35\cdot 10^{-3}\), so its relative error blows up; the shifted proposal places roughly half the samples in the rare region, making the variance of order \(1/N\) instead of \(1/(N\,\Phi^c(3))\).

Solution. Computing the sum of squares, \[\sum_i w_i^2 = 0.49 + 0.04 + 0.01 = 0.54,\] so \[\text{ESS} = \frac{1}{0.54} = \frac{50}{27} \approx 1.85.\] Three samples carry less than two effective ones — the first weight dominates and signals severe degeneracy.

Antithetic variates. Let \(U \sim \text{Uniform}(0, 1)\) and consider the estimator \(\tfrac{1}{2}[g(U) + g(1 - U)]\). Show that when \(g\) is monotone, \(\text{Cov}(g(U), g(1-U)) \leq 0\) and the estimator has variance no larger than the single-sample estimator.

Solution. Metropolis acceptance. For target \(\pi(x) \propto e^{-x^2/2}\) on \(\mathbb{R}\) with a symmetric proposal \(q(x' \mid x) = q(x \mid x')\), write the acceptance probability \(\alpha(x \to x')\) in closed form and identify when it equals \(1\).

Solution. Symmetry of \(q\) cancels the proposal ratio, leaving \[\alpha(x \to x') = \min\!\left(1,\,\frac{\pi(x')}{\pi(x)}\right) = \min\!\left(1,\,\exp\!\left[\tfrac{1}{2}(x^2 - x'^2)\right]\right).\] Acceptance equals \(1\) whenever \(x'^2 \leq x^2\), i.e. the proposal moves closer to the mode at the origin; otherwise it accepts with probability \(\exp[(x^2 - x'^2)/2] < 1\).

(Define and explain.) For a stationary Markov chain \(\{x_t\}_{t=1}^N\) and observable \(A(x)\), define the autocorrelation function \(C_A(\tau)\), the normalised autocorrelation \(\rho_A(\tau)\), and the integrated autocorrelation time \(\tau_{\text{int}}\). State the formula for the effective sample size \(N_{\text{eff}}\).

Solution. The autocorrelation, its normalised version, and the integrated time are \[C_A(\tau) = E[A(x_t)A(x_{t+\tau})] - E[A]^2, \quad \rho_A(\tau) = \frac{C_A(\tau)}{C_A(0)}, \quad \tau_{\text{int}} = 1 + 2\sum_{\tau=1}^\infty \rho_A(\tau).\] The effective sample size after \(N\) correlated draws is \[N_{\text{eff}} \approx \frac{N}{\tau_{\text{int}}},\] i.e. the chain carries \(N_{\text{eff}}\) independent draws’ worth of information about \(E_\pi[A]\).

(Define and explain.) Explain in one paragraph why Monte Carlo methods are central in Bayesian inference. Contrast with grid-based numerical integration in \(d\) dimensions. What is the difference between using independent posterior samples and samples from a Markov chain?

Solution. Bayesian inference reduces to posterior expectations \(E[f(\theta) \mid D] = \int f(\theta)\pi(\theta \mid D)\,d\theta\), which Monte Carlo replaces by \(\widehat{I}_N = N^{-1}\sum_i f(\theta_i)\). The Monte Carlo error scales as \(\mathcal{O}(N^{-1/2})\) regardless of dimension, whereas a grid with \(n\) points per axis costs \(n^d\) evaluations — exponentially worse. Independent samples give \(\text{Var}(\widehat{I}_N) = \text{Var}_\pi(f)/N\), while an MCMC chain inflates this by the integrated autocorrelation time, \[\text{Var}(\widehat{I}_N) \approx \frac{\tau_{\text{int}}}{N}\,\text{Var}_\pi(f),\] so the relevant figure of merit is the effective sample size \(N_{\text{eff}} = N/\tau_{\text{int}}\), not the raw number of stored draws.

(Compare and contrast.) Define rejection sampling and importance sampling. State one situation where each one is preferable over the other. What is the cost of a bad proposal in each method?

Solution. Rejection sampling draws \(x \sim q\) and accepts with probability \(\tilde\pi(x)/[M\,q(x)]\), producing exact i.i.d. samples from \(\pi\); importance sampling keeps every draw \(x_i \sim q\) and weights it by \(w(x_i) = \pi(x_i)/q(x_i)\) in \(\widehat I = N^{-1}\sum_i f(x_i) w(x_i)\). Rejection is preferable when downstream code requires unweighted samples (e.g. visualisation, plugging into a black-box estimator); importance sampling is preferable for rare events or tail probabilities where \(q\) can be shifted into the region of interest. The cost of a bad proposal is: in rejection, a vanishing acceptance rate \(1/M\) and roughly \(M\) proposals per sample; in importance sampling, weight degeneracy quantified by \(\text{ESS} = 1/\sum_i w_i^2 \ll N\) and unbounded estimator variance.

(Define and explain.) State the conditions for a Markov chain to converge to its stationary distribution. Define detailed balance and explain why it is sufficient (but not necessary) for stationarity.

Solution. A Markov chain with transition \(T(x, y)\) converges to a unique stationary law \(\pi\) provided it is irreducible (any state reachable from any other in finite time), aperiodic (no cyclic class), and admits \(\pi\) as an invariant measure satisfying \[\pi(y) = \int T(x, y)\,\pi(x)\,dx.\] Detailed balance is the local-equilibrium condition \[\pi(x)\,T(x, y) = \pi(y)\,T(y, x).\] Integrating both sides over \(x\) gives the global balance equation above, so detailed balance implies stationarity. It is not necessary: chains can be stationary with non-zero probability currents that close up globally without balancing pairwise (e.g. deterministic cyclic chains).

## Motivation and the curse of dimensionality

In Bayesian inference, the central computational challenge is to evaluate expectations of the form
$$\mathbb{E}_\pi[f(\theta)] = \int f(\theta) \pi(\theta \mid D) \, d\theta,$$
where $\pi(\theta \mid D)$ is the posterior distribution. These integrals appear everywhere: computing the evidence, credible intervals, point estimates, and predictions. Most realistic models lack analytical solutions, especially in high dimensions.

Classical numerical integration methods fail catastrophically in high dimensions. Consider a grid-based quadrature with $n$ points per dimension in a $d$-dimensional space. The total number of grid points grows as $N = n^d$—exponential in dimension. This is the **curse of dimensionality** for deterministic methods.

:::{.example}
Consider the integral over a unit hypercube $[0,1]^d$ with an indicator function that counts volume inside a ball of radius $r = 1/2$ centered at the origin:
$$I_d = \int_{[0,1]^d} \mathbb{1}_{\|x\| < 1/2} \, dx.$$

The volume of the region is proportional to $r^d$ while the hypercube has volume 1. As $d$ grows, nearly all of the hypercube's volume lies outside the ball (since $V_{\text{ball}} / V_{\text{cube}} \sim (1/2)^d \to 0$). A uniform grid wastes almost all points. Even with $N = 10^6$ samples in dimension $d = 20$, we expect essentially zero hits, making the variance unbounded and estimation impossible.
:::

**Monte Carlo to the rescue.** Monte Carlo methods replace high-dimensional integration with **averaging over random samples**. The fundamental principle is

$$\mathbb{E}_p[f(X)] \approx \frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^{N} f(x_i), \quad x_i \sim p(x).$$

By the law of large numbers, this average converges to the true expectation as $N \to \infty$. Crucially, the error does **not depend explicitly on dimension**.

:::{.theorem}
Let $x_1, \ldots, x_N \sim p(x)$ be i.i.d. samples. The Monte Carlo estimator
$$\widehat{I}_N = \frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^{N} f(x_i)$$
satisfies:
- **Unbiasedness**: $\mathbb{E}[\widehat{I}_N] = \mathbb{E}_p[f(X)]$
- **Variance**: $\text{Var}(\widehat{I}_N) = \frac{1}{N} \text{Var}_p(f)$
- **Convergence**: $\widehat{I}_N - \mathbb{E}_p[f] = \mathcal{O}(N^{-1/2})$

The error scales universally as $N^{-1/2}$ regardless of dimension $d$.
:::

However, there is a catch: while the error rate is independent of dimension, the variance $\text{Var}_p(f)$ itself can grow exponentially with $d$. For the hypercube example, the variance approaches zero as $d \to \infty$ (since $I_d \to 0$), but the *relative* error
$$\frac{\sqrt{\text{Var}(\widehat{I}_N)}}{\mathbb{E}[\widehat{I}_N]} \sim \frac{1}{\sqrt{N I_d}} \to \infty$$
explodes exponentially. We need $N \sim 1/I_d$ to maintain constant relative error—which is infeasible for rare events. Techniques like **rejection sampling** and **importance sampling** exist precisely to mitigate this degeneracy by concentrating samples where the integrand is large.

::: {.example name="Why Monte Carlo in Bayesian inference (exam-style)"}
Explain why Monte Carlo methods are central in Bayesian inference. What is the difference between using independent posterior samples and samples generated by a Markov chain?

**Solution.** Bayesian inference is built on posterior expectations,
$$E[f(\theta) \mid D] = \int f(\theta)\,p(\theta \mid D)\,d\theta.$$
In moderate to high dimensions, deterministic grid integration becomes impractical because the number of grid points grows exponentially with the parameter dimension (curse of dimensionality). Monte Carlo replaces the integral by an average over samples:
$$E[f(\theta) \mid D] \approx \frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^N f(\theta_i), \qquad \theta_i \sim p(\theta \mid D).$$
If the samples $\theta_i$ are *independent*, the variance of the estimator scales as $\text{Var}(f)/N$. With **MCMC** the samples are correlated by construction, so the variance is inflated by the integrated autocorrelation time $\tau_{\text{int}}$:
$$\text{Var}(\widehat I_N) \approx \frac{\tau_{\text{int}}}{N}\,\text{Var}_\pi(f).$$
The relevant sample size is therefore the **effective sample size** $N_{\text{eff}} \approx N/\tau_{\text{int}}$, not the raw chain length. MCMC remains valid after burn-in and convergence to the stationary distribution, but reporting $N_{\text{eff}}$ — not $N$ — is essential.
:::

## Direct sampling: inverse transform method

The most straightforward sampling approach is the **inverse transform method** (or inverse CDF method). It is exact and works whenever the CDF can be computed and inverted analytically.

:::{.definition}
Let $F(x) = P(X \le x) = \int_{-\infty}^x p(x') \, dx'$ be the cumulative distribution function of a continuous random variable $X \sim p(x)$. The **inverse transform method** generates samples by:
1. Compute $F(x)$ and its inverse $F^{-1}(u)$ (the quantile function).
2. Sample $U \sim \text{Uniform}(0,1)$.
3. Set $X = F^{-1}(U)$.

Then $X \sim p(x)$.
:::

**Proof**: By the probability integral transform,
$$P(X \le x) = P(F^{-1}(U) \le x) = P(U \le F(x)) = F(x),$$
so the distribution of $X$ is $p(x)$ as required.

**Example: exponential distribution.** For $p(x) = \lambda e^{-\lambda x}$ with $x \ge 0$, the CDF is $F(x) = 1 - e^{-\lambda x}$. Inverting $u = 1 - e^{-\lambda x}$ gives
$$x = -\frac{1}{\lambda} \log(1-u).$$
Since $1 - U \sim \text{Uniform}(0,1)$ when $U \sim \text{Uniform}(0,1)$, we have
$$X = -\frac{1}{\lambda} \log(U).$$

#| fig-cap: "Overlaid histograms show agreement between inverse-transform (red) and built-in (blue) exponential samples."

The inverse transform method is **exact**, **simple**, and **efficient in one dimension**. However, it requires analytical inversion of $F$, which is rarely available for complex distributions (e.g., posteriors) or in high dimensions.

::: {.example name="Inverse-CDF sampling of the exponential (exam-style)"}
Let $U \sim \text{Uniform}(0, 1)$ and define $X = -\lambda^{-1}\log(1 - U)$ with $\lambda > 0$. Find the density of $X$ and explain the Monte Carlo interpretation.

**Solution.** For $x \geq 0$,
$$P(X \leq x) = P\!\left(-\lambda^{-1}\log(1 - U) \leq x\right) = P\!\left(U \leq 1 - e^{-\lambda x}\right) = 1 - e^{-\lambda x}.$$
Differentiating gives $f_X(x) = \lambda e^{-\lambda x}$ for $x \geq 0$. Thus the inverse-CDF construction transforms uniform random numbers into exponential waiting times with rate $\lambda$. This is the simplest non-trivial Monte Carlo recipe and underlies almost every random-number generator in Poisson process simulation.
:::

## Rejection sampling

When the target density $\pi(x)$ is difficult to sample from directly, **rejection sampling** uses a proposal distribution $q(x)$ from which sampling is easy, and accepts or rejects each proposal to asymptotically recover the target.

:::{.definition}
Suppose we want to sample from a target $\pi(x) = \tilde{\pi}(x) / Z$ where $\tilde{\pi}(x)$ is the unnormalized density and $Z$ is unknown. Choose a proposal density $q(x)$ with the same support. If there exists $M > 0$ such that
$$\tilde{\pi}(x) \le M \, q(x) \quad \forall x,$$
then the following algorithm produces samples from $\pi(x)$:

**Rejection Sampling Algorithm:**
1. Sample $x \sim q(x)$.
2. Sample $u \sim \text{Uniform}(0,1)$.
3. Accept $x$ if $u \le \tilde{\pi}(x) / (M q(x))$; else reject and repeat.

The accepted samples are i.i.d. from $\pi(x)$.
:::

**Correctness**: The joint density of proposing and accepting is
$$p(x, \text{accept}) = q(x) \cdot \frac{\tilde{\pi}(x)}{M q(x)} = \frac{\tilde{\pi}(x)}{M}.$$
The marginal acceptance probability is
$$P(\text{accept}) = \int \frac{\tilde{\pi}(x)}{M} \, dx = \frac{Z}{M},$$
and conditioning on acceptance yields
$$p(x \mid \text{accept}) = \frac{\tilde{\pi}(x)/M}{Z/M} = \frac{\tilde{\pi}(x)}{Z} = \pi(x).$$

**Acceptance ratio and efficiency.** The acceptance probability is $P(\text{accept}) = Z / M$. For **efficiency**, we want $M$ as small as possible (ideally $M^* = \max_x [\tilde{\pi}(x) / q(x)]$). When acceptance probability is low, most computational effort is wasted on rejected proposals.

In high dimensions, rejection sampling becomes pathologically inefficient. Consider sampling from a Gaussian target in dimension $d$. A reasonably-scaled proposal will have acceptance probability that typically decays as $e^{-\alpha d}$ for some constant $\alpha > 0$. The algorithm wastes an exponentially growing fraction of samples, making it impractical for large $d$.

## Importance sampling

**Importance sampling** avoids the rejection step by using weighted samples. Every sample is retained, but weighted by how much the proposal underestimates the target.

:::{.definition}
To compute $\mathbb{E}_\pi[f(\theta)] = \int f(\theta) \pi(\theta) \, d\theta$, rewrite the integral in terms of a proposal $q(\theta)$ with the same support:
$$\mathbb{E}_\pi[f] = \int f(\theta) \pi(\theta) \, d\theta = \int f(\theta) \frac{\pi(\theta)}{q(\theta)} q(\theta) \, d\theta = \mathbb{E}_q\left[ f(\theta) w(\theta) \right],$$
where the **importance weight** is
$$w(\theta) = \frac{\pi(\theta)}{q(\theta)}.$$

Sample $\theta_i \sim q(\theta)$ i.i.d. and estimate
$$\widehat{I} = \frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^{N} f(\theta_i) w(\theta_i).$$
This is unbiased when $\pi$ is known up to a normalizing constant.
:::

**Self-normalized importance sampling.** In practice, the target $\pi(\theta)$ is often known only up to normalization: $\pi(\theta) = \tilde{\pi}(\theta) / Z$ where $Z$ is unknown (as in Bayesian posteriors). Use **unnormalized weights** $\tilde{w}_i = \tilde{\pi}(\theta_i) / q(\theta_i)$ and estimate via the ratio
$$\widehat{I} = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^N f(\theta_i) \tilde{w}_i}{\sum_{i=1}^N \tilde{w}_i}.$$
This estimator is biased but **consistent** (converges to the true value as $N \to \infty$).

The efficiency of importance sampling depends critically on how well the weights match the target distribution. If the proposal $q$ is far from the target $\pi$, most weight concentrates on a few samples, and the effective sample size collapses.

:::{.definition}
The **effective sample size (ESS)** quantifies this degeneracy:
$$\text{ESS} = \frac{(\sum_i \tilde{w}_i)^2}{\sum_i \tilde{w}_i^2}.$$

Intuitively, if all weights are equal ($\tilde{w}_i = \tilde{w}^*$), then $\text{ESS} = N$. If one weight dominates, $\text{ESS} \ll N$, signaling **weight degeneracy**. As a rule of thumb, $\text{ESS} < N / 10$ indicates serious efficiency loss.
:::

**Optimal proposal.** The variance of the importance-sampling estimator is proportional to $\mathbb{E}_q[(f(X) w(X))^2]$. By calculus of variations, the variance-minimizing proposal is
$$q^*(x) \propto |f(x)| \pi(x).$$
However, this optimal proposal is itself as difficult to sample from as $\pi$, so it is rarely useful in practice. Practical guidance: match the shape and tail behavior of $\pi$, ensure heavy tails to avoid undersampling the tail regions, and capture correlations.

**High-dimensional limitations.** In very high dimensions, any suboptimal proposal leads to catastrophic weight degeneracy. The mass of $\pi$ concentrates in thin, high-curvature regions (e.g., the typical set of a high-dimensional Gaussian). Unless $q$ places comparable mass in those regions, the ESS shrinks exponentially with dimension. This is a **fundamental curse of dimensionality** for importance sampling, which motivates the turn to **Markov chain methods**.

## Variance reduction

Before moving to MCMC, two classical variance-reduction tricks deserve mention.

:::{.note}
**Antithetic sampling**: Use correlated pairs to reduce variance. If $U \sim \text{Uniform}(0,1)$, sample both $U$ and $1-U$ (which is also uniform). Since $f(F^{-1}(U))$ and $f(F^{-1}(1-U))$ are negatively correlated for many smooth functions, averaging them reduces variance.

**Control variates**: If you know the expectation of a related function $g(x)$ under $\pi$, use
$$\widehat{I}_{\text{cv}} = \frac{1}{N} \sum_i [f(x_i) - c(g(x_i) - \mathbb{E}[g])]$$
for a suitably chosen $c$. The term $c(g(x_i) - \mathbb{E}[g])$ has zero mean but can have large negative correlation with $f(x_i) - \mathbb{E}[f]$, reducing variance.
:::

## Markov chains and stationarity

When neither inverse sampling, rejection, nor importance sampling are practical, we turn to **Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC)**. The key idea is to construct a stochastic dynamics whose long-time distribution equals the target $\pi(x)$.

:::{.definition}
A **Markov chain** is a discrete-time stochastic process $\{X_t\}_{t \ge 0}$ where the next state depends only on the current state (the Markov property):
$$P(X_{t+1} = x' \mid X_t = x, X_{t-1}, \ldots, X_0) = P(X_{t+1} = x' \mid X_t = x) \equiv T(x \to x').$$

The transition operator $T(x \to x')$ is the core object; it specifies how the chain evolves in a single step.
:::

**Stationarity**: A distribution $\pi(x)$ is stationary with respect to $T$ if
$$\pi(x) = \sum_{x'} T(x' \to x) \pi(x'),$$
or in integral form,
$$\pi(x) = \int T(x' \to x) \pi(x') \, dx'.$$
Under one transition step, the distribution remains unchanged.

**Convergence**: For the chain to be useful, we need it to converge to $\pi$ starting from any initial distribution $p_0$. This requires:
- **Irreducibility**: all states (or regions) are mutually reachable with positive probability.
- **Aperiodicity**: the chain does not cycle with a fixed period.

Under these conditions, the distribution evolves as
$$p_t(x) = T^t p_0(x) \xrightarrow{t \to \infty} \pi(x).$$

## Detailed balance and reversibility

A sufficient condition for stationarity is **detailed balance**:
$$\pi(x) T(x \to x') = \pi(x') T(x' \to x) \quad \forall x, x'.$$

This says that the probability current from $x$ to $x'$ balances the current from $x'$ to $x$ at equilibrium. While not necessary for stationarity (some stationary distributions have non-zero net currents), detailed balance is mathematically convenient and physically intuitive—it ensures the chain is **reversible**.

::: {.example name="Two-state Markov chain — stationarity and detailed balance (exam-style)"}
A Markov chain on states $\{0, 1\}$ has transition probabilities $P(0 \to 1) = a$ and $P(1 \to 0) = b$, with $a, b \in (0, 1)$. Find the stationary distribution and verify detailed balance.

**Solution.** Let the stationary distribution be $(\pi_0, \pi_1)$. Stationarity requires the probability flow out of $0$ to equal the flow in:
$$\pi_0\,a = \pi_1\,b, \qquad \pi_0 + \pi_1 = 1.$$
Solving,
$$\pi_0 = \frac{b}{a + b}, \qquad \pi_1 = \frac{a}{a + b}.$$
Detailed balance asks $\pi_0\,P(0 \to 1) = \pi_1\,P(1 \to 0)$. Substituting,
$$\frac{b}{a + b}\,a = \frac{a}{a + b}\,b,$$
which holds trivially. In two-state chains stationarity and detailed balance are equivalent — any stationary distribution is automatically reversible.
:::

## Metropolis–Hastings algorithm

The **Metropolis–Hastings (MH) algorithm** constructs a transition operator satisfying detailed balance by combining a **proposal** $q(x' \mid x)$ with an **accept/reject step**.

:::{.definition}
Given a target $\pi(x)$ (known up to normalization), the MH transition is
$$T(x \to x') = q(x' \mid x) \, \alpha(x \to x'),$$
where the **acceptance probability** is
$$\alpha(x \to x') = \min\left(1, \frac{\pi(x') q(x \mid x')}{\pi(x) q(x' \mid x)}\right).$$

This is the **MH ratio** or **Hastings ratio**.
:::

**Proof of detailed balance**:
Define the ratio $r(x, x') = \frac{\pi(x') q(x \mid x')}{\pi(x) q(x' \mid x)}$.

*Case 1*: $r \ge 1$. Then $\alpha(x \to x') = 1$ and $\alpha(x' \to x) = 1/r$, so
$$\pi(x) q(x' \mid x) \alpha(x \to x') = \pi(x) q(x' \mid x) = \pi(x') q(x \mid x') \cdot \frac{1}{r} = \pi(x') q(x \mid x') \alpha(x' \to x).$$

*Case 2*: $r < 1$. Then $\alpha(x \to x') = r$ and $\alpha(x' \to x) = 1$, giving the same identity.

Thus detailed balance holds, and $\pi$ is stationary. $\square$

**Practical MH algorithm.** One step from the current state $x_t$:

1. **Propose** $x' \sim q(x' \mid x_t)$.
2. **Compute the ratio** $r = \pi(x') q(x_t \mid x') / [\pi(x_t) q(x' \mid x_t)]$.
3. **Draw** $u \sim \text{Uniform}(0,1)$.
4. **Accept** if $u < \min(1, r)$ and set $x_{t+1} = x'$; **reject** otherwise and set $x_{t+1} = x_t$.

For a **symmetric proposal** $q(x' \mid x) = q(x \mid x')$ (e.g., a Gaussian random walk), the acceptance probability simplifies to
$$\alpha(x \to x') = \min\left(1, \frac{\pi(x')}{\pi(x)}\right).$$

## Convergence and ergodicity

After an initial **burn-in** period during which the chain forgets its starting point, the samples $x_t$ are approximately distributed as $\pi(x)$. Estimates are formed as time averages:
$$\widehat{I} = \frac{1}{N} \sum_{t=1}^N f(x_t) \approx \mathbb{E}_\pi[f].$$

However, successive samples are **correlated**. The integrated autocorrelation time
$$\tau_{\text{int}} = 1 + 2 \sum_{k=1}^\infty \rho_f(k)$$
quantifies this correlation, where $\rho_f(k)$ is the normalized autocorrelation at lag $k$. The effective number of independent samples is
$$N_{\text{eff}} \approx \frac{N}{2 \tau_{\text{int}}},$$
which can be much smaller than the total number of draws.

The decay of autocorrelation is controlled by the **spectral gap** of the transition operator—the inverse of the largest non-zero eigenvalue. A small gap indicates slow mixing and long memory, forcing either longer chains or better proposals.

::: {.example name="$\\tau_{\\text{int}}$ and ESS from a geometric autocorrelation (exam-style)"}
A single MCMC chain has $N = 20\,000$ stored samples. The estimated normalised autocorrelation is approximately $\rho(\tau) = 0.8^\tau$ for $\tau \geq 1$. Estimate $\tau_{\text{int}}$ and $N_{\text{eff}}$ using
$$\tau_{\text{int}} = 1 + 2\sum_{\tau=1}^\infty \rho(\tau).$$

**Solution.** The tail is a geometric series:
$$\sum_{\tau=1}^\infty 0.8^\tau = \frac{0.8}{1 - 0.8} = 4.$$
Hence
$$\tau_{\text{int}} = 1 + 2\cdot 4 = 9, \qquad N_{\text{eff}} \approx \frac{N}{\tau_{\text{int}}} = \frac{20\,000}{9} \approx 2\,222.$$
The chain contains far less information than its raw length suggests — long-memory autocorrelation discounts every additional sample.
:::

::: {.example name="Total ESS from $M$ parallel chains (exam-style)"}
For a stationary Markov chain $\{x_t\}_{t=1}^N$ and an observable $A(x)$, define the autocorrelation function, the normalised autocorrelation function, and the integrated autocorrelation time. If $M = 5$ independent chains each have length $N = 10^4$ and the integrated autocorrelation time is $\tau_{\text{int}} = 250$, estimate the total effective sample size. Is it acceptable or should the proposal be improved?

**Solution.** The autocorrelation function of $A$ is
$$C_A(\tau) = E[A(x_t)\,A(x_{t+\tau})] - E[A]^2,$$
the normalised version is $\rho_A(\tau) = C_A(\tau)/C_A(0)$, and
$$\tau_{\text{int}} = 1 + 2\sum_{\tau=1}^\infty \rho_A(\tau).$$
With $M = 5$ chains of length $N = 10^4$, the total number of stored samples is $N_{\text{tot}} = M N = 5\cdot 10^4$. Inflating the variance by $\tau_{\text{int}}$,
$$N_{\text{eff}} \approx \frac{N_{\text{tot}}}{\tau_{\text{int}}} = \frac{5\cdot 10^4}{250} = 200.$$
Five correlated chains carry the same information as roughly $2\cdot 10^2$ independent draws. The ratio $N/\tau_{\text{int}} = 40$ per chain shows that most moves are wasteful — the proposal is poorly matched to the target geometry, and either a better proposal (HMC, adapted scale) or a reparametrisation is needed.
:::

mh_sample <- function(n_steps, x0, proposal_sd) {
  x <- numeric(n_steps)
  x[1] <- x0
  n_accept <- 0

    x_current <- x[t - 1]

      x[t] <- x_prop
      n_accept <- n_accept + 1
    } else {
      x[t] <- x_current
    }
  }

## Summary and roadmap

This chapter has covered the foundational sampling methods:

- **Direct sampling** (inverse transform) is exact when the CDF is invertible, but unavailable for complex posteriors.
- **Rejection sampling** corrects for sampling from the wrong density but wastes samples in high dimensions.
- **Importance sampling** avoids rejection but suffers from weight degeneracy when the proposal is misspecified.
- **Markov chain Monte Carlo** constructs a dynamics whose stationary distribution is the target, enabling asymptotically exact sampling even in high dimensions.

The next chapters develop practical refinements and extensions:

- **Chapter 8** explores advanced MCMC algorithms: Hamiltonian Monte Carlo (which uses gradient information to reduce autocorrelation), adaptive proposal tuning, and parallel tempering for multimodal distributions.
- **Chapter 9** covers **variational inference**—a fast, approximate alternative that trades accuracy for speed via optimization.
- **Chapter 10** introduces **particle methods**: sequential Monte Carlo and resampling strategies for filtering and online inference.

If $g$ is monotone, $g(U)$ and $g(1-U)$ are monotone in $U$ in opposite senses, so by the Chebyshev/FKG correlation inequality

With $\sigma^2 = \text{Var}\,g(U) = \text{Var}\,g(1-U)$,

Metropolis acceptance. For target $\pi(x) \propto e^{-x^2/2}$ on $\mathbb{R}$ with a symmetric proposal $q(x' \mid x) = q(x \mid x')$, write the acceptance probability $\alpha(x \to x')$ in closed form and identify when it equals $1$.

*(Define and explain.)* For a stationary Markov chain $\{x_t\}_{t=1}^N$ and observable $A(x)$, define the autocorrelation function $C_A(\tau)$, the normalised autocorrelation $\rho_A(\tau)$, and the integrated autocorrelation time $\tau_{\text{int}}$. State the formula for the effective sample size $N_{\text{eff}}$.

The autocorrelation, its normalised version, and the integrated time are
$$C_A(\tau) = E[A(x_t)A(x_{t+\tau})] - E[A]^2, \quad \rho_A(\tau) = \frac{C_A(\tau)}{C_A(0)}, \quad \tau_{\text{int}} = 1 + 2\sum_{\tau=1}^\infty \rho_A(\tau).$$
The effective sample size after $N$ correlated draws is
$$N_{\text{eff}} \approx \frac{N}{\tau_{\text{int}}},$$
i.e.\ the chain carries $N_{\text{eff}}$ independent draws' worth of information about $E_\pi[A]$.
:::

::: {.exercise}

*(Define and explain.)* Explain in one paragraph why Monte Carlo methods are central in Bayesian inference. Contrast with grid-based numerical integration in $d$ dimensions. What is the difference between using independent posterior samples and samples from a Markov chain?
:::

::: {.solution}
Bayesian inference reduces to posterior expectations $E[f(\theta) \mid D] = \int f(\theta)\pi(\theta \mid D)\,d\theta$, which Monte Carlo replaces by $\widehat{I}_N = N^{-1}\sum_i f(\theta_i)$. The Monte Carlo error scales as $\mathcal{O}(N^{-1/2})$ regardless of dimension, whereas a grid with $n$ points per axis costs $n^d$ evaluations — exponentially worse. Independent samples give $\text{Var}(\widehat{I}_N) = \text{Var}_\pi(f)/N$, while an MCMC chain inflates this by the integrated autocorrelation time,
$$\text{Var}(\widehat{I}_N) \approx \frac{\tau_{\text{int}}}{N}\,\text{Var}_\pi(f),$$
so the relevant figure of merit is the effective sample size $N_{\text{eff}} = N/\tau_{\text{int}}$, not the raw number of stored draws.
:::

::: {.exercise}

*(Compare and contrast.)* Define rejection sampling and importance sampling. State one situation where each one is preferable over the other. What is the cost of a bad proposal in each method?
:::

::: {.solution}
**Rejection sampling** draws $x \sim q$ and accepts with probability $\tilde\pi(x)/[M\,q(x)]$, producing exact i.i.d.\ samples from $\pi$; **importance sampling** keeps every draw $x_i \sim q$ and weights it by $w(x_i) = \pi(x_i)/q(x_i)$ in $\widehat I = N^{-1}\sum_i f(x_i) w(x_i)$. Rejection is preferable when downstream code requires unweighted samples (e.g.\ visualisation, plugging into a black-box estimator); importance sampling is preferable for rare events or tail probabilities where $q$ can be shifted into the region of interest. The cost of a bad proposal is: in rejection, a vanishing acceptance rate $1/M$ and roughly $M$ proposals per sample; in importance sampling, weight degeneracy quantified by $\text{ESS} = 1/\sum_i w_i^2 \ll N$ and unbounded estimator variance.
:::

::: {.exercise}

*(Define and explain.)* State the conditions for a Markov chain to converge to its stationary distribution. Define detailed balance and explain why it is sufficient (but not necessary) for stationarity.
:::

::: {.solution}
A Markov chain with transition $T(x, y)$ converges to a unique stationary law $\pi$ provided it is **irreducible** (any state reachable from any other in finite time), **aperiodic** (no cyclic class), and admits $\pi$ as an invariant measure satisfying
$$\pi(y) = \int T(x, y)\,\pi(x)\,dx.$$
**Detailed balance** is the local-equilibrium condition
$$\pi(x)\,T(x, y) = \pi(y)\,T(y, x).$$
Integrating both sides over $x$ gives the global balance equation above, so detailed balance implies stationarity. It is *not* necessary: chains can be stationary with non-zero probability currents that close up globally without balancing pairwise (e.g.\ deterministic cyclic chains).
:::
