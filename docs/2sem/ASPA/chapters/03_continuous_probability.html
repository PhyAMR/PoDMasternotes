4.1 Introduction

This chapter covers continuous random variables and the fundamental limit theorems that govern the behavior of sums of independent samples. These results form the mathematical backbone of Bayesian statistics: they explain why posteriors concentrate, why Gaussian approximations emerge, and how rare events scale exponentially with sample size. Together, they provide a unified picture of how randomness resolves into determinism and structure as data accumulates.

4.2 Continuous random variables

A continuous random variable \(X\) is characterized by a probability density function (pdf) \(f(x)\) defined over some interval (often \(\mathbb{R}\)). Unlike discrete random variables, \(X\) cannot take specific values with positive probability; instead, we specify probabilities over intervals: \[P(a \leq X \leq b) = \int_a^b f(x) \, dx,\] with the normalization \(\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} f(x) \, dx = 1\). At any single point, \(P(X = x) = 0\).

The cumulative distribution function (CDF) of a continuous random variable \(X\) is \[F(x) = P(X \leq x) = \int_{-\infty}^x f(t) \, dt.\] The pdf is recovered by differentiation: \(f(x) = \frac{d}{dx} F(x)\).

For multivariate distributions, the concepts extend naturally. Given random variables \(X\) and \(Y\): - The joint pdf \(f(x, y)\) satisfies \(\iint f(x, y) \, dx \, dy = 1\). - The marginal pdf of \(X\) is \(f(x) = \int f(x, y) \, dy\). - The conditional pdf of \(X\) given \(Y = y\) is \(f(x \mid y) = \frac{f(x, y)}{f(y)}\) (when \(f(y) > 0\)).

4.3 Expectation and moments

For a continuous random variable \(X\) with pdf \(f(x)\), the expectation is \[E[X] = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} x f(x) \, dx.\]

More generally, for any function \(g(X)\), \[E[g(X)] = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} g(x) f(x) \, dx.\]

The \(n\)-th raw moment is \(\mu_n' = E[X^n]\), and the \(n\)-th central moment is \(\mu_n = E[(X - E[X])^n]\). The second central moment is the variance: \[\text{Var}(X) = \mu_2 = E[(X - E[X])^2] = E[X^2] - (E[X])^2.\]

The standard deviation is \(\sigma = \sqrt{\text{Var}(X)}\).

The moment generating function (MGF) of \(X\) is \[M(t) = E[e^{tX}],\] defined in a neighborhood of \(t = 0\). The \(n\)-th moment is recovered via \[E[X^n] = \frac{d^n M}{dt^n}\bigg|_{t=0}.\] The MGF uniquely characterizes the distribution (when it exists) and is particularly useful for analyzing sums of independent random variables.

For random variables \(X\) and \(Y\), the covariance is \[\text{Cov}(X, Y) = E[(X - E[X])(Y - E[Y])].\] The correlation coefficient is \[\rho(X, Y) = \frac{\text{Cov}(X, Y)}{\sigma_X \sigma_Y},\] which lies in \([-1, 1]\) and measures linear association.

4.4 Standard continuous distributions

The distributions below recur throughout Bayesian inference, either as likelihoods, as conjugate priors, or as limit shapes that emerge asymptotically. For each, we record the density, the first two moments, the role in conjugate analysis, and the physical setting in which it typically arises.

  • Uniform. The uniform distribution on \([a, b]\) has constant density \(f(x) = 1/(b - a)\) for \(x \in [a, b]\). For \(X \sim U(a, b)\), the mean is \(E[X] = (a + b)/2\) and the variance is \(\text{Var}(X) = (b - a)^2 / 12\). It is the canonical non-informative prior on a bounded interval, encoding complete ignorance within fixed bounds (the classic example is a draw from a random lottery).

  • Normal (Gaussian). The normal distribution with mean \(\mu\) and variance \(\sigma^2\) has pdf \[f(x) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi\sigma^2}} \exp\left(-\frac{(x - \mu)^2}{2\sigma^2}\right).\] For \(X \sim N(\mu, \sigma^2)\), \(E[X] = \mu\) and \(\text{Var}(X) = \sigma^2\). It is stable under linear combinations: if \(X_i \sim N(\mu_i, \sigma_i^2)\) are independent, then \(Y = \sum_i a_i X_i \sim N(\sum_i a_i \mu_i, \sum_i a_i^2 \sigma_i^2)\). It is the conjugate prior for normal likelihoods, emerges via the Central Limit Theorem, and dominates applied work as a model for errors, noise, and aggregates of many small effects (e.g. heights in a population approximate \(N(170, 100)\) in cm when averaged over many groups).

Consider two measurements with uncertainties: \(X_1 \sim N(0, 1)\) and \(X_2 \sim N(2, 0.5^2)\). A comparison is easily visualized by overlaying their pdfs. The first is centered at 0 with width \(\sim 2\); the second is centered at 2 with narrower width \(\sim 1\). A posterior combining both observations would be pulled toward the more precise measurement.

  • Exponential. The exponential distribution with rate \(\lambda > 0\) has pdf \(f(t) = \lambda e^{-\lambda t}\) for \(t \geq 0\). For \(X \sim \text{Exp}(\lambda)\), \(E[X] = 1/\lambda\) and \(\text{Var}(X) = 1/\lambda^2\). It is conjugate to itself and arises as the waiting time between successive events in a Poisson process, in radioactive decay, and in reliability lifetimes (e.g. if calls arrive at rate \(\lambda = 2\) per minute, the time until the next call is \(\text{Exp}(2)\) with expected value \(0.5\) minutes). Its defining feature is the memoryless property \(P(X > t + s \mid X > t) = P(X > s)\) for all \(s, t \geq 0\): the residual waiting time does not depend on how long we have already waited, and the exponential is the unique continuous distribution with this property.

  • Erlang and Gamma. The Erlang distribution describes the sum of \(n\) independent exponentials with the same rate \(\lambda\) and has pdf \(f(t) = \lambda^n t^{n-1} e^{-\lambda t} / (n-1)!\) for \(t \geq 0\). The Gamma distribution generalises Erlang by allowing the shape parameter \(n \to \alpha \in \mathbb{R}^+\), \[f(t) = \frac{\lambda^\alpha}{\Gamma(\alpha)} t^{\alpha-1} e^{-\lambda t}, \quad t \geq 0,\] where \(\Gamma(\alpha) = \int_0^\infty t^{\alpha-1} e^{-t} \, dt\) extends the factorial via \(\Gamma(n) = (n-1)!\) for positive integers. For \(X \sim \text{Gamma}(\alpha, \lambda)\), \(E[X] = \alpha/\lambda\) and \(\text{Var}(X) = \alpha/\lambda^2\); it scales as \(cX \sim \text{Gamma}(\alpha, \lambda/c)\) for \(c > 0\). It is the conjugate prior for Poisson and exponential likelihoods and arises as sums of exponentials, occurrence times in Poisson processes, rainfall accumulation, and insurance claim sizes (e.g. the time until the 5th customer at a store with arrival rate \(\lambda = 1\) per minute follows \(\text{Gamma}(5, 1)\)). A special case is the chi-squared distribution \(\chi^2_k\): with \(\alpha = k/2\) and \(\lambda = 1/2\) we recover the distribution of \(\sum_{i=1}^k Z_i^2\) for independent \(Z_i \sim N(0, 1)\), which underlies goodness-of-fit testing.

  • Beta. The Beta distribution on \([0, 1]\) with shape parameters \(\alpha, \beta > 0\) has pdf \[f(x) = \frac{1}{B(\alpha, \beta)} x^{\alpha-1} (1-x)^{\beta-1}, \quad x \in [0, 1],\] where \(B(\alpha, \beta) = \int_0^1 t^{\alpha-1} (1-t)^{\beta-1} \, dt = \Gamma(\alpha) \Gamma(\beta) / \Gamma(\alpha + \beta)\) is the Beta function. For \(X \sim \text{Beta}(\alpha, \beta)\), \(E[X] = \alpha/(\alpha + \beta)\) and \(\text{Var}(X) = \alpha\beta / [(\alpha + \beta)^2 (\alpha + \beta + 1)]\). The shape is unimodal when \(\alpha, \beta > 1\), uniform when \(\alpha = \beta = 1\), and U-shaped when \(\alpha, \beta < 1\). It is the conjugate prior for Bernoulli and binomial likelihoods and is the natural model for probabilities, proportions constrained to \([0, 1]\), and order statistics (e.g. for inference on a coin bias \(p\), a \(\text{Beta}(2, 2)\) prior — symmetric, peaked near \(0.5\) — is more informative than \(\text{Beta}(1, 1)\), which is uniform). Multimodal distributions on \([0, 1]\) are easily obtained as convex combinations of Betas, e.g. \(\tfrac{1}{2}\text{Beta}(\alpha_1, \beta_1) + \tfrac{1}{2}\text{Beta}(\alpha_2, \beta_2)\).

Several other continuous distributions appear repeatedly as priors, sampling distributions, or generalisations of the families above:

  • Student’s \(t\). With \(\nu\) degrees of freedom, its pdf is \[f(x) = \frac{\Gamma(\frac{\nu+1}{2})}{\sqrt{\pi\nu}\,\Gamma(\frac{\nu}{2})} \left(1 + \frac{x^2}{\nu}\right)^{-(\nu+1)/2}.\] It has mean 0 (for \(\nu > 1\)) and variance \(\nu / (\nu - 2)\) (for \(\nu > 2\)), with tails heavier than the normal — especially for small \(\nu\) — and converges to \(N(0, 1)\) as \(\nu \to \infty\). It arises as the distribution of \(\sqrt{n}(\bar{X}_n - \mu) / S_n\) where \(S_n\) is the sample standard deviation, making it fundamental for frequentist confidence intervals.

  • Inverse Gamma. With shape \(\alpha\) and rate \(\lambda\), its pdf is \[f(x) = \frac{\lambda^\alpha}{\Gamma(\alpha)} x^{-\alpha-1} \exp\left(-\frac{\lambda}{x}\right), \quad x > 0,\] with mean \(\lambda / (\alpha - 1)\) for \(\alpha > 1\). It is conjugate to the variance parameter of a normal likelihood and is commonly used as a prior for precision and variance parameters.

  • Dirichlet. On the \((k-1)\)-simplex (vectors \(\mathbf{x} = (x_1, \ldots, x_k)\) with \(x_i \geq 0\) and \(\sum_i x_i = 1\)), with parameters \(\boldsymbol{\alpha} = (\alpha_1, \ldots, \alpha_k)\), \[f(\mathbf{x}) = \frac{\Gamma(\sum_i \alpha_i)}{\prod_i \Gamma(\alpha_i)} \prod_{i=1}^k x_i^{\alpha_i - 1}.\] It is conjugate to the multinomial likelihood and is the natural extension of the Beta to multiple categories.

  • Cauchy. With location \(\mu\) and scale \(\sigma\), \(f(x) = (1/\pi\sigma) \cdot 1/(1 + ((x - \mu)/\sigma)^2)\). The Cauchy has no finite mean or variance and its tails decay as \(1/x^2\), making it useful as a prior that admits extreme values while remaining non-informative in a heavy-tailed sense.

4.5 Transformation of variables and sums of random variables

Often we need the distribution of a transformed random variable or a sum. Two key techniques are the Jacobian and the moment-generating function.

If \(Y = g(X)\) where \(g\) is invertible and differentiable, then \[f_Y(y) = f_X(g^{-1}(y)) \left| \frac{d g^{-1}}{dy} \right|.\] For multivariate transformations \(\mathbf{Y} = \mathbf{g}(\mathbf{X})\) with Jacobian matrix \(\mathbf{J}\), \[f_\mathbf{Y}(\mathbf{y}) = f_\mathbf{X}(\mathbf{g}^{-1}(\mathbf{y})) \left| \det \mathbf{J}^{-1}(\mathbf{y}) \right|.\]

Let \(X_1, X_2\) be independent \(U(0, 1)\) random variables. We seek the distribution of \(Y = X_1 + X_2\).

By definition, \(f_Y(y) = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} f_{X_1}(x_1) f_{X_2}(y - x_1) \, dx_1\). Since \(f_{X_1} = f_{X_2} = 1\) on \([0, 1]\), we need both \(0 \leq x_1 \leq 1\) and \(0 \leq y - x_1 \leq 1\), which constrains \(x_1 \in [\max(0, y-1), \min(1, y)]\).

For \(y \in [0, 2]\): \[f_Y(y) = \int_{\max(0, y-1)}^{\min(1, y)} 1 \, dx_1 = \min(1, y) - \max(0, y-1) = \begin{cases} y & 0 \leq y \leq 1 \\ 2 - y & 1 < y \leq 2 \end{cases}\]

This is the triangular distribution—a classic result showing how convolution of uniform pdfs yields a piecewise linear shape. Generalizing to \(n\) uniforms gives the Irwin–Hall distribution.

Let \(X\) and \(Y\) be independent and identically distributed exponential random variables with rate \(\lambda > 0\), i.e. \(f_X(x) = \lambda e^{-\lambda x}\) for \(x \geq 0\). Find the density of \(Z = X + Y\).

Solution. Since \(X\) and \(Y\) are independent, the density of the sum is the convolution \[f_Z(z) = \int_{-\infty}^{+\infty} f_X(x)\,f_Y(z - x)\,dx = \int_{-\infty}^{+\infty} f_X(x)\,f_X(z - x)\,dx.\] The integrand is non-zero only for \(0 \leq x \leq z\), so for \(z \geq 0\), \[f_Z(z) = \int_0^z \lambda e^{-\lambda x}\,\lambda e^{-\lambda (z - x)}\,dx = \lambda^2 e^{-\lambda z} \int_0^z dx = \lambda^2 z\, e^{-\lambda z}.\] Hence \[f_Z(z) = \begin{cases} \lambda^2 z\, e^{-\lambda z}, & z \geq 0,\\ 0, & z < 0. \end{cases}\] This is the Erlang / Gamma distribution with shape 2 and rate \(\lambda\).

A detector has a deterministic dead time \(t_0 > 0\). After this dead time, the waiting time to the next event is exponential with rate \(\lambda\). If \(Z = t_0 + T\) with \(T \sim \text{Exp}(\lambda)\), find the density and mean of \(Z\).

Solution. A deterministic shift just translates the support. For \(z \geq t_0\), \[f_Z(z) = \lambda e^{-\lambda(z - t_0)}\,\mathbb{1}_{\{z \geq t_0\}}.\] The mean shifts by the dead time: \[E[Z] = t_0 + E[T] = t_0 + \frac{1}{\lambda}.\] The variance is unchanged: \(\text{Var}(Z) = 1/\lambda^2\). A deterministic shift does not add variability.

4.6 Law of Large Numbers

The Law of Large Numbers (LLN) states that as we accumulate more samples, their average converges to the population mean. This is the mathematical foundation for why empirical frequencies approach probabilities.

Let \(\{X_i\}_{i=1}^\infty\) be i.i.d. random variables with \(E[X_i] = \mu\) and \(\text{Var}(X_i) = \sigma^2 < \infty\). Then for any \(\epsilon > 0\), \[\lim_{n \to \infty} P\left(\left|\bar{X}_n - \mu\right| > \epsilon\right) = 0,\] where \(\bar{X}_n = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n X_i\). We write \(\bar{X}_n \xrightarrow{P} \mu\) (convergence in probability).

The weak LLN says that the sample mean concentrates around its expected value, with deviations becoming arbitrarily unlikely as \(n\) grows. There is also a strong LLN, which states almost-sure convergence: \(\bar{X}_n \xrightarrow{\text{a.s.}} \mu\) with probability 1.

In Bayesian inference, the LLN underlies posterior consistency: as data accumulate, the posterior concentrates near the true parameter value, and the prior becomes negligible. This is why Bayesian and frequentist estimators often agree asymptotically, despite their philosophies differing.

4.7 Central Limit Theorem

The Central Limit Theorem (CLT) is the most important result in probability theory for statistics. It says that sample means converge in distribution to a normal, regardless of the underlying distribution.

Let \(\{X_i\}_{i=1}^\infty\) be i.i.d. random variables with \(E[X_i] = \mu\) and \(\text{Var}(X_i) = \sigma^2 > 0\). Then \[\sqrt{n}(\bar{X}_n - \mu) \xrightarrow{d} N(0, \sigma^2)\] as \(n \to \infty\), where \(\xrightarrow{d}\) denotes convergence in distribution. Equivalently, \[\bar{X}_n \approx N\left(\mu, \frac{\sigma^2}{n}\right) \quad \text{for large } n.\]

The CLT is universal: the form of the limiting normal depends only on the mean and variance, not on higher moments or the original shape of the distribution. This explains why the normal distribution is so ubiquitous in practice.

Suppose we draw \(n = 50\) samples from \(U(0, 1)\), which is decidedly non-normal (uniform and bounded). The mean \(\bar{X}_{50}\) is approximately \(N(0.5, 1/(12 \cdot 50)) = N(0.5, 0.00167)\), so \(\bar{X}_{50}\) has standard deviation \(\approx 0.04\). Repeating this experiment 1000 times and histogramming the sample means shows a bell curve, despite the underlying distribution being uniform. As \(n\) increases, the histogram becomes ever more Gaussian.

The CLT extends naturally to multivariate settings: if \(\mathbf{X}_1, \ldots, \mathbf{X}_n\) are i.i.d. \(d\)-dimensional random vectors with mean \(\boldsymbol{\mu}\) and covariance matrix \(\boldsymbol{\Sigma}\), then \[\sqrt{n}(\bar{\mathbf{X}}_n - \boldsymbol{\mu}) \xrightarrow{d} N_d(\mathbf{0}, \boldsymbol{\Sigma}).\] This is crucial for Bayesian inference on multiple parameters, since it justifies Gaussian posterior approximations via Laplace’s method.

In Bayesian inference, the CLT explains why: - Posteriors are approximately Gaussian for large \(n\) (Bernstein–von Mises theorem) - Fisher information quantifies curvature of the log-likelihood at its mode - Credible intervals around the posterior mean approximate confidence intervals

4.8 Large Deviation Principle

The CLT describes typical fluctuations (of order \(\sqrt{n}\)), but it does not capture rare, atypical events. The Large Deviation Principle (LDP) describes how the probability of observing a sample mean far from the true mean decays exponentially with sample size.

Let \(\{X_i\}_{i=1}^\infty\) be i.i.d. random variables with MGF \(M(t) = E[e^{tX}]\) existing in a neighborhood of zero. Then for any \(x\) in the interior of the support, \[P(\bar{X}_n \approx x) \asymp e^{-nI(x)},\] where the rate function is \[I(x) = \sup_t [tx - \log M(t)].\] This is the Fenchel–Legendre transform of the log-MGF.

The rate function has the properties: - \(I(\mu) = 0\) (typical events at the mean have zero cost) - \(I(x) > 0\) for \(x \neq \mu\) (atypical events carry exponential cost) - Expanding \(I(x)\) to second order around \(x = \mu\) recovers the Gaussian behavior of the CLT

If \(X_i \sim N(\mu, \sigma^2)\), then \(M(t) = \exp(\mu t + \sigma^2 t^2/2)\), so \[\log M(t) = \mu t + \frac{\sigma^2 t^2}{2}.\] The rate function is \[I(x) = \sup_t\left[tx - \mu t - \frac{\sigma^2 t^2}{2}\right].\] Optimizing: \(\frac{d}{dt}[\cdots] = x - \mu - \sigma^2 t = 0 \Rightarrow t^* = (x - \mu)/\sigma^2\), yielding \[I(x) = \frac{(x - \mu)^2}{2\sigma^2}.\] Thus, \(P(\bar{X}_n \approx x) \sim \exp\left(-\frac{n(x - \mu)^2}{2\sigma^2}\right)\), which is consistent with the Gaussian CLT: rare events decay as a Gaussian tail.

4.9 Unified picture of asymptotics

These three results form a hierarchy of limiting behavior as \(n \to \infty\):

  1. Law of Large Numbers: The sample mean becomes deterministic. Fluctuations scale as \(O(n^{-1})\) relative to the mean.

  2. Central Limit Theorem: Gaussian fluctuations of order \(\sqrt{n}^{-1}\) emerge around the LLN limit.

  3. Large Deviation Principle: Rare events with \(\bar{X}_n \approx x\) occur with probability \(\sim e^{-nI(x)}\). For events beyond a few standard deviations, this exponential suppression dominates.

Remark. Conceptually, the picture is: Randomness = Determinism + Fluctuations + Rare Events. As data accumulate, the deterministic part grows, fluctuations remain Gaussian and relatively shrink, and rare events become exponentially suppressed. This is why Bayesian posteriors concentrate, why frequentist intervals shrink, and why model misspecification becomes detectable: the data force all inferences toward the truth under the asymptotic regime.

4.10 Exercises

Find the CDF and PDF of the half-normal random variable \(Y = |X|\) when \(X \sim \mathcal{N}(0, 1)\).

Solution. For \(y \geq 0\), \(F_Y(y) = P(|X| \leq y) = P(-y \leq X \leq y) = 2\Phi(y) - 1\), where \(\Phi\) is the standard normal CDF. Differentiating, \[f_Y(y) = 2\varphi(y) = \sqrt{\tfrac{2}{\pi}}\, e^{-y^2/2}, \quad y \geq 0,\] and \(f_Y(y) = 0\) for \(y < 0\).

Let \(X \sim \mathcal{N}(0, 1)\) and \(Y = X^2\). Derive the density of \(Y\) using the change-of-variables formula. Identify the resulting distribution by name.

Solution. The map \(y = x^2\) is two-to-one, with inverse branches \(x = \pm\sqrt{y}\) and \(|dx/dy| = 1/(2\sqrt{y})\). Summing both branches, \[f_Y(y) = 2 \cdot \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}} e^{-y/2} \cdot \frac{1}{2\sqrt{y}} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi y}}\, e^{-y/2}, \quad y > 0.\] This is the \(\chi^2_1\) distribution, i.e. \(\text{Gamma}(\tfrac{1}{2}, \tfrac{1}{2})\).

Let \(X \sim \mathcal{N}(\mu, \sigma^2)\) and \(Y = e^X\). Derive the density of \(Y\) (log-normal). Compute \(E[Y]\) in closed form using the MGF of \(X\).

Solution. With \(x = \ln y\) and \(|dx/dy| = 1/y\), \[f_Y(y) = \frac{1}{y\sqrt{2\pi\sigma^2}} \exp\!\left(-\frac{(\ln y - \mu)^2}{2\sigma^2}\right), \quad y > 0.\] By definition \(E[Y] = E[e^X] = M_X(1)\), and since \(M_X(t) = \exp(\mu t + \tfrac{1}{2}\sigma^2 t^2)\), \[E[Y] = \exp\!\left(\mu + \tfrac{1}{2}\sigma^2\right).\]

Let \(X, Y\) be independent with \(X \sim \mathcal{N}(\mu_1, \sigma_1^2)\) and \(Y \sim \mathcal{N}(\mu_2, \sigma_2^2)\). Use MGFs to show that \(X + Y \sim \mathcal{N}(\mu_1 + \mu_2,\ \sigma_1^2 + \sigma_2^2)\).

Solution. By independence, \(M_{X+Y}(t) = M_X(t)\, M_Y(t)\). Substituting the Gaussian MGFs, \[M_{X+Y}(t) = e^{\mu_1 t + \tfrac{1}{2}\sigma_1^2 t^2}\, e^{\mu_2 t + \tfrac{1}{2}\sigma_2^2 t^2} = \exp\!\left((\mu_1+\mu_2)t + \tfrac{1}{2}(\sigma_1^2+\sigma_2^2)t^2\right),\] which is the MGF of \(\mathcal{N}(\mu_1+\mu_2,\, \sigma_1^2+\sigma_2^2)\). By uniqueness of the MGF, \(X+Y\) has that distribution.

Two independent exponentials with rate \(\lambda\). Find the density of \(M = \min(X, Y)\). Identify it by name.

Solution. Using the survival function for the minimum of independents, \[P(M > m) = P(X > m)\, P(Y > m) = e^{-\lambda m}\, e^{-\lambda m} = e^{-2\lambda m}, \quad m \geq 0.\] Hence \(f_M(m) = 2\lambda\, e^{-2\lambda m}\) for \(m \geq 0\), so \(M \sim \text{Exp}(2\lambda)\).

Two independent \(\text{Uniform}(0, 1)\) variables. Find the density of \(M = \max(X, Y)\) and compute \(E[M]\).

Solution. For \(m \in [0,1]\), \(F_M(m) = P(X \leq m)\,P(Y \leq m) = m^2\), so \(f_M(m) = 2m\). The mean is \[E[M] = \int_0^1 m \cdot 2m\, dm = \frac{2}{3}.\]

For \(X \sim \text{Uniform}(a, b)\), compute \(E[X]\) and \(\text{Var}(X)\) directly from the PDF.

Solution. With \(f(x) = 1/(b-a)\) on \([a,b]\), \[E[X] = \frac{1}{b-a}\int_a^b x\, dx = \frac{a+b}{2}, \quad E[X^2] = \frac{1}{b-a}\int_a^b x^2\, dx = \frac{a^2+ab+b^2}{3}.\] Subtracting gives \[\text{Var}(X) = E[X^2] - E[X]^2 = \frac{(b-a)^2}{12}.\]

For \(X \sim \text{Gamma}(\alpha, \beta)\) with rate \(\beta\), derive \(E[X] = \alpha/\beta\) and \(\text{Var}(X) = \alpha/\beta^2\) via the MGF. Then derive the same results via the cumulant generating function.

Solution. The MGF is \(M(t) = (1 - t/\beta)^{-\alpha}\) for \(t < \beta\). Then \(M'(0) = \alpha/\beta\) and \(M''(0) = \alpha(\alpha+1)/\beta^2\), so \(E[X] = \alpha/\beta\) and \(\text{Var}(X) = M''(0) - M'(0)^2 = \alpha/\beta^2\). Via the CGF \[K(t) = -\alpha \log(1 - t/\beta),\] we get \(K'(0) = \alpha/\beta = E[X]\) and \(K''(0) = \alpha/\beta^2 = \text{Var}(X)\) directly.

Let \(X, Y\) be i.i.d. \(\text{Exp}(1)\). Find the density of \(Z = X / (X + Y)\). Identify it by name.

Solution. This is a standard Gamma ratio: if \(X \sim \text{Gamma}(\alpha_1, \lambda)\) and \(Y \sim \text{Gamma}(\alpha_2, \lambda)\) are independent, then \(X/(X+Y) \sim \text{Beta}(\alpha_1, \alpha_2)\), independent of \(X+Y\). Here \(\alpha_1 = \alpha_2 = 1\), giving \[f_Z(z) = 1, \quad z \in [0,1],\] i.e. \(Z \sim \text{Uniform}(0,1) = \text{Beta}(1,1)\).

Compute the MGF of \(X \sim \text{Beta}(\alpha, \beta)\) as a series (no closed form is required). Use the series to derive \(E[X] = \alpha/(\alpha + \beta)\).

Solution. Expanding \(e^{tx} = \sum_{k\geq 0} t^k x^k / k!\) and integrating term-by-term against the Beta density, \[M(t) = \sum_{k=0}^{\infty} \frac{t^k}{k!}\, E[X^k] = \sum_{k=0}^\infty \frac{t^k}{k!}\, \frac{B(\alpha+k, \beta)}{B(\alpha,\beta)} = \sum_{k=0}^\infty \frac{t^k}{k!}\, \prod_{j=0}^{k-1}\frac{\alpha+j}{\alpha+\beta+j}.\] The coefficient of \(t\) is \(E[X] = B(\alpha+1,\beta)/B(\alpha,\beta) = \alpha/(\alpha+\beta)\), using \(\Gamma(\alpha+1) = \alpha\Gamma(\alpha)\).

Central limit theorem. Let \(X_1, \ldots, X_n \sim \text{Exp}(\lambda)\) i.i.d. Give the approximate distribution of \(\bar X_n\) for large \(n\) in terms of \(\lambda\) and \(n\).

Solution. Here \(\mu = 1/\lambda\) and \(\sigma^2 = 1/\lambda^2\), so the CLT gives \[\bar X_n \approx \mathcal{N}\!\left(\frac{1}{\lambda},\, \frac{1}{n\lambda^2}\right) \quad \text{for large } n.\]

Inverse-CDF for the Cauchy distribution. The Cauchy CDF is \(F(x) = \frac{1}{2} + \frac{1}{\pi}\arctan x\). Derive an explicit sampler \(X = g(U)\) with \(U \sim \text{Uniform}(0, 1)\).

Solution. Setting \(U = F(X)\) and solving, \(\arctan X = \pi(U - \tfrac{1}{2})\), hence \[X = \tan\!\left(\pi\!\left(U - \tfrac{1}{2}\right)\right), \quad U \sim \text{Uniform}(0,1).\]

Three independent Gaussians with the same mean \(\mu\) and variances \(\sigma_i^2\). Compute the variance of the precision-weighted mean \(\hat\mu = \sum_i x_i / \sigma_i^2 \big/ \sum_i 1/\sigma_i^2\). Compare with the unweighted mean.

Solution. Write \(\hat\mu = \sum_i w_i x_i\) with \(w_i = (1/\sigma_i^2)/S\) and \(S = \sum_j 1/\sigma_j^2\). By independence, \[\text{Var}(\hat\mu) = \sum_i w_i^2 \sigma_i^2 = \frac{1}{S^2}\sum_i \frac{1}{\sigma_i^2} = \frac{1}{\sum_i 1/\sigma_i^2}.\] The unweighted mean \(\bar X = \tfrac{1}{3}\sum_i x_i\) has \(\text{Var}(\bar X) = \tfrac{1}{9}\sum_i \sigma_i^2\). By the Cauchy–Schwarz / AM–HM inequality \(\text{Var}(\hat\mu) \leq \text{Var}(\bar X)\), with equality iff all \(\sigma_i\) are equal.

Sum of two independent Gammas with the same rate. Show, using MGFs, that \(\text{Gamma}(\alpha_1, \beta) + \text{Gamma}(\alpha_2, \beta) \sim \text{Gamma}(\alpha_1 + \alpha_2, \beta)\).

Solution. With \(M_i(t) = (1 - t/\beta)^{-\alpha_i}\) for \(t < \beta\), independence gives \[M_{X_1+X_2}(t) = (1 - t/\beta)^{-\alpha_1}\,(1 - t/\beta)^{-\alpha_2} = (1 - t/\beta)^{-(\alpha_1+\alpha_2)},\] which is the MGF of \(\text{Gamma}(\alpha_1 + \alpha_2, \beta)\). Uniqueness of the MGF yields the identification.

A random variable has CGF \(K(t) = \mu t + \tfrac{1}{2}\sigma^2 t^2\). What is its distribution? Give a one-line justification using the uniqueness of the MGF.

Solution. Exponentiating, the MGF is \[M(t) = e^{K(t)} = \exp\!\left(\mu t + \tfrac{1}{2}\sigma^2 t^2\right),\] which is the MGF of \(\mathcal{N}(\mu, \sigma^2)\); by uniqueness of the MGF, \(X \sim \mathcal{N}(\mu, \sigma^2)\).

## Introduction

This chapter covers continuous random variables and the fundamental limit theorems that govern the behavior of sums of independent samples. These results form the mathematical backbone of Bayesian statistics: they explain why posteriors concentrate, why Gaussian approximations emerge, and how rare events scale exponentially with sample size. Together, they provide a unified picture of how randomness resolves into determinism and structure as data accumulates.

## Continuous random variables

A continuous random variable $X$ is characterized by a probability density function (pdf) $f(x)$ defined over some interval (often $\mathbb{R}$). Unlike discrete random variables, $X$ cannot take specific values with positive probability; instead, we specify probabilities over intervals:
$$P(a \leq X \leq b) = \int_a^b f(x) \, dx,$$
with the normalization $\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} f(x) \, dx = 1$. At any single point, $P(X = x) = 0$.

::: {.definition name="Cumulative distribution function"}
The **cumulative distribution function** (CDF) of a continuous random variable $X$ is
$$F(x) = P(X \leq x) = \int_{-\infty}^x f(t) \, dt.$$
The pdf is recovered by differentiation: $f(x) = \frac{d}{dx} F(x)$.
:::

For multivariate distributions, the concepts extend naturally. Given random variables $X$ and $Y$:
- The **joint pdf** $f(x, y)$ satisfies $\iint f(x, y) \, dx \, dy = 1$.
- The **marginal pdf** of $X$ is $f(x) = \int f(x, y) \, dy$.
- The **conditional pdf** of $X$ given $Y = y$ is $f(x \mid y) = \frac{f(x, y)}{f(y)}$ (when $f(y) > 0$).

## Expectation and moments

For a continuous random variable $X$ with pdf $f(x)$, the **expectation** is
$$E[X] = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} x f(x) \, dx.$$

More generally, for any function $g(X)$,
$$E[g(X)] = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} g(x) f(x) \, dx.$$

The $n$-th **raw moment** is $\mu_n' = E[X^n]$, and the $n$-th **central moment** is $\mu_n = E[(X - E[X])^n]$. The second central moment is the **variance**:
$$\text{Var}(X) = \mu_2 = E[(X - E[X])^2] = E[X^2] - (E[X])^2.$$

The **standard deviation** is $\sigma = \sqrt{\text{Var}(X)}$.

::: {.definition name="Moment generating function"}
The **moment generating function** (MGF) of $X$ is
$$M(t) = E[e^{tX}],$$
defined in a neighborhood of $t = 0$. The $n$-th moment is recovered via
$$E[X^n] = \frac{d^n M}{dt^n}\bigg|_{t=0}.$$
The MGF uniquely characterizes the distribution (when it exists) and is particularly useful for analyzing sums of independent random variables.
:::

::: {.definition name="Covariance and correlation"}
For random variables $X$ and $Y$, the **covariance** is
$$\text{Cov}(X, Y) = E[(X - E[X])(Y - E[Y])].$$
The **correlation coefficient** is
$$\rho(X, Y) = \frac{\text{Cov}(X, Y)}{\sigma_X \sigma_Y},$$
which lies in $[-1, 1]$ and measures linear association.
:::

## Standard continuous distributions

The distributions below recur throughout Bayesian inference, either as likelihoods, as conjugate priors, or as limit shapes that emerge asymptotically. For each, we record the density, the first two moments, the role in conjugate analysis, and the physical setting in which it typically arises.

- **Uniform.** The **uniform distribution** on $[a, b]$ has constant density $f(x) = 1/(b - a)$ for $x \in [a, b]$. For $X \sim U(a, b)$, the mean is $E[X] = (a + b)/2$ and the variance is $\text{Var}(X) = (b - a)^2 / 12$. It is the canonical non-informative prior on a bounded interval, encoding complete ignorance within fixed bounds (the classic example is a draw from a random lottery).

- **Normal (Gaussian).** The **normal distribution** with mean $\mu$ and variance $\sigma^2$ has pdf
$$f(x) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi\sigma^2}} \exp\left(-\frac{(x - \mu)^2}{2\sigma^2}\right).$$
For $X \sim N(\mu, \sigma^2)$, $E[X] = \mu$ and $\text{Var}(X) = \sigma^2$. It is **stable** under linear combinations: if $X_i \sim N(\mu_i, \sigma_i^2)$ are independent, then $Y = \sum_i a_i X_i \sim N(\sum_i a_i \mu_i, \sum_i a_i^2 \sigma_i^2)$. It is the conjugate prior for normal likelihoods, emerges via the Central Limit Theorem, and dominates applied work as a model for errors, noise, and aggregates of many small effects (e.g. heights in a population approximate $N(170, 100)$ in cm when averaged over many groups).

::: {.example name="Two Gaussian distributions"}
Consider two measurements with uncertainties: $X_1 \sim N(0, 1)$ and $X_2 \sim N(2, 0.5^2)$. A comparison is easily visualized by overlaying their pdfs. The first is centered at 0 with width $\sim 2$; the second is centered at 2 with narrower width $\sim 1$. A posterior combining both observations would be pulled toward the more precise measurement.
:::

- **Exponential.** The **exponential distribution** with rate $\lambda > 0$ has pdf $f(t) = \lambda e^{-\lambda t}$ for $t \geq 0$. For $X \sim \text{Exp}(\lambda)$, $E[X] = 1/\lambda$ and $\text{Var}(X) = 1/\lambda^2$. It is conjugate to itself and arises as the waiting time between successive events in a Poisson process, in radioactive decay, and in reliability lifetimes (e.g. if calls arrive at rate $\lambda = 2$ per minute, the time until the next call is $\text{Exp}(2)$ with expected value $0.5$ minutes). Its defining feature is the **memoryless property** $P(X > t + s \mid X > t) = P(X > s)$ for all $s, t \geq 0$: the residual waiting time does not depend on how long we have already waited, and the exponential is the unique continuous distribution with this property.

- **Erlang and Gamma.** The **Erlang distribution** describes the sum of $n$ independent exponentials with the same rate $\lambda$ and has pdf $f(t) = \lambda^n t^{n-1} e^{-\lambda t} / (n-1)!$ for $t \geq 0$. The **Gamma distribution** generalises Erlang by allowing the shape parameter $n \to \alpha \in \mathbb{R}^+$,
$$f(t) = \frac{\lambda^\alpha}{\Gamma(\alpha)} t^{\alpha-1} e^{-\lambda t}, \quad t \geq 0,$$
where $\Gamma(\alpha) = \int_0^\infty t^{\alpha-1} e^{-t} \, dt$ extends the factorial via $\Gamma(n) = (n-1)!$ for positive integers. For $X \sim \text{Gamma}(\alpha, \lambda)$, $E[X] = \alpha/\lambda$ and $\text{Var}(X) = \alpha/\lambda^2$; it scales as $cX \sim \text{Gamma}(\alpha, \lambda/c)$ for $c > 0$. It is the conjugate prior for Poisson and exponential likelihoods and arises as sums of exponentials, occurrence times in Poisson processes, rainfall accumulation, and insurance claim sizes (e.g. the time until the 5th customer at a store with arrival rate $\lambda = 1$ per minute follows $\text{Gamma}(5, 1)$). A special case is the **chi-squared distribution** $\chi^2_k$: with $\alpha = k/2$ and $\lambda = 1/2$ we recover the distribution of $\sum_{i=1}^k Z_i^2$ for independent $Z_i \sim N(0, 1)$, which underlies goodness-of-fit testing.

- **Beta.** The **Beta distribution** on $[0, 1]$ with shape parameters $\alpha, \beta > 0$ has pdf
$$f(x) = \frac{1}{B(\alpha, \beta)} x^{\alpha-1} (1-x)^{\beta-1}, \quad x \in [0, 1],$$
where $B(\alpha, \beta) = \int_0^1 t^{\alpha-1} (1-t)^{\beta-1} \, dt = \Gamma(\alpha) \Gamma(\beta) / \Gamma(\alpha + \beta)$ is the **Beta function**. For $X \sim \text{Beta}(\alpha, \beta)$, $E[X] = \alpha/(\alpha + \beta)$ and $\text{Var}(X) = \alpha\beta / [(\alpha + \beta)^2 (\alpha + \beta + 1)]$. The shape is unimodal when $\alpha, \beta > 1$, uniform when $\alpha = \beta = 1$, and U-shaped when $\alpha, \beta < 1$. It is the conjugate prior for Bernoulli and binomial likelihoods and is the natural model for probabilities, proportions constrained to $[0, 1]$, and order statistics (e.g. for inference on a coin bias $p$, a $\text{Beta}(2, 2)$ prior — symmetric, peaked near $0.5$ — is more informative than $\text{Beta}(1, 1)$, which is uniform). Multimodal distributions on $[0, 1]$ are easily obtained as convex combinations of Betas, e.g. $\tfrac{1}{2}\text{Beta}(\alpha_1, \beta_1) + \tfrac{1}{2}\text{Beta}(\alpha_2, \beta_2)$.

Several other continuous distributions appear repeatedly as priors, sampling distributions, or generalisations of the families above:

- **Student's $t$.** With $\nu$ degrees of freedom, its pdf is
$$f(x) = \frac{\Gamma(\frac{\nu+1}{2})}{\sqrt{\pi\nu}\,\Gamma(\frac{\nu}{2})} \left(1 + \frac{x^2}{\nu}\right)^{-(\nu+1)/2}.$$
It has mean 0 (for $\nu > 1$) and variance $\nu / (\nu - 2)$ (for $\nu > 2$), with tails heavier than the normal — especially for small $\nu$ — and converges to $N(0, 1)$ as $\nu \to \infty$. It arises as the distribution of $\sqrt{n}(\bar{X}_n - \mu) / S_n$ where $S_n$ is the sample standard deviation, making it fundamental for frequentist confidence intervals.

- **Inverse Gamma.** With shape $\alpha$ and rate $\lambda$, its pdf is
$$f(x) = \frac{\lambda^\alpha}{\Gamma(\alpha)} x^{-\alpha-1} \exp\left(-\frac{\lambda}{x}\right), \quad x > 0,$$
with mean $\lambda / (\alpha - 1)$ for $\alpha > 1$. It is conjugate to the variance parameter of a normal likelihood and is commonly used as a prior for precision and variance parameters.

- **Dirichlet.** On the $(k-1)$-simplex (vectors $\mathbf{x} = (x_1, \ldots, x_k)$ with $x_i \geq 0$ and $\sum_i x_i = 1$), with parameters $\boldsymbol{\alpha} = (\alpha_1, \ldots, \alpha_k)$,
$$f(\mathbf{x}) = \frac{\Gamma(\sum_i \alpha_i)}{\prod_i \Gamma(\alpha_i)} \prod_{i=1}^k x_i^{\alpha_i - 1}.$$
It is conjugate to the multinomial likelihood and is the natural extension of the Beta to multiple categories.

- **Cauchy.** With location $\mu$ and scale $\sigma$, $f(x) = (1/\pi\sigma) \cdot 1/(1 + ((x - \mu)/\sigma)^2)$. The Cauchy has no finite mean or variance and its tails decay as $1/x^2$, making it useful as a prior that admits extreme values while remaining non-informative in a heavy-tailed sense.

## Transformation of variables and sums of random variables

Often we need the distribution of a transformed random variable or a sum. Two key techniques are the Jacobian and the moment-generating function.

::: {.proposition name="Transformation via Jacobian"}
If $Y = g(X)$ where $g$ is invertible and differentiable, then
$$f_Y(y) = f_X(g^{-1}(y)) \left| \frac{d g^{-1}}{dy} \right|.$$
For multivariate transformations $\mathbf{Y} = \mathbf{g}(\mathbf{X})$ with Jacobian matrix $\mathbf{J}$,
$$f_\mathbf{Y}(\mathbf{y}) = f_\mathbf{X}(\mathbf{g}^{-1}(\mathbf{y})) \left| \det \mathbf{J}^{-1}(\mathbf{y}) \right|.$$
:::

::: {.example name="Sum of uniform random variables"}
Let $X_1, X_2$ be independent $U(0, 1)$ random variables. We seek the distribution of $Y = X_1 + X_2$.

By definition, $f_Y(y) = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} f_{X_1}(x_1) f_{X_2}(y - x_1) \, dx_1$. Since $f_{X_1} = f_{X_2} = 1$ on $[0, 1]$, we need both $0 \leq x_1 \leq 1$ and $0 \leq y - x_1 \leq 1$, which constrains $x_1 \in [\max(0, y-1), \min(1, y)]$.

For $y \in [0, 2]$:
$$f_Y(y) = \int_{\max(0, y-1)}^{\min(1, y)} 1 \, dx_1 = \min(1, y) - \max(0, y-1) = \begin{cases} y & 0 \leq y \leq 1 \\ 2 - y & 1 < y \leq 2 \end{cases}$$

This is the triangular distribution—a classic result showing how convolution of uniform pdfs yields a piecewise linear shape. Generalizing to $n$ uniforms gives the Irwin–Hall distribution.
:::

::: {.example name="Sum of two independent exponentials (exam-style)"}
Let $X$ and $Y$ be independent and identically distributed exponential random variables with rate $\lambda > 0$, i.e.\ $f_X(x) = \lambda e^{-\lambda x}$ for $x \geq 0$. Find the density of $Z = X + Y$.

**Solution.** Since $X$ and $Y$ are independent, the density of the sum is the convolution
$$f_Z(z) = \int_{-\infty}^{+\infty} f_X(x)\,f_Y(z - x)\,dx = \int_{-\infty}^{+\infty} f_X(x)\,f_X(z - x)\,dx.$$
The integrand is non-zero only for $0 \leq x \leq z$, so for $z \geq 0$,
$$f_Z(z) = \int_0^z \lambda e^{-\lambda x}\,\lambda e^{-\lambda (z - x)}\,dx = \lambda^2 e^{-\lambda z} \int_0^z dx = \lambda^2 z\, e^{-\lambda z}.$$
Hence
$$f_Z(z) = \begin{cases} \lambda^2 z\, e^{-\lambda z}, & z \geq 0,\\ 0, & z < 0. \end{cases}$$
This is the Erlang / Gamma distribution with shape 2 and rate $\lambda$.
:::

::: {.example name="Detector dead time plus exponential waiting (exam-style)"}
A detector has a deterministic dead time $t_0 > 0$. After this dead time, the waiting time to the next event is exponential with rate $\lambda$. If $Z = t_0 + T$ with $T \sim \text{Exp}(\lambda)$, find the density and mean of $Z$.

**Solution.** A deterministic shift just translates the support. For $z \geq t_0$,
$$f_Z(z) = \lambda e^{-\lambda(z - t_0)}\,\mathbb{1}_{\{z \geq t_0\}}.$$
The mean shifts by the dead time:
$$E[Z] = t_0 + E[T] = t_0 + \frac{1}{\lambda}.$$
The variance is unchanged: $\text{Var}(Z) = 1/\lambda^2$. A deterministic shift does not add variability.
:::

::: {.proposition name="MGF of sums"}
If $X_1, \ldots, X_n$ are independent with MGFs $M_1(t), \ldots, M_n(t)$, then the MGF of $S_n = \sum_i X_i$ is
$$M_{S_n}(t) = \prod_{i=1}^n M_i(t).$$
This provides a direct route to the mean and variance of sums via derivatives.
:::

## Law of Large Numbers

The **Law of Large Numbers** (LLN) states that as we accumulate more samples, their average converges to the population mean. This is the mathematical foundation for why empirical frequencies approach probabilities.

::: {.theorem name="Weak Law of Large Numbers"}
Let $\{X_i\}_{i=1}^\infty$ be i.i.d. random variables with $E[X_i] = \mu$ and $\text{Var}(X_i) = \sigma^2 < \infty$. Then for any $\epsilon > 0$,
$$\lim_{n \to \infty} P\left(\left|\bar{X}_n - \mu\right| > \epsilon\right) = 0,$$
where $\bar{X}_n = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n X_i$. We write $\bar{X}_n \xrightarrow{P} \mu$ (convergence in probability).
:::

The weak LLN says that the sample mean concentrates around its expected value, with deviations becoming arbitrarily unlikely as $n$ grows. There is also a **strong LLN**, which states almost-sure convergence: $\bar{X}_n \xrightarrow{\text{a.s.}} \mu$ with probability 1.

::: {.note}
In Bayesian inference, the LLN underlies **posterior consistency**: as data accumulate, the posterior concentrates near the true parameter value, and the prior becomes negligible. This is why Bayesian and frequentist estimators often agree asymptotically, despite their philosophies differing.
:::

```{r}
#| label: lst-lln-demo
#| lst-cap: "Law of Large Numbers illustrated by running sample mean"
#| fig-cap: "Convergence of sample mean to true expected value"
set.seed(42)
par(mar = c(4.4, 4.4, 2.2, 1.2))
n <- 10000
y <- runif(n, -1, 1)
my <- cumsum(y) / (1:n)
plot(my, type = "l", lwd = 1, xlab = "n", ylab = "Sample mean",
     main = "Law of Large Numbers (Uniform on [-1, 1])")
abline(h = 0, col = "gray40", lwd = 1, lty = 2)
legend("topright", bty = "n", cex = 0.75,
       legend = c("Sample mean", "True mean = 0"),
       lwd = 1, lty = c(1, 2), col = c("black", "gray40"))
```

## Central Limit Theorem

The **Central Limit Theorem** (CLT) is the most important result in probability theory for statistics. It says that sample means converge in distribution to a normal, regardless of the underlying distribution.

::: {.theorem name="Central Limit Theorem"}
Let $\{X_i\}_{i=1}^\infty$ be i.i.d. random variables with $E[X_i] = \mu$ and $\text{Var}(X_i) = \sigma^2 > 0$. Then
$$\sqrt{n}(\bar{X}_n - \mu) \xrightarrow{d} N(0, \sigma^2)$$
as $n \to \infty$, where $\xrightarrow{d}$ denotes convergence in distribution. Equivalently,
$$\bar{X}_n \approx N\left(\mu, \frac{\sigma^2}{n}\right) \quad \text{for large } n.$$
:::

The CLT is **universal**: the form of the limiting normal depends only on the mean and variance, not on higher moments or the original shape of the distribution. This explains why the normal distribution is so ubiquitous in practice.

::: {.example name="CLT with non-normal data"}
Suppose we draw $n = 50$ samples from $U(0, 1)$, which is decidedly non-normal (uniform and bounded). The mean $\bar{X}_{50}$ is approximately $N(0.5, 1/(12 \cdot 50)) = N(0.5, 0.00167)$, so $\bar{X}_{50}$ has standard deviation $\approx 0.04$. Repeating this experiment 1000 times and histogramming the sample means shows a bell curve, despite the underlying distribution being uniform. As $n$ increases, the histogram becomes ever more Gaussian.
:::

```{r}
#| label: lst-clt-demo
#| lst-cap: "Central Limit Theorem with uniform samples"
#| fig-cap: "Distribution of sample means converges to normal despite uniform underlying data"
set.seed(42)
par(mar = c(4.4, 4.4, 2.2, 1.2))
n <- 50
m <- replicate(1000, mean(runif(n)))
hist(m, prob = TRUE, breaks = 30, ylim = c(0, 20),
     xlab = "Sample mean", ylab = "Density",
     main = "CLT: means of 1000 samples of size 50 from Uniform(0,1)")
curve(dnorm(x, 0.5, sd = sqrt(1 / (12 * n))), add = TRUE,
      col = "#b8123e", lwd = 1)
legend("topright", bty = "n", cex = 0.75,
       legend = c("Empirical", "N(0.5, 0.00167)"),
       fill = c("gray70", NA), lwd = c(NA, 1), col = c(NA, "#b8123e"))
```

The CLT extends naturally to **multivariate settings**: if $\mathbf{X}_1, \ldots, \mathbf{X}_n$ are i.i.d. $d$-dimensional random vectors with mean $\boldsymbol{\mu}$ and covariance matrix $\boldsymbol{\Sigma}$, then
$$\sqrt{n}(\bar{\mathbf{X}}_n - \boldsymbol{\mu}) \xrightarrow{d} N_d(\mathbf{0}, \boldsymbol{\Sigma}).$$
This is crucial for Bayesian inference on multiple parameters, since it justifies Gaussian posterior approximations via Laplace's method.

::: {.note}
In Bayesian inference, the CLT explains why:
- Posteriors are approximately Gaussian for large $n$ (Bernstein–von Mises theorem)
- Fisher information quantifies curvature of the log-likelihood at its mode
- Credible intervals around the posterior mean approximate confidence intervals
:::

## Large Deviation Principle

The CLT describes typical fluctuations (of order $\sqrt{n}$), but it does not capture rare, atypical events. The **Large Deviation Principle** (LDP) describes how the probability of observing a sample mean far from the true mean decays exponentially with sample size.

::: {.theorem name="Large Deviation Principle (Cramér)"}
Let $\{X_i\}_{i=1}^\infty$ be i.i.d. random variables with MGF $M(t) = E[e^{tX}]$ existing in a neighborhood of zero. Then for any $x$ in the interior of the support,
$$P(\bar{X}_n \approx x) \asymp e^{-nI(x)},$$
where the **rate function** is
$$I(x) = \sup_t [tx - \log M(t)].$$
This is the **Fenchel–Legendre transform** of the log-MGF.
:::

The rate function has the properties:
- $I(\mu) = 0$ (typical events at the mean have zero cost)
- $I(x) > 0$ for $x \neq \mu$ (atypical events carry exponential cost)
- Expanding $I(x)$ to second order around $x = \mu$ recovers the Gaussian behavior of the CLT

::: {.example name="LDP for normal samples"}
If $X_i \sim N(\mu, \sigma^2)$, then $M(t) = \exp(\mu t + \sigma^2 t^2/2)$, so
$$\log M(t) = \mu t + \frac{\sigma^2 t^2}{2}.$$
The rate function is
$$I(x) = \sup_t\left[tx - \mu t - \frac{\sigma^2 t^2}{2}\right].$$
Optimizing: $\frac{d}{dt}[\cdots] = x - \mu - \sigma^2 t = 0 \Rightarrow t^* = (x - \mu)/\sigma^2$, yielding
$$I(x) = \frac{(x - \mu)^2}{2\sigma^2}.$$
Thus, $P(\bar{X}_n \approx x) \sim \exp\left(-\frac{n(x - \mu)^2}{2\sigma^2}\right)$, which is consistent with the Gaussian CLT: rare events decay as a Gaussian tail.
:::

## Unified picture of asymptotics

These three results form a hierarchy of limiting behavior as $n \to \infty$:

1. **Law of Large Numbers**: The sample mean becomes deterministic. Fluctuations scale as $O(n^{-1})$ relative to the mean.

2. **Central Limit Theorem**: Gaussian fluctuations of order $\sqrt{n}^{-1}$ emerge around the LLN limit.

3. **Large Deviation Principle**: Rare events with $\bar{X}_n \approx x$ occur with probability $\sim e^{-nI(x)}$. For events beyond a few standard deviations, this exponential suppression dominates.

::: {.remark}
Conceptually, the picture is: **Randomness = Determinism + Fluctuations + Rare Events**. As data accumulate, the deterministic part grows, fluctuations remain Gaussian and relatively shrink, and rare events become exponentially suppressed. This is why Bayesian posteriors concentrate, why frequentist intervals shrink, and why model misspecification becomes detectable: the data force all inferences toward the truth under the asymptotic regime.
:::

## Exercises

::: {.exercise}

Find the CDF and PDF of the half-normal random variable $Y = |X|$ when $X \sim \mathcal{N}(0, 1)$.
:::

::: {.solution}
For $y \geq 0$, $F_Y(y) = P(|X| \leq y) = P(-y \leq X \leq y) = 2\Phi(y) - 1$, where $\Phi$ is the standard normal CDF. Differentiating,
$$f_Y(y) = 2\varphi(y) = \sqrt{\tfrac{2}{\pi}}\, e^{-y^2/2}, \quad y \geq 0,$$
and $f_Y(y) = 0$ for $y < 0$.
:::

::: {.exercise}

Let $X \sim \mathcal{N}(0, 1)$ and $Y = X^2$. Derive the density of $Y$ using the change-of-variables formula. Identify the resulting distribution by name.
:::

::: {.solution}
The map $y = x^2$ is two-to-one, with inverse branches $x = \pm\sqrt{y}$ and $|dx/dy| = 1/(2\sqrt{y})$. Summing both branches,
$$f_Y(y) = 2 \cdot \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}} e^{-y/2} \cdot \frac{1}{2\sqrt{y}} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi y}}\, e^{-y/2}, \quad y > 0.$$
This is the $\chi^2_1$ distribution, i.e.\ $\text{Gamma}(\tfrac{1}{2}, \tfrac{1}{2})$.
:::

::: {.exercise}

Let $X \sim \mathcal{N}(\mu, \sigma^2)$ and $Y = e^X$. Derive the density of $Y$ (log-normal). Compute $E[Y]$ in closed form using the MGF of $X$.
:::

::: {.solution}
With $x = \ln y$ and $|dx/dy| = 1/y$,
$$f_Y(y) = \frac{1}{y\sqrt{2\pi\sigma^2}} \exp\!\left(-\frac{(\ln y - \mu)^2}{2\sigma^2}\right), \quad y > 0.$$
By definition $E[Y] = E[e^X] = M_X(1)$, and since $M_X(t) = \exp(\mu t + \tfrac{1}{2}\sigma^2 t^2)$,
$$E[Y] = \exp\!\left(\mu + \tfrac{1}{2}\sigma^2\right).$$
:::

::: {.exercise}

Let $X, Y$ be independent with $X \sim \mathcal{N}(\mu_1, \sigma_1^2)$ and $Y \sim \mathcal{N}(\mu_2, \sigma_2^2)$. Use MGFs to show that $X + Y \sim \mathcal{N}(\mu_1 + \mu_2,\ \sigma_1^2 + \sigma_2^2)$.
:::

::: {.solution}
By independence, $M_{X+Y}(t) = M_X(t)\, M_Y(t)$. Substituting the Gaussian MGFs,
$$M_{X+Y}(t) = e^{\mu_1 t + \tfrac{1}{2}\sigma_1^2 t^2}\, e^{\mu_2 t + \tfrac{1}{2}\sigma_2^2 t^2} = \exp\!\left((\mu_1+\mu_2)t + \tfrac{1}{2}(\sigma_1^2+\sigma_2^2)t^2\right),$$
which is the MGF of $\mathcal{N}(\mu_1+\mu_2,\, \sigma_1^2+\sigma_2^2)$. By uniqueness of the MGF, $X+Y$ has that distribution.
:::

::: {.exercise}

Two independent exponentials with rate $\lambda$. Find the density of $M = \min(X, Y)$. Identify it by name.
:::

::: {.solution}
Using the survival function for the minimum of independents,
$$P(M > m) = P(X > m)\, P(Y > m) = e^{-\lambda m}\, e^{-\lambda m} = e^{-2\lambda m}, \quad m \geq 0.$$
Hence $f_M(m) = 2\lambda\, e^{-2\lambda m}$ for $m \geq 0$, so $M \sim \text{Exp}(2\lambda)$.
:::

::: {.exercise}

Two independent $\text{Uniform}(0, 1)$ variables. Find the density of $M = \max(X, Y)$ and compute $E[M]$.
:::

::: {.solution}
For $m \in [0,1]$, $F_M(m) = P(X \leq m)\,P(Y \leq m) = m^2$, so $f_M(m) = 2m$. The mean is
$$E[M] = \int_0^1 m \cdot 2m\, dm = \frac{2}{3}.$$
:::

::: {.exercise}

For $X \sim \text{Uniform}(a, b)$, compute $E[X]$ and $\text{Var}(X)$ directly from the PDF.
:::

::: {.solution}
With $f(x) = 1/(b-a)$ on $[a,b]$,
$$E[X] = \frac{1}{b-a}\int_a^b x\, dx = \frac{a+b}{2}, \quad E[X^2] = \frac{1}{b-a}\int_a^b x^2\, dx = \frac{a^2+ab+b^2}{3}.$$
Subtracting gives
$$\text{Var}(X) = E[X^2] - E[X]^2 = \frac{(b-a)^2}{12}.$$
:::

::: {.exercise}

For $X \sim \text{Gamma}(\alpha, \beta)$ with rate $\beta$, derive $E[X] = \alpha/\beta$ and $\text{Var}(X) = \alpha/\beta^2$ via the MGF. Then derive the same results via the cumulant generating function.
:::

::: {.solution}
The MGF is $M(t) = (1 - t/\beta)^{-\alpha}$ for $t < \beta$. Then $M'(0) = \alpha/\beta$ and $M''(0) = \alpha(\alpha+1)/\beta^2$, so $E[X] = \alpha/\beta$ and $\text{Var}(X) = M''(0) - M'(0)^2 = \alpha/\beta^2$. Via the CGF
$$K(t) = -\alpha \log(1 - t/\beta),$$
we get $K'(0) = \alpha/\beta = E[X]$ and $K''(0) = \alpha/\beta^2 = \text{Var}(X)$ directly.
:::

::: {.exercise}

Let $X, Y$ be i.i.d.\ $\text{Exp}(1)$. Find the density of $Z = X / (X + Y)$. Identify it by name.
:::

::: {.solution}
This is a standard Gamma ratio: if $X \sim \text{Gamma}(\alpha_1, \lambda)$ and $Y \sim \text{Gamma}(\alpha_2, \lambda)$ are independent, then $X/(X+Y) \sim \text{Beta}(\alpha_1, \alpha_2)$, independent of $X+Y$. Here $\alpha_1 = \alpha_2 = 1$, giving
$$f_Z(z) = 1, \quad z \in [0,1],$$
i.e.\ $Z \sim \text{Uniform}(0,1) = \text{Beta}(1,1)$.
:::

::: {.exercise}

Compute the MGF of $X \sim \text{Beta}(\alpha, \beta)$ as a series (no closed form is required). Use the series to derive $E[X] = \alpha/(\alpha + \beta)$.
:::

::: {.solution}
Expanding $e^{tx} = \sum_{k\geq 0} t^k x^k / k!$ and integrating term-by-term against the Beta density,
$$M(t) = \sum_{k=0}^{\infty} \frac{t^k}{k!}\, E[X^k] = \sum_{k=0}^\infty \frac{t^k}{k!}\, \frac{B(\alpha+k, \beta)}{B(\alpha,\beta)} = \sum_{k=0}^\infty \frac{t^k}{k!}\, \prod_{j=0}^{k-1}\frac{\alpha+j}{\alpha+\beta+j}.$$
The coefficient of $t$ is $E[X] = B(\alpha+1,\beta)/B(\alpha,\beta) = \alpha/(\alpha+\beta)$, using $\Gamma(\alpha+1) = \alpha\Gamma(\alpha)$.
:::

::: {.exercise}

Central limit theorem. Let $X_1, \ldots, X_n \sim \text{Exp}(\lambda)$ i.i.d. Give the approximate distribution of $\bar X_n$ for large $n$ in terms of $\lambda$ and $n$.
:::

::: {.solution}
Here $\mu = 1/\lambda$ and $\sigma^2 = 1/\lambda^2$, so the CLT gives
$$\bar X_n \approx \mathcal{N}\!\left(\frac{1}{\lambda},\, \frac{1}{n\lambda^2}\right) \quad \text{for large } n.$$
:::

::: {.exercise}

Inverse-CDF for the Cauchy distribution. The Cauchy CDF is $F(x) = \frac{1}{2} + \frac{1}{\pi}\arctan x$. Derive an explicit sampler $X = g(U)$ with $U \sim \text{Uniform}(0, 1)$.
:::

::: {.solution}
Setting $U = F(X)$ and solving, $\arctan X = \pi(U - \tfrac{1}{2})$, hence
$$X = \tan\!\left(\pi\!\left(U - \tfrac{1}{2}\right)\right), \quad U \sim \text{Uniform}(0,1).$$
:::

::: {.exercise}

Three independent Gaussians with the same mean $\mu$ and variances $\sigma_i^2$. Compute the variance of the precision-weighted mean $\hat\mu = \sum_i x_i / \sigma_i^2 \big/ \sum_i 1/\sigma_i^2$. Compare with the unweighted mean.
:::

::: {.solution}
Write $\hat\mu = \sum_i w_i x_i$ with $w_i = (1/\sigma_i^2)/S$ and $S = \sum_j 1/\sigma_j^2$. By independence,
$$\text{Var}(\hat\mu) = \sum_i w_i^2 \sigma_i^2 = \frac{1}{S^2}\sum_i \frac{1}{\sigma_i^2} = \frac{1}{\sum_i 1/\sigma_i^2}.$$
The unweighted mean $\bar X = \tfrac{1}{3}\sum_i x_i$ has $\text{Var}(\bar X) = \tfrac{1}{9}\sum_i \sigma_i^2$. By the Cauchy–Schwarz / AM–HM inequality $\text{Var}(\hat\mu) \leq \text{Var}(\bar X)$, with equality iff all $\sigma_i$ are equal.
:::

::: {.exercise}

Sum of two independent Gammas with the same rate. Show, using MGFs, that $\text{Gamma}(\alpha_1, \beta) + \text{Gamma}(\alpha_2, \beta) \sim \text{Gamma}(\alpha_1 + \alpha_2, \beta)$.
:::

::: {.solution}
With $M_i(t) = (1 - t/\beta)^{-\alpha_i}$ for $t < \beta$, independence gives
$$M_{X_1+X_2}(t) = (1 - t/\beta)^{-\alpha_1}\,(1 - t/\beta)^{-\alpha_2} = (1 - t/\beta)^{-(\alpha_1+\alpha_2)},$$
which is the MGF of $\text{Gamma}(\alpha_1 + \alpha_2, \beta)$. Uniqueness of the MGF yields the identification.
:::

::: {.exercise}

A random variable has CGF $K(t) = \mu t + \tfrac{1}{2}\sigma^2 t^2$. What is its distribution? Give a one-line justification using the uniqueness of the MGF.
:::

::: {.solution}
Exponentiating, the MGF is
$$M(t) = e^{K(t)} = \exp\!\left(\mu t + \tfrac{1}{2}\sigma^2 t^2\right),$$
which is the MGF of $\mathcal{N}(\mu, \sigma^2)$; by uniqueness of the MGF, $X \sim \mathcal{N}(\mu, \sigma^2)$.
:::
