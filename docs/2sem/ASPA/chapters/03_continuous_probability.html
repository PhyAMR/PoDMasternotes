4.1 Introduction

This chapter covers continuous random variables and the fundamental limit theorems that govern the behavior of sums of independent samples. These results form the mathematical backbone of Bayesian statistics: they explain why posteriors concentrate, why Gaussian approximations emerge, and how rare events scale exponentially with sample size. Together, they provide a unified picture of how randomness resolves into determinism and structure as data accumulates.

4.2 Continuous random variables

A continuous random variable \(X\) is characterized by a probability density function (pdf) \(f(x)\) defined over some interval (often \(\mathbb{R}\)). Unlike discrete random variables, \(X\) cannot take specific values with positive probability; instead, we specify probabilities over intervals: \[P(a \leq X \leq b) = \int_a^b f(x) \, dx,\] with the normalization \(\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} f(x) \, dx = 1\). At any single point, \(P(X = x) = 0\).

The cumulative distribution function (CDF) of a continuous random variable \(X\) is \[F(x) = P(X \leq x) = \int_{-\infty}^x f(t) \, dt.\] The pdf is recovered by differentiation: \(f(x) = \frac{d}{dx} F(x)\).

For multivariate distributions, the concepts extend naturally. Given random variables \(X\) and \(Y\): - The joint pdf \(f(x, y)\) satisfies \(\iint f(x, y) \, dx \, dy = 1\). - The marginal pdf of \(X\) is \(f(x) = \int f(x, y) \, dy\). - The conditional pdf of \(X\) given \(Y = y\) is \(f(x \mid y) = \frac{f(x, y)}{f(y)}\) (when \(f(y) > 0\)).

4.3 Expectation and moments

For a continuous random variable \(X\) with pdf \(f(x)\), the expectation is \[E[X] = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} x f(x) \, dx.\]

More generally, for any function \(g(X)\), \[E[g(X)] = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} g(x) f(x) \, dx.\]

The \(n\)-th raw moment is \(\mu_n' = E[X^n]\), and the \(n\)-th central moment is \(\mu_n = E[(X - E[X])^n]\). The second central moment is the variance: \[\text{Var}(X) = \mu_2 = E[(X - E[X])^2] = E[X^2] - (E[X])^2.\]

The standard deviation is \(\sigma = \sqrt{\text{Var}(X)}\).

The moment generating function (MGF) of \(X\) is \[M(t) = E[e^{tX}],\] defined in a neighborhood of \(t = 0\). The \(n\)-th moment is recovered via \[E[X^n] = \frac{d^n M}{dt^n}\bigg|_{t=0}.\] The MGF uniquely characterizes the distribution (when it exists) and is particularly useful for analyzing sums of independent random variables.

For random variables \(X\) and \(Y\), the covariance is \[\text{Cov}(X, Y) = E[(X - E[X])(Y - E[Y])].\] The correlation coefficient is \[\rho(X, Y) = \frac{\text{Cov}(X, Y)}{\sigma_X \sigma_Y},\] which lies in \([-1, 1]\) and measures linear association.

4.4 Standard continuous distributions

4.4.1 Uniform distribution

The uniform distribution on \([a, b]\) has constant density: \[f(x) = \frac{1}{b - a}, \quad x \in [a, b].\]

For \(X \sim U(a, b)\): - Mean: \(E[X] = \frac{a + b}{2}\) - Variance: \(\text{Var}(X) = \frac{(b - a)^2}{12}\) - Conjugate role: Used as a non-informative prior on bounded intervals - When it arises: Complete ignorance within fixed bounds; example draw in a random lottery

4.4.2 Normal distribution

The normal (or Gaussian) distribution with mean \(\mu\) and variance \(\sigma^2\) has pdf \[f(x) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi\sigma^2}} \exp\left(-\frac{(x - \mu)^2}{2\sigma^2}\right).\]

For \(X \sim N(\mu, \sigma^2)\): - Mean: \(E[X] = \mu\) - Variance: \(\text{Var}(X) = \sigma^2\) - Stability: If \(X_i \sim N(\mu_i, \sigma_i^2)\) are independent, then \(Y = \sum_i a_i X_i \sim N\left(\sum_i a_i \mu_i, \sum_i a_i^2 \sigma_i^2\right)\) - Conjugate role: Conjugate prior for normal likelihoods; emerges via the Central Limit Theorem - When it arises: Errors, noise, aggregates of many small effects; most common distribution in applied work - Example: Heights in a population approximate \(N(170, 100)\) (in cm) when averaged over many groups

Consider two measurements with uncertainties: \(X_1 \sim N(0, 1)\) and \(X_2 \sim N(2, 0.5^2)\). A comparison is easily visualized by overlaying their pdfs. The first is centered at 0 with width \(\sim 2\); the second is centered at 2 with narrower width \(\sim 1\). A posterior combining both observations would be pulled toward the more precise measurement.

4.4.3 Exponential distribution

The exponential distribution with rate \(\lambda > 0\) has pdf \[f(t) = \lambda e^{-\lambda t}, \quad t \geq 0.\]

For \(X \sim \text{Exp}(\lambda)\): - Mean: \(E[X] = 1/\lambda\) - Variance: \(\text{Var}(X) = 1/\lambda^2\) - Memoryless property: \(P(X > t + s \mid X > t) = P(X > s)\) for all \(s, t \geq 0\) - Conjugate role: Conjugate prior for exponential likelihoods - When it arises: Waiting times between successive events in a Poisson process; radioactive decay; lifetimes in reliability studies - Example: If calls arrive at a phone center at rate \(\lambda = 2\) per minute, the time until the next call is \(\text{Exp}(2)\) with expected value \(0.5\) minutes

The memoryless property is fundamental: it says that the residual waiting time does not depend on how long we have already waited. This makes the exponential the unique continuous memoryless distribution.

4.4.4 Erlang and Gamma distributions

The Erlang distribution describes the sum of \(n\) independent exponential random variables with the same rate \(\lambda\). It has pdf \[f(t) = \frac{\lambda^n t^{n-1}}{(n-1)!} e^{-\lambda t}, \quad t \geq 0.\]

The Gamma distribution generalizes Erlang by allowing the shape parameter \(n \to \alpha \in \mathbb{R}^+\): \[f(t) = \frac{\lambda^\alpha}{\Gamma(\alpha)} t^{\alpha-1} e^{-\lambda t}, \quad t \geq 0,\] where \(\Gamma(\alpha) = \int_0^\infty t^{\alpha-1} e^{-t} \, dt\) is the Gamma function (an extension of the factorial: \(\Gamma(n) = (n-1)!\) for positive integers).

For \(X \sim \text{Gamma}(\alpha, \lambda)\) with shape \(\alpha\) and rate \(\lambda\): - Mean: \(E[X] = \alpha/\lambda\) - Variance: \(\text{Var}(X) = \alpha/\lambda^2\) - Scaling: If \(X \sim \text{Gamma}(\alpha, \lambda)\), then \(cX \sim \text{Gamma}(\alpha, \lambda/c)\) for \(c > 0\) - Conjugate role: Conjugate prior for Poisson and exponential likelihoods - When it arises: Sums of exponentials; occurrence times in Poisson processes; rainfall accumulation; insurance claim sizes - Example: The total waiting time until the 5th customer arrives at a store, given arrivals at rate \(\lambda = 1\) per minute, follows \(\text{Gamma}(5, 1)\) with mean 5 minutes and variance 5

A special case is the chi-squared distribution \(\chi^2_k\): when \(\alpha = k/2\) and \(\lambda = 1/2\), we recover the distribution of \(\sum_{i=1}^k Z_i^2\) where \(Z_i \sim N(0, 1)\) are independent standard normals. This is crucial for goodness-of-fit testing.

4.4.5 Beta distribution

The Beta distribution on \([0, 1]\) with shape parameters \(\alpha, \beta > 0\) has pdf \[f(x) = \frac{1}{B(\alpha, \beta)} x^{\alpha-1} (1-x)^{\beta-1}, \quad x \in [0, 1],\] where the Beta function is \[B(\alpha, \beta) = \int_0^1 t^{\alpha-1} (1-t)^{\beta-1} \, dt = \frac{\Gamma(\alpha) \Gamma(\beta)}{\Gamma(\alpha + \beta)}.\]

For \(X \sim \text{Beta}(\alpha, \beta)\): - Mean: \(E[X] = \frac{\alpha}{\alpha + \beta}\) - Variance: \(\text{Var}(X) = \frac{\alpha \beta}{(\alpha + \beta)^2 (\alpha + \beta + 1)}\) - Conjugate role: Conjugate prior for Bernoulli and binomial likelihoods - Shape: Unimodal when \(\alpha, \beta > 1\); uniform when \(\alpha = \beta = 1\); U-shaped when \(\alpha, \beta < 1\) - When it arises: Probabilities (parameters of Bernoulli trials); proportions constrained to \([0, 1]\); order statistics - Example: For inference on the bias \(p\) of a coin, a prior \(\text{Beta}(2, 2)\) (symmetric, peaked near \(0.5\)) is more informative than \(\text{Beta}(1, 1)\) (uniform)

4.4.6 Other important continuous distributions

Student’s t-distribution with \(\nu\) degrees of freedom has pdf \[f(x) = \frac{\Gamma(\frac{\nu+1}{2})}{\sqrt{\pi\nu}\,\Gamma(\frac{\nu}{2})} \left(1 + \frac{x^2}{\nu}\right)^{-(\nu+1)/2}.\] It has mean 0 (for \(\nu > 1\)) and variance \(\nu / (\nu - 2)\) (for \(\nu > 2\)). The tails are heavier than normal, especially for small \(\nu\). As \(\nu \to \infty\), it converges to \(N(0, 1)\). It arises as the distribution of \(\sqrt{n}(\bar{X}_n - \mu) / S_n\) where \(S_n\) is the sample standard deviation, making it fundamental for frequentist confidence intervals.

Inverse Gamma distribution with shape \(\alpha\) and rate \(\lambda\) has pdf \[f(x) = \frac{\lambda^\alpha}{\Gamma(\alpha)} x^{-\alpha-1} \exp\left(-\frac{\lambda}{x}\right), \quad x > 0.\] Mean is \(\lambda / (\alpha - 1)\) (for \(\alpha > 1\)). It is conjugate to the variance parameter of a normal likelihood and is commonly used as a prior for precision/variance parameters.

Dirichlet distribution on the \((k-1)\)-simplex (i.e., vectors \(\mathbf{x} = (x_1, \ldots, x_k)\) with \(x_i \geq 0\) and \(\sum_i x_i = 1\)) with parameters \(\boldsymbol{\alpha} = (\alpha_1, \ldots, \alpha_k)\) has pdf \[f(\mathbf{x}) = \frac{\Gamma(\sum_i \alpha_i)}{\prod_i \Gamma(\alpha_i)} \prod_{i=1}^k x_i^{\alpha_i - 1}.\] It is conjugate to the multinomial likelihood and is the natural extension of the Beta distribution to multiple categories.

Cauchy distribution with location \(\mu\) and scale \(\sigma\) has pdf \[f(x) = \frac{1}{\pi\sigma} \frac{1}{1 + ((x - \mu)/\sigma)^2}.\] Notably, the Cauchy has no finite mean or variance. Its tails decay as \(1/x^2\), making it useful as a prior that allows for extreme values while remaining non-informative in a certain sense.

4.5 Transformation of variables and sums of random variables

Often we need the distribution of a transformed random variable or a sum. Two key techniques are the Jacobian and the moment-generating function.

If \(Y = g(X)\) where \(g\) is invertible and differentiable, then \[f_Y(y) = f_X(g^{-1}(y)) \left| \frac{d g^{-1}}{dy} \right|.\] For multivariate transformations \(\mathbf{Y} = \mathbf{g}(\mathbf{X})\) with Jacobian matrix \(\mathbf{J}\), \[f_\mathbf{Y}(\mathbf{y}) = f_\mathbf{X}(\mathbf{g}^{-1}(\mathbf{y})) \left| \det \mathbf{J}^{-1}(\mathbf{y}) \right|.\]

Let \(X_1, X_2\) be independent \(U(0, 1)\) random variables. We seek the distribution of \(Y = X_1 + X_2\).

By definition, \(f_Y(y) = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} f_{X_1}(x_1) f_{X_2}(y - x_1) \, dx_1\). Since \(f_{X_1} = f_{X_2} = 1\) on \([0, 1]\), we need both \(0 \leq x_1 \leq 1\) and \(0 \leq y - x_1 \leq 1\), which constrains \(x_1 \in [\max(0, y-1), \min(1, y)]\).

For \(y \in [0, 2]\): \[f_Y(y) = \int_{\max(0, y-1)}^{\min(1, y)} 1 \, dx_1 = \min(1, y) - \max(0, y-1) = \begin{cases} y & 0 \leq y \leq 1 \\ 2 - y & 1 < y \leq 2 \end{cases}\]

This is the triangular distribution—a classic result showing how convolution of uniform pdfs yields a piecewise linear shape. Generalizing to \(n\) uniforms gives the Irwin–Hall distribution.

If \(X_1, \ldots, X_n\) are independent with MGFs \(M_1(t), \ldots, M_n(t)\), then the MGF of \(S_n = \sum_i X_i\) is \[M_{S_n}(t) = \prod_{i=1}^n M_i(t).\] This provides a direct route to the mean and variance of sums via derivatives.

4.6 Law of Large Numbers

The Law of Large Numbers (LLN) states that as we accumulate more samples, their average converges to the population mean. This is the mathematical foundation for why empirical frequencies approach probabilities.

Let \(\{X_i\}_{i=1}^\infty\) be i.i.d. random variables with \(E[X_i] = \mu\) and \(\text{Var}(X_i) = \sigma^2 < \infty\). Then for any \(\epsilon > 0\), \[\lim_{n \to \infty} P\left(\left|\bar{X}_n - \mu\right| > \epsilon\right) = 0,\] where \(\bar{X}_n = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n X_i\). We write \(\bar{X}_n \xrightarrow{P} \mu\) (convergence in probability).

The weak LLN says that the sample mean concentrates around its expected value, with deviations becoming arbitrarily unlikely as \(n\) grows. There is also a strong LLN, which states almost-sure convergence: \(\bar{X}_n \xrightarrow{\text{a.s.}} \mu\) with probability 1.

In Bayesian inference, the LLN underlies posterior consistency: as data accumulate, the posterior concentrates near the true parameter value, and the prior becomes negligible. This is why Bayesian and frequentist estimators often agree asymptotically, despite their philosophies differing.

4.7 Central Limit Theorem

The Central Limit Theorem (CLT) is the most important result in probability theory for statistics. It says that sample means converge in distribution to a normal, regardless of the underlying distribution.

Let \(\{X_i\}_{i=1}^\infty\) be i.i.d. random variables with \(E[X_i] = \mu\) and \(\text{Var}(X_i) = \sigma^2 > 0\). Then \[\sqrt{n}(\bar{X}_n - \mu) \xrightarrow{d} N(0, \sigma^2)\] as \(n \to \infty\), where \(\xrightarrow{d}\) denotes convergence in distribution. Equivalently, \[\bar{X}_n \approx N\left(\mu, \frac{\sigma^2}{n}\right) \quad \text{for large } n.\]

The CLT is universal: the form of the limiting normal depends only on the mean and variance, not on higher moments or the original shape of the distribution. This explains why the normal distribution is so ubiquitous in practice.

Suppose we draw \(n = 50\) samples from \(U(0, 1)\), which is decidedly non-normal (uniform and bounded). The mean \(\bar{X}_{50}\) is approximately \(N(0.5, 1/(12 \cdot 50)) = N(0.5, 0.00167)\), so \(\bar{X}_{50}\) has standard deviation \(\approx 0.04\). Repeating this experiment 1000 times and histogramming the sample means shows a bell curve, despite the underlying distribution being uniform. As \(n\) increases, the histogram becomes ever more Gaussian.

set.seed(42)

4.7.1 Multivariate Central Limit Theorem

The CLT extends to multivariate settings. If \(\mathbf{X}_1, \ldots, \mathbf{X}_n\) are i.i.d. \(d\)-dimensional random vectors with mean \(\boldsymbol{\mu}\) and covariance matrix \(\boldsymbol{\Sigma}\), then \[\sqrt{n}(\bar{\mathbf{X}}_n - \boldsymbol{\mu}) \xrightarrow{d} N_d(\mathbf{0}, \boldsymbol{\Sigma}).\]

This is crucial for Bayesian inference on multiple parameters: it justifies Gaussian posterior approximations via Laplace’s method.

In Bayesian inference, the CLT explains why: - Posteriors are approximately Gaussian for large \(n\) (Bernstein–von Mises theorem) - Fisher information quantifies curvature of the log-likelihood at its mode - Credible intervals around the posterior mean approximate confidence intervals

4.8 Large Deviation Principle

The CLT describes typical fluctuations (of order \(\sqrt{n}\)), but it does not capture rare, atypical events. The Large Deviation Principle (LDP) describes how the probability of observing a sample mean far from the true mean decays exponentially with sample size.

Let \(\{X_i\}_{i=1}^\infty\) be i.i.d. random variables with MGF \(M(t) = E[e^{tX}]\) existing in a neighborhood of zero. Then for any \(x\) in the interior of the support, \[P(\bar{X}_n \approx x) \asymp e^{-nI(x)},\] where the rate function is \[I(x) = \sup_t [tx - \log M(t)].\] This is the Fenchel–Legendre transform of the log-MGF.

The rate function has the properties: - \(I(\mu) = 0\) (typical events at the mean have zero cost) - \(I(x) > 0\) for \(x \neq \mu\) (atypical events carry exponential cost) - Expanding \(I(x)\) to second order around \(x = \mu\) recovers the Gaussian behavior of the CLT

If \(X_i \sim N(\mu, \sigma^2)\), then \(M(t) = \exp(\mu t + \sigma^2 t^2/2)\), so \[\log M(t) = \mu t + \frac{\sigma^2 t^2}{2}.\] The rate function is \[I(x) = \sup_t\left[tx - \mu t - \frac{\sigma^2 t^2}{2}\right].\] Optimizing: \(\frac{d}{dt}[\cdots] = x - \mu - \sigma^2 t = 0 \Rightarrow t^* = (x - \mu)/\sigma^2\), yielding \[I(x) = \frac{(x - \mu)^2}{2\sigma^2}.\] Thus, \(P(\bar{X}_n \approx x) \sim \exp\left(-\frac{n(x - \mu)^2}{2\sigma^2}\right)\), which is consistent with the Gaussian CLT: rare events decay as a Gaussian tail.

4.9 Unified picture of asymptotics

These three results form a hierarchy of limiting behavior as \(n \to \infty\):

  1. Law of Large Numbers: The sample mean becomes deterministic. Fluctuations scale as \(O(n^{-1})\) relative to the mean.

  2. Central Limit Theorem: Gaussian fluctuations of order \(\sqrt{n}^{-1}\) emerge around the LLN limit.

  3. Large Deviation Principle: Rare events with \(\bar{X}_n \approx x\) occur with probability \(\sim e^{-nI(x)}\). For events beyond a few standard deviations, this exponential suppression dominates.

Remark. Conceptually, the picture is: Randomness = Determinism + Fluctuations + Rare Events. As data accumulate, the deterministic part grows, fluctuations remain Gaussian and relatively shrink, and rare events become exponentially suppressed. This is why Bayesian posteriors concentrate, why frequentist intervals shrink, and why model misspecification becomes detectable: the data force all inferences toward the truth under the asymptotic regime.

4.10 Generating functions, cumulants and characteristic functions

The moment generating function provides a systematic route to extracting and comparing moments of continuous distributions. Beyond moments, the cumulant generating function offers deeper structural insights, while the characteristic function enables rigorous proofs of limit theorems.

Extracting moments from the MGF

For a random variable \(X\) with MGF \(M_X(t) = \mathbb{E}[e^{tX}]\) (defined in a neighborhood of \(t = 0\)), the \(r\)-th moment is \[\mathbb{E}[X^r] = M_X^{(r)}(0) = \frac{d^r M_X}{dt^r}\bigg|_{t=0}.\]

Proof sketch: By definition, \(M_X(t) = \sum_{r=0}^\infty \frac{1}{r!} \mathbb{E}[X^r] t^r\) (power series expansion). Differentiating \(r\) times and setting \(t = 0\) isolates the \(r\)-th moment term. ∎

Worked moment extraction examples

For \(X \sim N(\mu, \sigma^2)\), the MGF is \[M(t) = \exp\left(\mu t + \frac{\sigma^2 t^2}{2}\right).\] Differentiating: \(M'(t) = \left(\mu + \sigma^2 t\right) M(t)\), so \(M'(0) = \mu = \mathbb{E}[X]\). Differentiating again: \(M''(t) = \sigma^2 M(t) + (\mu + \sigma^2 t) M'(t)\), so \(M''(0) = \sigma^2 + \mu^2 = \mathbb{E}[X^2]\). Thus \(\text{Var}(X) = \mathbb{E}[X^2] - (\mathbb{E}[X])^2 = \sigma^2\).

For \(X \sim \text{Exp}(\lambda)\), the MGF is \[M(t) = \frac{\lambda}{\lambda - t} \quad \text{for } t < \lambda.\] By the quotient rule, \(M'(t) = \frac{\lambda}{(\lambda - t)^2}\), so \(M'(0) = 1/\lambda = \mathbb{E}[X]\). Also, \(M''(t) = \frac{2\lambda}{(\lambda - t)^3}\), giving \(M''(0) = 2/\lambda^2 = \mathbb{E}[X^2]\). Therefore, \(\text{Var}(X) = \mathbb{E}[X^2] - (\mathbb{E}[X])^2 = 2/\lambda^2 - 1/\lambda^2 = 1/\lambda^2\).

For \(X \sim \text{Gamma}(\alpha, \beta)\) with shape \(\alpha\) and rate \(\beta\), the MGF is \[M(t) = \left(\frac{\beta}{\beta - t}\right)^\alpha \quad \text{for } t < \beta.\] Taking derivatives: \(M'(t) = \alpha \beta^\alpha (\beta - t)^{-\alpha-1}\), so \(M'(0) = \alpha/\beta = \mathbb{E}[X]\). The second derivative is \(M''(t) = \alpha(\alpha+1) \beta^\alpha (\beta - t)^{-\alpha-2}\), giving \(M''(0) = \alpha(\alpha+1)/\beta^2\). Thus, \(\text{Var}(X) = \alpha(\alpha+1)/\beta^2 - (\alpha/\beta)^2 = \alpha/\beta^2\).

Cumulant generating function and cumulants

While moments are the standard way to describe a distribution, cumulants offer better additive properties and often reveal deeper structure:

The cumulant generating function (CGF) of a random variable \(X\) is \[K_X(t) = \log M_X(t),\] where \(M_X(t)\) is the MGF. The \(r\)-th cumulant is defined as \[\kappa_r = K_X^{(r)}(0) = \frac{d^r K_X}{dt^r}\bigg|_{t=0}.\]

The first few cumulants relate to familiar statistics: - \(\kappa_1 = \mathbb{E}[X]\) (mean) - \(\kappa_2 = \text{Var}(X)\) (variance) - \(\kappa_3 = \mathbb{E}[(X - \mu)^3]\) (third central moment, related to skewness) - \(\kappa_4 = \mathbb{E}[(X - \mu)^4] - 3(\text{Var}(X))^2\) (related to excess kurtosis)

A key advantage: cumulants are additive for independent random variables. If \(X\) and \(Y\) are independent, \(K_{X+Y}(t) = K_X(t) + K_Y(t)\), so \(\kappa_r(X+Y) = \kappa_r(X) + \kappa_r(Y)\). This makes cumulants natural for analyzing sums of i.i.d. random variables.

Skewness and excess kurtosis

Once cumulants are computed, normalized statistics readily follow: - Skewness \(= \gamma_1 = \frac{\kappa_3}{\kappa_2^{3/2}}\) measures asymmetry (zero for symmetric distributions). - Excess kurtosis \(= \gamma_2 = \frac{\kappa_4}{\kappa_2^2}\) measures tail heaviness (zero for normal; positive for heavy-tailed).

Worked cumulant examples

For \(X \sim N(\mu, \sigma^2)\), the CGF is \[K(t) = \log\left[\exp\left(\mu t + \frac{\sigma^2 t^2}{2}\right)\right] = \mu t + \frac{\sigma^2 t^2}{2}.\] By inspection: \(\kappa_1 = \mu\), \(\kappa_2 = \sigma^2\), and \(\kappa_r = 0\) for \(r \geq 3\). This is the defining property of the Gaussian: it is the unique distribution whose cumulants vanish beyond the second. All higher cumulants are zero, making the normal distribution “maximally simple” in a cumulant sense.

For \(X \sim \text{Exp}(\lambda)\), the MGF is \(M(t) = \lambda/(\lambda - t)\), so \[K(t) = \log\left(\frac{\lambda}{\lambda - t}\right) = \log \lambda - \log(\lambda - t) = -\log\left(1 - \frac{t}{\lambda}\right).\] Using the series \(-\log(1 - u) = \sum_{r=1}^\infty \frac{u^r}{r}\): \[K(t) = \sum_{r=1}^\infty \frac{1}{r}\left(\frac{t}{\lambda}\right)^r.\] Extracting cumulants: \(\kappa_r = \frac{(r-1)!}{\lambda^r}\). In particular, \(\kappa_1 = 1/\lambda\) and \(\kappa_2 = 1/\lambda^2\), confirming \(\text{Var}(X) = 1/\lambda^2\).

For \(X \sim \text{Gamma}(\alpha, \beta)\), the MGF is \(M(t) = (\beta/(\beta - t))^\alpha\), so \[K(t) = \alpha \log\left(\frac{\beta}{\beta - t}\right) = \alpha \sum_{r=1}^\infty \frac{1}{r}\left(\frac{t}{\beta}\right)^r.\] Thus \(\kappa_r = \frac{(r-1)!}{\beta^r} \cdot \alpha\). If \(X_1 \sim \text{Gamma}(\alpha_1, \beta)\) and \(X_2 \sim \text{Gamma}(\alpha_2, \beta)\) are independent (same rate), then \[K_{X_1 + X_2}(t) = (\alpha_1 + \alpha_2) \sum_{r=1}^\infty \frac{1}{r}\left(\frac{t}{\beta}\right)^r = K_{\text{Gamma}(\alpha_1 + \alpha_2, \beta)}(t).\] So \(X_1 + X_2 \sim \text{Gamma}(\alpha_1 + \alpha_2, \beta)\). The Gamma family is closed under convolution with the same rate parameter.

Characteristic function and uniqueness

For rigorous analysis and limit theorems, we require a generating function that always exists:

The characteristic function of a random variable \(X\) is \[\varphi_X(t) = \mathbb{E}[e^{itX}],\] where \(i = \sqrt{-1}\) is the imaginary unit. Unlike the MGF, the characteristic function exists for all \(t \in \mathbb{R}\) and all distributions (no integrability issues).

The characteristic function uniquely determines the distribution. This is the content of the Lévy continuity theorem: if \(\varphi_{X_n}(t) \to \varphi_X(t)\) for all \(t\), then \(X_n \xrightarrow{d} X\) (convergence in distribution). This theorem is the rigorous foundation for proving the Central Limit Theorem.

Connection to the CLT via characteristic functions

Here is a sketch of why the characteristic function proves the CLT. Let \(X_1, X_2, \ldots\) be i.i.d. with mean 0 and variance 1. The characteristic function of \(S_n / \sqrt{n}\) is \[\varphi_{S_n/\sqrt{n}}(t) = \left[\varphi_X\left(\frac{t}{\sqrt{n}}\right)\right]^n.\] Expanding \(\varphi_X(t/\sqrt{n})\) to second order: \(\varphi_X(t/\sqrt{n}) \approx 1 - \frac{1}{2} \cdot \frac{t^2}{n}\). Thus, \[\varphi_{S_n/\sqrt{n}}(t) \approx \left[1 - \frac{t^2}{2n}\right]^n \to e^{-t^2/2} \quad \text{as } n \to \infty.\] But \(e^{-t^2/2}\) is the characteristic function of \(N(0, 1)\). By Lévy’s theorem, \(S_n/\sqrt{n} \xrightarrow{d} N(0, 1)\). This is the CLT. ∎

For a complete proof, see van der Linde & Toussaint (2011, §8) or Sivia & Skilling (2006, Appendix).

Application: analytical moments of the posterior

Remark. In Bayesian inference, when the posterior distribution has a tractable MGF or CGF, moments can be computed analytically without resorting to Markov chain Monte Carlo. For instance, if the posterior of a parameter is Gaussian or can be expressed as a Gamma or Normal-Gamma conjugate pair, moment derivatives are available in closed form. This is one reason conjugate priors remain valuable in practical Bayesian workflows: they yield posteriors whose moments are computable from simple formulas, enabling fast posterior inference and model diagnostics.

## Introduction

This chapter covers continuous random variables and the fundamental limit theorems that govern the behavior of sums of independent samples. These results form the mathematical backbone of Bayesian statistics: they explain why posteriors concentrate, why Gaussian approximations emerge, and how rare events scale exponentially with sample size. Together, they provide a unified picture of how randomness resolves into determinism and structure as data accumulates.

## Continuous random variables

A continuous random variable $X$ is characterized by a probability density function (pdf) $f(x)$ defined over some interval (often $\mathbb{R}$). Unlike discrete random variables, $X$ cannot take specific values with positive probability; instead, we specify probabilities over intervals:
$$P(a \leq X \leq b) = \int_a^b f(x) \, dx,$$
with the normalization $\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} f(x) \, dx = 1$. At any single point, $P(X = x) = 0$.

::: {.definition name="Cumulative distribution function"}
The **cumulative distribution function** (CDF) of a continuous random variable $X$ is
$$F(x) = P(X \leq x) = \int_{-\infty}^x f(t) \, dt.$$
The pdf is recovered by differentiation: $f(x) = \frac{d}{dx} F(x)$.
:::

For multivariate distributions, the concepts extend naturally. Given random variables $X$ and $Y$:
- The **joint pdf** $f(x, y)$ satisfies $\iint f(x, y) \, dx \, dy = 1$.
- The **marginal pdf** of $X$ is $f(x) = \int f(x, y) \, dy$.
- The **conditional pdf** of $X$ given $Y = y$ is $f(x \mid y) = \frac{f(x, y)}{f(y)}$ (when $f(y) > 0$).

## Expectation and moments

For a continuous random variable $X$ with pdf $f(x)$, the **expectation** is
$$E[X] = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} x f(x) \, dx.$$

More generally, for any function $g(X)$,
$$E[g(X)] = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} g(x) f(x) \, dx.$$

The $n$-th **raw moment** is $\mu_n' = E[X^n]$, and the $n$-th **central moment** is $\mu_n = E[(X - E[X])^n]$. The second central moment is the **variance**:
$$\text{Var}(X) = \mu_2 = E[(X - E[X])^2] = E[X^2] - (E[X])^2.$$

The **standard deviation** is $\sigma = \sqrt{\text{Var}(X)}$.

::: {.definition name="Moment generating function"}
The **moment generating function** (MGF) of $X$ is
$$M(t) = E[e^{tX}],$$
defined in a neighborhood of $t = 0$. The $n$-th moment is recovered via
$$E[X^n] = \frac{d^n M}{dt^n}\bigg|_{t=0}.$$
The MGF uniquely characterizes the distribution (when it exists) and is particularly useful for analyzing sums of independent random variables.
:::

::: {.definition name="Covariance and correlation"}
For random variables $X$ and $Y$, the **covariance** is
$$\text{Cov}(X, Y) = E[(X - E[X])(Y - E[Y])].$$
The **correlation coefficient** is
$$\rho(X, Y) = \frac{\text{Cov}(X, Y)}{\sigma_X \sigma_Y},$$
which lies in $[-1, 1]$ and measures linear association.
:::

## Standard continuous distributions

### Uniform distribution

The **uniform distribution** on $[a, b]$ has constant density:
$$f(x) = \frac{1}{b - a}, \quad x \in [a, b].$$

::: {.definition name="Uniform distribution parameters"}
For $X \sim U(a, b)$:
- Mean: $E[X] = \frac{a + b}{2}$
- Variance: $\text{Var}(X) = \frac{(b - a)^2}{12}$
- Conjugate role: Used as a non-informative prior on bounded intervals
- When it arises: Complete ignorance within fixed bounds; example draw in a random lottery
:::

### Normal distribution

The **normal** (or Gaussian) **distribution** with mean $\mu$ and variance $\sigma^2$ has pdf
$$f(x) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi\sigma^2}} \exp\left(-\frac{(x - \mu)^2}{2\sigma^2}\right).$$

::: {.definition name="Normal distribution parameters"}
For $X \sim N(\mu, \sigma^2)$:
- Mean: $E[X] = \mu$
- Variance: $\text{Var}(X) = \sigma^2$
- Stability: If $X_i \sim N(\mu_i, \sigma_i^2)$ are independent, then $Y = \sum_i a_i X_i \sim N\left(\sum_i a_i \mu_i, \sum_i a_i^2 \sigma_i^2\right)$
- Conjugate role: Conjugate prior for normal likelihoods; emerges via the Central Limit Theorem
- When it arises: Errors, noise, aggregates of many small effects; most common distribution in applied work
- Example: Heights in a population approximate $N(170, 100)$ (in cm) when averaged over many groups
:::

::: {.example name="Two Gaussian distributions"}
Consider two measurements with uncertainties: $X_1 \sim N(0, 1)$ and $X_2 \sim N(2, 0.5^2)$. A comparison is easily visualized by overlaying their pdfs. The first is centered at 0 with width $\sim 2$; the second is centered at 2 with narrower width $\sim 1$. A posterior combining both observations would be pulled toward the more precise measurement.
:::

```{r}

#| lst-cap: "Overlaid normal distributions showing effect of mean and precision"
#| fig-cap: "Two normal distributions with different parameters"
```

### Exponential distribution

The **exponential distribution** with rate $\lambda > 0$ has pdf
$$f(t) = \lambda e^{-\lambda t}, \quad t \geq 0.$$

::: {.definition name="Exponential distribution parameters"}
For $X \sim \text{Exp}(\lambda)$:
- Mean: $E[X] = 1/\lambda$
- Variance: $\text{Var}(X) = 1/\lambda^2$
- Memoryless property: $P(X > t + s \mid X > t) = P(X > s)$ for all $s, t \geq 0$
- Conjugate role: Conjugate prior for exponential likelihoods
- When it arises: Waiting times between successive events in a Poisson process; radioactive decay; lifetimes in reliability studies
- Example: If calls arrive at a phone center at rate $\lambda = 2$ per minute, the time until the next call is $\text{Exp}(2)$ with expected value $0.5$ minutes
:::

The memoryless property is fundamental: it says that the residual waiting time does not depend on how long we have already waited. This makes the exponential the unique continuous memoryless distribution.

### Erlang and Gamma distributions

The **Erlang distribution** describes the sum of $n$ independent exponential random variables with the same rate $\lambda$. It has pdf
$$f(t) = \frac{\lambda^n t^{n-1}}{(n-1)!} e^{-\lambda t}, \quad t \geq 0.$$

The **Gamma distribution** generalizes Erlang by allowing the shape parameter $n \to \alpha \in \mathbb{R}^+$:
$$f(t) = \frac{\lambda^\alpha}{\Gamma(\alpha)} t^{\alpha-1} e^{-\lambda t}, \quad t \geq 0,$$
where $\Gamma(\alpha) = \int_0^\infty t^{\alpha-1} e^{-t} \, dt$ is the Gamma function (an extension of the factorial: $\Gamma(n) = (n-1)!$ for positive integers).

::: {.definition name="Gamma distribution parameters"}
For $X \sim \text{Gamma}(\alpha, \lambda)$ with shape $\alpha$ and rate $\lambda$:
- Mean: $E[X] = \alpha/\lambda$
- Variance: $\text{Var}(X) = \alpha/\lambda^2$
- Scaling: If $X \sim \text{Gamma}(\alpha, \lambda)$, then $cX \sim \text{Gamma}(\alpha, \lambda/c)$ for $c > 0$
- Conjugate role: Conjugate prior for Poisson and exponential likelihoods
- When it arises: Sums of exponentials; occurrence times in Poisson processes; rainfall accumulation; insurance claim sizes
- Example: The total waiting time until the 5th customer arrives at a store, given arrivals at rate $\lambda = 1$ per minute, follows $\text{Gamma}(5, 1)$ with mean 5 minutes and variance 5
:::

A special case is the **chi-squared distribution** $\chi^2_k$: when $\alpha = k/2$ and $\lambda = 1/2$, we recover the distribution of $\sum_{i=1}^k Z_i^2$ where $Z_i \sim N(0, 1)$ are independent standard normals. This is crucial for goodness-of-fit testing.

### Beta distribution

The **Beta distribution** on $[0, 1]$ with shape parameters $\alpha, \beta > 0$ has pdf
$$f(x) = \frac{1}{B(\alpha, \beta)} x^{\alpha-1} (1-x)^{\beta-1}, \quad x \in [0, 1],$$
where the **Beta function** is
$$B(\alpha, \beta) = \int_0^1 t^{\alpha-1} (1-t)^{\beta-1} \, dt = \frac{\Gamma(\alpha) \Gamma(\beta)}{\Gamma(\alpha + \beta)}.$$

::: {.definition name="Beta distribution parameters"}
For $X \sim \text{Beta}(\alpha, \beta)$:
- Mean: $E[X] = \frac{\alpha}{\alpha + \beta}$
- Variance: $\text{Var}(X) = \frac{\alpha \beta}{(\alpha + \beta)^2 (\alpha + \beta + 1)}$
- Conjugate role: Conjugate prior for Bernoulli and binomial likelihoods
- Shape: Unimodal when $\alpha, \beta > 1$; uniform when $\alpha = \beta = 1$; U-shaped when $\alpha, \beta < 1$
- When it arises: Probabilities (parameters of Bernoulli trials); proportions constrained to $[0, 1]$; order statistics
- Example: For inference on the bias $p$ of a coin, a prior $\text{Beta}(2, 2)$ (symmetric, peaked near $0.5$) is more informative than $\text{Beta}(1, 1)$ (uniform)
:::

### Other important continuous distributions

**Student's t-distribution** with $\nu$ degrees of freedom has pdf
$$f(x) = \frac{\Gamma(\frac{\nu+1}{2})}{\sqrt{\pi\nu}\,\Gamma(\frac{\nu}{2})} \left(1 + \frac{x^2}{\nu}\right)^{-(\nu+1)/2}.$$
It has mean 0 (for $\nu > 1$) and variance $\nu / (\nu - 2)$ (for $\nu > 2$). The tails are heavier than normal, especially for small $\nu$. As $\nu \to \infty$, it converges to $N(0, 1)$. It arises as the distribution of $\sqrt{n}(\bar{X}_n - \mu) / S_n$ where $S_n$ is the sample standard deviation, making it fundamental for frequentist confidence intervals.

**Inverse Gamma distribution** with shape $\alpha$ and rate $\lambda$ has pdf
$$f(x) = \frac{\lambda^\alpha}{\Gamma(\alpha)} x^{-\alpha-1} \exp\left(-\frac{\lambda}{x}\right), \quad x > 0.$$
Mean is $\lambda / (\alpha - 1)$ (for $\alpha > 1$). It is conjugate to the variance parameter of a normal likelihood and is commonly used as a prior for precision/variance parameters.

**Dirichlet distribution** on the $(k-1)$-simplex (i.e., vectors $\mathbf{x} = (x_1, \ldots, x_k)$ with $x_i \geq 0$ and $\sum_i x_i = 1$) with parameters $\boldsymbol{\alpha} = (\alpha_1, \ldots, \alpha_k)$ has pdf
$$f(\mathbf{x}) = \frac{\Gamma(\sum_i \alpha_i)}{\prod_i \Gamma(\alpha_i)} \prod_{i=1}^k x_i^{\alpha_i - 1}.$$
It is conjugate to the multinomial likelihood and is the natural extension of the Beta distribution to multiple categories.

**Cauchy distribution** with location $\mu$ and scale $\sigma$ has pdf
$$f(x) = \frac{1}{\pi\sigma} \frac{1}{1 + ((x - \mu)/\sigma)^2}.$$
Notably, the Cauchy has no finite mean or variance. Its tails decay as $1/x^2$, making it useful as a prior that allows for extreme values while remaining non-informative in a certain sense.

## Transformation of variables and sums of random variables

Often we need the distribution of a transformed random variable or a sum. Two key techniques are the Jacobian and the moment-generating function.

::: {.proposition name="Transformation via Jacobian"}
If $Y = g(X)$ where $g$ is invertible and differentiable, then
$$f_Y(y) = f_X(g^{-1}(y)) \left| \frac{d g^{-1}}{dy} \right|.$$
For multivariate transformations $\mathbf{Y} = \mathbf{g}(\mathbf{X})$ with Jacobian matrix $\mathbf{J}$,
$$f_\mathbf{Y}(\mathbf{y}) = f_\mathbf{X}(\mathbf{g}^{-1}(\mathbf{y})) \left| \det \mathbf{J}^{-1}(\mathbf{y}) \right|.$$
:::

::: {.example name="Sum of uniform random variables"}
Let $X_1, X_2$ be independent $U(0, 1)$ random variables. We seek the distribution of $Y = X_1 + X_2$.

By definition, $f_Y(y) = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} f_{X_1}(x_1) f_{X_2}(y - x_1) \, dx_1$. Since $f_{X_1} = f_{X_2} = 1$ on $[0, 1]$, we need both $0 \leq x_1 \leq 1$ and $0 \leq y - x_1 \leq 1$, which constrains $x_1 \in [\max(0, y-1), \min(1, y)]$.

For $y \in [0, 2]$:
$$f_Y(y) = \int_{\max(0, y-1)}^{\min(1, y)} 1 \, dx_1 = \min(1, y) - \max(0, y-1) = \begin{cases} y & 0 \leq y \leq 1 \\ 2 - y & 1 < y \leq 2 \end{cases}$$

This is the triangular distribution—a classic result showing how convolution of uniform pdfs yields a piecewise linear shape. Generalizing to $n$ uniforms gives the Irwin–Hall distribution.
:::

::: {.proposition name="MGF of sums"}
If $X_1, \ldots, X_n$ are independent with MGFs $M_1(t), \ldots, M_n(t)$, then the MGF of $S_n = \sum_i X_i$ is
$$M_{S_n}(t) = \prod_{i=1}^n M_i(t).$$
This provides a direct route to the mean and variance of sums via derivatives.
:::

## Law of Large Numbers

The **Law of Large Numbers** (LLN) states that as we accumulate more samples, their average converges to the population mean. This is the mathematical foundation for why empirical frequencies approach probabilities.

::: {.theorem name="Weak Law of Large Numbers"}
Let $\{X_i\}_{i=1}^\infty$ be i.i.d. random variables with $E[X_i] = \mu$ and $\text{Var}(X_i) = \sigma^2 < \infty$. Then for any $\epsilon > 0$,
$$\lim_{n \to \infty} P\left(\left|\bar{X}_n - \mu\right| > \epsilon\right) = 0,$$
where $\bar{X}_n = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n X_i$. We write $\bar{X}_n \xrightarrow{P} \mu$ (convergence in probability).
:::

The weak LLN says that the sample mean concentrates around its expected value, with deviations becoming arbitrarily unlikely as $n$ grows. There is also a **strong LLN**, which states almost-sure convergence: $\bar{X}_n \xrightarrow{\text{a.s.}} \mu$ with probability 1.

::: {.note}
In Bayesian inference, the LLN underlies **posterior consistency**: as data accumulate, the posterior concentrates near the true parameter value, and the prior becomes negligible. This is why Bayesian and frequentist estimators often agree asymptotically, despite their philosophies differing.
:::

```{r}
#| label: lst-lln-demo
#| lst-cap: "Law of Large Numbers illustrated by running sample mean"
#| fig-cap: "Convergence of sample mean to true expected value"
set.seed(42)
par(mar = c(4.4, 4.4, 2.2, 1.2))
n <- 10000
y <- runif(n, -1, 1)
my <- cumsum(y) / (1:n)
plot(my, type = "l", lwd = 1, xlab = "n", ylab = "Sample mean",
     main = "Law of Large Numbers (Uniform on [-1, 1])")
abline(h = 0, col = "gray40", lwd = 1, lty = 2)
legend("topright", bty = "n", cex = 0.75,
       legend = c("Sample mean", "True mean = 0"),
       lwd = 1, lty = c(1, 2), col = c("black", "gray40"))
```

## Central Limit Theorem

The **Central Limit Theorem** (CLT) is the most important result in probability theory for statistics. It says that sample means converge in distribution to a normal, regardless of the underlying distribution.

::: {.theorem name="Central Limit Theorem"}
Let $\{X_i\}_{i=1}^\infty$ be i.i.d. random variables with $E[X_i] = \mu$ and $\text{Var}(X_i) = \sigma^2 > 0$. Then
$$\sqrt{n}(\bar{X}_n - \mu) \xrightarrow{d} N(0, \sigma^2)$$
as $n \to \infty$, where $\xrightarrow{d}$ denotes convergence in distribution. Equivalently,
$$\bar{X}_n \approx N\left(\mu, \frac{\sigma^2}{n}\right) \quad \text{for large } n.$$
:::

The CLT is **universal**: the form of the limiting normal depends only on the mean and variance, not on higher moments or the original shape of the distribution. This explains why the normal distribution is so ubiquitous in practice.

::: {.example name="CLT with non-normal data"}
Suppose we draw $n = 50$ samples from $U(0, 1)$, which is decidedly non-normal (uniform and bounded). The mean $\bar{X}_{50}$ is approximately $N(0.5, 1/(12 \cdot 50)) = N(0.5, 0.00167)$, so $\bar{X}_{50}$ has standard deviation $\approx 0.04$. Repeating this experiment 1000 times and histogramming the sample means shows a bell curve, despite the underlying distribution being uniform. As $n$ increases, the histogram becomes ever more Gaussian.
:::

```{r}
#| label: lst-clt-demo
#| lst-cap: "Central Limit Theorem with uniform samples"
#| fig-cap: "Distribution of sample means converges to normal despite uniform underlying data"
set.seed(42)
par(mar = c(4.4, 4.4, 2.2, 1.2))
n <- 50
m <- replicate(1000, mean(runif(n)))
hist(m, prob = TRUE, breaks = 30, ylim = c(0, 20),
     xlab = "Sample mean", ylab = "Density",
     main = "CLT: means of 1000 samples of size 50 from Uniform(0,1)")
curve(dnorm(x, 0.5, sd = sqrt(1 / (12 * n))), add = TRUE,
      col = "#b8123e", lwd = 1)
legend("topright", bty = "n", cex = 0.75,
       legend = c("Empirical", "N(0.5, 0.00167)"),
       fill = c("gray70", NA), lwd = c(NA, 1), col = c(NA, "#b8123e"))
```

### Multivariate Central Limit Theorem

The CLT extends to multivariate settings. If $\mathbf{X}_1, \ldots, \mathbf{X}_n$ are i.i.d. $d$-dimensional random vectors with mean $\boldsymbol{\mu}$ and covariance matrix $\boldsymbol{\Sigma}$, then
$$\sqrt{n}(\bar{\mathbf{X}}_n - \boldsymbol{\mu}) \xrightarrow{d} N_d(\mathbf{0}, \boldsymbol{\Sigma}).$$

This is crucial for Bayesian inference on multiple parameters: it justifies Gaussian posterior approximations via Laplace's method.

::: {.note}
In Bayesian inference, the CLT explains why:
- Posteriors are approximately Gaussian for large $n$ (Bernstein–von Mises theorem)
- Fisher information quantifies curvature of the log-likelihood at its mode
- Credible intervals around the posterior mean approximate confidence intervals
:::

## Large Deviation Principle

The CLT describes typical fluctuations (of order $\sqrt{n}$), but it does not capture rare, atypical events. The **Large Deviation Principle** (LDP) describes how the probability of observing a sample mean far from the true mean decays exponentially with sample size.

::: {.theorem name="Large Deviation Principle (Cramér)"}
Let $\{X_i\}_{i=1}^\infty$ be i.i.d. random variables with MGF $M(t) = E[e^{tX}]$ existing in a neighborhood of zero. Then for any $x$ in the interior of the support,
$$P(\bar{X}_n \approx x) \asymp e^{-nI(x)},$$
where the **rate function** is
$$I(x) = \sup_t [tx - \log M(t)].$$
This is the **Fenchel–Legendre transform** of the log-MGF.
:::

The rate function has the properties:
- $I(\mu) = 0$ (typical events at the mean have zero cost)
- $I(x) > 0$ for $x \neq \mu$ (atypical events carry exponential cost)
- Expanding $I(x)$ to second order around $x = \mu$ recovers the Gaussian behavior of the CLT

::: {.example name="LDP for normal samples"}
If $X_i \sim N(\mu, \sigma^2)$, then $M(t) = \exp(\mu t + \sigma^2 t^2/2)$, so
$$\log M(t) = \mu t + \frac{\sigma^2 t^2}{2}.$$
The rate function is
$$I(x) = \sup_t\left[tx - \mu t - \frac{\sigma^2 t^2}{2}\right].$$
Optimizing: $\frac{d}{dt}[\cdots] = x - \mu - \sigma^2 t = 0 \Rightarrow t^* = (x - \mu)/\sigma^2$, yielding
$$I(x) = \frac{(x - \mu)^2}{2\sigma^2}.$$
Thus, $P(\bar{X}_n \approx x) \sim \exp\left(-\frac{n(x - \mu)^2}{2\sigma^2}\right)$, which is consistent with the Gaussian CLT: rare events decay as a Gaussian tail.
:::

## Unified picture of asymptotics

These three results form a hierarchy of limiting behavior as $n \to \infty$:

1. **Law of Large Numbers**: The sample mean becomes deterministic. Fluctuations scale as $O(n^{-1})$ relative to the mean.

2. **Central Limit Theorem**: Gaussian fluctuations of order $\sqrt{n}^{-1}$ emerge around the LLN limit.

3. **Large Deviation Principle**: Rare events with $\bar{X}_n \approx x$ occur with probability $\sim e^{-nI(x)}$. For events beyond a few standard deviations, this exponential suppression dominates.

::: {.remark}
Conceptually, the picture is: **Randomness = Determinism + Fluctuations + Rare Events**. As data accumulate, the deterministic part grows, fluctuations remain Gaussian and relatively shrink, and rare events become exponentially suppressed. This is why Bayesian posteriors concentrate, why frequentist intervals shrink, and why model misspecification becomes detectable: the data force all inferences toward the truth under the asymptotic regime.
:::

## Generating functions, cumulants and characteristic functions

The moment generating function provides a systematic route to extracting and comparing moments of continuous distributions. Beyond moments, the **cumulant generating function** offers deeper structural insights, while the **characteristic function** enables rigorous proofs of limit theorems.

**Extracting moments from the MGF**

::: {.proposition name="Moments from the MGF"}
For a random variable $X$ with MGF $M_X(t) = \mathbb{E}[e^{tX}]$ (defined in a neighborhood of $t = 0$), the $r$-th moment is
$$\mathbb{E}[X^r] = M_X^{(r)}(0) = \frac{d^r M_X}{dt^r}\bigg|_{t=0}.$$

**Proof sketch**: By definition, $M_X(t) = \sum_{r=0}^\infty \frac{1}{r!} \mathbb{E}[X^r] t^r$ (power series expansion). Differentiating $r$ times and setting $t = 0$ isolates the $r$-th moment term. ∎
:::

**Worked moment extraction examples**

::: {.example name="Normal distribution"}
For $X \sim N(\mu, \sigma^2)$, the MGF is
$$M(t) = \exp\left(\mu t + \frac{\sigma^2 t^2}{2}\right).$$
Differentiating: $M'(t) = \left(\mu + \sigma^2 t\right) M(t)$, so $M'(0) = \mu = \mathbb{E}[X]$. Differentiating again: $M''(t) = \sigma^2 M(t) + (\mu + \sigma^2 t) M'(t)$, so $M''(0) = \sigma^2 + \mu^2 = \mathbb{E}[X^2]$. Thus $\text{Var}(X) = \mathbb{E}[X^2] - (\mathbb{E}[X])^2 = \sigma^2$.
:::

::: {.example name="Exponential distribution"}
For $X \sim \text{Exp}(\lambda)$, the MGF is
$$M(t) = \frac{\lambda}{\lambda - t} \quad \text{for } t < \lambda.$$
By the quotient rule, $M'(t) = \frac{\lambda}{(\lambda - t)^2}$, so $M'(0) = 1/\lambda = \mathbb{E}[X]$. Also, $M''(t) = \frac{2\lambda}{(\lambda - t)^3}$, giving $M''(0) = 2/\lambda^2 = \mathbb{E}[X^2]$. Therefore, $\text{Var}(X) = \mathbb{E}[X^2] - (\mathbb{E}[X])^2 = 2/\lambda^2 - 1/\lambda^2 = 1/\lambda^2$.
:::

::: {.example name="Gamma distribution"}
For $X \sim \text{Gamma}(\alpha, \beta)$ with shape $\alpha$ and rate $\beta$, the MGF is
$$M(t) = \left(\frac{\beta}{\beta - t}\right)^\alpha \quad \text{for } t < \beta.$$
Taking derivatives: $M'(t) = \alpha \beta^\alpha (\beta - t)^{-\alpha-1}$, so $M'(0) = \alpha/\beta = \mathbb{E}[X]$. The second derivative is $M''(t) = \alpha(\alpha+1) \beta^\alpha (\beta - t)^{-\alpha-2}$, giving $M''(0) = \alpha(\alpha+1)/\beta^2$. Thus, $\text{Var}(X) = \alpha(\alpha+1)/\beta^2 - (\alpha/\beta)^2 = \alpha/\beta^2$.
:::

**Cumulant generating function and cumulants**

While moments are the standard way to describe a distribution, cumulants offer better additive properties and often reveal deeper structure:

::: {.definition name="Cumulant generating function"}
The **cumulant generating function** (CGF) of a random variable $X$ is
$$K_X(t) = \log M_X(t),$$
where $M_X(t)$ is the MGF. The $r$-th **cumulant** is defined as
$$\kappa_r = K_X^{(r)}(0) = \frac{d^r K_X}{dt^r}\bigg|_{t=0}.$$

The first few cumulants relate to familiar statistics:
- $\kappa_1 = \mathbb{E}[X]$ (mean)
- $\kappa_2 = \text{Var}(X)$ (variance)
- $\kappa_3 = \mathbb{E}[(X - \mu)^3]$ (third central moment, related to **skewness**)
- $\kappa_4 = \mathbb{E}[(X - \mu)^4] - 3(\text{Var}(X))^2$ (related to **excess kurtosis**)

A key advantage: cumulants are **additive for independent random variables**. If $X$ and $Y$ are independent, $K_{X+Y}(t) = K_X(t) + K_Y(t)$, so $\kappa_r(X+Y) = \kappa_r(X) + \kappa_r(Y)$. This makes cumulants natural for analyzing sums of i.i.d. random variables.
:::

**Skewness and excess kurtosis**

::: {.note}
Once cumulants are computed, normalized statistics readily follow:
- **Skewness** $= \gamma_1 = \frac{\kappa_3}{\kappa_2^{3/2}}$ measures asymmetry (zero for symmetric distributions).
- **Excess kurtosis** $= \gamma_2 = \frac{\kappa_4}{\kappa_2^2}$ measures tail heaviness (zero for normal; positive for heavy-tailed).
:::

**Worked cumulant examples**

::: {.example name="Gaussian distribution (cumulants)"}
For $X \sim N(\mu, \sigma^2)$, the CGF is
$$K(t) = \log\left[\exp\left(\mu t + \frac{\sigma^2 t^2}{2}\right)\right] = \mu t + \frac{\sigma^2 t^2}{2}.$$
By inspection: $\kappa_1 = \mu$, $\kappa_2 = \sigma^2$, and $\kappa_r = 0$ for $r \geq 3$. This is the defining property of the Gaussian: it is the unique distribution whose cumulants vanish beyond the second. All higher cumulants are zero, making the normal distribution "maximally simple" in a cumulant sense.
:::

::: {.example name="Exponential distribution (cumulants)"}
For $X \sim \text{Exp}(\lambda)$, the MGF is $M(t) = \lambda/(\lambda - t)$, so
$$K(t) = \log\left(\frac{\lambda}{\lambda - t}\right) = \log \lambda - \log(\lambda - t) = -\log\left(1 - \frac{t}{\lambda}\right).$$
Using the series $-\log(1 - u) = \sum_{r=1}^\infty \frac{u^r}{r}$:
$$K(t) = \sum_{r=1}^\infty \frac{1}{r}\left(\frac{t}{\lambda}\right)^r.$$
Extracting cumulants: $\kappa_r = \frac{(r-1)!}{\lambda^r}$. In particular, $\kappa_1 = 1/\lambda$ and $\kappa_2 = 1/\lambda^2$, confirming $\text{Var}(X) = 1/\lambda^2$.
:::

::: {.example name="Gamma distribution (cumulants and closure)"}
For $X \sim \text{Gamma}(\alpha, \beta)$, the MGF is $M(t) = (\beta/(\beta - t))^\alpha$, so
$$K(t) = \alpha \log\left(\frac{\beta}{\beta - t}\right) = \alpha \sum_{r=1}^\infty \frac{1}{r}\left(\frac{t}{\beta}\right)^r.$$
Thus $\kappa_r = \frac{(r-1)!}{\beta^r} \cdot \alpha$. If $X_1 \sim \text{Gamma}(\alpha_1, \beta)$ and $X_2 \sim \text{Gamma}(\alpha_2, \beta)$ are independent (same rate), then
$$K_{X_1 + X_2}(t) = (\alpha_1 + \alpha_2) \sum_{r=1}^\infty \frac{1}{r}\left(\frac{t}{\beta}\right)^r = K_{\text{Gamma}(\alpha_1 + \alpha_2, \beta)}(t).$$
So $X_1 + X_2 \sim \text{Gamma}(\alpha_1 + \alpha_2, \beta)$. The Gamma family is **closed under convolution** with the same rate parameter.
:::

**Characteristic function and uniqueness**

For rigorous analysis and limit theorems, we require a generating function that always exists:

::: {.definition name="Characteristic function"}
The **characteristic function** of a random variable $X$ is
$$\varphi_X(t) = \mathbb{E}[e^{itX}],$$
where $i = \sqrt{-1}$ is the imaginary unit. Unlike the MGF, the characteristic function exists for all $t \in \mathbb{R}$ and all distributions (no integrability issues).

The characteristic function uniquely determines the distribution. This is the content of the **Lévy continuity theorem**: if $\varphi_{X_n}(t) \to \varphi_X(t)$ for all $t$, then $X_n \xrightarrow{d} X$ (convergence in distribution). This theorem is the rigorous foundation for proving the Central Limit Theorem.
:::

**Connection to the CLT via characteristic functions**

::: {.note}
Here is a sketch of why the characteristic function proves the CLT. Let $X_1, X_2, \ldots$ be i.i.d. with mean 0 and variance 1. The characteristic function of $S_n / \sqrt{n}$ is
$$\varphi_{S_n/\sqrt{n}}(t) = \left[\varphi_X\left(\frac{t}{\sqrt{n}}\right)\right]^n.$$
Expanding $\varphi_X(t/\sqrt{n})$ to second order: $\varphi_X(t/\sqrt{n}) \approx 1 - \frac{1}{2} \cdot \frac{t^2}{n}$. Thus,
$$\varphi_{S_n/\sqrt{n}}(t) \approx \left[1 - \frac{t^2}{2n}\right]^n \to e^{-t^2/2} \quad \text{as } n \to \infty.$$
But $e^{-t^2/2}$ is the characteristic function of $N(0, 1)$. By Lévy's theorem, $S_n/\sqrt{n} \xrightarrow{d} N(0, 1)$. This is the CLT. ∎

For a complete proof, see van der Linde & Toussaint (2011, §8) or Sivia & Skilling (2006, Appendix).
:::

**Application: analytical moments of the posterior**

::: {.remark}
In Bayesian inference, when the posterior distribution has a tractable MGF or CGF, moments can be computed analytically without resorting to Markov chain Monte Carlo. For instance, if the posterior of a parameter is Gaussian or can be expressed as a Gamma or Normal-Gamma conjugate pair, moment derivatives are available in closed form. This is one reason conjugate priors remain valuable in practical Bayesian workflows: they yield posteriors whose moments are computable from simple formulas, enabling fast posterior inference and model diagnostics.
:::
