14.1 Introduction: When linear regression fails

Classical Gaussian linear regression assumes that the response variable \(y_i\) is continuous and approximately normally distributed with mean \(\mu_i = f(x_i; \beta)\) and constant variance \(\sigma^2\). This assumption works well for many physical measurements with small, additive noise.

However, in many experiments we encounter response variables that violate this assumption fundamentally:

  • Count data: number of detected photons in a time window, decay events in a bin, or success counts in repeated trials. These are non-negative integers with variance proportional to the mean, not constant.
  • Binary outcomes: detection (yes/no), selection success (pass/fail), or signal presence (present/absent). The mean must lie in \((0,1)\) to represent a probability.
  • Positive-only data: time-to-event, energy, or rate parameters. The mean must be strictly positive.

Fitting a standard linear model \(y_i \sim N(\beta_0 + \beta_1 x_i, \sigma^2)\) to such data produces:

  • Invalid predictions: fitted means may fall outside the natural parameter space (e.g., negative counts or probabilities outside \([0,1]\)).
  • Incorrect uncertainty quantification: constant-variance assumptions badly misrepresent the variability structure of heteroscedastic responses.
  • Biased inference: parameter estimates become inefficient and confidence intervals lose their intended coverage.

Generalized linear models extend regression to non-Gaussian responses by replacing the normality assumption with a member of the exponential family and introducing a link function that maps the linear predictor onto the valid parameter space.

14.2 The three ingredients of a GLM

A generalized linear model comprises three interconnected components:

Definition (GLM structure): A generalized linear model for observations \(y_1, \ldots, y_N\) is specified by:

  1. Random component: \(y_i \mid \theta_i \sim p(y_i; \theta_i)\), where \(p\) belongs to an exponential-family distribution parameterized by a natural parameter \(\theta_i\).

  2. Systematic component: a linear predictor \[\eta_i = \beta_0 + \beta_1 x_{i,1} + \cdots + \beta_p x_{i,p} = \mathbf{x}_i^\top \boldsymbol{\beta},\] where \(\boldsymbol{\beta}\) is a vector of regression coefficients and \(\mathbf{x}_i\) is the design vector for observation \(i\).

  3. Link function: a smooth, invertible function \(g(\cdot)\) that relates the mean \(\mu_i = \mathbb{E}[y_i \mid \mathbf{x}_i]\) to the linear predictor: \[g(\mu_i) = \eta_i, \quad \text{equivalently} \quad \mu_i = g^{-1}(\eta_i).\] The inverse link, \(g^{-1}\), is called the mean function.

The link function ensures that even though \(\eta_i\) can take any real value, the predicted mean \(\mu_i = g^{-1}(\eta_i)\) respects the constraints of the response distribution.

14.3 The exponential family and canonical form

The exponential family is a large class of distributions sharing a common parameterization that unifies likelihood inference for GLMs.

Definition (Exponential family canonical form): A probability distribution for \(y\) belongs to the exponential family if it can be written as \[p(y; \theta, \phi) = h(y, \phi) \exp\left(\frac{\theta y - A(\theta)}{\phi}\right),\] where: - \(\theta\) is the natural parameter (or canonical parameter). - \(\phi > 0\) is the dispersion parameter, often related to variance. - \(A(\theta)\) is the cumulant function or log-partition function. - \(h(y, \phi)\) is the base measure (carries \(y\)-dependent normalization and \(\phi\) terms not involving \(\theta\)).

The term \(A(\theta)\) ensures the distribution integrates or sums to one.

Theorem (Moment relations): For a distribution in exponential-family form, \[\mathbb{E}[y] = A'(\theta), \quad \mathrm{Var}(y) = \phi \cdot A''(\theta).\]

Proof sketch: The log-likelihood is \(\ell(\theta; y) = \theta y - A(\theta) + \text{const}\). The score is \(\frac{\partial \ell}{\partial \theta} = y - A'(\theta)\). Setting \(\mathbb{E}[\text{score}] = 0\) gives \(\mathbb{E}[y] = A'(\theta)\). The variance follows from the Hessian of \(\ell\).

These moment relations are powerful: they immediately connect the natural parameter \(\theta\) to the mean \(\mu\) and relate the mean to the variance through \(A''(\theta)\).

14.5 Logistic regression: Bernoulli response

14.5.1 Model and likelihood

Logistic regression models a binary response \(y_i \in \{0, 1\}\) as a Bernoulli trial: \[y_i \mid \pi_i \sim \text{Bernoulli}(\pi_i),\] where \(\pi_i = \Pr(y_i = 1 \mid \mathbf{x}_i)\) is the success probability.

The Bernoulli distribution has mean \(\mu_i = \pi_i\) and variance \(\pi_i(1 - \pi_i)\). The cumulant function is \(A(\theta) = \log(1 + e^\theta)\), so the canonical link is the logit: \[g(\pi_i) = \log\frac{\pi_i}{1 - \pi_i} = \eta_i = \mathbf{x}_i^\top \boldsymbol{\beta}.\]

The inverse logit or logistic function recovers the probability: \[\pi_i = \sigma(\eta_i) = \frac{1}{1 + e^{-\eta_i}}.\]

The likelihood for \(N\) independent observations is \[p(D \mid \boldsymbol{\beta}) = \prod_{i=1}^{N} \pi_i^{y_i} (1 - \pi_i)^{1 - y_i},\] with log-likelihood \[\ell(\boldsymbol{\beta}; D) = \sum_{i=1}^{N} \left[ y_i \eta_i - \log(1 + e^{\eta_i}) \right].\]

14.5.2 Interpretation: log-odds and odds ratios

The logit transforms the probability \(\pi\) into the log-odds. The odds are \(\frac{\pi}{1-\pi}\), the ratio of success to failure probability. Logistic regression is linear on the log-odds scale: \[\log\frac{\pi_i}{1-\pi_i} = \beta_0 + \beta_1 x_{i,1} + \cdots.\]

A one-unit increase in \(x_j\) changes the log-odds by \(\beta_j\), or equivalently, multiplies the odds by \(e^{\beta_j}\). If \(\beta_1 = 0.5\), a one-unit increase in \(x_1\) multiplies the odds of success by \(e^{0.5} \approx 1.65\).

The change in probability is nonlinear: the same increase in \(x\) produces a larger change in \(\pi\) near \(\pi = 0.5\) and smaller changes near 0 or 1.

14.6 Poisson regression: Count response

14.6.1 Model and likelihood

Poisson regression models count data \(y_i \in \{0, 1, 2, \ldots\}\) as a Poisson distribution: \[y_i \mid \lambda_i \sim \text{Poisson}(\lambda_i),\] where \(\lambda_i > 0\) is the expected count or rate. Both mean and variance equal \(\lambda_i\): \(\mathbb{E}[y_i] = \mathrm{Var}(y_i) = \lambda_i\).

The cumulant function is \(A(\theta) = e^\theta\), and the canonical link is the log link: \[g(\lambda_i) = \log \lambda_i = \eta_i = \beta_0 + \beta_1 x_{i,1} + \cdots,\] so \(\lambda_i = e^{\eta_i}\).

The Poisson likelihood is \[p(D \mid \boldsymbol{\beta}) = \prod_{i=1}^{N} \frac{\lambda_i^{y_i} e^{-\lambda_i}}{y_i!},\] with log-likelihood \[\ell(\boldsymbol{\beta}; D) = \sum_{i=1}^{N} \left[ y_i \log \lambda_i - \lambda_i - \log(y_i!) \right].\]

14.6.2 Interpretation: exponential effects

The log link means that coefficients have exponential interpretation. A one-unit increase in \(x_j\) multiplies the expected count by \(e^{\beta_j}\): \[\frac{\lambda(x_j + 1)}{\lambda(x_j)} = e^{\beta_j}.\]

If \(\beta_1 = 0.3\), then increasing \(x_1\) by one unit multiplies the expected count by \(e^{0.3} \approx 1.35\), a 35% increase.

14.6.3 Offsets and exposure

In many applications, observations have different exposure times or bin widths. For example, detector data collected over different durations \(E_i\). The expected count scales with exposure: \(\lambda_i = E_i r_i\), where \(r_i\) is the event rate.

Using the log link, \[\log \lambda_i = \log(E_i r_i) = \log E_i + \log r_i = \log E_i + \eta_i,\] where \(\eta_i = \beta_0 + \beta_1 x_{i,1} + \cdots\). The term \(\log E_i\) is a known offset, a coefficient fixed at 1.

In Stan or R, specify log(exposure) + w0 + w1*x in the linear predictor; the software interprets the known contribution separately.

14.6.5 Overdispersion and negative binomial extension

A fundamental property of the Poisson is that variance equals the mean. In real count data, the variance often exceeds the mean—a phenomenon called overdispersion. Causes include:

  • Unobserved heterogeneity: different subgroups have different rates.
  • Clustering: observations within groups are not independent.
  • Extra-Poisson variability: a genuine excess beyond Poisson noise.

Overdispersion test: Compute \(\hat{\lambda}\) from fitted Poisson regression. If the sample variance of residuals \(\sum_i (y_i - \hat{\lambda}_i)^2 / (N - p)\) is much larger than the average fitted rate, overdispersion is present.

The negative binomial distribution extends Poisson regression by adding a shape parameter \(\phi\) that allows variance to exceed the mean: \[\mathrm{Var}(y_i) = \lambda_i + \frac{\lambda_i^2}{\phi}.\] As \(\phi \to \infty\), the negative binomial approaches Poisson. A negative-binomial GLM is fitted similarly to Poisson but with an additional dispersion parameter to estimate.

14.8 Bayesian inference via MCMC

Frequentist GLMs are typically fitted by maximum likelihood using iteratively reweighted least squares (IRLS). The Bayesian approach places a prior on the coefficients and explores the posterior via Markov chain Monte Carlo.

14.8.1 Logistic regression in Stan

The following Stan model fits logistic regression with weakly informative normal priors:

data {
  int<lower=1> N;
  vector[N] x;
  array[N] int<lower=0, upper=1> y;
}
parameters {
  real w0;
  real w1;
}
model {
  w0 ~ normal(0, 5);
  w1 ~ normal(0, 5);
  y ~ bernoulli_logit(w0 + w1 * x);
}
generated quantities {
  array[N] int y_rep;
  for (i in 1:N) {
    y_rep[i] = bernoulli_logit_rng(w0 + w1 * x[i]);
  }
}

Stan’s bernoulli_logit function is numerically stable; it computes the log-likelihood directly without forming \(\pi_i\) explicitly.

14.8.2 Poisson regression in Stan

data {
  int<lower=1> N;
  vector[N] x;
  array[N] int<lower=0> y;
}
parameters {
  real w0;
  real w1;
}
model {
  w0 ~ normal(0, 5);
  w1 ~ normal(0, 5);
  y ~ poisson_log(w0 + w1 * x);
}
generated quantities {
  array[N] int y_rep;
  for (i in 1:N) {
    y_rep[i] = poisson_log_rng(w0 + w1 * x[i]);
  }
}

The poisson_log function computes the Poisson likelihood with log link: given \(\eta = w_0 + w_1 x\), it increments log-probability by \(y \eta - e^\eta - \log(y!)\). The generated quantities block produces posterior predictive replicates for model checking.

14.9 Residual diagnostics and posterior predictive checks

14.9.1 Deviance and residuals

In a fitted GLM, the deviance is twice the log-likelihood difference between the saturated model (one parameter per observation) and the fitted model: \[D = 2 \sum_{i=1}^{N} \left[ \ell_{\text{sat}}(y_i) - \ell_{\text{fitted}}(\hat{\mu}_i, y_i) \right].\]

For logistic regression, this simplifies to a sum of log-likelihood comparisons; for Poisson, it involves Kullback–Leibler divergence between observed and fitted counts.

Pearson residuals scale differences by the estimated standard deviation: \[r_i^{\text{Pearson}} = \frac{y_i - \hat{\mu}_i}{\sqrt{\widehat{\mathrm{Var}}(y_i)}}.\]

In well-fitting models, Pearson residuals should be approximately standard normal. Large residuals flag individual observations that the model does not explain well.

14.9.2 Posterior predictive checks

In Bayesian inference, we can generate replicated data \(y_{\text{rep}}^{(s)}\) from the posterior predictive distribution for each MCMC draw \((s)\). By comparing summaries of observed data \(y\) to summaries of replicated data, we can assess whether the model is plausible:

  • Poisson: Check the proportion of zeros, the maximum count, the ratio of variance to mean.
  • Bernoulli: Check the total number of successes, the success fraction in subgroups, the patterns of runs.

If the observed summary is consistently in the tail of the replicated-data distribution, the model misses important structure.

14.10 Coefficient interpretation and prediction

14.10.1 Logistic regression coefficients

In logistic regression with predictor \(x_j\), a one-unit increase in \(x_j\) (holding others fixed) changes the odds by a factor of \(e^{\beta_j}\): - If \(\beta_j = 0.5\), the odds increase by a factor of \(e^{0.5} \approx 1.65\) (65% increase). - If \(\beta_j = -0.3\), the odds decrease by a factor of \(e^{-0.3} \approx 0.74\) (26% decrease).

The corresponding probability change depends on the current probability level. Near \(\pi = 0.5\) (maximum sensitivity), a one-unit change in \(x_j\) shifts \(\pi\) by approximately \(\beta_j / 4\). Near extreme probabilities, the shift is much smaller.

Always report uncertainty: credible intervals for \(\beta_j\) quantify the precision of the estimate. Transform endpoints through \(e^{\beta_j}\) to report odds ratios and their intervals.

14.10.2 Poisson regression coefficients

In Poisson regression, \(\beta_j\) is the change in log rate per unit increase in \(x_j\). The expected count is multiplied by \(e^{\beta_j}\) per unit increase: - If \(\beta_1 = 0.3\), a one-unit increase in \(x_1\) multiplies the expected count by \(e^{0.3} \approx 1.35\) (35% increase). - If \(\beta_2 = -0.2\), a one-unit increase in \(x_2\) multiplies the expected count by \(e^{-0.2} \approx 0.82\) (18% decrease).

These are relative effects: they describe multiplicative changes in the rate, not absolute changes. In a detector experiment, a 35% increase in count rate might be substantial or negligible depending on the baseline and the experimental goal.

14.10.3 Posterior predictive intervals

After fitting, we typically want to predict for new observations. For logistic regression, posterior draws of \((w_0, w_1)\) give posterior samples of \(\pi_* = \sigma(w_0 + w_1 x_*)\) at a new location \(x_*\). The credible interval for \(\pi_*\) quantifies uncertainty in the probability; a separate predictive interval addresses the binary outcome itself (\(y_* \in \{0, 1\}\) with some probability).

For Poisson regression, the posterior predictive interval for a new count \(y_*\) at \(x_*\) involves: 1. Drawing \((w_0^{(s)}, w_1^{(s)})\) from the posterior. 2. Computing \(\lambda_*^{(s)} = \exp(w_0^{(s)} + w_1^{(s)} x_*)\). 3. Drawing \(y_*^{(s)} \sim \text{Poisson}(\lambda_*^{(s)})\). 4. Summarizing the empirical distribution of \(y_*^{(s)}\) by quantiles (e.g., 2.5%, 50%, 97.5%).

The predictive interval is discrete and generally asymmetric, reflecting the discrete and skewed nature of Poisson noise.

14.11 Overdispersion illustration: simulated data with variance inflation

Show code 
set.seed(303)
N <- 100
x <- seq(0, 4, length.out = N)
lambda <- 2 + 3 * x

# Poisson: variance = mean
y_poisson <- rpois(N, lambda = lambda)

# Overdispersed: inflate variance by factor of 2
y_overdispersed <- sapply(lambda, function(mu) {
  nb_size <- mu / 1  # size parameter for negative binomial
  rnbinom(1, size = nb_size, mu = mu)
})

plot(x, y_poisson, pch = 19, col = "gray40", cex = 0.8,
     xlab = "x", ylab = "count", las = 1, ylim = c(0, 30))
points(x, y_overdispersed, pch = 1, col = "#b8123e", cex = 0.8)
lines(x, lambda, lwd = 2, col = "black")
legend("topleft", legend = c("Poisson (Var=mean)", "Overdispersed (Var>mean)", "true mean"),
       pch = c(19, 1, NA), lty = c(NA, NA, 1), col = c("gray40", "#b8123e", "black"),
       bty = "n")

14.12 Common pitfalls and best practices

Common mistakes:

  1. Ignoring distributional mismatch: Fitting Gaussian regression to counts or binary data leads to invalid predictions and misaligned uncertainty.

  2. Forgetting the offset: In exposure-weighted Poisson models, omitting the offset biases rate estimates and inflates uncertainty.

  3. Misinterpreting coefficients: Logistic coefficients are log-odds, not probability changes. Poisson coefficients are log-rates, not expected count changes.

  4. Reporting point estimates without credible intervals: GLM coefficients are estimated quantities; always include uncertainty via posterior quantiles.

  5. Confusing credible and predictive intervals: The credible interval for \(\mu\) (or \(\pi\), \(\lambda\)) reflects estimation uncertainty. The predictive interval for \(y\) also includes observation noise and is wider.

  6. Ignoring posterior predictive checks: A high likelihood value does not guarantee model adequacy. Generate replicated data and compare to observed summaries.

  7. Trusting diagnostics without thought: High \(\hat{R}\) and large ESS are necessary but not sufficient. Check trace plots, residual patterns, and posterior predictive checks for deeper assessment.

Remark. Best practices:

  • Center or standardize predictors before fitting to reduce collinearity and improve MCMC mixing. A unit increase in a centered \(x\) is meaningful and comparable across covariates.
  • Visualize the fitted curve with uncertainty bands overlaid on the data. This reveals whether the model captures the dominant patterns.
  • Use weakly informative priors that regularize without imposing strong prior beliefs. For coefficients on a standardized scale, \(\text{Normal}(0, 2)\) or \(\text{Normal}(0, 3)\) is reasonable.
  • Check trace plots for all parameters after sampling. Chains should mix well (no trending, not stuck, high autocorrelation).
  • Report both point and interval estimates and be explicit about the scale (log-odds, log-rate, probability, count).
  • Document the model specification (link function, prior, offset, if any) to enable reproducibility and interpretation.

14.13 Conclusion

Generalized linear models extend Bayesian regression to count, binary, and other non-Gaussian responses by:

  • Specifying a response distribution from the exponential family (Poisson, Bernoulli, etc.).
  • Connecting the mean to a linear predictor via a link function that respects parameter constraints.
  • Inferring the linear coefficients while propagating uncertainty through the posterior.

The three ingredients—random component, linear predictor, and link function—unify a diverse set of regression tasks under one framework. Logistic regression models probabilities; Poisson regression models rates and counts; gamma and inverse-Gaussian regressions model positive continuous data. MCMC and probabilistic programming allow flexible, fully Bayesian inference, with posterior checks guiding model refinement.

## Introduction: When linear regression fails

Classical Gaussian linear regression assumes that the response variable $y_i$ is continuous and approximately normally distributed with mean $\mu_i = f(x_i; \beta)$ and constant variance $\sigma^2$. This assumption works well for many physical measurements with small, additive noise.

However, in many experiments we encounter response variables that violate this assumption fundamentally:

- **Count data**: number of detected photons in a time window, decay events in a bin, or success counts in repeated trials. These are non-negative integers with variance proportional to the mean, not constant.
- **Binary outcomes**: detection (yes/no), selection success (pass/fail), or signal presence (present/absent). The mean must lie in $(0,1)$ to represent a probability.
- **Positive-only data**: time-to-event, energy, or rate parameters. The mean must be strictly positive.

Fitting a standard linear model $y_i \sim N(\beta_0 + \beta_1 x_i, \sigma^2)$ to such data produces:

- **Invalid predictions**: fitted means may fall outside the natural parameter space (e.g., negative counts or probabilities outside $[0,1]$).
- **Incorrect uncertainty quantification**: constant-variance assumptions badly misrepresent the variability structure of heteroscedastic responses.
- **Biased inference**: parameter estimates become inefficient and confidence intervals lose their intended coverage.

**Generalized linear models** extend regression to non-Gaussian responses by replacing the normality assumption with a member of the exponential family and introducing a **link function** that maps the linear predictor onto the valid parameter space.

## The three ingredients of a GLM

A generalized linear model comprises three interconnected components:

:::{.definition}
**Definition (GLM structure)**: A generalized linear model for observations $y_1, \ldots, y_N$ is specified by:

1. **Random component**: $y_i \mid \theta_i \sim p(y_i; \theta_i)$, where $p$ belongs to an exponential-family distribution parameterized by a natural parameter $\theta_i$.

2. **Systematic component**: a linear predictor
   $$\eta_i = \beta_0 + \beta_1 x_{i,1} + \cdots + \beta_p x_{i,p} = \mathbf{x}_i^\top \boldsymbol{\beta},$$
   where $\boldsymbol{\beta}$ is a vector of regression coefficients and $\mathbf{x}_i$ is the design vector for observation $i$.

3. **Link function**: a smooth, invertible function $g(\cdot)$ that relates the mean $\mu_i = \mathbb{E}[y_i \mid \mathbf{x}_i]$ to the linear predictor:
   $$g(\mu_i) = \eta_i, \quad \text{equivalently} \quad \mu_i = g^{-1}(\eta_i).$$
   The inverse link, $g^{-1}$, is called the **mean function**.
:::

The link function ensures that even though $\eta_i$ can take any real value, the predicted mean $\mu_i = g^{-1}(\eta_i)$ respects the constraints of the response distribution.

## The exponential family and canonical form

The exponential family is a large class of distributions sharing a common parameterization that unifies likelihood inference for GLMs.

:::{.definition}
**Definition (Exponential family canonical form)**: A probability distribution for $y$ belongs to the exponential family if it can be written as
$$p(y; \theta, \phi) = h(y, \phi) \exp\left(\frac{\theta y - A(\theta)}{\phi}\right),$$
where:
- $\theta$ is the **natural parameter** (or canonical parameter).
- $\phi > 0$ is the **dispersion parameter**, often related to variance.
- $A(\theta)$ is the **cumulant function** or **log-partition function**.
- $h(y, \phi)$ is the **base measure** (carries $y$-dependent normalization and $\phi$ terms not involving $\theta$).

The term $A(\theta)$ ensures the distribution integrates or sums to one.
:::

:::{.theorem}
**Theorem (Moment relations)**: For a distribution in exponential-family form,
$$\mathbb{E}[y] = A'(\theta), \quad \mathrm{Var}(y) = \phi \cdot A''(\theta).$$

**Proof sketch**: The log-likelihood is $\ell(\theta; y) = \theta y - A(\theta) + \text{const}$. The score is $\frac{\partial \ell}{\partial \theta} = y - A'(\theta)$. Setting $\mathbb{E}[\text{score}] = 0$ gives $\mathbb{E}[y] = A'(\theta)$. The variance follows from the Hessian of $\ell$.
:::

These moment relations are powerful: they immediately connect the natural parameter $\theta$ to the mean $\mu$ and relate the mean to the variance through $A''(\theta)$.

## Canonical links and the link-function table

A **canonical link** is the link function such that $g(\mu) = \theta$. For canonical links, the natural parameter equals the linear predictor directly, which simplifies both interpretation and computation (the sufficient statistics for $\boldsymbol{\beta}$ are linear in the data).

The following table summarizes the most common GLM families used in practice:

| Distribution | $A(\theta)$ | $\theta = g(\mu)$ (canonical) | $g^{-1}(\eta)$ (inverse link) | Constraint |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Gaussian** | $\theta^2/2$ | $\mu$ | $\eta$ | $\mu \in \mathbb{R}$ |
| **Bernoulli** | $\log(1 + e^\theta)$ | $\log\frac{\mu}{1-\mu}$ (logit) | $\frac{1}{1+e^{-\eta}}$ | $\mu \in (0,1)$ |
| **Poisson** | $e^\theta$ | $\log \mu$ | $e^\eta$ | $\mu > 0$ |
| **Gamma** | $-\log(-\theta)$ | $-1/\mu$ | $-1/\eta$ | $\mu > 0$ |

## Logistic regression: Bernoulli response

### Model and likelihood

Logistic regression models a **binary response** $y_i \in \{0, 1\}$ as a Bernoulli trial:
$$y_i \mid \pi_i \sim \text{Bernoulli}(\pi_i),$$
where $\pi_i = \Pr(y_i = 1 \mid \mathbf{x}_i)$ is the success probability.

The Bernoulli distribution has mean $\mu_i = \pi_i$ and variance $\pi_i(1 - \pi_i)$. The cumulant function is $A(\theta) = \log(1 + e^\theta)$, so the canonical link is the **logit**:
$$g(\pi_i) = \log\frac{\pi_i}{1 - \pi_i} = \eta_i = \mathbf{x}_i^\top \boldsymbol{\beta}.$$

The **inverse logit** or **logistic function** recovers the probability:
$$\pi_i = \sigma(\eta_i) = \frac{1}{1 + e^{-\eta_i}}.$$

The likelihood for $N$ independent observations is
$$p(D \mid \boldsymbol{\beta}) = \prod_{i=1}^{N} \pi_i^{y_i} (1 - \pi_i)^{1 - y_i},$$
with log-likelihood
$$\ell(\boldsymbol{\beta}; D) = \sum_{i=1}^{N} \left[ y_i \eta_i - \log(1 + e^{\eta_i}) \right].$$

### Interpretation: log-odds and odds ratios

The logit transforms the probability $\pi$ into the **log-odds**. The **odds** are $\frac{\pi}{1-\pi}$, the ratio of success to failure probability. Logistic regression is linear on the log-odds scale:
$$\log\frac{\pi_i}{1-\pi_i} = \beta_0 + \beta_1 x_{i,1} + \cdots.$$

A one-unit increase in $x_j$ changes the log-odds by $\beta_j$, or equivalently, **multiplies the odds by $e^{\beta_j}$**. If $\beta_1 = 0.5$, a one-unit increase in $x_1$ multiplies the odds of success by $e^{0.5} \approx 1.65$.

The change in probability is nonlinear: the same increase in $x$ produces a larger change in $\pi$ near $\pi = 0.5$ and smaller changes near 0 or 1.

## Poisson regression: Count response

### Model and likelihood

Poisson regression models **count data** $y_i \in \{0, 1, 2, \ldots\}$ as a Poisson distribution:
$$y_i \mid \lambda_i \sim \text{Poisson}(\lambda_i),$$
where $\lambda_i > 0$ is the expected count or rate. Both mean and variance equal $\lambda_i$: $\mathbb{E}[y_i] = \mathrm{Var}(y_i) = \lambda_i$.

The cumulant function is $A(\theta) = e^\theta$, and the canonical link is the **log link**:
$$g(\lambda_i) = \log \lambda_i = \eta_i = \beta_0 + \beta_1 x_{i,1} + \cdots,$$
so $\lambda_i = e^{\eta_i}$.

The Poisson likelihood is
$$p(D \mid \boldsymbol{\beta}) = \prod_{i=1}^{N} \frac{\lambda_i^{y_i} e^{-\lambda_i}}{y_i!},$$
with log-likelihood
$$\ell(\boldsymbol{\beta}; D) = \sum_{i=1}^{N} \left[ y_i \log \lambda_i - \lambda_i - \log(y_i!) \right].$$

### Interpretation: exponential effects

The log link means that coefficients have **exponential interpretation**. A one-unit increase in $x_j$ multiplies the expected count by $e^{\beta_j}$:
$$\frac{\lambda(x_j + 1)}{\lambda(x_j)} = e^{\beta_j}.$$

If $\beta_1 = 0.3$, then increasing $x_1$ by one unit multiplies the expected count by $e^{0.3} \approx 1.35$, a 35\% increase.

### Offsets and exposure

In many applications, observations have different **exposure times** or **bin widths**. For example, detector data collected over different durations $E_i$. The expected count scales with exposure: $\lambda_i = E_i r_i$, where $r_i$ is the event rate.

Using the log link,
$$\log \lambda_i = \log(E_i r_i) = \log E_i + \log r_i = \log E_i + \eta_i,$$
where $\eta_i = \beta_0 + \beta_1 x_{i,1} + \cdots$. The term $\log E_i$ is a known **offset**, a coefficient fixed at 1.

In Stan or R, specify `log(exposure) + w0 + w1*x` in the linear predictor; the software interprets the known contribution separately.

### Overdispersion and negative binomial extension

A fundamental property of the Poisson is that **variance equals the mean**. In real count data, the variance often exceeds the mean—a phenomenon called **overdispersion**. Causes include:

- Unobserved heterogeneity: different subgroups have different rates.
- Clustering: observations within groups are not independent.
- Extra-Poisson variability: a genuine excess beyond Poisson noise.

:::{.proposition}
**Overdispersion test**: Compute $\hat{\lambda}$ from fitted Poisson regression. If the sample variance of residuals $\sum_i (y_i - \hat{\lambda}_i)^2 / (N - p)$ is much larger than the average fitted rate, overdispersion is present.
:::

The **negative binomial** distribution extends Poisson regression by adding a shape parameter $\phi$ that allows variance to exceed the mean:
$$\mathrm{Var}(y_i) = \lambda_i + \frac{\lambda_i^2}{\phi}.$$
As $\phi \to \infty$, the negative binomial approaches Poisson. A negative-binomial GLM is fitted similarly to Poisson but with an additional dispersion parameter to estimate.

## Bayesian inference via MCMC

Frequentist GLMs are typically fitted by maximum likelihood using iteratively reweighted least squares (IRLS). The Bayesian approach places a prior on the coefficients and explores the posterior via Markov chain Monte Carlo.

### Logistic regression in Stan

The following Stan model fits logistic regression with weakly informative normal priors:

```stan
data {
  int<lower=1> N;
  vector[N] x;
  array[N] int<lower=0, upper=1> y;
}
parameters {
  real w0;
  real w1;
}
model {
  w0 ~ normal(0, 5);
  w1 ~ normal(0, 5);
  y ~ bernoulli_logit(w0 + w1 * x);
}
generated quantities {
  array[N] int y_rep;
  for (i in 1:N) {
    y_rep[i] = bernoulli_logit_rng(w0 + w1 * x[i]);
  }
}
```

Stan's `bernoulli_logit` function is numerically stable; it computes the log-likelihood directly without forming $\pi_i$ explicitly.

### Poisson regression in Stan

```stan
data {
  int<lower=1> N;
  vector[N] x;
  array[N] int<lower=0> y;
}
parameters {
  real w0;
  real w1;
}
model {
  w0 ~ normal(0, 5);
  w1 ~ normal(0, 5);
  y ~ poisson_log(w0 + w1 * x);
}
generated quantities {
  array[N] int y_rep;
  for (i in 1:N) {
    y_rep[i] = poisson_log_rng(w0 + w1 * x[i]);
  }
}
```

The `poisson_log` function computes the Poisson likelihood with log link: given $\eta = w_0 + w_1 x$, it increments log-probability by $y \eta - e^\eta - \log(y!)$. The generated quantities block produces posterior predictive replicates for model checking.

## Residual diagnostics and posterior predictive checks

### Deviance and residuals

In a fitted GLM, the **deviance** is twice the log-likelihood difference between the saturated model (one parameter per observation) and the fitted model:
$$D = 2 \sum_{i=1}^{N} \left[ \ell_{\text{sat}}(y_i) - \ell_{\text{fitted}}(\hat{\mu}_i, y_i) \right].$$

For logistic regression, this simplifies to a sum of log-likelihood comparisons; for Poisson, it involves Kullback–Leibler divergence between observed and fitted counts.

**Pearson residuals** scale differences by the estimated standard deviation:
$$r_i^{\text{Pearson}} = \frac{y_i - \hat{\mu}_i}{\sqrt{\widehat{\mathrm{Var}}(y_i)}}.$$

In well-fitting models, Pearson residuals should be approximately standard normal. Large residuals flag individual observations that the model does not explain well.

### Posterior predictive checks

In Bayesian inference, we can generate **replicated data** $y_{\text{rep}}^{(s)}$ from the posterior predictive distribution for each MCMC draw $(s)$. By comparing summaries of observed data $y$ to summaries of replicated data, we can assess whether the model is plausible:

- **Poisson**: Check the proportion of zeros, the maximum count, the ratio of variance to mean.
- **Bernoulli**: Check the total number of successes, the success fraction in subgroups, the patterns of runs.

If the observed summary is consistently in the tail of the replicated-data distribution, the model misses important structure.

## Coefficient interpretation and prediction

### Logistic regression coefficients

In logistic regression with predictor $x_j$, a one-unit increase in $x_j$ (holding others fixed) changes the **odds** by a factor of $e^{\beta_j}$:
- If $\beta_j = 0.5$, the odds increase by a factor of $e^{0.5} \approx 1.65$ (65\% increase).
- If $\beta_j = -0.3$, the odds decrease by a factor of $e^{-0.3} \approx 0.74$ (26\% decrease).

The corresponding **probability** change depends on the current probability level. Near $\pi = 0.5$ (maximum sensitivity), a one-unit change in $x_j$ shifts $\pi$ by approximately $\beta_j / 4$. Near extreme probabilities, the shift is much smaller.

Always report uncertainty: credible intervals for $\beta_j$ quantify the precision of the estimate. Transform endpoints through $e^{\beta_j}$ to report odds ratios and their intervals.

### Poisson regression coefficients

In Poisson regression, $\beta_j$ is the change in log rate per unit increase in $x_j$. The expected count is multiplied by $e^{\beta_j}$ per unit increase:
- If $\beta_1 = 0.3$, a one-unit increase in $x_1$ multiplies the expected count by $e^{0.3} \approx 1.35$ (35\% increase).
- If $\beta_2 = -0.2$, a one-unit increase in $x_2$ multiplies the expected count by $e^{-0.2} \approx 0.82$ (18\% decrease).

These are **relative effects**: they describe multiplicative changes in the rate, not absolute changes. In a detector experiment, a 35\% increase in count rate might be substantial or negligible depending on the baseline and the experimental goal.

### Posterior predictive intervals

After fitting, we typically want to predict for new observations. For **logistic regression**, posterior draws of $(w_0, w_1)$ give posterior samples of $\pi_* = \sigma(w_0 + w_1 x_*)$ at a new location $x_*$. The credible interval for $\pi_*$ quantifies uncertainty in the **probability**; a separate predictive interval addresses the binary outcome itself ($y_* \in \{0, 1\}$ with some probability).

For **Poisson regression**, the posterior predictive interval for a new count $y_*$ at $x_*$ involves:
1. Drawing $(w_0^{(s)}, w_1^{(s)})$ from the posterior.
2. Computing $\lambda_*^{(s)} = \exp(w_0^{(s)} + w_1^{(s)} x_*)$.
3. Drawing $y_*^{(s)} \sim \text{Poisson}(\lambda_*^{(s)})$.
4. Summarizing the empirical distribution of $y_*^{(s)}$ by quantiles (e.g., 2.5\%, 50\%, 97.5\%).

The predictive interval is discrete and generally asymmetric, reflecting the discrete and skewed nature of Poisson noise.


## Common pitfalls and best practices

:::{.note}
**Common mistakes**:

1. **Ignoring distributional mismatch**: Fitting Gaussian regression to counts or binary data leads to invalid predictions and misaligned uncertainty.

2. **Forgetting the offset**: In exposure-weighted Poisson models, omitting the offset biases rate estimates and inflates uncertainty.

3. **Misinterpreting coefficients**: Logistic coefficients are log-odds, not probability changes. Poisson coefficients are log-rates, not expected count changes.

4. **Reporting point estimates without credible intervals**: GLM coefficients are estimated quantities; always include uncertainty via posterior quantiles.

5. **Confusing credible and predictive intervals**: The credible interval for $\mu$ (or $\pi$, $\lambda$) reflects estimation uncertainty. The predictive interval for $y$ also includes observation noise and is wider.

6. **Ignoring posterior predictive checks**: A high likelihood value does not guarantee model adequacy. Generate replicated data and compare to observed summaries.

7. **Trusting diagnostics without thought**: High $\hat{R}$ and large ESS are necessary but not sufficient. Check trace plots, residual patterns, and posterior predictive checks for deeper assessment.
:::

:::{.remark}
**Best practices**:

- **Center or standardize predictors** before fitting to reduce collinearity and improve MCMC mixing. A unit increase in a centered $x$ is meaningful and comparable across covariates.
- **Visualize the fitted curve** with uncertainty bands overlaid on the data. This reveals whether the model captures the dominant patterns.
- **Use weakly informative priors** that regularize without imposing strong prior beliefs. For coefficients on a standardized scale, $\text{Normal}(0, 2)$ or $\text{Normal}(0, 3)$ is reasonable.
- **Check trace plots** for all parameters after sampling. Chains should mix well (no trending, not stuck, high autocorrelation).
- **Report both point and interval estimates** and be explicit about the scale (log-odds, log-rate, probability, count).
- **Document the model specification** (link function, prior, offset, if any) to enable reproducibility and interpretation.
:::

## Conclusion

Generalized linear models extend Bayesian regression to count, binary, and other non-Gaussian responses by:

- Specifying a response distribution from the exponential family (Poisson, Bernoulli, etc.).
- Connecting the mean to a linear predictor via a link function that respects parameter constraints.
- Inferring the linear coefficients while propagating uncertainty through the posterior.

The three ingredients—random component, linear predictor, and link function—unify a diverse set of regression tasks under one framework. Logistic regression models probabilities; Poisson regression models rates and counts; gamma and inverse-Gaussian regressions model positive continuous data. MCMC and probabilistic programming allow flexible, fully Bayesian inference, with posterior checks guiding model refinement.
