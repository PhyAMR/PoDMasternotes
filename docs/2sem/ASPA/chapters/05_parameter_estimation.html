6.1 Introduction

Parameter estimation lies at the heart of Bayesian inference. Once we observe data, we want to infer the unknown parameters that generated it. Unlike frequentist approaches that treat parameters as fixed but unknown, the Bayesian framework treats parameters as random variables described by probability distributions. This chapter synthesizes three core topics: how to estimate parameters using loss functions, how to quantify uncertainty via credible intervals, and how to make predictions about future observations.

The structure follows a natural progression: we begin with the Bayesian estimation problem and loss functions, explore how to characterize posterior shapes and construct intervals, then move to prediction and compare with frequentist confidence intervals.

6.2 The Bayesian estimation problem

Bayesian inference proceeds through Bayes’ theorem: \[ p(\theta \mid D, H) = \frac{p(D \mid \theta, H) \, p(\theta \mid H)}{p(D \mid H)}, \] where - \(p(D \mid \theta, H)\) is the likelihood: the probability of observing data \(D\) under parameter \(\theta\) and model \(H\), - \(p(\theta \mid H)\) is the prior: our belief about \(\theta\) before seeing data, - \(p(\theta \mid D, H)\) is the posterior: our updated belief after data, - \(p(D \mid H) = \int p(D \mid \theta, H) p(\theta \mid H) d\theta\) is the evidence (normalizing constant).

The posterior distribution contains all information about \(\theta\) given the data. Once we have it, we face a practical question: should we quote a point estimate or a full distribution?

6.3 Point estimation under loss functions

The Bayesian answer to point estimation is principled: choose an estimate by minimizing expected loss given the data. Different loss functions lead to different estimators.

Given data \(D\), the Bayesian estimator is \[ \hat{\theta}(D) = \arg\min_a E[L(a, \theta) \mid D], \] where \(L(a, \theta)\) is a loss function penalizing how far the estimate \(a\) is from the true parameter \(\theta\).

6.3.1 Three canonical loss functions

1. Squared loss and the posterior mean

\[ L(a, \theta) = (a - \theta)^2 \quad \Rightarrow \quad \hat{\theta}_{\text{LMS}} = E[\theta \mid D] \]

Minimizing squared loss yields the posterior mean (Least Mean Square estimator). Completing the square: \[ E[(a - \theta)^2 \mid D] = (a - E[\theta \mid D])^2 + \text{Var}(\theta \mid D). \] The posterior mean uses the full posterior, is invariant under reparametrization, but can be pulled by heavy tails.

2. Absolute loss and the posterior median

\[ L(a, \theta) = |a - \theta| \quad \Rightarrow \quad \hat{\theta}_{\text{med}} = \text{median}(\theta \mid D) \]

The posterior median is more robust to outliers than the mean and divides the posterior into equal probability masses.

3. 0-1 loss and the maximum a posteriori (MAP)

\[ L(a, \theta) = \begin{cases} 0 & \text{if } a = \theta \\ 1 & \text{if } a \neq \theta \end{cases} \quad \Rightarrow \quad \hat{\theta}_{\text{MAP}} = \arg\max_\theta p(\theta \mid D) \]

The MAP is the posterior mode — computationally convenient but uses only local information around the peak. Critically, MAP is not invariant under reparametrization: if we change variables \(\phi = g(\theta)\), the mode of \(p(\phi \mid D)\) generally does not equal \(g(\hat{\theta}_{\text{MAP}})\).

6.4 Asymmetric posteriors: MAP vs mean vs median

When the posterior is sharply peaked and symmetric (e.g., Gaussian), the MAP, mean, and median coincide. However, realistic posteriors are often skewed or multimodal.

Bernoulli parameter estimation with small samples

Suppose we toss a coin \(n = 10\) times and observe \(k = 3\) heads. Using a uniform prior \(\text{Beta}(1, 1)\), the posterior is \(\text{Beta}(1 + 3, 1 + 7) = \text{Beta}(4, 8)\).

Show code 
p <- seq(0, 1, length.out = 1000)
posterior <- dbeta(p, shape1 = 4, shape2 = 8)

# Compute statistics
p_map <- (4 - 1) / (4 + 8 - 2)
p_mean <- 4 / (4 + 8)
p_median <- qbeta(0.5, 4, 8)

plot(p, posterior, type = "l", lwd = 2.5, col = "#d62728",
     main = "Asymmetric Beta(4,8) Posterior",
     xlab = expression(p), ylab = "Density")
abline(v = p_map, col = "#2ca02c", lwd = 2, lty = 2)
abline(v = p_median, col = "#1f77b4", lwd = 2, lty = 2)
abline(v = p_mean, col = "#9467bd", lwd = 2, lty = 2)
legend("topright", legend = c("Posterior", "MAP", "Median", "Mean"),
       col = c("#d62728", "#2ca02c", "#1f77b4", "#9467bd"),
       lwd = c(2.5, 2, 2, 2), lty = c(1, 2, 2, 2), bty = "n")
grid(col = "grey90")

Here, \(\hat{p}_{\text{MAP}} \approx 0.27\), \(\text{median} \approx 0.31\), and \(E[p \mid D] \approx 0.33\). The mean is pulled toward 0.5 (the neutral Beta(1,1) prior), while the MAP stays closer to the data.

A Bernoulli parameter has prior \(p \sim \text{Beta}(1/2, 1/2)\). You observe \(n = 1\) trial and it is a success. Compute the posterior mean and the MAP, if it exists. Why are they different?

Solution. Conjugate updating gives \[p \mid D \sim \text{Beta}\!\left(\tfrac{1}{2} + 1,\ \tfrac{1}{2}\right) = \text{Beta}\!\left(\tfrac{3}{2},\ \tfrac{1}{2}\right).\] The posterior mean is the standard Beta mean, \[E[p \mid D] = \frac{3/2}{3/2 + 1/2} = \frac{3}{4}.\] The interior MAP formula \(\hat p_{\text{MAP}} = (\alpha - 1)/(\alpha + \beta - 2)\) requires both shape parameters greater than \(1\). Here the second shape is \(1/2 < 1\), so the posterior density \(p^{1/2}(1-p)^{-1/2}\) diverges as \(p \to 1\) and the MAP sits at the boundary \(\hat p_{\text{MAP}} = 1\). The mean averages over the full posterior, while the MAP just tracks the local maximum — and in this case the maximum is a boundary singularity. This is a classic case where the MAP is a misleading point summary.

6.4.1 The reparametrization invariance problem

This is a critical issue for MAP estimation. Consider the logit transformation \(\phi = \log\frac{p}{1-p}\), so \(p = \frac{e^\phi}{1 + e^\phi}\).

When we change variables, the Jacobian enters the posterior density: \[ p(\phi \mid D) = p(p \mid D) \left\vert \frac{dp}{d\phi} \right\vert = p(p \mid D) \cdot p(1-p). \] This Jacobian modifies the exponents in the likelihood, so \(\arg\max_\phi p(\phi \mid D) \neq g(\arg\max_p p(p \mid D))\) unless the Jacobian is constant.

The posterior mean and median are invariant under reparametrization because they are defined via integration, not pointwise optimization. This makes them more principled choices.

6.5 Reliability: Gaussian approximation and Laplace’s method

For large samples or sharply peaked posteriors, we can approximate the posterior as Gaussian using a Taylor expansion around the MAP.

Laplace’s method: If the posterior is sharply unimodal around \(\hat{\theta}_{\text{MAP}}\), then \[ p(\theta \mid D, H) \approx N(\hat{\theta}_{\text{MAP}}, C), \] where the covariance matrix is \[ C = H^{-1}, \quad H_{ij} = -\frac{\partial^2 \ln p(\theta \mid D)}{\partial \theta_i \partial \theta_j}\bigg|_{\theta = \hat{\theta}_{\text{MAP}}} \] is the negative Hessian of the log-posterior.

The uncertainty in each parameter is \(\sigma_i = \sqrt{C_{ii}}\).

6.5.1 When is Laplace reliable?

The Gaussian approximation works well when: - The posterior is unimodal and symmetric. - The sample size is large (CLT effect). - Parameters stay away from natural boundaries.

It breaks down when: - Posterior is broad, skewed, or multimodal. - Data are sparse (small \(n\), far from the CLT regime). - Parameters are bounded (e.g., \(p \in [0,1]\), \(\sigma^2 > 0\)).

Bernoulli reliability

For the Bernoulli likelihood with a Beta prior, the log-posterior is \[ L(p) = (a-1) \ln p + (b-1) \ln(1-p), \] where \(a = \alpha + k\) and \(b = \beta + n - k\).

The second derivative at the MAP is \[ L''(\hat{p}_{\text{MAP}}) = -\frac{(a+b-2)^2}{(a-1)(b-1)}. \]

The uncertainty scales as \(\sigma \sim (n + \alpha + \beta)^{-1/2}\). For small \(n\) or extreme counts, the quadratic approximation is poor because the Beta posterior has heavy tails.

6.6 Credible intervals

When the posterior is broad or asymmetric, a single point estimate with symmetric error bars is misleading. Instead, we summarize the posterior with credible intervals: regions containing a specified probability mass.

A credible interval \([\theta^-, \theta^+]\) at credibility level \(\gamma\) satisfies \[ P(\theta \in [\theta^-, \theta^+] \mid D, H) = \gamma. \] The posterior probability that \(\theta\) lies in this interval is \(\gamma\), conditioned on the observed data.

6.6.1 Central credible intervals

The central (or equal-tail) credible interval places equal probability mass in each tail: \[ \int_{-\infty}^{\theta^-} p(\theta \mid D) d\theta = \int_{\theta^+}^{\infty} p(\theta \mid D) d\theta = \frac{1-\gamma}{2}. \]

The endpoints are quantiles: \(\theta^\pm = F^{-1}\left(\frac{1 \pm \gamma}{2}\right)\), where \(F\) is the CDF of the posterior.

For a Gaussian posterior with mean \(\mu\) and std \(\sigma\), the 68%, 95%, and 99.7% central intervals are the familiar \(\mu \pm 1\sigma\), \(\mu \pm 1.96\sigma\), and \(\mu \pm 3\sigma\).

6.6.2 Highest Posterior Density (HPD) intervals

A Highest Posterior Density (HPD) interval at credibility level \(\gamma\) is the smallest-width region containing probability mass \(\gamma\) with the highest posterior density. Equivalently, \[ \text{HPD}_\gamma = \{\theta : p(\theta \mid D) \geq k_\gamma\}, \] where \(k_\gamma\) is the largest constant such that \(\int_{\text{HPD}_\gamma} p(\theta \mid D) d\theta = \gamma\).

Properties: - Every point inside the HPD has higher density than every point outside. - All points in the HPD have posterior density \(\geq k_\gamma\). - For symmetric distributions, HPD coincides with the central interval. - For asymmetric or multimodal posteriors, the HPD can be disconnected. - HPD is the minimum-width interval.

6.6.3 HPD construction by threshold

The practical algorithm is simple: sort posterior density values in descending order, accumulate probability mass until reaching \(\gamma\), and find the density threshold \(k_\gamma\) where this occurs. Then the HPD is the set of all \(\theta\) with \(p(\theta \mid D) \geq k_\gamma\).

Show code 
x <- seq(0, 1, length.out = 2000)
dens <- 0.5 * dbeta(x, 2, 8) + 0.5 * dbeta(x, 8, 2)

gamma <- 0.80
dx <- x[2] - x[1]
dens_sorted <- sort(dens, decreasing = TRUE)
cum_prob <- cumsum(dens_sorted) * dx
k_gamma <- dens_sorted[which(cum_prob >= gamma)[1]]

# Central interval (for comparison)
cum_dens <- cumsum(dens) * dx
cum_dens_norm <- cum_dens / max(cum_dens)
idx_central <- which(cum_dens_norm <= (1 + gamma)/2 & cum_dens_norm >= (1 - gamma)/2)

plot(x, dens, type = "l", lwd = 2.5, col = "#1f77b4",
     main = paste0("HPD vs Central Interval (gamma = ", gamma, ")"),
     xlab = expression(theta), ylab = "Posterior density")

        col = rgb(1, 0, 0, 0.3), border = NA)

6.8 Posterior predictive distribution

Having estimated \(\theta\), we often want to predict a future observation \(x_{\text{new}}\).

The posterior predictive distribution integrates over parameter uncertainty: \[ p(x_{\text{new}} \mid D) = \int p(x_{\text{new}} \mid \theta) \, p(\theta \mid D) \, d\theta. \] This is the probability distribution for a new observation, having learned \(\theta\) from data \(D\). No point estimate is needed; we marginalize over the posterior.

6.8.1 Prediction vs plug-in estimation

A naive approach is to plug in the point estimate \(\hat{\theta}\) (e.g., the posterior mean): \[ p_{\text{plug-in}}(x_{\text{new}} \mid D) \approx p(x_{\text{new}} \mid \hat{\theta}). \]

This ignores parameter uncertainty, which can be substantial with sparse data.

6.8.2 Worked example: Poisson rate with zero counts

Suppose we observe zero events in \(n = 5\) time intervals: \(x_1 = \cdots = x_5 = 0\), so \(y = \sum_i x_i = 0\).

With a uniform prior \(\text{Gamma}(1, 0)\) (improper), the posterior is \(\lambda \mid D \sim \text{Gamma}(1, 5)\).

Plug-in estimate: \(\hat{\lambda}_{\text{MLE}} = y / n = 0\), so \(p(x_{\text{new}} = 1 \mid \hat{\lambda}) = 0\). This is unphysical: zero observed events do not mean events are impossible!

Posterior predictive: Integrating over the posterior, \[ p(x_{\text{new}} = 1 \mid D) = \int_0^\infty \frac{\lambda e^{-\lambda}}{1!} \cdot 5e^{-5\lambda} d\lambda = \int_0^\infty 5\lambda e^{-6\lambda} d\lambda = \frac{5}{36} \approx 0.139. \]

Even with zero observed counts, the posterior predicts a 14% chance of seeing an event in the next interval — a reasonable and physically sensible conclusion.

During an exposure of length \(T\), no events are observed. With a uniform prior on the Poisson rate \(\lambda \geq 0\), what is the posterior predictive probability of observing at least one event in a future exposure of the same length \(T\)?

Solution. With the improper uniform prior \(p(\lambda) \propto 1\) and zero observed counts, the posterior is \[p(\lambda \mid D) \propto e^{-\lambda T} \cdot 1 \quad\Rightarrow\quad \lambda \mid D \sim \text{Exp}(T).\] Conditional on \(\lambda\), the probability of zero future events in a fresh exposure of length \(T\) is \(e^{-\lambda T}\). Hence \[P(X_{\text{new}} = 0 \mid D) = \int_0^\infty e^{-\lambda T}\,T\,e^{-T\lambda}\,d\lambda = \int_0^\infty T\,e^{-2T\lambda}\,d\lambda = \frac{1}{2}.\] Therefore \(P(X_{\text{new}} \geq 1 \mid D) = 1/2\). Although no event was observed, the predictive probability of a future event is not zero — residual uncertainty in \(\lambda\) keeps the predictive distribution wide. This is the predictive cousin of the Rule of Three.

With prior \(p \sim \text{Beta}(\alpha, \beta)\), \(k\) successes are observed in \(n\) Bernoulli trials. What is the predictive probability that the next \(m\) trials are all failures?

Solution. The posterior is \(p \mid D \sim \text{Beta}(\alpha + k,\ \beta + n - k)\). Conditional on \(p\), the probability of \(m\) consecutive failures is \((1-p)^m\). The predictive probability is \[P(m\text{ failures} \mid D) = E\!\left[(1-p)^m \mid D\right].\] For \(p \sim \text{Beta}(a, b)\) one has \(E[(1-p)^m] = B(a, b + m)/B(a, b)\). Therefore \[P(m\text{ failures} \mid D) = \frac{B(\alpha + k,\ \beta + n - k + m)}{B(\alpha + k,\ \beta + n - k)}.\] This is not equal to \((1 - E[p \mid D])^m\) because the predictive integrates over residual uncertainty in \(p\) — a feature that the plug-in estimate ignores.

6.8.3 Variance decomposition

The predictive variance splits naturally into two sources: \[ \text{Var}(x_{\text{new}} \mid D) = E_\theta[\text{Var}(x_{\text{new}} \mid \theta) \mid D] + \text{Var}_\theta(E[x_{\text{new}} \mid \theta] \mid D). \] The first term is data noise (aleatoric uncertainty); the second is parameter uncertainty (epistemic uncertainty). The full predictive captures both.

6.9 Sequential learning and posterior updating

A key advantage of Bayesian inference is that learning is sequential: today’s posterior becomes tomorrow’s prior.

A coin has prior \(p \sim \text{Beta}(2, 2)\). In a first run, \(3\) successes and \(1\) failure are observed. In a second run, \(2\) successes and \(4\) failures are observed. Compute the final posterior both sequentially and in one step.

Solution. Sequentially, after the first run, \[p \mid D_1 \sim \text{Beta}(2 + 3,\ 2 + 1) = \text{Beta}(5, 3).\] Treating that as the new prior and updating on the second run, \[p \mid D_1, D_2 \sim \text{Beta}(5 + 2,\ 3 + 4) = \text{Beta}(7, 7).\] In one step, the combined data have \(5\) successes and \(5\) failures, so \[p \mid D \sim \text{Beta}(2 + 5,\ 2 + 5) = \text{Beta}(7, 7).\] The same posterior is obtained either way — conjugate updating is associative, which is just a restatement of the product rule.

Sequential coin tosses

Start with a Beta(1,1) uniform prior. After observing data, we compute the posterior. If we observe more data, the posterior becomes the new prior.

6.10 Frequentist confidence intervals: brief contrast

To highlight what is unique about Bayesian credible intervals, we briefly compare with frequentist confidence intervals.

A frequentist confidence interval is a random interval \(C(X)\) with coverage probability \(1 - \alpha\): \[ P(\theta \in C(X)) = 1 - \alpha, \] where the probability is over repeated sampling of data \(X \sim p(x \mid \theta)\) under a fixed but unknown \(\theta\).

Interpretation: In repeated experiments, \(100(1-\alpha)\%\) of the confidence intervals will contain the true \(\theta\).

Key differences:

Aspect Bayesian Credible Interval Frequentist Confidence Interval
Probability on Parameter Data
Conditioned on Observed data \(D\) Hypothetical repeated datasets
Interpretation \(P(\theta \in [a,b] \mid D) = \gamma\) In repeated experiments, \(100\gamma\%\) of intervals cover \(\theta\)
Reparametrization Invariant Not invariant

Once we observe data, the frequentist interval either contains \(\theta\) or it does not — there is no probability statement about the observed interval.

Define a Bayesian credible interval for a scalar parameter \(\theta\). How does its interpretation differ from a frequentist confidence interval?

Solution. A Bayesian credible interval \([a, b]\) at credibility level \(\gamma\) satisfies \[\int_a^b p(\theta \mid D)\,d\theta = \gamma.\] A central credible interval excludes equal posterior probability from each tail; a highest-posterior-density (HPD) interval is the smallest set containing posterior mass \(\gamma\) (it collects the highest-density region first).

Its interpretation is direct: conditional on the data, model, and prior, the parameter lies in \([a, b]\) with posterior probability \(\gamma\). A frequentist confidence interval instead has a repeated-sampling coverage interpretation: before seeing the data, the random procedure \(C(X)\) contains the fixed true \(\theta\) with probability \(\gamma\). After conditioning on the observed data, the random interval is fixed and either contains \(\theta\) or does not — no probability statement is attached to the realised interval.

6.11 One-sided credible intervals

For some problems, we care only about an upper or lower bound on the parameter. A one-sided credible interval \([0, \theta^*]\) at level \(\gamma\) satisfies \[ P(\theta \leq \theta^* \mid D) = \gamma. \]

6.11.1 Rule of Three

A famous result in statistics applies to rare-event detection. If you observe zero events in \(n\) trials under a Binomial model with unknown rate \(p\), the 95% upper credible bound (using a uniform Beta(1,1) prior) is approximately \[ p^* \approx \frac{3}{n}. \]

This is the “Rule of Three”: if you see zero occurrences in \(n\) trials, you can be 95% confident that the true rate is below \(3/n\).

Zero-count drug safety

If a drug is tested on 100 patients and zero adverse events are observed, the Rule of Three gives a 95% upper bound of \(3/100 = 0.03\) or 3%. We can be reasonably confident (in the Bayesian sense, given a weak prior) that the true adverse event rate is below 3%, even though we observed none.

This addresses the zero-count problem: the MLE would say the rate is exactly zero, which is overconfident. The Bayesian posterior hedges appropriately.

6.12 Choosing priors: conjugacy and regularization

A prior \(\pi(\theta)\) is conjugate to a likelihood \(p(x \mid \theta)\) if the posterior is in the same family as the prior: \[ p(\theta \mid x) \propto p(x \mid \theta) \pi(\theta) \in \text{same family as } \pi(\theta). \]

Conjugate priors are analytically tractable and allow simple sequential updating. They also naturally encode prior beliefs as “pseudo-data” or “pseudo-observations.”

6.12.1 Example: Beta-Binomial conjugacy

  • Prior: \(p \sim \text{Beta}(\alpha, \beta)\) with density \(\pi(p) \propto p^{\alpha-1}(1-p)^{\beta-1}\).
  • Likelihood: \(P(k \text{ heads} \mid p, n) \propto p^k (1-p)^{n-k}\).
  • Posterior: \(p \mid k, n \sim \text{Beta}(\alpha + k, \beta + n - k)\).

The hyperparameters have an intuitive interpretation: \(\alpha - 1\) and \(\beta - 1\) are pseudo-successes and pseudo-failures, representing prior pseudo-data.

6.12.2 Example: Gamma-Poisson conjugacy

  • Prior: \(\lambda \sim \text{Gamma}(\alpha, \beta)\) with density \(\pi(\lambda) \propto \lambda^{\alpha-1} e^{-\beta \lambda}\).
  • Likelihood: \(P(y \text{ counts} \mid \lambda, n) \propto \lambda^y e^{-n\lambda}\).
  • Posterior: \(\lambda \mid y, n \sim \text{Gamma}(\alpha + y, \beta + n)\).

Here, \(\alpha\) is a pseudo-count and \(\beta\) is a pseudo-exposure time.

6.13 Fisher information, Cramér–Rao, and the Jeffreys prior

The theory of parametric estimation rests on a few fundamental objects: the Fisher information matrix, which quantifies the curvature of the likelihood; the Cramér–Rao bound, which sets a lower limit on estimator variance; and the Jeffreys prior, which adapts to the geometry of the parameter space. This section develops these concepts in general, showing how they unify point estimation (Cramér–Rao), prior specification (Jeffreys), and asymptotic posterior behaviour (Bernstein–von Mises).

6.13.1 Score and Fisher Information

Given a model \(p(x \mid \theta)\) and an observation \(x\) (or dataset), the score vector is \[s(\theta; x) = \nabla_\theta \log p(x \mid \theta) = \frac{\partial}{\partial \theta} \log p(x \mid \theta).\]

The Fisher information matrix is the expected outer product of the score: \[\mathcal{I}(\theta) = \mathbb{E}_x\big[s(\theta;x)\,s(\theta;x)^{\top}\big],\] with \((i,j)\)-entry \(\mathcal{I}_{ij}(\theta) = \mathbb{E}[s_i(\theta;x) s_j(\theta;x)]\).

Equivalently, under regularity conditions, \[\mathcal{I}(\theta) = -\mathbb{E}_x\left[\nabla_\theta^2 \log p(x\mid\theta)\right] = -\mathbb{E}[H(\theta)],\] where \(H(\theta)\) is the Hessian of the log-likelihood.

Proof sketch for the equivalence: Differentiating \(\int p(x\mid\theta) dx = 1\) with respect to \(\theta\): \[\int \frac{\partial}{\partial\theta} p(x\mid\theta) dx = \int p(x\mid\theta) \cdot s(\theta;x) dx = \mathbb{E}[s(\theta;x)] = 0.\] Differentiating again and swapping derivatives and integration (regularity): \[\mathbb{E}\left[\frac{\partial s(\theta;x)}{\partial\theta}\right] + \mathbb{E}[s(\theta;x)^2] = 0,\] which gives \(\mathbb{E}[s(\theta;x)^2] = -\mathbb{E}[H(\theta)]\).

For an i.i.d. sample of size \(n\), the total score is the sum of individual scores, and the Fisher information scales linearly: \(\mathcal{I}_n(\theta) = n\,\mathcal{I}(\theta)\).

6.13.2 Reparametrization Formula

Suppose we reparameterize via a smooth bijection \(\psi = \phi(\theta)\) with Jacobian matrix \(J_{ij} = \partial\psi_i / \partial\theta_j\). Then the Fisher information with respect to \(\psi\) is \[\mathcal{I}_\psi(\psi) = J^\top \mathcal{I}_\theta(\theta) \, J,\] where \(J = \partial\phi(\theta) / \partial\theta\) evaluated at the appropriate \(\theta = \phi^{-1}(\psi)\).

Proof: By the chain rule, \(s_\psi(\psi; x) = J^\top s_\theta(\theta; x)\). Hence, \[\mathcal{I}_\psi(\psi) = \mathbb{E}[J^\top s_\theta s_\theta^\top J] = J^\top \mathcal{I}_\theta(\theta) J.\]

This reparametrization formula is the key to understanding why \(\sqrt{\det \mathcal{I}(\theta)}\) behaves like an invariant density element. Under a change of variables \(\psi = \phi(\theta)\), \[\sqrt{\det \mathcal{I}_\psi(\psi)} = \sqrt{\det(J^\top J)} \cdot \sqrt{\det \mathcal{I}_\theta(\theta)} = |\det J| \cdot \sqrt{\det \mathcal{I}_\theta(\theta)},\] which is precisely the Jacobian rule for transforming a density. This geometric insight motivates the Jeffreys prior.

6.13.3 Cramér–Rao Lower Bound

Let \(\hat\theta(X)\) be an unbiased estimator of \(\theta\) from an i.i.d. sample \(X = (X_1, \ldots, X_n)\). Then \[\mathrm{Cov}(\hat\theta) \succeq \mathcal{I}_n(\theta)^{-1} = \frac{1}{n} \mathcal{I}(\theta)^{-1},\] where \(\succeq\) denotes the positive semidefinite order (i.e., \(\mathrm{Cov}(\hat\theta) - \mathcal{I}_n(\theta)^{-1}\) is positive semidefinite).

Proof outline (scalar case): Write the unbiasedness condition as \[\mathbb{E}_\theta[\hat\theta - \theta] = 0.\] Differentiating with respect to \(\theta\) and invoking regularity (swapping derivative and expectation): \[\mathbb{E}_\theta\left[\frac{\partial}{\partial\theta}(\hat\theta - \theta) \right] + \mathbb{E}_\theta[s(\theta;X)] = 0.\] Since \(\mathbb{E}[s] = 0\), we have \[\mathbb{E}[s(\theta;X)] = -1.\] Wait, I need to be more careful; let me re-phrase. Consider \(\mathbb{E}[\hat\theta \cdot s(\theta)] = \theta \mathbb{E}[s(\theta)]\). Differentiating the unbiasedness condition \(\mathbb{E}[\hat\theta] = \theta\): \[\mathbb{E}\left[\frac{\partial \hat\theta}{\partial\theta}\right] = 1 \quad \text{(in scalar case)}.\] By Cauchy–Schwarz applied to \(\hat\theta \cdot s\) and rearranging, one recovers \(\mathrm{Var}(\hat\theta) \ge \mathcal{I}(θ)^{-1}\).

The Cramér–Rao bound sets a fundamental speed limit on estimation: no unbiased estimator can have variance smaller than this bound. Remarkably, the maximum likelihood estimator (MLE) achieves this bound asymptotically.

6.13.4 Asymptotic Normality of the MLE

Under standard regularity conditions, the MLE \(\hat\theta_{\mathrm{ML}}\) of \(\theta\) from a sample of size \(n\) satisfies \[\sqrt{n}\,(\hat\theta_{\mathrm{ML}} - \theta_*) \xrightarrow{d} \mathcal{N}\big(0,\,\mathcal{I}(\theta_*)^{-1}\big),\] where \(\theta_*\) is the true parameter and \(\mathcal{I}(\theta_*)\) is the Fisher information at the truth.

This result, whose proof relies on Tayor expansions of the score and regularity conditions, shows that for large \(n\) the MLE is approximately \(\mathcal{N}(\hat\theta_{\mathrm{ML}}, \mathcal{I}_n(\theta_*)^{-1})\). Since \(\mathcal{I}_n(\theta_*) = n \mathcal{I}(\theta_*)\), the variance shrinks as \(1/n\), asymptotically achieving the Cramér–Rao bound. For reference on the detailed conditions and proof, see van der Vaart (1998), Chapter 5.

6.13.5 The Jeffreys Prior

The Jeffreys prior is defined as \[p_J(\theta) \propto \sqrt{\det \mathcal{I}(\theta)},\] the square root of the determinant of the Fisher information matrix.

Intuitively, the Jeffreys prior allocates more prior mass to regions where the likelihood is less informative (where \(\mathcal{I}(\theta)\) is smaller) and less prior mass where the likelihood is highly peaked (where \(\mathcal{I}(\theta)\) is large). This gives an objective prior that “lets the data speak” in a geometric sense.

6.13.6 Reparametrisation Invariance of Jeffreys

If \(p_J(\theta) \propto \sqrt{\det \mathcal{I}_\theta(\theta)}\) is the Jeffreys prior in the \(\theta\) parameterization, and we reparametrize via \(\psi = \phi(\theta)\) with Jacobian \(J\), then the Jeffreys prior in the \(\psi\) parameterization satisfies \[p_J(\psi) \propto \sqrt{\det \mathcal{I}_\psi(\psi)} = \sqrt{\det(J^\top J)} \cdot \sqrt{\det \mathcal{I}_\theta(\theta)} = |\det J| \cdot \sqrt{\det \mathcal{I}_\theta(\theta)}.\]

Under the change of variables, the prior density transforms as \[p_J(\psi) \, d\psi = p_J(\theta)\,|\det J| \, d\theta,\] which shows that the Jeffreys prior is invariant under reparametrisation.

Significance: This is the defining property that distinguishes Jeffreys from a flat prior. A uniform prior \(p(\theta) \propto 1\) does not remain uniform after reparametrization; its density changes by the Jacobian. In contrast, the Jeffreys prior automatically adapts its form to remain “flat” in an information-geometric sense, independent of how we parameterize the model.

6.13.7 Examples of Jeffreys Priors

For a Bernoulli(\(p\)) model with a single observation \(x \in \{0,1\}\), the log-likelihood is \(x \log p + (1-x) \log(1-p)\). The Fisher information is \[\mathcal{I}(p) = \mathbb{E}\left[\left(\frac{\partial}{\partial p} \log p(x \mid p)\right)^2\right] = \frac{1}{p(1-p)}.\]

The Jeffreys prior is thus \[p_J(p) \propto \sqrt{\frac{1}{p(1-p)}} = p^{-1/2}(1-p)^{-1/2},\] which is the Beta\((1/2, 1/2)\) distribution.

Note: the uniform prior is Beta\((1,1)\), which depends on the choice of the \(p\) parameterization. If we instead parameterize via the log-odds \(\phi = \log(p/(1-p))\), the uniform prior in \(\phi\) is different from the reparametrized form of Beta\((1,1)\). But Beta\((1/2, 1/2)\) remains the same (up to the appropriate density form) under any reparametrization.

For Poisson(\(\lambda\)), the log-likelihood is \(x \log \lambda - \lambda\), and the Fisher information is \[\mathcal{I}(\lambda) = \frac{1}{\lambda}.\]

The Jeffreys prior is \[p_J(\lambda) \propto \lambda^{-1/2},\] an improper prior. However, any observed data (\(x > 0\)) yields a proper posterior, because the likelihood \(\lambda^x e^{-\lambda}\) combined with \(\lambda^{-1/2}\) gives \(\lambda^{x-1/2} e^{-\lambda}\), a Gamma distribution.

For a Poisson observation \(X \sim \text{Poisson}(\lambda)\), Jeffreys prior is \(\pi(\lambda) \propto \lambda^{-1/2}\). Let \(\phi = \sqrt\lambda\). Find the induced prior on \(\phi\).

Solution. Since \(\lambda = \phi^2\) and \(d\lambda/d\phi = 2\phi\), the change-of-variables rule gives \[\pi_\phi(\phi) = \pi_\lambda(\phi^2)\,\left|\frac{d\lambda}{d\phi}\right| \propto (\phi^2)^{-1/2}\cdot 2\phi = 2.\] The Jeffreys prior for the Poisson rate becomes flat in \(\phi = \sqrt\lambda\). This is the geometric content of Jeffreys: in the parameterisation that makes the Fisher information constant, the prior is uniform.

The Lévy distribution with location \(0\) and scale \(c > 0\) has density \[f(x \mid c) = \sqrt{\frac{c}{2\pi}}\,x^{-3/2}\exp\!\left(-\frac{c}{2x}\right), \qquad x > 0.\] Useful moments are \(E[1/X] = 1/c\) and \(E[1/X^2] = 3/c^2\). Compute the Jeffreys prior for \(c\).

Solution. The log-likelihood is \[\log f(x \mid c) = \tfrac{1}{2}\log c - \tfrac{1}{2}\log(2\pi) - \tfrac{3}{2}\log x - \frac{c}{2x}.\] Differentiating with respect to \(c\), \[\frac{\partial}{\partial c}\log f(x \mid c) = \frac{1}{2c} - \frac{1}{2x}.\] The Fisher information is therefore \[\mathcal{I}(c) = E\!\left[\left(\frac{1}{2c} - \frac{1}{2X}\right)^2\right] = \frac{1}{4c^2} - \frac{1}{2c}\,E\!\left[\frac{1}{X}\right] + \frac{1}{4}\,E\!\left[\frac{1}{X^2}\right] = \frac{1}{4c^2} - \frac{1}{2c^2} + \frac{3}{4c^2} = \frac{1}{2c^2}.\] Hence \[\pi_J(c) \propto \sqrt{\mathcal{I}(c)} = \sqrt{\frac{1}{2c^2}} \propto \frac{1}{c}, \qquad c > 0.\] This is the improper scale-invariant prior \(p(c)\,dc \propto d\log c\) — the canonical non-informative choice for a scale parameter.

With \(\sigma^2\) known, the log-likelihood for the mean \(\mu\) is proportional to \(-\frac{1}{2\sigma^2}(x - \mu)^2\). The Fisher information is \[\mathcal{I}(\mu) = \frac{1}{\sigma^2},\] a positive constant independent of \(\mu\). The Jeffreys prior is thus \[p_J(\mu) \propto 1,\] a uniform (improper) prior—the standard choice for the unknown mean when the scale is known.

With \(\mu\) known, the log-likelihood is \(-\frac{n}{2}\log\sigma^2 - \frac{1}{2\sigma^2}\sum(x - \mu)^2\). Differentiating twice with respect to \(\sigma^2\) gives \[\mathcal{I}(\sigma^2) = \frac{n}{2(\sigma^2)^2}.\]

The Jeffreys prior is \[p_J(\sigma^2) \propto \frac{1}{\sigma^2},\] or equivalently, \(p_J(\log \sigma) \propto 1\) (uniform on the log scale). This is the scale-invariant prior for variance, classical in Bayesian inference.

For the Gaussian with both \(\mu\) and \(\sigma^2\) unknown, the Fisher information matrix is \[\mathcal{I}(\mu, \sigma^2) = \begin{pmatrix} 1/\sigma^2 & 0 \\ 0 & 2/\sigma^4 \end{pmatrix},\] so \(\det \mathcal{I} = 2/\sigma^6\), and the Jeffreys prior is \[p_J(\mu, \sigma^2) \propto \sqrt{\frac{2}{\sigma^6}} = \frac{1}{\sigma^3}.\]

Equivalently, \(p_J(\mu, \sigma) \propto 1/\sigma^2\) in terms of the standard deviation.

Subtle point: The joint Jeffreys prior \(p_J(\mu, \sigma^2) \propto 1/\sigma^3\) does not equal the product of the marginal Jeffreys priors for \(\mu\) (uniform) and \(\sigma^2\) (proportional to \(1/\sigma^2\)), which would give \(p(\mu) \cdot p(\sigma^2) \propto 1/\sigma^2\). Many practitioners use this product-of-marginals prior and call it “Jeffreys,” but it is technically the reference prior or a compromise prior. The true joint Jeffreys prior is \(1/\sigma^3\). This distinction matters for hypothesis tests and Bayes factors.

6.13.8 Reference Priors

Remark. When the parameter dimension is high, computing or integrating over the Jeffreys prior becomes cumbersome. Reference priors (Bernardo, 1979; Berger, Bernardo & Sun, 2009) generalize the idea: they maximize expected Kullback–Leibler divergence between posterior and prior, thus minimizing the information contained in the prior itself. For one-parameter problems, reference priors coincide with Jeffreys; for multiparameter problems, they offer an alternative construction that often performs better in frequentist coverage. Reference priors are not invariant to reparametrization but are designed to be more “objective” in the sense of information loss.

6.13.9 The Bernstein–von Mises Theorem

Under standard regularity conditions, for large sample size \(n\), the posterior distribution \(p(\theta \mid X_1, \ldots, X_n)\) is approximately Gaussian, centered at the MLE \(\hat\theta_{\mathrm{ML}}\) with covariance given by the inverse Fisher information: \[p(\theta \mid X_1, \ldots, X_n) \approx \mathcal{N}\big(\hat\theta_{\mathrm{ML}},\,\mathcal{I}_n(\theta_*)^{-1}\big),\] provided the prior is continuous and positive at \(\theta_*\) (the true parameter). This holds independently of the choice of prior.

Interpretation and significance: The Bernstein–von Mises theorem is one of the deepest results in Bayesian asymptotics. It says that, given enough data, the posterior “forgets” the prior (as long as it is not zero at the truth) and concentrates around the MLE with the canonical frequentist covariance \(\mathcal{I}_n(\theta_*)^{-1}\). This explains why, asymptotically, Bayesian credible intervals and frequentist confidence intervals coincide: both converge to the same Gaussian limit. In other words, there is no fundamental conflict between Bayesian and frequentist inference in the large-sample regime—they merge. For a detailed proof, see van der Vaart (1998), Chapter 10.

6.13.10 Unifying Theme

Fisher information, Cramér–Rao, Jeffreys, and Bernstein–von Mises are tightly interwoven:

  • Cramér–Rao sets the performance standard for any unbiased estimator.
  • MLE asymptotics show that the MLE achieves this standard with variance \(\mathcal{I}_n(\theta)^{-1}\).
  • Jeffreys prior is constructed from \(\mathcal{I}(\theta)\), ensuring objective prior choice via reparametrization invariance.
  • Bernstein–von Mises guarantees that the posterior, regardless of the prior, concentrates at the MLE with covariance \(\mathcal{I}_n(\theta)^{-1}\).

Together, these results form the backbone of both frequentist and Bayesian asymptotic theory.

## Introduction

Parameter estimation lies at the heart of Bayesian inference. Once we observe data, we want to infer the unknown parameters that generated it. Unlike frequentist approaches that treat parameters as fixed but unknown, the Bayesian framework treats parameters as random variables described by probability distributions. This chapter synthesizes three core topics: how to estimate parameters using loss functions, how to quantify uncertainty via credible intervals, and how to make predictions about future observations.

The structure follows a natural progression: we begin with the Bayesian estimation problem and loss functions, explore how to characterize posterior shapes and construct intervals, then move to prediction and compare with frequentist confidence intervals.

## The Bayesian estimation problem

::: {.definition}
**Bayesian inference** proceeds through Bayes' theorem:
$$
p(\theta \mid D, H) = \frac{p(D \mid \theta, H) \, p(\theta \mid H)}{p(D \mid H)},
$$
where
- $p(D \mid \theta, H)$ is the **likelihood**: the probability of observing data $D$ under parameter $\theta$ and model $H$,
- $p(\theta \mid H)$ is the **prior**: our belief about $\theta$ before seeing data,
- $p(\theta \mid D, H)$ is the **posterior**: our updated belief after data,
- $p(D \mid H) = \int p(D \mid \theta, H) p(\theta \mid H) d\theta$ is the **evidence** (normalizing constant).
:::

The posterior distribution contains all information about $\theta$ given the data. Once we have it, we face a practical question: should we quote a point estimate or a full distribution?

## Point estimation under loss functions

The Bayesian answer to point estimation is principled: choose an estimate by **minimizing expected loss given the data**. Different loss functions lead to different estimators.

::: {.definition}
Given data $D$, the **Bayesian estimator** is
$$
\hat{\theta}(D) = \arg\min_a E[L(a, \theta) \mid D],
$$
where $L(a, \theta)$ is a loss function penalizing how far the estimate $a$ is from the true parameter $\theta$.
:::

### Three canonical loss functions

**1. Squared loss and the posterior mean**

$$
L(a, \theta) = (a - \theta)^2 \quad \Rightarrow \quad \hat{\theta}_{\text{LMS}} = E[\theta \mid D]
$$

Minimizing squared loss yields the **posterior mean** (Least Mean Square estimator). Completing the square:
$$
E[(a - \theta)^2 \mid D] = (a - E[\theta \mid D])^2 + \text{Var}(\theta \mid D).
$$
The posterior mean uses the full posterior, is invariant under reparametrization, but can be pulled by heavy tails.

**2. Absolute loss and the posterior median**

$$
L(a, \theta) = |a - \theta| \quad \Rightarrow \quad \hat{\theta}_{\text{med}} = \text{median}(\theta \mid D)
$$

The posterior median is more robust to outliers than the mean and divides the posterior into equal probability masses.

**3. 0-1 loss and the maximum a posteriori (MAP)**

$$
L(a, \theta) = \begin{cases} 0 & \text{if } a = \theta \\ 1 & \text{if } a \neq \theta \end{cases}
\quad \Rightarrow \quad \hat{\theta}_{\text{MAP}} = \arg\max_\theta p(\theta \mid D)
$$

The MAP is the **posterior mode** — computationally convenient but uses only local information around the peak. **Critically, MAP is not invariant under reparametrization**: if we change variables $\phi = g(\theta)$, the mode of $p(\phi \mid D)$ generally does not equal $g(\hat{\theta}_{\text{MAP}})$.

## Asymmetric posteriors: MAP vs mean vs median

When the posterior is sharply peaked and symmetric (e.g., Gaussian), the MAP, mean, and median coincide. However, realistic posteriors are often skewed or multimodal.

::: {.example}
**Bernoulli parameter estimation with small samples**

Suppose we toss a coin $n = 10$ times and observe $k = 3$ heads. Using a uniform prior $\text{Beta}(1, 1)$, the posterior is $\text{Beta}(1 + 3, 1 + 7) = \text{Beta}(4, 8)$.

```{r}
#| label: lst-asymmetric-posterior
#| lst-cap: "Asymmetric posterior"
#| fig-cap: "Beta(4,8) posterior (red) is skewed right. MAP (green), median (blue), and mean (purple) differ substantially."

p <- seq(0, 1, length.out = 1000)
posterior <- dbeta(p, shape1 = 4, shape2 = 8)

# Compute statistics
p_map <- (4 - 1) / (4 + 8 - 2)
p_mean <- 4 / (4 + 8)
p_median <- qbeta(0.5, 4, 8)

plot(p, posterior, type = "l", lwd = 2.5, col = "#d62728",
     main = "Asymmetric Beta(4,8) Posterior",
     xlab = expression(p), ylab = "Density")
abline(v = p_map, col = "#2ca02c", lwd = 2, lty = 2)
abline(v = p_median, col = "#1f77b4", lwd = 2, lty = 2)
abline(v = p_mean, col = "#9467bd", lwd = 2, lty = 2)
legend("topright", legend = c("Posterior", "MAP", "Median", "Mean"),
       col = c("#d62728", "#2ca02c", "#1f77b4", "#9467bd"),
       lwd = c(2.5, 2, 2, 2), lty = c(1, 2, 2, 2), bty = "n")
grid(col = "grey90")
```

Here, $\hat{p}_{\text{MAP}} \approx 0.27$, $\text{median} \approx 0.31$, and $E[p \mid D] \approx 0.33$. The mean is pulled toward 0.5 (the neutral Beta(1,1) prior), while the MAP stays closer to the data.
:::

::: {.example name="MAP at the boundary — Beta(1/2,1/2) prior (exam-style)"}
A Bernoulli parameter has prior $p \sim \text{Beta}(1/2, 1/2)$. You observe $n = 1$ trial and it is a success. Compute the posterior mean and the MAP, if it exists. Why are they different?

**Solution.** Conjugate updating gives
$$p \mid D \sim \text{Beta}\!\left(\tfrac{1}{2} + 1,\ \tfrac{1}{2}\right) = \text{Beta}\!\left(\tfrac{3}{2},\ \tfrac{1}{2}\right).$$
The posterior mean is the standard Beta mean,
$$E[p \mid D] = \frac{3/2}{3/2 + 1/2} = \frac{3}{4}.$$
The interior MAP formula $\hat p_{\text{MAP}} = (\alpha - 1)/(\alpha + \beta - 2)$ requires *both* shape parameters greater than $1$. Here the second shape is $1/2 < 1$, so the posterior density $p^{1/2}(1-p)^{-1/2}$ diverges as $p \to 1$ and the MAP sits at the boundary $\hat p_{\text{MAP}} = 1$. The mean averages over the full posterior, while the MAP just tracks the local maximum — and in this case the maximum is a boundary singularity. This is a classic case where the MAP is a misleading point summary.
:::

### The reparametrization invariance problem

This is a critical issue for MAP estimation. Consider the logit transformation $\phi = \log\frac{p}{1-p}$, so $p = \frac{e^\phi}{1 + e^\phi}$.

::: {.note}
When we change variables, the Jacobian enters the posterior density:
$$
p(\phi \mid D) = p(p \mid D) \left\vert \frac{dp}{d\phi} \right\vert = p(p \mid D) \cdot p(1-p).
$$
This Jacobian modifies the exponents in the likelihood, so $\arg\max_\phi p(\phi \mid D) \neq g(\arg\max_p p(p \mid D))$ unless the Jacobian is constant.

**The posterior mean and median are invariant under reparametrization** because they are defined via integration, not pointwise optimization. This makes them more principled choices.
:::

## Reliability: Gaussian approximation and Laplace's method

For large samples or sharply peaked posteriors, we can approximate the posterior as Gaussian using a Taylor expansion around the MAP.

::: {.theorem}
**Laplace's method**: If the posterior is sharply unimodal around $\hat{\theta}_{\text{MAP}}$, then
$$
p(\theta \mid D, H) \approx N(\hat{\theta}_{\text{MAP}}, C),
$$
where the covariance matrix is
$$
C = H^{-1}, \quad H_{ij} = -\frac{\partial^2 \ln p(\theta \mid D)}{\partial \theta_i \partial \theta_j}\bigg|_{\theta = \hat{\theta}_{\text{MAP}}}
$$
is the negative Hessian of the log-posterior.

The uncertainty in each parameter is $\sigma_i = \sqrt{C_{ii}}$.
:::

### When is Laplace reliable?

The Gaussian approximation works well when:
- The posterior is unimodal and symmetric.
- The sample size is large (CLT effect).
- Parameters stay away from natural boundaries.

It breaks down when:
- Posterior is broad, skewed, or multimodal.
- Data are sparse (small $n$, far from the CLT regime).
- Parameters are bounded (e.g., $p \in [0,1]$, $\sigma^2 > 0$).

::: {.example}
**Bernoulli reliability**

For the Bernoulli likelihood with a Beta prior, the log-posterior is
$$
L(p) = (a-1) \ln p + (b-1) \ln(1-p),
$$
where $a = \alpha + k$ and $b = \beta + n - k$.

The second derivative at the MAP is
$$
L''(\hat{p}_{\text{MAP}}) = -\frac{(a+b-2)^2}{(a-1)(b-1)}.
$$

The uncertainty scales as $\sigma \sim (n + \alpha + \beta)^{-1/2}$. For small $n$ or extreme counts, the quadratic approximation is poor because the Beta posterior has heavy tails.
:::

## Credible intervals

When the posterior is broad or asymmetric, a single point estimate with symmetric error bars is misleading. Instead, we summarize the posterior with **credible intervals**: regions containing a specified probability mass.

::: {.definition}
A **credible interval** $[\theta^-, \theta^+]$ at **credibility level** $\gamma$ satisfies
$$
P(\theta \in [\theta^-, \theta^+] \mid D, H) = \gamma.
$$
The posterior probability that $\theta$ lies in this interval is $\gamma$, **conditioned on the observed data**.
:::

### Central credible intervals

The **central (or equal-tail) credible interval** places equal probability mass in each tail:
$$
\int_{-\infty}^{\theta^-} p(\theta \mid D) d\theta = \int_{\theta^+}^{\infty} p(\theta \mid D) d\theta = \frac{1-\gamma}{2}.
$$

The endpoints are quantiles: $\theta^\pm = F^{-1}\left(\frac{1 \pm \gamma}{2}\right)$, where $F$ is the CDF of the posterior.

For a Gaussian posterior with mean $\mu$ and std $\sigma$, the 68%, 95%, and 99.7% central intervals are the familiar $\mu \pm 1\sigma$, $\mu \pm 1.96\sigma$, and $\mu \pm 3\sigma$.

### Highest Posterior Density (HPD) intervals

::: {.definition}
A **Highest Posterior Density (HPD) interval** at credibility level $\gamma$ is the smallest-width region containing probability mass $\gamma$ with the highest posterior density. Equivalently,
$$
\text{HPD}_\gamma = \{\theta : p(\theta \mid D) \geq k_\gamma\},
$$
where $k_\gamma$ is the largest constant such that $\int_{\text{HPD}_\gamma} p(\theta \mid D) d\theta = \gamma$.

**Properties:**
- Every point inside the HPD has higher density than every point outside.
- All points in the HPD have posterior density $\geq k_\gamma$.
- For symmetric distributions, HPD coincides with the central interval.
- For asymmetric or multimodal posteriors, the HPD can be disconnected.
- HPD is the minimum-width interval.
:::

### HPD construction by threshold

The practical algorithm is simple: sort posterior density values in descending order, accumulate probability mass until reaching $\gamma$, and find the density threshold $k_\gamma$ where this occurs. Then the HPD is the set of all $\theta$ with $p(\theta \mid D) \geq k_\gamma$.

```{r}
#| label: lst-hpd-intervals
#| lst-cap: "Hpd intervals"
#| fig-cap: "HPD interval (red shaded) vs central credible interval (blue shaded) for a bimodal mixture of Beta distributions. HPD is disconnected; central interval includes low-density region between modes."

x <- seq(0, 1, length.out = 2000)
dens <- 0.5 * dbeta(x, 2, 8) + 0.5 * dbeta(x, 8, 2)

gamma <- 0.80
dx <- x[2] - x[1]
dens_sorted <- sort(dens, decreasing = TRUE)
cum_prob <- cumsum(dens_sorted) * dx
k_gamma <- dens_sorted[which(cum_prob >= gamma)[1]]

# Central interval (for comparison)
cum_dens <- cumsum(dens) * dx
cum_dens_norm <- cum_dens / max(cum_dens)
idx_central <- which(cum_dens_norm <= (1 + gamma)/2 & cum_dens_norm >= (1 - gamma)/2)

plot(x, dens, type = "l", lwd = 2.5, col = "#1f77b4",
     main = paste0("HPD vs Central Interval (gamma = ", gamma, ")"),
     xlab = expression(theta), ylab = "Posterior density")

# Shade HPD
hpd_idx <- dens >= k_gamma
polygon(c(x[hpd_idx], rev(x[hpd_idx])), c(dens[hpd_idx], rep(0, sum(hpd_idx))),
        col = rgb(1, 0, 0, 0.3), border = NA)

# Threshold line
abline(h = k_gamma, col = "red", lwd = 1.5, lty = 2)

legend("top", legend = c("Posterior density", "HPD region", expression(k[gamma])),
       col = c("#1f77b4", rgb(1,0,0,0.3), "red"),
       lwd = c(2.5, NA, 1.5), lty = c(1, NA, 2), pch = c(NA, 15, NA), bty = "n")
grid(col = "grey90")
```

## Worked example: Beta posterior with equal-tail and HPD intervals

Suppose we observe $k = 5$ heads in $n = 20$ coin flips with a uniform prior Beta(1,1). The posterior is Beta(6, 16).

```{r}
#| label: lst-beta-credible-intervals
#| lst-cap: "Beta credible intervals"
#| fig-cap: "Beta(6,16) posterior with 95% equal-tail interval (blue) and HPD interval (red). Equal-tail is symmetric; HPD follows the peak and is slightly narrower."

p <- seq(0, 1, length.out = 1000)
alpha_post <- 6
beta_post <- 16
posterior <- dbeta(p, alpha_post, beta_post)

gamma <- 0.95

# Equal-tail interval
p_lower_central <- qbeta((1 - gamma) / 2, alpha_post, beta_post)
p_upper_central <- qbeta((1 + gamma) / 2, alpha_post, beta_post)

# HPD: find threshold
dens_sorted <- sort(posterior, decreasing = TRUE)
dx <- p[2] - p[1]
cum_prob <- cumsum(dens_sorted) * dx
k_gamma <- dens_sorted[which(cum_prob >= gamma)[1]]
hpd_idx <- posterior >= k_gamma

# Plot
plot(p, posterior, type = "l", lwd = 2.5, col = "#1f77b4",
     main = "Beta(6,16): Equal-tail vs HPD (95%)",
     xlab = expression(p), ylab = "Density")

# Shade equal-tail
idx_et <- (p >= p_lower_central) & (p <= p_upper_central)
polygon(c(p[idx_et], rev(p[idx_et])), c(posterior[idx_et], rep(0, sum(idx_et))),
        col = rgb(0, 0, 1, 0.2), border = NA)

# Shade HPD
polygon(c(p[hpd_idx], rev(p[hpd_idx])), c(posterior[hpd_idx], rep(0, sum(hpd_idx))),
        col = rgb(1, 0, 0, 0.25), border = NA)

# Mean and mode
p_mean <- alpha_post / (alpha_post + beta_post)
p_mode <- (alpha_post - 1) / (alpha_post + beta_post - 2)
abline(v = p_mean, col = "#9467bd", lwd = 1.5, lty = 3)
abline(v = p_mode, col = "#2ca02c", lwd = 1.5, lty = 3)

legend("topright",
       legend = c("Posterior", "Equal-tail [95%]", "HPD [95%]", "Mean", "Mode"),
       col = c("#1f77b4", rgb(0,0,1,0.2), rgb(1,0,0,0.25), "#9467bd", "#2ca02c"),
       lwd = c(2.5, NA, NA, 1.5, 1.5), lty = c(1, NA, NA, 3, 3),
       pch = c(NA, 15, 15, NA, NA), bty = "n")

cat("Equal-tail:  [", sprintf("%.4f", p_lower_central), ",", sprintf("%.4f", p_upper_central), "]\n", sep = "")
cat("HPD:         [", sprintf("%.4f", min(p[hpd_idx])), ",", sprintf("%.4f", max(p[hpd_idx])), "]\n", sep = "")
```

## Posterior predictive distribution

Having estimated $\theta$, we often want to predict a future observation $x_{\text{new}}$.

::: {.definition}
The **posterior predictive distribution** integrates over parameter uncertainty:
$$
p(x_{\text{new}} \mid D) = \int p(x_{\text{new}} \mid \theta) \, p(\theta \mid D) \, d\theta.
$$
This is the probability distribution for a new observation, having learned $\theta$ from data $D$. No point estimate is needed; we marginalize over the posterior.
:::

### Prediction vs plug-in estimation

A naive approach is to plug in the point estimate $\hat{\theta}$ (e.g., the posterior mean):
$$
p_{\text{plug-in}}(x_{\text{new}} \mid D) \approx p(x_{\text{new}} \mid \hat{\theta}).
$$

This **ignores parameter uncertainty**, which can be substantial with sparse data.

### Worked example: Poisson rate with zero counts

Suppose we observe zero events in $n = 5$ time intervals: $x_1 = \cdots = x_5 = 0$, so $y = \sum_i x_i = 0$.

With a uniform prior $\text{Gamma}(1, 0)$ (improper), the posterior is $\lambda \mid D \sim \text{Gamma}(1, 5)$.

**Plug-in estimate:** $\hat{\lambda}_{\text{MLE}} = y / n = 0$, so $p(x_{\text{new}} = 1 \mid \hat{\lambda}) = 0$. This is unphysical: zero observed events do not mean events are impossible!

**Posterior predictive:** Integrating over the posterior,
$$
p(x_{\text{new}} = 1 \mid D) = \int_0^\infty \frac{\lambda e^{-\lambda}}{1!} \cdot 5e^{-5\lambda} d\lambda = \int_0^\infty 5\lambda e^{-6\lambda} d\lambda = \frac{5}{36} \approx 0.139.
$$

Even with zero observed counts, the posterior predicts a 14% chance of seeing an event in the next interval — a reasonable and physically sensible conclusion.

::: {.example name="Posterior predictive after a null exposure (exam-style)"}
During an exposure of length $T$, no events are observed. With a uniform prior on the Poisson rate $\lambda \geq 0$, what is the posterior predictive probability of observing at least one event in a future exposure of the same length $T$?

**Solution.** With the improper uniform prior $p(\lambda) \propto 1$ and zero observed counts, the posterior is
$$p(\lambda \mid D) \propto e^{-\lambda T} \cdot 1 \quad\Rightarrow\quad \lambda \mid D \sim \text{Exp}(T).$$
Conditional on $\lambda$, the probability of zero future events in a fresh exposure of length $T$ is $e^{-\lambda T}$. Hence
$$P(X_{\text{new}} = 0 \mid D) = \int_0^\infty e^{-\lambda T}\,T\,e^{-T\lambda}\,d\lambda = \int_0^\infty T\,e^{-2T\lambda}\,d\lambda = \frac{1}{2}.$$
Therefore $P(X_{\text{new}} \geq 1 \mid D) = 1/2$. Although no event was observed, the predictive probability of a future event is not zero — residual uncertainty in $\lambda$ keeps the predictive distribution wide. This is the predictive cousin of the *Rule of Three*.
:::

::: {.example name="Predictive probability of $m$ failures (exam-style)"}
With prior $p \sim \text{Beta}(\alpha, \beta)$, $k$ successes are observed in $n$ Bernoulli trials. What is the predictive probability that the next $m$ trials are all failures?

**Solution.** The posterior is $p \mid D \sim \text{Beta}(\alpha + k,\ \beta + n - k)$. Conditional on $p$, the probability of $m$ consecutive failures is $(1-p)^m$. The predictive probability is
$$P(m\text{ failures} \mid D) = E\!\left[(1-p)^m \mid D\right].$$
For $p \sim \text{Beta}(a, b)$ one has $E[(1-p)^m] = B(a, b + m)/B(a, b)$. Therefore
$$P(m\text{ failures} \mid D) = \frac{B(\alpha + k,\ \beta + n - k + m)}{B(\alpha + k,\ \beta + n - k)}.$$
This is **not** equal to $(1 - E[p \mid D])^m$ because the predictive integrates over residual uncertainty in $p$ — a feature that the plug-in estimate ignores.
:::

### Variance decomposition

The predictive variance splits naturally into two sources:
$$
\text{Var}(x_{\text{new}} \mid D) = E_\theta[\text{Var}(x_{\text{new}} \mid \theta) \mid D] + \text{Var}_\theta(E[x_{\text{new}} \mid \theta] \mid D).
$$
The first term is **data noise** (aleatoric uncertainty); the second is **parameter uncertainty** (epistemic uncertainty). The full predictive captures both.

## Sequential learning and posterior updating

A key advantage of Bayesian inference is that learning is sequential: today's posterior becomes tomorrow's prior.

::: {.example name="Sequential vs batch updating (exam-style)"}
A coin has prior $p \sim \text{Beta}(2, 2)$. In a first run, $3$ successes and $1$ failure are observed. In a second run, $2$ successes and $4$ failures are observed. Compute the final posterior both sequentially and in one step.

**Solution.** Sequentially, after the first run,
$$p \mid D_1 \sim \text{Beta}(2 + 3,\ 2 + 1) = \text{Beta}(5, 3).$$
Treating that as the new prior and updating on the second run,
$$p \mid D_1, D_2 \sim \text{Beta}(5 + 2,\ 3 + 4) = \text{Beta}(7, 7).$$
In one step, the combined data have $5$ successes and $5$ failures, so
$$p \mid D \sim \text{Beta}(2 + 5,\ 2 + 5) = \text{Beta}(7, 7).$$
The same posterior is obtained either way — conjugate updating is associative, which is just a restatement of the product rule.
:::

::: {.example}
**Sequential coin tosses**

Start with a Beta(1,1) uniform prior. After observing data, we compute the posterior. If we observe more data, the posterior becomes the new prior.

```{r}
#| label: lst-sequential-updating
#| lst-cap: "Sequential updating"
#| fig-cap: "Sequential Bayesian learning for a coin. Top: prior and first observation (1 head, 2 tails). Middle and bottom: posterior becomes new prior as data accumulate. Posterior concentrates around true bias p=0.7."

set.seed(42)
p_true <- 0.7
n_obs_per_step <- 3
n_steps <- 4

par(mfrow = c(n_steps, 1), mar = c(3, 3, 2, 1), cex.main = 0.85)

p_grid <- seq(0, 1, length.out = 1000)

# Initial prior
alpha <- 1
beta <- 1
prior_dens <- dbeta(p_grid, alpha, beta)

plot(p_grid, prior_dens, type = "l", lwd = 2, col = "#1f77b4",
     main = "Prior: Beta(1,1)", xlab = expression(p), ylab = "Density",
     ylim = c(0, 4))
abline(v = p_true, col = "#d62728", lwd = 1, lty = 2, alpha = 0.7)
grid(col = "grey90")

# Sequential updates
for (step in 1:n_steps) {
  # Generate new observation
  obs <- rbinom(n_obs_per_step, 1, p_true)
  k <- sum(obs)
  n <- n_obs_per_step

  # Update posterior (which becomes new prior)
  alpha <- alpha + k
  beta <- beta + n - k
  posterior_dens <- dbeta(p_grid, alpha, beta)

  # Plot
  plot(p_grid, posterior_dens, type = "l", lwd = 2, col = "#1f77b4",
       main = paste0("After ", step * n_obs_per_step, " observations"),
       xlab = expression(p), ylab = "Density", ylim = c(0, 4))
```

Notice how the posterior tightens and centers on the true value as we accumulate data.

## Frequentist confidence intervals: brief contrast

To highlight what is unique about Bayesian credible intervals, we briefly compare with frequentist confidence intervals.

::: {.definition}
A **frequentist confidence interval** is a random interval $C(X)$ with **coverage probability** $1 - \alpha$:
$$
P(\theta \in C(X)) = 1 - \alpha,
$$
where the probability is over repeated sampling of data $X \sim p(x \mid \theta)$ under a **fixed but unknown** $\theta$.

**Interpretation:** In repeated experiments, $100(1-\alpha)\%$ of the confidence intervals will contain the true $\theta$.
:::

::: {.note}
**Key differences:**

| Aspect | Bayesian Credible Interval | Frequentist Confidence Interval |
|--------|---------------------------|-------------------------------|
| Probability on | Parameter | Data |
| Conditioned on | Observed data $D$ | Hypothetical repeated datasets |
| Interpretation | $P(\theta \in [a,b] \mid D) = \gamma$ | In repeated experiments, $100\gamma\%$ of intervals cover $\theta$ |
| Reparametrization | Invariant | Not invariant |

Once we observe data, the frequentist interval either contains $\theta$ or it does not — there is no probability statement about the observed interval.
:::

::: {.example name="Credible vs confidence intervals (exam-style)"}
Define a Bayesian credible interval for a scalar parameter $\theta$. How does its interpretation differ from a frequentist confidence interval?

**Solution.** A Bayesian credible interval $[a, b]$ at credibility level $\gamma$ satisfies
$$\int_a^b p(\theta \mid D)\,d\theta = \gamma.$$
A **central** credible interval excludes equal posterior probability from each tail; a **highest-posterior-density** (HPD) interval is the smallest set containing posterior mass $\gamma$ (it collects the highest-density region first).

Its interpretation is direct: conditional on the data, model, and prior, the parameter lies in $[a, b]$ with posterior probability $\gamma$. A frequentist confidence interval instead has a repeated-sampling coverage interpretation: *before* seeing the data, the random procedure $C(X)$ contains the fixed true $\theta$ with probability $\gamma$. After conditioning on the observed data, the random interval is fixed and either contains $\theta$ or does not — no probability statement is attached to the realised interval.
:::

## One-sided credible intervals

For some problems, we care only about an upper or lower bound on the parameter. A **one-sided credible interval** $[0, \theta^*]$ at level $\gamma$ satisfies
$$
P(\theta \leq \theta^* \mid D) = \gamma.
$$

### Rule of Three

A famous result in statistics applies to rare-event detection. If you observe zero events in $n$ trials under a Binomial model with unknown rate $p$, the **95% upper credible bound** (using a uniform Beta(1,1) prior) is approximately
$$
p^* \approx \frac{3}{n}.
$$

This is the "**Rule of Three**": if you see zero occurrences in $n$ trials, you can be 95% confident that the true rate is below $3/n$.

::: {.example}
**Zero-count drug safety**

If a drug is tested on 100 patients and zero adverse events are observed, the Rule of Three gives a 95% upper bound of $3/100 = 0.03$ or 3%. We can be reasonably confident (in the Bayesian sense, given a weak prior) that the true adverse event rate is below 3%, even though we observed none.

This addresses the zero-count problem: the MLE would say the rate is exactly zero, which is overconfident. The Bayesian posterior hedges appropriately.
:::

## Choosing priors: conjugacy and regularization

::: {.definition}
A prior $\pi(\theta)$ is **conjugate** to a likelihood $p(x \mid \theta)$ if the posterior is in the same family as the prior:
$$
p(\theta \mid x) \propto p(x \mid \theta) \pi(\theta) \in \text{same family as } \pi(\theta).
$$
:::

Conjugate priors are analytically tractable and allow simple sequential updating. They also naturally encode prior beliefs as "pseudo-data" or "pseudo-observations."

### Example: Beta-Binomial conjugacy

- **Prior:** $p \sim \text{Beta}(\alpha, \beta)$ with density $\pi(p) \propto p^{\alpha-1}(1-p)^{\beta-1}$.
- **Likelihood:** $P(k \text{ heads} \mid p, n) \propto p^k (1-p)^{n-k}$.
- **Posterior:** $p \mid k, n \sim \text{Beta}(\alpha + k, \beta + n - k)$.

The hyperparameters have an intuitive interpretation: $\alpha - 1$ and $\beta - 1$ are pseudo-successes and pseudo-failures, representing prior pseudo-data.

### Example: Gamma-Poisson conjugacy

- **Prior:** $\lambda \sim \text{Gamma}(\alpha, \beta)$ with density $\pi(\lambda) \propto \lambda^{\alpha-1} e^{-\beta \lambda}$.
- **Likelihood:** $P(y \text{ counts} \mid \lambda, n) \propto \lambda^y e^{-n\lambda}$.
- **Posterior:** $\lambda \mid y, n \sim \text{Gamma}(\alpha + y, \beta + n)$.

Here, $\alpha$ is a pseudo-count and $\beta$ is a pseudo-exposure time.

## Fisher information, Cramér–Rao, and the Jeffreys prior

The theory of parametric estimation rests on a few fundamental objects: the Fisher information matrix, which quantifies the curvature of the likelihood; the Cramér–Rao bound, which sets a lower limit on estimator variance; and the Jeffreys prior, which adapts to the geometry of the parameter space. This section develops these concepts in general, showing how they unify point estimation (Cramér–Rao), prior specification (Jeffreys), and asymptotic posterior behaviour (Bernstein–von Mises).

### Score and Fisher Information

:::{.definition name="Score and Fisher information matrix"}
Given a model $p(x \mid \theta)$ and an observation $x$ (or dataset), the **score vector** is
$$s(\theta; x) = \nabla_\theta \log p(x \mid \theta) = \frac{\partial}{\partial \theta} \log p(x \mid \theta).$$

The **Fisher information matrix** is the expected outer product of the score:
$$\mathcal{I}(\theta) = \mathbb{E}_x\big[s(\theta;x)\,s(\theta;x)^{\top}\big],$$
with $(i,j)$-entry $\mathcal{I}_{ij}(\theta) = \mathbb{E}[s_i(\theta;x) s_j(\theta;x)]$.

Equivalently, under regularity conditions,
$$\mathcal{I}(\theta) = -\mathbb{E}_x\left[\nabla_\theta^2 \log p(x\mid\theta)\right] = -\mathbb{E}[H(\theta)],$$
where $H(\theta)$ is the Hessian of the log-likelihood.
:::

**Proof sketch for the equivalence:** Differentiating $\int p(x\mid\theta) dx = 1$ with respect to $\theta$:
$$\int \frac{\partial}{\partial\theta} p(x\mid\theta) dx = \int p(x\mid\theta) \cdot s(\theta;x) dx = \mathbb{E}[s(\theta;x)] = 0.$$
Differentiating again and swapping derivatives and integration (regularity):
$$\mathbb{E}\left[\frac{\partial s(\theta;x)}{\partial\theta}\right] + \mathbb{E}[s(\theta;x)^2] = 0,$$
which gives $\mathbb{E}[s(\theta;x)^2] = -\mathbb{E}[H(\theta)]$.

For an i.i.d. sample of size $n$, the total score is the sum of individual scores, and the Fisher information scales linearly: $\mathcal{I}_n(\theta) = n\,\mathcal{I}(\theta)$.

### Reparametrization Formula

:::{.proposition name="Fisher information under reparametrisation"}
Suppose we reparameterize via a smooth bijection $\psi = \phi(\theta)$ with Jacobian matrix $J_{ij} = \partial\psi_i / \partial\theta_j$. Then the Fisher information with respect to $\psi$ is
$$\mathcal{I}_\psi(\psi) = J^\top \mathcal{I}_\theta(\theta) \, J,$$
where $J = \partial\phi(\theta) / \partial\theta$ evaluated at the appropriate $\theta = \phi^{-1}(\psi)$.
:::

**Proof:** By the chain rule, $s_\psi(\psi; x) = J^\top s_\theta(\theta; x)$. Hence,
$$\mathcal{I}_\psi(\psi) = \mathbb{E}[J^\top s_\theta s_\theta^\top J] = J^\top \mathcal{I}_\theta(\theta) J.$$

This reparametrization formula is the key to understanding why $\sqrt{\det \mathcal{I}(\theta)}$ behaves like an invariant density element. Under a change of variables $\psi = \phi(\theta)$,
$$\sqrt{\det \mathcal{I}_\psi(\psi)} = \sqrt{\det(J^\top J)} \cdot \sqrt{\det \mathcal{I}_\theta(\theta)} = |\det J| \cdot \sqrt{\det \mathcal{I}_\theta(\theta)},$$
which is precisely the Jacobian rule for transforming a density. This geometric insight motivates the Jeffreys prior.

### Cramér–Rao Lower Bound

:::{.theorem name="Cramér–Rao lower bound"}
Let $\hat\theta(X)$ be an unbiased estimator of $\theta$ from an i.i.d. sample $X = (X_1, \ldots, X_n)$. Then
$$\mathrm{Cov}(\hat\theta) \succeq \mathcal{I}_n(\theta)^{-1} = \frac{1}{n} \mathcal{I}(\theta)^{-1},$$
where $\succeq$ denotes the positive semidefinite order (i.e., $\mathrm{Cov}(\hat\theta) - \mathcal{I}_n(\theta)^{-1}$ is positive semidefinite).
:::

**Proof outline (scalar case):** Write the unbiasedness condition as
$$\mathbb{E}_\theta[\hat\theta - \theta] = 0.$$
Differentiating with respect to $\theta$ and invoking regularity (swapping derivative and expectation):
$$\mathbb{E}_\theta\left[\frac{\partial}{\partial\theta}(\hat\theta - \theta) \right] + \mathbb{E}_\theta[s(\theta;X)] = 0.$$
Since $\mathbb{E}[s] = 0$, we have
$$\mathbb{E}[s(\theta;X)] = -1.$$
Wait, I need to be more careful; let me re-phrase. Consider $\mathbb{E}[\hat\theta \cdot s(\theta)] = \theta \mathbb{E}[s(\theta)]$. Differentiating the unbiasedness condition $\mathbb{E}[\hat\theta] = \theta$:
$$\mathbb{E}\left[\frac{\partial \hat\theta}{\partial\theta}\right] = 1 \quad \text{(in scalar case)}.$$
By Cauchy–Schwarz applied to $\hat\theta \cdot s$ and rearranging, one recovers $\mathrm{Var}(\hat\theta) \ge \mathcal{I}(θ)^{-1}$.

The Cramér–Rao bound sets a fundamental speed limit on estimation: no unbiased estimator can have variance smaller than this bound. Remarkably, the maximum likelihood estimator (MLE) achieves this bound asymptotically.

### Asymptotic Normality of the MLE

:::{.theorem name="Asymptotic normality of the MLE"}
Under standard regularity conditions, the MLE $\hat\theta_{\mathrm{ML}}$ of $\theta$ from a sample of size $n$ satisfies
$$\sqrt{n}\,(\hat\theta_{\mathrm{ML}} - \theta_*) \xrightarrow{d} \mathcal{N}\big(0,\,\mathcal{I}(\theta_*)^{-1}\big),$$
where $\theta_*$ is the true parameter and $\mathcal{I}(\theta_*)$ is the Fisher information at the truth.
:::

This result, whose proof relies on Tayor expansions of the score and regularity conditions, shows that for large $n$ the MLE is approximately $\mathcal{N}(\hat\theta_{\mathrm{ML}}, \mathcal{I}_n(\theta_*)^{-1})$. Since $\mathcal{I}_n(\theta_*) = n \mathcal{I}(\theta_*)$, the variance shrinks as $1/n$, asymptotically achieving the Cramér–Rao bound. For reference on the detailed conditions and proof, see van der Vaart (1998), Chapter 5.

### The Jeffreys Prior

:::{.definition name="Jeffreys prior"}
The **Jeffreys prior** is defined as
$$p_J(\theta) \propto \sqrt{\det \mathcal{I}(\theta)},$$
the square root of the determinant of the Fisher information matrix.
:::

Intuitively, the Jeffreys prior allocates more prior mass to regions where the likelihood is less informative (where $\mathcal{I}(\theta)$ is smaller) and less prior mass where the likelihood is highly peaked (where $\mathcal{I}(\theta)$ is large). This gives an *objective* prior that "lets the data speak" in a geometric sense.

### Reparametrisation Invariance of Jeffreys

:::{.theorem name="Jeffreys prior is reparametrisation-invariant"}
If $p_J(\theta) \propto \sqrt{\det \mathcal{I}_\theta(\theta)}$ is the Jeffreys prior in the $\theta$ parameterization, and we reparametrize via $\psi = \phi(\theta)$ with Jacobian $J$, then the Jeffreys prior in the $\psi$ parameterization satisfies
$$p_J(\psi) \propto \sqrt{\det \mathcal{I}_\psi(\psi)} = \sqrt{\det(J^\top J)} \cdot \sqrt{\det \mathcal{I}_\theta(\theta)} = |\det J| \cdot \sqrt{\det \mathcal{I}_\theta(\theta)}.$$

Under the change of variables, the prior density transforms as
$$p_J(\psi) \, d\psi = p_J(\theta)\,|\det J| \, d\theta,$$
which shows that the Jeffreys prior is **invariant under reparametrisation**.
:::

**Significance:** This is the defining property that distinguishes Jeffreys from a flat prior. A uniform prior $p(\theta) \propto 1$ does *not* remain uniform after reparametrization; its density changes by the Jacobian. In contrast, the Jeffreys prior automatically adapts its form to remain "flat" in an information-geometric sense, independent of how we parameterize the model.

### Examples of Jeffreys Priors

:::{.example name="Bernoulli parameter"}
For a Bernoulli($p$) model with a single observation $x \in \{0,1\}$, the log-likelihood is $x \log p + (1-x) \log(1-p)$. The Fisher information is
$$\mathcal{I}(p) = \mathbb{E}\left[\left(\frac{\partial}{\partial p} \log p(x \mid p)\right)^2\right] = \frac{1}{p(1-p)}.$$

The Jeffreys prior is thus
$$p_J(p) \propto \sqrt{\frac{1}{p(1-p)}} = p^{-1/2}(1-p)^{-1/2},$$
which is the **Beta$(1/2, 1/2)$ distribution**.

Note: the uniform prior is Beta$(1,1)$, which depends on the choice of the $p$ parameterization. If we instead parameterize via the log-odds $\phi = \log(p/(1-p))$, the uniform prior in $\phi$ is different from the reparametrized form of Beta$(1,1)$. But Beta$(1/2, 1/2)$ remains the same (up to the appropriate density form) under any reparametrization.
:::

:::{.example name="Poisson rate"}
For Poisson($\lambda$), the log-likelihood is $x \log \lambda - \lambda$, and the Fisher information is
$$\mathcal{I}(\lambda) = \frac{1}{\lambda}.$$

The Jeffreys prior is
$$p_J(\lambda) \propto \lambda^{-1/2},$$
an improper prior. However, any observed data ($x > 0$) yields a proper posterior, because the likelihood $\lambda^x e^{-\lambda}$ combined with $\lambda^{-1/2}$ gives $\lambda^{x-1/2} e^{-\lambda}$, a Gamma distribution.
:::

:::{.example name="Jeffreys is reparametrisation-invariant — Poisson with $\phi = \sqrt\lambda$ (exam-style)"}
For a Poisson observation $X \sim \text{Poisson}(\lambda)$, Jeffreys prior is $\pi(\lambda) \propto \lambda^{-1/2}$. Let $\phi = \sqrt\lambda$. Find the induced prior on $\phi$.

**Solution.** Since $\lambda = \phi^2$ and $d\lambda/d\phi = 2\phi$, the change-of-variables rule gives
$$\pi_\phi(\phi) = \pi_\lambda(\phi^2)\,\left|\frac{d\lambda}{d\phi}\right| \propto (\phi^2)^{-1/2}\cdot 2\phi = 2.$$
The Jeffreys prior for the Poisson rate becomes *flat* in $\phi = \sqrt\lambda$. This is the geometric content of Jeffreys: in the parameterisation that makes the Fisher information constant, the prior is uniform.
:::

:::{.example name="Jeffreys prior for the Lévy scale (exam-style)"}
The Lévy distribution with location $0$ and scale $c > 0$ has density
$$f(x \mid c) = \sqrt{\frac{c}{2\pi}}\,x^{-3/2}\exp\!\left(-\frac{c}{2x}\right), \qquad x > 0.$$
Useful moments are $E[1/X] = 1/c$ and $E[1/X^2] = 3/c^2$. Compute the Jeffreys prior for $c$.

**Solution.** The log-likelihood is
$$\log f(x \mid c) = \tfrac{1}{2}\log c - \tfrac{1}{2}\log(2\pi) - \tfrac{3}{2}\log x - \frac{c}{2x}.$$
Differentiating with respect to $c$,
$$\frac{\partial}{\partial c}\log f(x \mid c) = \frac{1}{2c} - \frac{1}{2x}.$$
The Fisher information is therefore
$$\mathcal{I}(c) = E\!\left[\left(\frac{1}{2c} - \frac{1}{2X}\right)^2\right] = \frac{1}{4c^2} - \frac{1}{2c}\,E\!\left[\frac{1}{X}\right] + \frac{1}{4}\,E\!\left[\frac{1}{X^2}\right] = \frac{1}{4c^2} - \frac{1}{2c^2} + \frac{3}{4c^2} = \frac{1}{2c^2}.$$
Hence
$$\pi_J(c) \propto \sqrt{\mathcal{I}(c)} = \sqrt{\frac{1}{2c^2}} \propto \frac{1}{c}, \qquad c > 0.$$
This is the improper scale-invariant prior $p(c)\,dc \propto d\log c$ — the canonical non-informative choice for a scale parameter.
:::

:::{.example name="Gaussian location (known scale)"}
With $\sigma^2$ known, the log-likelihood for the mean $\mu$ is proportional to $-\frac{1}{2\sigma^2}(x - \mu)^2$. The Fisher information is
$$\mathcal{I}(\mu) = \frac{1}{\sigma^2},$$
a positive constant independent of $\mu$. The Jeffreys prior is thus
$$p_J(\mu) \propto 1,$$
a **uniform (improper) prior**—the standard choice for the unknown mean when the scale is known.
:::

:::{.example name="Gaussian scale (known location)"}
With $\mu$ known, the log-likelihood is $-\frac{n}{2}\log\sigma^2 - \frac{1}{2\sigma^2}\sum(x - \mu)^2$. Differentiating twice with respect to $\sigma^2$ gives
$$\mathcal{I}(\sigma^2) = \frac{n}{2(\sigma^2)^2}.$$

The Jeffreys prior is
$$p_J(\sigma^2) \propto \frac{1}{\sigma^2},$$
or equivalently, $p_J(\log \sigma) \propto 1$ (uniform on the log scale). This is the **scale-invariant prior** for variance, classical in Bayesian inference.
:::

:::{.example name="Gaussian, joint mean and variance"}
For the Gaussian with both $\mu$ and $\sigma^2$ unknown, the Fisher information matrix is
$$\mathcal{I}(\mu, \sigma^2) = \begin{pmatrix} 1/\sigma^2 & 0 \\ 0 & 2/\sigma^4 \end{pmatrix},$$
so $\det \mathcal{I} = 2/\sigma^6$, and the Jeffreys prior is
$$p_J(\mu, \sigma^2) \propto \sqrt{\frac{2}{\sigma^6}} = \frac{1}{\sigma^3}.$$

Equivalently, $p_J(\mu, \sigma) \propto 1/\sigma^2$ in terms of the standard deviation.

**Subtle point:** The *joint* Jeffreys prior $p_J(\mu, \sigma^2) \propto 1/\sigma^3$ does *not* equal the product of the marginal Jeffreys priors for $\mu$ (uniform) and $\sigma^2$ (proportional to $1/\sigma^2$), which would give $p(\mu) \cdot p(\sigma^2) \propto 1/\sigma^2$. Many practitioners use this product-of-marginals prior and call it "Jeffreys," but it is technically the **reference prior** or a compromise prior. The true joint Jeffreys prior is $1/\sigma^3$. This distinction matters for hypothesis tests and Bayes factors.
:::

### Reference Priors

:::{.remark}
When the parameter dimension is high, computing or integrating over the Jeffreys prior becomes cumbersome. **Reference priors** (Bernardo, 1979; Berger, Bernardo & Sun, 2009) generalize the idea: they maximize expected Kullback–Leibler divergence between posterior and prior, thus minimizing the information contained in the prior itself. For one-parameter problems, reference priors coincide with Jeffreys; for multiparameter problems, they offer an alternative construction that often performs better in frequentist coverage. Reference priors are not invariant to reparametrization but are designed to be more "objective" in the sense of information loss.
:::

### The Bernstein–von Mises Theorem

:::{.theorem name="Bernstein–von Mises"}
Under standard regularity conditions, for large sample size $n$, the posterior distribution $p(\theta \mid X_1, \ldots, X_n)$ is approximately Gaussian, centered at the MLE $\hat\theta_{\mathrm{ML}}$ with covariance given by the inverse Fisher information:
$$p(\theta \mid X_1, \ldots, X_n) \approx \mathcal{N}\big(\hat\theta_{\mathrm{ML}},\,\mathcal{I}_n(\theta_*)^{-1}\big),$$
provided the prior is continuous and positive at $\theta_*$ (the true parameter). This holds independently of the choice of prior.
:::

**Interpretation and significance:** The Bernstein–von Mises theorem is one of the deepest results in Bayesian asymptotics. It says that, given enough data, the posterior "forgets" the prior (as long as it is not zero at the truth) and concentrates around the MLE with the canonical frequentist covariance $\mathcal{I}_n(\theta_*)^{-1}$. This explains why, asymptotically, Bayesian credible intervals and frequentist confidence intervals coincide: both converge to the same Gaussian limit. In other words, there is no fundamental conflict between Bayesian and frequentist inference in the large-sample regime—they merge. For a detailed proof, see van der Vaart (1998), Chapter 10.

### Unifying Theme

Fisher information, Cramér–Rao, Jeffreys, and Bernstein–von Mises are tightly interwoven:

- **Cramér–Rao** sets the performance standard for any unbiased estimator.
- **MLE asymptotics** show that the MLE achieves this standard with variance $\mathcal{I}_n(\theta)^{-1}$.
- **Jeffreys prior** is constructed from $\mathcal{I}(\theta)$, ensuring objective prior choice via reparametrization invariance.
- **Bernstein–von Mises** guarantees that the posterior, regardless of the prior, concentrates at the MLE with covariance $\mathcal{I}_n(\theta)^{-1}$.

Together, these results form the backbone of both frequentist and Bayesian asymptotic theory.
