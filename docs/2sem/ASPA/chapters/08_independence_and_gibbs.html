9.1 Introduction

The Metropolis–Hastings algorithm, introduced in the previous chapter, is a general framework for constructing Markov chains with a target stationary distribution. However, the choice of proposal distribution critically affects both computational efficiency and the quality of samples. In this chapter, we explore two important special cases that reveal a fundamental trade-off in MCMC design: global vs. local exploration, and the role of acceptance rates in controlling mixing.

The independence sampler uses a fixed global proposal, independent of the current state. This allows long-distance jumps but requires the proposal to approximate the target well. The Gibbs sampler, by contrast, updates coordinates sequentially using exact full conditional distributions, guaranteeing acceptance but potentially moving slowly through high-dimensional spaces when parameters are correlated. Both methods illustrate how MCMC designers must balance the competing demands of efficient exploration and computational tractability.

9.2 The independence sampler: global proposals

9.2.1 Idea and motivation

In a random-walk Metropolis algorithm, the proposal distribution depends on the current state: \[ q(x' \mid x) \propto \exp\left( -\frac{\|x' - x\|^2}{2\sigma^2} \right). \]

This locality allows the chain to take small, tentative steps and gradually explore the target space. However, if the proposal variance is too small, the chain mixes slowly; if too large, it wastes computation on distant, low-probability regions.

The independence sampler takes a different approach: the proposal is independent of the current state, \[ q(x') = q(x' \mid x), \] depending only on \(x'\). This allows the chain to jump from anywhere to anywhere in a single step, potentially escaping local modes and exploring the target space more globally.

9.2.2 Acceptance probability

For the Metropolis–Hastings algorithm, the acceptance probability is \[ \alpha(x \to x') = \min\left(1, \frac{\pi(x') \, q(x \mid x')}{\pi(x) \, q(x' \mid x)}\right). \]

For the independence sampler, \(q(x \mid x') = q(x)\) and \(q(x' \mid x) = q(x')\), so \[ \alpha(x \to x') = \min\left(1, \frac{\pi(x') \, q(x)}{\pi(x) \, q(x')}\right). \]

Rearranging in terms of importance weights \(w(x) = \frac{\pi(x)}{q(x)}\): \[ \alpha(x \to x') = \min\left(1, \frac{w(x')}{w(x)}\right). \]

The acceptance probability compares the importance weights at the proposed and current states. If \(w(x')\) is larger, the proposal is always accepted. If \(w(x')\) is smaller, it is accepted with probability \(w(x') / w(x)\). When the proposal closely matches the target, weights are nearly constant and acceptance is high.

9.2.3 Connection to importance sampling and effective sample size

The independence sampler inherits its theoretical motivation from importance sampling. In importance sampling, we approximate an expectation \[ E_\pi[f(x)] = \int f(x) \, \pi(x) \, dx \] by sampling from a simpler proposal \(q\) and weighting: \[ \widehat{E_\pi}[f(x)] = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^N w(x_i) f(x_i)}{\sum_{i=1}^N w(x_i)}, \quad w(x_i) = \frac{\pi(x_i)}{q(x_i)}. \]

The effective sample size (ESS) reflects how much information the weighted samples contain. If weights are unequal—as they are when \(q\) deviates from \(\pi\)—the ESS is smaller than \(N\): \[ \text{ESS} = \frac{1}{\sum_i (w_i / \bar{w})^2}. \]

When \(w(x)\) varies widely, many samples receive vanishingly small weight, and the variance of the estimator becomes very large.

The independence sampler’s acceptance rate is directly related to the relative variation in weights. A narrow proposal (or a poorly-matched proposal) produces highly variable weights, leading to low acceptance and slow exploration. This illustrates the narrow-proposal failure mode: if the proposal is too concentrated, most proposed states fall in high-weight regions, but the chain accepts rarely and gets stuck.

9.2.4 When does the independence sampler work well?

The key requirement is that \(q\) must approximate \(\pi\) globally. A sufficient ergodicity condition is \[ \pi(x) \leq C \, q(x) \quad \forall x \] for some finite constant \(C > 0\). This ensures the chain does not get trapped in regions of arbitrarily high relative weight.

The independence sampler works best when: - We have a good global approximation to the posterior, e.g., from a Laplace approximation, variational inference, or other fast approximate methods. - The target distribution is unimodal or has well-separated modes that the proposal can accommodate.

It fails when: - The proposal underestimates the tails or misses modes of the target. - States with large \(\pi(x) / q(x)\) are rare, so the chain cannot easily reach them.

9.2.5 Curse of dimensionality for independence sampling

A profound limitation emerges in high dimensions. Suppose \(\log w(x) = \log \pi(x) - \log q(x)\) decomposes as a sum of roughly independent contributions: \[ \log w(x) \approx \sum_{i=1}^d \Delta_i(x_i), \] where each \(\Delta_i\) represents the local mismatch between \(\pi\) and \(q\) in coordinate \(i\).

Even if each \(\Delta_i\) is small (order \(O(1)\)), the total deviation is \(\log w(x) = O(d)\), so \(w(x) = \exp(O(d))\). The weights vary exponentially in dimension.

Concentration of measure: In high dimensions, probability mass concentrates on thin regions. For a standard Gaussian \(\pi = N(0, I_d)\), the norm \(\|x\|\) concentrates around \(\sqrt{d}\). Even a slightly mis-scaled proposal \(q\) will place its probability mass in regions irrelevant to \(\pi\), causing acceptance to decay exponentially with \(d\).

This is the motivation for local proposals (random-walk Metropolis), gradient-based methods (Hamiltonian MC), and coordinate-wise samplers (Gibbs sampling).

9.3 The Gibbs sampler: coordinate-wise updates

9.3.1 Full conditional distributions

In many Bayesian models, jointly sampling from \(p(\theta \mid D)\) where \(\theta = (\theta_1, \ldots, \theta_d)\) is intractable, but sampling from each full conditional distribution is simple.

The full conditional distribution of \(\theta_i\) is \[ p(\theta_i \mid \theta_{-i}, D), \] where \(\theta_{-i} = (\theta_1, \ldots, \theta_{i-1}, \theta_{i+1}, \ldots, \theta_d)\) denotes all other parameters. It is obtained by treating \(\theta_{-i}\) as fixed and proportional to the joint posterior: \[ p(\theta_i \mid \theta_{-i}, D) \propto p(\theta_i, \theta_{-i} \mid D). \]

9.3.2 Algorithm: deterministic and random scan

Gibbs sampling iteratively samples each parameter from its full conditional:

Gibbs Sampler Algorithm

Initialize \(\theta^{(0)} = (\theta_1^{(0)}, \ldots, \theta_d^{(0)})\).

For \(t = 1, 2, \ldots\):

  1. \(\theta_1^{(t)} \sim p(\theta_1 \mid \theta_2^{(t-1)}, \ldots, \theta_d^{(t-1)}, D)\)
  2. \(\theta_2^{(t)} \sim p(\theta_2 \mid \theta_1^{(t)}, \theta_3^{(t-1)}, \ldots, \theta_d^{(t-1)}, D)\)
  3. \(\vdots\)
  4. \(\theta_d^{(t)} \sim p(\theta_d \mid \theta_1^{(t)}, \ldots, \theta_{d-1}^{(t)}, D)\)

One complete cycle of all \(d\) updates constitutes one iteration of the Gibbs sampler.

Variants: The random-scan Gibbs sampler updates coordinates in random order instead of a fixed sequence. For convergence, both deterministic and random scans are ergodic under mild conditions.

9.3.3 Gibbs as a special case of Metropolis–Hastings

Gibbs sampling can be viewed as Metropolis–Hastings with the proposal being the full conditional itself. At step \(t\), we propose a new value for \(\theta_i\) by sampling from \[ q(\theta_i' \mid \theta_i^{(t-1)}, \theta_{-i}^{(t)}) = p(\theta_i' \mid \theta_{-i}^{(t)}, D). \]

The acceptance probability is \[ \alpha(\theta^{(t-1)} \to \theta^{(t)}) = \min\left(1, \frac{p(\theta_i^{(t)}, \theta_{-i}^{(t)} \mid D) \, p(\theta_i^{(t-1)} \mid \theta_{-i}^{(t)}, D)}{p(\theta_i^{(t-1)}, \theta_{-i}^{(t-1)} \mid D) \, p(\theta_i^{(t)} \mid \theta_{-i}^{(t)}, D)}\right). \]

Using the factorization \(p(\theta_i, \theta_{-i} \mid D) = p(\theta_i \mid \theta_{-i}, D) \, p(\theta_{-i} \mid D)\), the ratio simplifies: \[ \frac{p(\theta_i^{(t)} \mid \theta_{-i}^{(t)}, D) \, p(\theta_{-i}^{(t)} \mid D) \, p(\theta_i^{(t-1)} \mid \theta_{-i}^{(t)}, D)}{p(\theta_i^{(t-1)} \mid \theta_{-i}^{(t-1)}, D) \, p(\theta_{-i}^{(t-1)} \mid D) \, p(\theta_i^{(t)} \mid \theta_{-i}^{(t)}, D)} = 1. \]

Therefore, \(\alpha = 1\): every Gibbs step is accepted.

Acceptance is guaranteed, but samples are still correlated. The chain does not jump to a new state at each step; instead, it updates coordinates sequentially, and autocorrelation arises because coordinates are strongly dependent.

9.3.4 Detailed balance verification for Gibbs

For the full Gibbs update (one complete sweep of all \(d\) coordinates), detailed balance holds with respect to the joint posterior. We verify that \[ p(\theta \mid D) \, T(\theta \to \theta') = p(\theta' \mid D) \, T(\theta' \to \theta), \] where \(T\) is the transition kernel of one complete sweep.

For a single coordinate update \(T_i\) applied to coordinate \(i\), left-multiply by \(p(\theta \mid D)\) and expand using the factorization: \[\begin{align} p(\theta \mid D) \, T_i(\theta \to \theta') &= p(\theta_i, \theta_{-i} \mid D) \, p(\theta_i' \mid \theta_{-i}, D) \, \delta(\theta_{-i}' - \theta_{-i})\\ &= p(\theta_i \mid \theta_{-i}, D) \, p(\theta_{-i} \mid D) \, p(\theta_i' \mid \theta_{-i}, D) \, \delta(\theta_{-i}' - \theta_{-i}). \end{align}\]

Permuting terms and using \(p(\theta_i \mid \theta_{-i}, D) = p(\theta_i \mid \theta_{-i}', D)\) (since \(\theta_{-i} = \theta_{-i}'\)): \[ = p(\theta_i' \mid \theta_{-i}, D) \, p(\theta_{-i} \mid D) \, p(\theta_i \mid \theta_{-i}, D) \, \delta(\theta_{-i}' - \theta_{-i}) = p(\theta' \mid D) \, T_i(\theta' \to \theta). \]

Thus each coordinate-wise update preserves the posterior. Since the full sweep is a composition of such updates, it also satisfies detailed balance.

9.3.5 Mixing and slow mixing from correlation

Unlike the independence sampler, Gibbs always accepts. However, this does not imply fast mixing. When parameters are strongly correlated—as in high-dimensional hierarchical models or when the posterior is elongated along a narrow ridge—coordinate-wise updates must take many small steps to explore the space.

Bivariate Gaussian with high correlation

Consider a bivariate normal distribution: \[ \begin{pmatrix} X \\ Y \end{pmatrix} \sim N\left( \begin{pmatrix} 0 \\ 0 \end{pmatrix}, \begin{pmatrix} 1 & \rho \\ \rho & 1 \end{pmatrix} \right). \]

The full conditionals are: \[ X \mid Y = y \sim N(\rho y, 1 - \rho^2), \quad Y \mid X = x \sim N(\rho x, 1 - \rho^2). \]

When \(\rho = 0.9\), the posterior concentrates on a narrow diagonal ridge. A Gibbs step first moves horizontally (X-update), then vertically (Y-update), taking many iterations to traverse the ridge. In contrast, updating both coordinates jointly (e.g., via a rotated-coordinate Gibbs or HMC) moves along the ridge in fewer steps.

9.3.6 Metropolis-within-Gibbs and block Gibbs

When the full conditional for a component is intractable, we can replace the Gibbs step with a Metropolis–Hastings step:

Metropolis-within-Gibbs: Replace the Gibbs step for \(\theta_i\) with a Metropolis–Hastings move using a local proposal \(q_i(\theta_i' \mid \theta_i)\). The acceptance probability is \[ \alpha_i(\theta_i \mid \theta_{-i}) = \min\left(1, \frac{p(\theta_i' \mid \theta_{-i}, D) \, q_i(\theta_i \mid \theta_i')}{p(\theta_i \mid \theta_{-i}, D) \, q_i(\theta_i' \mid \theta_i)}\right). \]

The chain remains valid (it still has \(p(\theta \mid D)\) as stationary distribution), but some steps are rejected.

Similarly, block Gibbs sampling updates multiple coordinates jointly: \[ (\theta_1, \ldots, \theta_k) \sim p(\theta_1, \ldots, \theta_k \mid \theta_{k+1}, \ldots, \theta_d, D). \]

This is effective when joint conditionals are tractable, as it can dramatically reduce autocorrelation in highly correlated subsets.

9.4 Worked example: Gibbs sampling for a bivariate Gaussian

We now demonstrate Gibbs sampling on the bivariate Gaussian with \(\rho = 0.9\) to illustrate slow mixing due to correlation, and show how a simple coordinate rotation accelerates mixing.

9.4.1 Slow mixing at high correlation

Show code 
set.seed(42)

gibbs_bivariate <- function(n_steps, rho, start = c(0, 0)) {
  x <- numeric(n_steps)
  y <- numeric(n_steps)
  x[1] <- start[1]
  y[1] <- start[2]
  cond_sd <- sqrt(1 - rho^2)

  for (t in 2:n_steps) {
    # Update X | Y
    x[t] <- rnorm(1, mean = rho * y[t - 1], sd = cond_sd)
    # Update Y | X
    y[t] <- rnorm(1, mean = rho * x[t], sd = cond_sd)
  }

  cbind(x = x, y = y)
}

rho_high <- 0.9
chain_high <- gibbs_bivariate(n_steps = 10000, rho = rho_high, start = c(-3, 3))

# Diagnostics
burn_in <- 1000
samples_high <- chain_high[(burn_in + 1):nrow(chain_high), ]

par(mfrow = c(3, 1), mar = c(4, 4, 3, 1))

# Trace plots

     main = paste0("Gibbs trace (X), rho = ", rho_high),
     xlab = "Iteration", ylab = "X")
abline(v = burn_in, col = "grey", lty = 2)

9.4.2 Rotated coordinates for faster mixing

The slow mixing arises because the Gibbs updates align with the coordinate axes, while the posterior concentrates along the diagonal. We can decouple the correlation by rotating coordinates. Let \(u = \frac{x + y}{\sqrt{2}}\) and \(v = \frac{x - y}{\sqrt{2}}\). For a standard bivariate Gaussian, the joint distribution of \((u, v)\) is: \[ u \sim N(0, (1 + \rho)), \quad v \sim N(0, (1 - \rho)), \] and \(u\) and \(v\) are independent.

Show code 
gibbs_rotated <- function(n_steps, rho, start = c(0, 0)) {
  # Transform starting point to rotated coordinates
  u_start <- (start[1] + start[2]) / sqrt(2)
  v_start <- (start[1] - start[2]) / sqrt(2)

  u <- numeric(n_steps)
  v <- numeric(n_steps)
  u[1] <- u_start
  v[1] <- v_start

  sd_u <- sqrt(1 + rho)
  sd_v <- sqrt(1 - rho)

  for (t in 2:n_steps) {
    # u and v are now independent, so Gibbs reduces to independent sampling
    u[t] <- rnorm(1, mean = 0, sd = sd_u)
    v[t] <- rnorm(1, mean = 0, sd = sd_v)
  }

  # Transform back to original coordinates
  x <- (u + v) / sqrt(2)
  y <- (u - v) / sqrt(2)

  cbind(x = x, y = y)
}

chain_rotated <- gibbs_rotated(n_steps = 10000, rho = rho_high, start = c(-3, 3))
samples_rotated <- chain_rotated[(burn_in + 1):nrow(chain_rotated), ]

9.5 Comparison: independence sampler vs. Gibbs sampler vs. random-walk Metropolis

The three samplers embody different philosophies and trade-offs:

Aspect Independence Sampler Gibbs Sampler Random-Walk MH
Proposal \(q(x')\) (global, fixed) \(p(\theta_i \mid \theta_{-i}, D)\) \(x' \sim N(x, \Sigma)\)
Move type Global jumps anywhere Coordinate-wise updates Local steps
Acceptance MH ratio (often low) Always 1 (deterministic) Variable (tunable)
Main requirement Good global approximation Tractable conditionals Reasonable scale estimate
Fails when \(q\) poorly matches \(\pi\) Strong parameter correlations Proposal scale wrong
Autocorrelation Low if \(q \approx \pi\) High if correlated structure Moderate (tunable)
Dimension scaling Exponential decay \(\sim \exp(-cd)\) Polynomial (depends on correlation) Exponential decay
Typical use Approximate posteriors Conjugate/hierarchical models Flexible fallback

Remark. In practice, these are rarely used in isolation. Modern samplers often combine ideas:

  • Metropolis-within-Gibbs: Use Gibbs for tractable coordinates, MH for intractable ones.
  • Block Gibbs: Group correlated coordinates and sample jointly to reduce autocorrelation.
  • Adaptive proposals: Learn a proposal from preliminary runs (adaptive MCMC).
  • HMC within Gibbs: Combine gradient-based efficient exploration with block structure.

The choice depends on the model structure, the availability of efficient samplers for conditionals, and computational constraints.

9.6 Summary

The independence sampler represents a global exploration strategy: it can jump anywhere but only works well if the proposal closely approximates the target. Its acceptance probability directly reflects the importance weights, making it a bridge between importance sampling and MCMC. However, the exponential degradation of weights in high dimensions—a manifestation of concentration of measure—limits its applicability to high-dimensional problems.

The Gibbs sampler exploits model structure by updating parameters from their full conditionals. Every step is accepted, eliminating the rejection waste of Metropolis algorithms. However, when parameters are strongly correlated, the chain mixes slowly because coordinate-wise moves struggle to explore elongated posterior geometries efficiently. Rotation to decorrelated coordinates, block updates, or auxiliary variable methods can dramatically improve performance.

Both methods highlight the central tension in MCMC: acceptance rates, exploration speed, and computational tractability are not independent. A low acceptance rate signals a poorly matched proposal; a high rate (even 100%) does not guarantee fast mixing if the effective step size is small. Successful MCMC practice requires diagnosing mixing, adapting proposals, and sometimes redesigning the parameterization to align with the posterior structure.

## Introduction

The Metropolis–Hastings algorithm, introduced in the previous chapter, is a general framework for constructing Markov chains with a target stationary distribution. However, the choice of proposal distribution critically affects both computational efficiency and the quality of samples. In this chapter, we explore two important special cases that reveal a fundamental trade-off in MCMC design: global vs. local exploration, and the role of acceptance rates in controlling mixing.

The **independence sampler** uses a fixed global proposal, independent of the current state. This allows long-distance jumps but requires the proposal to approximate the target well. The **Gibbs sampler**, by contrast, updates coordinates sequentially using exact full conditional distributions, guaranteeing acceptance but potentially moving slowly through high-dimensional spaces when parameters are correlated. Both methods illustrate how MCMC designers must balance the competing demands of efficient exploration and computational tractability.

## The independence sampler: global proposals

### Idea and motivation

In a random-walk Metropolis algorithm, the proposal distribution depends on the current state:
$$
q(x' \mid x) \propto \exp\left( -\frac{\|x' - x\|^2}{2\sigma^2} \right).
$$

This locality allows the chain to take small, tentative steps and gradually explore the target space. However, if the proposal variance is too small, the chain mixes slowly; if too large, it wastes computation on distant, low-probability regions.

The **independence sampler** takes a different approach: the proposal is **independent of the current state**,
$$
q(x') = q(x' \mid x),
$$
depending only on $x'$. This allows the chain to jump from anywhere to anywhere in a single step, potentially escaping local modes and exploring the target space more globally.

### Acceptance probability

For the Metropolis–Hastings algorithm, the acceptance probability is
$$
\alpha(x \to x') = \min\left(1, \frac{\pi(x') \, q(x \mid x')}{\pi(x) \, q(x' \mid x)}\right).
$$

For the independence sampler, $q(x \mid x') = q(x)$ and $q(x' \mid x) = q(x')$, so
$$
\alpha(x \to x') = \min\left(1, \frac{\pi(x') \, q(x)}{\pi(x) \, q(x')}\right).
$$

Rearranging in terms of **importance weights** $w(x) = \frac{\pi(x)}{q(x)}$:
$$
\alpha(x \to x') = \min\left(1, \frac{w(x')}{w(x)}\right).
$$

::::: {.note}
The acceptance probability compares the importance weights at the proposed and current states. If $w(x')$ is larger, the proposal is always accepted. If $w(x')$ is smaller, it is accepted with probability $w(x') / w(x)$. When the proposal closely matches the target, weights are nearly constant and acceptance is high.
:::::

### Connection to importance sampling and effective sample size

The independence sampler inherits its theoretical motivation from **importance sampling**. In importance sampling, we approximate an expectation
$$
E_\pi[f(x)] = \int f(x) \, \pi(x) \, dx
$$
by sampling from a simpler proposal $q$ and weighting:
$$
\widehat{E_\pi}[f(x)] = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^N w(x_i) f(x_i)}{\sum_{i=1}^N w(x_i)}, \quad w(x_i) = \frac{\pi(x_i)}{q(x_i)}.
$$

The **effective sample size (ESS)** reflects how much information the weighted samples contain. If weights are unequal—as they are when $q$ deviates from $\pi$—the ESS is smaller than $N$:
$$
\text{ESS} = \frac{1}{\sum_i (w_i / \bar{w})^2}.
$$

When $w(x)$ varies widely, many samples receive vanishingly small weight, and the variance of the estimator becomes very large.

The independence sampler's acceptance rate is directly related to the relative variation in weights. A narrow proposal (or a poorly-matched proposal) produces highly variable weights, leading to low acceptance and slow exploration. This illustrates the **narrow-proposal failure mode**: if the proposal is too concentrated, most proposed states fall in high-weight regions, but the chain accepts rarely and gets stuck.

### When does the independence sampler work well?

The key requirement is that **$q$ must approximate $\pi$ globally**. A sufficient ergodicity condition is
$$
\pi(x) \leq C \, q(x) \quad \forall x
$$
for some finite constant $C > 0$. This ensures the chain does not get trapped in regions of arbitrarily high relative weight.

The independence sampler works best when:
- We have a good global approximation to the posterior, e.g., from a **Laplace approximation**, **variational inference**, or other fast approximate methods.
- The target distribution is unimodal or has well-separated modes that the proposal can accommodate.

It fails when:
- The proposal underestimates the tails or misses modes of the target.
- States with large $\pi(x) / q(x)$ are rare, so the chain cannot easily reach them.

### Curse of dimensionality for independence sampling

A profound limitation emerges in high dimensions. Suppose $\log w(x) = \log \pi(x) - \log q(x)$ decomposes as a sum of roughly independent contributions:
$$
\log w(x) \approx \sum_{i=1}^d \Delta_i(x_i),
$$
where each $\Delta_i$ represents the local mismatch between $\pi$ and $q$ in coordinate $i$.

Even if each $\Delta_i$ is small (order $O(1)$), the total deviation is $\log w(x) = O(d)$, so $w(x) = \exp(O(d))$. The weights vary **exponentially** in dimension.

::::: {.definition}
**Concentration of measure**: In high dimensions, probability mass concentrates on thin regions. For a standard Gaussian $\pi = N(0, I_d)$, the norm $\|x\|$ concentrates around $\sqrt{d}$. Even a slightly mis-scaled proposal $q$ will place its probability mass in regions irrelevant to $\pi$, causing acceptance to decay exponentially with $d$.
:::::

This is the motivation for **local proposals** (random-walk Metropolis), **gradient-based methods** (Hamiltonian MC), and **coordinate-wise samplers** (Gibbs sampling).

## The Gibbs sampler: coordinate-wise updates

### Full conditional distributions

In many Bayesian models, jointly sampling from $p(\theta \mid D)$ where $\theta = (\theta_1, \ldots, \theta_d)$ is intractable, but sampling from each **full conditional distribution** is simple.

::::: {.definition}
The **full conditional distribution** of $\theta_i$ is
$$
p(\theta_i \mid \theta_{-i}, D),
$$
where $\theta_{-i} = (\theta_1, \ldots, \theta_{i-1}, \theta_{i+1}, \ldots, \theta_d)$ denotes all other parameters. It is obtained by treating $\theta_{-i}$ as fixed and proportional to the joint posterior:
$$
p(\theta_i \mid \theta_{-i}, D) \propto p(\theta_i, \theta_{-i} \mid D).
$$
:::::

### Algorithm: deterministic and random scan

**Gibbs sampling** iteratively samples each parameter from its full conditional:

::::: {.theorem}
**Gibbs Sampler Algorithm**

Initialize $\theta^{(0)} = (\theta_1^{(0)}, \ldots, \theta_d^{(0)})$.

For $t = 1, 2, \ldots$:

1. $\theta_1^{(t)} \sim p(\theta_1 \mid \theta_2^{(t-1)}, \ldots, \theta_d^{(t-1)}, D)$
2. $\theta_2^{(t)} \sim p(\theta_2 \mid \theta_1^{(t)}, \theta_3^{(t-1)}, \ldots, \theta_d^{(t-1)}, D)$
3. $\vdots$
4. $\theta_d^{(t)} \sim p(\theta_d \mid \theta_1^{(t)}, \ldots, \theta_{d-1}^{(t)}, D)$

One complete cycle of all $d$ updates constitutes one iteration of the Gibbs sampler.

**Variants**: The **random-scan Gibbs sampler** updates coordinates in random order instead of a fixed sequence. For convergence, both deterministic and random scans are ergodic under mild conditions.
:::::

### Gibbs as a special case of Metropolis–Hastings

Gibbs sampling can be viewed as Metropolis–Hastings with the proposal being the full conditional itself. At step $t$, we propose a new value for $\theta_i$ by sampling from
$$
q(\theta_i' \mid \theta_i^{(t-1)}, \theta_{-i}^{(t)}) = p(\theta_i' \mid \theta_{-i}^{(t)}, D).
$$

The acceptance probability is
$$
\alpha(\theta^{(t-1)} \to \theta^{(t)}) = \min\left(1, \frac{p(\theta_i^{(t)}, \theta_{-i}^{(t)} \mid D) \, p(\theta_i^{(t-1)} \mid \theta_{-i}^{(t)}, D)}{p(\theta_i^{(t-1)}, \theta_{-i}^{(t-1)} \mid D) \, p(\theta_i^{(t)} \mid \theta_{-i}^{(t)}, D)}\right).
$$

Using the factorization $p(\theta_i, \theta_{-i} \mid D) = p(\theta_i \mid \theta_{-i}, D) \, p(\theta_{-i} \mid D)$, the ratio simplifies:
$$
\frac{p(\theta_i^{(t)} \mid \theta_{-i}^{(t)}, D) \, p(\theta_{-i}^{(t)} \mid D) \, p(\theta_i^{(t-1)} \mid \theta_{-i}^{(t)}, D)}{p(\theta_i^{(t-1)} \mid \theta_{-i}^{(t-1)}, D) \, p(\theta_{-i}^{(t-1)} \mid D) \, p(\theta_i^{(t)} \mid \theta_{-i}^{(t)}, D)} = 1.
$$

Therefore, $\alpha = 1$: **every Gibbs step is accepted**.

::::: {.note}
Acceptance is guaranteed, but samples are still **correlated**. The chain does not jump to a new state at each step; instead, it updates coordinates sequentially, and autocorrelation arises because coordinates are strongly dependent.
:::::

### Detailed balance verification for Gibbs

For the full Gibbs update (one complete sweep of all $d$ coordinates), detailed balance holds with respect to the joint posterior. We verify that
$$
p(\theta \mid D) \, T(\theta \to \theta') = p(\theta' \mid D) \, T(\theta' \to \theta),
$$
where $T$ is the transition kernel of one complete sweep.

For a single coordinate update $T_i$ applied to coordinate $i$, left-multiply by $p(\theta \mid D)$ and expand using the factorization:
\begin{align}
p(\theta \mid D) \, T_i(\theta \to \theta') &= p(\theta_i, \theta_{-i} \mid D) \, p(\theta_i' \mid \theta_{-i}, D) \, \delta(\theta_{-i}' - \theta_{-i})\\
&= p(\theta_i \mid \theta_{-i}, D) \, p(\theta_{-i} \mid D) \, p(\theta_i' \mid \theta_{-i}, D) \, \delta(\theta_{-i}' - \theta_{-i}).
\end{align}

Permuting terms and using $p(\theta_i \mid \theta_{-i}, D) = p(\theta_i \mid \theta_{-i}', D)$ (since $\theta_{-i} = \theta_{-i}'$):
$$
= p(\theta_i' \mid \theta_{-i}, D) \, p(\theta_{-i} \mid D) \, p(\theta_i \mid \theta_{-i}, D) \, \delta(\theta_{-i}' - \theta_{-i}) = p(\theta' \mid D) \, T_i(\theta' \to \theta).
$$

Thus each coordinate-wise update preserves the posterior. Since the full sweep is a composition of such updates, it also satisfies detailed balance.

### Mixing and slow mixing from correlation

Unlike the independence sampler, Gibbs always accepts. However, this does not imply fast mixing. When parameters are strongly correlated—as in high-dimensional hierarchical models or when the posterior is elongated along a narrow ridge—coordinate-wise updates must take many small steps to explore the space.

::::: {.example}
**Bivariate Gaussian with high correlation**

Consider a bivariate normal distribution:
$$
\begin{pmatrix} X \\ Y \end{pmatrix} \sim N\left( \begin{pmatrix} 0 \\ 0 \end{pmatrix}, \begin{pmatrix} 1 & \rho \\ \rho & 1 \end{pmatrix} \right).
$$

The full conditionals are:
$$
X \mid Y = y \sim N(\rho y, 1 - \rho^2), \quad Y \mid X = x \sim N(\rho x, 1 - \rho^2).
$$

When $\rho = 0.9$, the posterior concentrates on a narrow diagonal ridge. A Gibbs step first moves horizontally (X-update), then vertically (Y-update), taking many iterations to traverse the ridge. In contrast, updating both coordinates jointly (e.g., via a rotated-coordinate Gibbs or HMC) moves along the ridge in fewer steps.
:::::

### Metropolis-within-Gibbs and block Gibbs

When the full conditional for a component is intractable, we can replace the Gibbs step with a Metropolis–Hastings step:

::::: {.definition}
**Metropolis-within-Gibbs**: Replace the Gibbs step for $\theta_i$ with a Metropolis–Hastings move using a local proposal $q_i(\theta_i' \mid \theta_i)$. The acceptance probability is
$$
\alpha_i(\theta_i \mid \theta_{-i}) = \min\left(1, \frac{p(\theta_i' \mid \theta_{-i}, D) \, q_i(\theta_i \mid \theta_i')}{p(\theta_i \mid \theta_{-i}, D) \, q_i(\theta_i' \mid \theta_i)}\right).
$$

The chain remains valid (it still has $p(\theta \mid D)$ as stationary distribution), but some steps are rejected.
:::::

Similarly, **block Gibbs sampling** updates multiple coordinates jointly:
$$
(\theta_1, \ldots, \theta_k) \sim p(\theta_1, \ldots, \theta_k \mid \theta_{k+1}, \ldots, \theta_d, D).
$$

This is effective when joint conditionals are tractable, as it can dramatically reduce autocorrelation in highly correlated subsets.

## Worked example: Gibbs sampling for a bivariate Gaussian

We now demonstrate Gibbs sampling on the bivariate Gaussian with $\rho = 0.9$ to illustrate slow mixing due to correlation, and show how a simple coordinate rotation accelerates mixing.

### Slow mixing at high correlation

```{r}
#| label: lst-gibbs-bivariate-high-corr
#| lst-cap: "Gibbs bivariate high corr"
#| fig-cap: "Gibbs sampling trace plots for a bivariate Gaussian with ρ = 0.9. The X-trace (top) shows high autocorrelation; the Y-trace (middle) is even slower due to dependence on X. The scatter plot (bottom) reveals the narrow diagonal structure."

set.seed(42)

gibbs_bivariate <- function(n_steps, rho, start = c(0, 0)) {
  x <- numeric(n_steps)
  y <- numeric(n_steps)
  x[1] <- start[1]
  y[1] <- start[2]
  cond_sd <- sqrt(1 - rho^2)

  for (t in 2:n_steps) {
    # Update X | Y
    x[t] <- rnorm(1, mean = rho * y[t - 1], sd = cond_sd)
    # Update Y | X
    y[t] <- rnorm(1, mean = rho * x[t], sd = cond_sd)
  }

  cbind(x = x, y = y)
}

rho_high <- 0.9
chain_high <- gibbs_bivariate(n_steps = 10000, rho = rho_high, start = c(-3, 3))

# Diagnostics
burn_in <- 1000
samples_high <- chain_high[(burn_in + 1):nrow(chain_high), ]

par(mfrow = c(3, 1), mar = c(4, 4, 3, 1))

# Trace plots
plot(chain_high[, "x"], type = "l", lwd = 0.8, col = "#d62728",
     main = paste0("Gibbs trace (X), rho = ", rho_high),
     xlab = "Iteration", ylab = "X")
abline(v = burn_in, col = "grey", lty = 2)

plot(chain_high[, "y"], type = "l", lwd = 0.8, col = "#1f77b4",
     main = "Gibbs trace (Y)", xlab = "Iteration", ylab = "Y")
abline(v = burn_in, col = "grey", lty = 2)

# Scatter of posterior samples
plot(samples_high[, "x"], samples_high[, "y"], pch = 16, cex = 0.3,
     col = rgb(0, 0, 0, 0.5), xlab = "X", ylab = "Y",
     main = "Posterior samples after burn-in")
```

### Rotated coordinates for faster mixing

The slow mixing arises because the Gibbs updates align with the coordinate axes, while the posterior concentrates along the diagonal. We can decouple the correlation by rotating coordinates. Let $u = \frac{x + y}{\sqrt{2}}$ and $v = \frac{x - y}{\sqrt{2}}$. For a standard bivariate Gaussian, the joint distribution of $(u, v)$ is:
$$
u \sim N(0, (1 + \rho)), \quad v \sim N(0, (1 - \rho)),
$$
and $u$ and $v$ are **independent**.

```{r}
#| label: lst-gibbs-rotated
#| lst-cap: "Gibbs rotated"
#| fig-cap: "Gibbs sampling in rotated coordinates. With ρ = 0.9, rotated coordinates decouple the posterior into independent components, yielding much lower autocorrelation and faster mixing."

gibbs_rotated <- function(n_steps, rho, start = c(0, 0)) {
  # Transform starting point to rotated coordinates
  u_start <- (start[1] + start[2]) / sqrt(2)
  v_start <- (start[1] - start[2]) / sqrt(2)

  u <- numeric(n_steps)
  v <- numeric(n_steps)
  u[1] <- u_start
  v[1] <- v_start

  sd_u <- sqrt(1 + rho)
  sd_v <- sqrt(1 - rho)

  for (t in 2:n_steps) {
    # u and v are now independent, so Gibbs reduces to independent sampling
    u[t] <- rnorm(1, mean = 0, sd = sd_u)
    v[t] <- rnorm(1, mean = 0, sd = sd_v)
  }

  # Transform back to original coordinates
  x <- (u + v) / sqrt(2)
  y <- (u - v) / sqrt(2)

  cbind(x = x, y = y)
}

chain_rotated <- gibbs_rotated(n_steps = 10000, rho = rho_high, start = c(-3, 3))
samples_rotated <- chain_rotated[(burn_in + 1):nrow(chain_rotated), ]

par(mfrow = c(3, 1), mar = c(4, 4, 3, 1))

# Trace plots for rotated coordinates
plot(chain_rotated[, "x"], type = "l", lwd = 0.8, col = "#2ca02c",
     main = "Rotated Gibbs trace (X after rotation)",
     xlab = "Iteration", ylab = "X")
abline(v = burn_in, col = "grey", lty = 2)

plot(chain_rotated[, "y"], type = "l", lwd = 0.8, col = "#ff7f0e",
     main = "Rotated Gibbs trace (Y after rotation)",
     xlab = "Iteration", ylab = "Y")
abline(v = burn_in, col = "grey", lty = 2)

# Scatter plot
plot(samples_rotated[, "x"], samples_rotated[, "y"], pch = 16, cex = 0.3,
     col = rgb(0, 0, 0, 0.5), xlab = "X", ylab = "Y",
     main = "Posterior samples (rotated Gibbs)")

par(mfrow = c(1, 1))
```

### Autocorrelation comparison

```{r}

#| lst-cap: "Gibbs acf comparison"
#| fig-cap: "Autocorrelation functions for X in both samplers. Standard Gibbs (dashed red) shows high persistence; rotated Gibbs (solid green) decorrelates in one or two lags."
```

## Comparison: independence sampler vs. Gibbs sampler vs. random-walk Metropolis

The three samplers embody different philosophies and trade-offs:

| **Aspect** | **Independence Sampler** | **Gibbs Sampler** | **Random-Walk MH** |
|:---|:---|:---|:---|
| **Proposal** | $q(x')$ (global, fixed) | $p(\theta_i \mid \theta_{-i}, D)$ | $x' \sim N(x, \Sigma)$ |
| **Move type** | Global jumps anywhere | Coordinate-wise updates | Local steps |
| **Acceptance** | MH ratio (often low) | Always 1 (deterministic) | Variable (tunable) |
| **Main requirement** | Good global approximation | Tractable conditionals | Reasonable scale estimate |
| **Fails when** | $q$ poorly matches $\pi$ | Strong parameter correlations | Proposal scale wrong |
| **Autocorrelation** | Low if $q \approx \pi$ | High if correlated structure | Moderate (tunable) |
| **Dimension scaling** | Exponential decay $\sim \exp(-cd)$ | Polynomial (depends on correlation) | Exponential decay |
| **Typical use** | Approximate posteriors | Conjugate/hierarchical models | Flexible fallback |

::::: {.remark}
In practice, these are rarely used in isolation. Modern samplers often combine ideas:

- **Metropolis-within-Gibbs**: Use Gibbs for tractable coordinates, MH for intractable ones.
- **Block Gibbs**: Group correlated coordinates and sample jointly to reduce autocorrelation.
- **Adaptive proposals**: Learn a proposal from preliminary runs (adaptive MCMC).
- **HMC within Gibbs**: Combine gradient-based efficient exploration with block structure.

The choice depends on the model structure, the availability of efficient samplers for conditionals, and computational constraints.
:::::

## Summary

The **independence sampler** represents a global exploration strategy: it can jump anywhere but only works well if the proposal closely approximates the target. Its acceptance probability directly reflects the importance weights, making it a bridge between importance sampling and MCMC. However, the exponential degradation of weights in high dimensions—a manifestation of concentration of measure—limits its applicability to high-dimensional problems.

The **Gibbs sampler** exploits model structure by updating parameters from their full conditionals. Every step is accepted, eliminating the rejection waste of Metropolis algorithms. However, when parameters are strongly correlated, the chain mixes slowly because coordinate-wise moves struggle to explore elongated posterior geometries efficiently. Rotation to decorrelated coordinates, block updates, or auxiliary variable methods can dramatically improve performance.

Both methods highlight the central tension in MCMC: acceptance rates, exploration speed, and computational tractability are not independent. A low acceptance rate signals a poorly matched proposal; a high rate (even 100%) does not guarantee fast mixing if the effective step size is small. Successful MCMC practice requires diagnosing mixing, adapting proposals, and sometimes redesigning the parameterization to align with the posterior structure.
