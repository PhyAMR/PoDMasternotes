5.1 Introduction

When faced with multiple competing hypotheses or models, scientists need a principled way to compare them in light of observed data. The Bayesian framework provides an elegant and automatic way to do this: the posterior odds ratio, built from the Bayes factor and the prior odds. Unlike frequentist hypothesis tests that focus on rejecting a single null hypothesis, Bayesian model comparison directly assigns probabilities to models and penalizes unjustified complexity.

The key insight—Occam’s razor—emerges automatically from the mathematics. Complex models that require fine-tuning of their parameters to fit data are penalized, while simple models that make robust predictions across a wide range of parameter values are favored. This chapter develops the theory and practice of Bayesian model selection, from the basic posterior odds formula to sophisticated applications in scientific inference.

State Bayes’ theorem for a continuous parameter \(\theta\) and data \(D\). Explain the meaning of likelihood, prior, posterior, and evidence. Why is the evidence often omitted in parameter estimation but essential in model comparison?

Solution. Bayes’ theorem reads \[p(\theta \mid D, H) = \frac{p(D \mid \theta, H)\,p(\theta \mid H)}{p(D \mid H)}, \qquad p(D \mid H) = \int p(D \mid \theta, H)\,p(\theta \mid H)\,d\theta.\] Here \(p(D \mid \theta, H)\) is the likelihood, \(p(\theta \mid H)\) is the prior, \(p(\theta \mid D, H)\) is the posterior, and \(p(D \mid H)\) is the evidence (or marginal likelihood).

In parameter estimation within a fixed model \(H\), the evidence is independent of \(\theta\) and acts only as a normalisation constant, so one writes \(p(\theta \mid D, H) \propto p(D \mid \theta, H)\,p(\theta \mid H)\). In model comparison, however, different models have different evidences, and the ratio of evidences is precisely the Bayes factor — so \(p(D \mid H)\) cannot be dropped.

5.2 Posterior odds and the Bayes factor

Suppose we have two mutually exclusive and exhaustive hypotheses \(H_0\) and \(H_1\), and we observe data \(D\). Bayes’ theorem tells us:

\[P(H_i | D) = \frac{P(D | H_i) P(H_i)}{P(D)}\]

where \(P(D) = P(D | H_0) P(H_0) + P(D | H_1) P(H_1)\) is the total probability of the data, obtained by marginalizing over both hypotheses.

The posterior odds ratio is the ratio of the posterior probabilities:

\[R = \frac{P(H_1 | D)}{P(H_0 | D)}\]

This directly quantifies which hypothesis is more likely given the data. Values \(R > 1\) favor \(H_1\), while \(R < 1\) favor \(H_0\).

Now divide the posterior for \(H_1\) by the posterior for \(H_0\):

\[R = \frac{P(H_1 | D)}{P(H_0 | D)} = \frac{P(D | H_1)}{P(D | H_0)} \cdot \frac{P(H_1)}{P(H_0)}\]

The first ratio is the Bayes factor; the second is the prior odds.

The Bayes factor is:

\[\text{BF}_{10} = \frac{P(D | H_1)}{P(D | H_0)}\]

It expresses how much the data shifts the odds between the two models, independent of prior beliefs.

Thus we have the fundamental equation of Bayesian model comparison:

\[R = \text{BF}_{10} \times \frac{P(H_1)}{P(H_0)}\]

Under uniform priors—when \(P(H_1) = P(H_0) = 1/2\)—the posterior odds equal the Bayes factor. This is why some practitioners use \(\text{BF} > 1\) as a criterion for favoring \(H_1\) “objectively,” removing apparent subjectivity of the prior. However, this merely replaces one prior assumption (about models) with another (equal prior probability).

Consider two models \(H_0\) and \(H_1\). Define the Bayes factor in favour of \(H_1\) against \(H_0\), both for parameter-free models and for parametric models. Show how it relates prior odds and posterior odds, and explain the role of the marginal likelihood.

Solution. The Bayes factor is \[\text{BF}_{10} = \frac{p(D \mid H_1)}{p(D \mid H_0)}.\] By Bayes’ theorem, the posterior odds satisfy \[\frac{p(H_1 \mid D)}{p(H_0 \mid D)} = \frac{p(D \mid H_1)\,p(H_1)}{p(D \mid H_0)\,p(H_0)} = \text{BF}_{10}\,\frac{p(H_1)}{p(H_0)}.\] The Bayes factor is therefore the multiplicative factor by which the data update prior odds into posterior odds. If \(\text{BF}_{10} > 1\), the data favour \(H_1\) over \(H_0\) relative to the prior odds; if \(\text{BF}_{10} < 1\), they favour \(H_0\).

For parametric models \(H_j\) with parameters \(\theta_j\) and prior \(p(\theta_j \mid H_j)\), \[p(D \mid H_j) = \int p(D \mid \theta_j, H_j)\,p(\theta_j \mid H_j)\,d\theta_j,\] so the evidence averages the likelihood over the prior. This integral automatically embeds an Occam penalty: models that spread prior mass over regions not supported by the data are penalised.

Two quick consequences round off the basic machinery before we move to the parametric case.

  • Posterior probabilities from the odds. Once \(R\) is known, the model probabilities are recovered from \(P(H_0 \mid D) = 1/(1+R)\) and \(P(H_1 \mid D) = R/(1+R)\).
  • Jeffreys’ interpretation scale. Jeffreys (1961) proposed a rough scale for the magnitude of a Bayes factor: \(\text{BF}_{10}\) in \((1,3]\) is weak evidence for \(H_1\), \((3,10]\) moderate, \((10,30]\) strong, \((30,100]\) very strong, and above \(100\) decisive (reverse the ratios for evidence against \(H_1\)). The cutoffs are conventional and context-dependent; report the magnitude alongside its uncertainty and the cost of being wrong rather than treating any threshold as binding.

Jeffreys’ scale is a communication device, not a decision rule. Combine it with a sensitivity analysis (see below) before reporting “strong” or “decisive” evidence.

5.3 The marginal likelihood and Occam’s razor

The Bayes factor depends on the marginal likelihood (also called the model evidence), the probability of the data averaged over all possible parameter values under that model:

\[P(D | H_i) = \int P(D | \theta, H_i) \pi(\theta | H_i) \, d\theta\]

Here \(\pi(\theta | H_i)\) is the prior distribution over parameters under hypothesis \(H_i\), and \(P(D | \theta, H_i)\) is the likelihood.

Marginal likelihoods automatically encode a penalty for model complexity. A complex model with many parameters typically spreads its prior mass over a large region of parameter space. Only a small region of this parameter space will fit the data well. When we integrate over all parameters, the contribution from the region that fits data is small relative to the total prior volume, yielding a low marginal likelihood—even if the model achieves a higher best-fit likelihood than a simpler competitor.

To see this concretely, consider two models: - \(H_0\): a simple model with a fixed parameter value \(\theta_0\). - \(H_1\): a complex model with parameter \(\theta\) uniformly distributed over the range \([a, b]\).

If both models fit the data equally well at their best-fit parameter values, then \(P(D | \theta_0, H_0) \approx P(D | \hat{\theta}, H_1)\) where \(\hat{\theta}\) is the MLE under \(H_1\).

But: \[P(D | H_0) = P(D | \theta_0, H_0)\] \[P(D | H_1) \approx \frac{1}{b-a} \int_a^b P(D | \theta, H_1) \, d\theta \approx P(D | \hat{\theta}, H_1) \cdot \frac{\Delta\theta_{\text{eff}}}{b-a}\]

where \(\Delta\theta_{\text{eff}}\) is the effective width of the likelihood peak. If \(\Delta\theta_{\text{eff}} \ll b - a\), then \(P(D | H_1) \ll P(D | H_0)\) despite \(H_1\) fitting the data equally well locally. The Bayes factor penalizes \(H_1\) for having a large prior parameter space most of which is inconsistent with the data.

This intuition becomes quantitative through Laplace’s approximation, which isolates the penalty as a distinct Occam factor.

  • Laplace approximation of the evidence. For continuous parameters, assume \(P(D | \theta, H)\) is sharply peaked around its maximum \(\hat{\theta}\) and the prior is smooth on the scale of that peak. Then \(P(D | H) \approx P(D | \hat{\theta}, H) \cdot \pi(\hat{\theta}) \cdot \Delta\theta_{\text{eff}}\), factoring the evidence into a best-fit likelihood and a prior-volume term.
  • Occam factor. The combination \(\text{Occam} = \pi(\hat{\theta}) \cdot \Delta\theta_{\text{eff}}\) is the prior probability of the region of parameter space that actually fits the data. For a \(d\)-dimensional model with roughly uniform prior of range \(L_i\) in each direction, \(\text{Occam} \approx \prod_i (\Delta\theta_{\text{eff},i}/L_i)\): narrow likelihood peaks inside wide priors are heavily penalised.
  • Decomposed Bayes factor. Combining the two ingredients gives \[\text{BF}_{10} = \frac{P(D | \hat{\theta}_1, H_1)}{P(D | \hat{\theta}_0, H_0)} \cdot \frac{\text{Occam}_1}{\text{Occam}_0}.\] The first ratio favours the more flexible model; the second penalises it for complexity. The balance — arising purely from integration, not from any ad-hoc penalty — determines the winner.

5.4 Example: Testing coin fairness

This classic example from Sivia (2006) illustrates the full machinery. Suppose we flip a coin \(n = 250\) times and observe \(h = 140\) heads. We compare:

  • \(H_0\): The coin is fair (\(\theta = 0.5\)).
  • \(H_1\): The coin has unknown bias \(\theta \sim \text{Beta}(1,1)\) (uniform on \([0,1]\)).

The likelihood for \(h\) heads in \(n\) flips is: \[P(D | \theta) = \binom{n}{h} \theta^h (1-\theta)^{n-h}\]

Under \(H_0\): \[P(D | H_0) = \binom{n}{h} (0.5)^n\]

Under \(H_1\), with the Beta(1,1) prior: \[P(D | H_1) = \int_0^1 \binom{n}{h} \theta^h (1-\theta)^{n-h} \, d\theta = \binom{n}{h} \cdot \frac{h! (n-h)!}{(n+1)!}\]

Therefore: \[\text{BF}_{10} = \frac{P(D | H_1)}{P(D | H_0)} = \frac{h! (n-h)!}{(n+1)!} \cdot 2^n\]

With \(n = 250\) and \(h = 140\): \[\text{BF}_{10} = \frac{140! \cdot 110!}{251!} \cdot 2^{250} \approx 0.044\]

This strong evidence against the coin being fair makes intuitive sense: observing 140 heads in 250 flips is notably different from 50-50 randomness.

Now suppose we use a wider prior for \(H_1\): \(\theta \sim \text{Beta}(\alpha, \alpha)\) for some \(\alpha > 1\). This concentrates the prior more around \(\theta = 0.5\). The Occam factor shrinks, raising the Bayes factor. Conversely, using \(\alpha < 1\) (a U-shaped prior favoring extreme values) lowers it.

This illustrates a key insight: the Bayes factor is not prior-independent. However, it responds sensibly: wider priors (more agnostic) increase the Occam penalty, favoring simpler models; narrower priors (more peaked) decrease the penalty. A practitioner should explore this sensitivity and report it alongside point estimates.

# Coin fairness example: n = 250 flips, h = 140 heads
# H0: theta = 0.5 (fair coin)
# H1: theta ~ Beta(alpha, alpha) with varying alpha

n <- 250
h <- 140

# Likelihood under H0
log_likelihood_H0 <- h * log(0.5) + (n - h) * log(0.5) + lchoose(n, h)

# Marginal likelihood under H1 with Beta(alpha, alpha) prior
# For Beta-Binomial model: P(D|H1) = C(n,h) * Beta(h+alpha, n-h+alpha) / Beta(alpha, alpha)
bayes_factor_beta_binomial <- function(n, h, alpha) {
  log_ll_H1 <- lchoose(n, h) +
    lbeta(h + alpha, n - h + alpha) -
    lbeta(alpha, alpha)
  exp(log_ll_H1 - log_likelihood_H0)
}

5.5 Lindley’s paradox

A famous puzzle arises when comparing a point null hypothesis (e.g., \(\theta = 0\)) to a broad alternative. As sample size increases, frequentist p-values eventually fall below any threshold (e.g., 0.05), calling for rejection of the null. Yet the Bayes factor may simultaneously favor the null, if the alternative prior is very diffuse.

Suppose we test \(H_0: \mu = 0\) versus \(H_1: \mu \sim N(0, \sigma_\mu^2)\) on data \(x_1, \ldots, x_n \sim N(\mu, 1)\). With a very large sample and \(\bar{x}\) only slightly away from zero, a frequentist test will reject \(H_0\) (small p-value), but if \(\sigma_\mu\) is very large (vague prior on \(\mu\)), the Occam penalty under \(H_1\) keeps the Bayes factor favoring \(H_0\).

The resolution is that the “paradox” reflects a real difference in how the two frameworks treat vague alternatives. Frequentist tests focus on the likelihood under \(H_0\). Bayesian tests also account for where the alternative prior was placed. This is not an error but a feature: it discourages chasing phantom effects in vague parameter spaces.

Let \(D = \{x_1, \ldots, x_n\}\) be i.i.d. observations from \(\mathcal{N}(\mu, \sigma^2)\) with known variance \(\sigma^2\). Compare \[H_0: \mu = 0, \qquad H_1: \mu \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \tau^2).\] Compute \(\text{BF}_{10}\) in terms of the sample mean \(\bar x\).

Solution. The sample mean \(\bar x\) is a sufficient statistic for \(\mu\) with \(\bar x \mid \mu \sim \mathcal{N}(\mu, \sigma^2/n)\). Under \(H_0\), \[\bar x \mid H_0 \sim \mathcal{N}\!\left(0, \frac{\sigma^2}{n}\right).\] Under \(H_1\), marginalising over the Gaussian prior on \(\mu\) gives \[\bar x \mid H_1 \sim \mathcal{N}\!\left(0,\, \tau^2 + \frac{\sigma^2}{n}\right).\] Therefore \[\text{BF}_{10} = \frac{p(\bar x \mid H_1)}{p(\bar x \mid H_0)} = \sqrt{\frac{\sigma^2/n}{\tau^2 + \sigma^2/n}}\,\exp\!\left[\frac{\bar x^2}{2}\!\left(\frac{n}{\sigma^2} - \frac{1}{\tau^2 + \sigma^2/n}\right)\right].\] Large \(|\bar x|\) favours the model with a free mean, while the prefactor \(\sqrt{(\sigma^2/n)/(\tau^2 + \sigma^2/n)} \ll 1\) penalises the broader prior-predictive distribution under \(H_1\) — the Occam factor. Letting \(\tau \to \infty\) at fixed \(\bar x\) drives the Bayes factor to favour \(H_0\): this is Lindley’s paradox.

5.6 Sensitivity analysis and recommendations

Because the Bayes factor depends on prior specification under each model, responsible practice demands a sensitivity analysis:

  1. Vary the prior width under the complex model within a reasonable range.
  2. Plot or tabulate the Bayes factor as a function of prior width.
  3. Report the range of Bayes factors across plausible priors.
  4. Use multiple Bayes factor values (e.g., the median and bounds) rather than a single point estimate.

If the Bayes factor remains in the same evidential category across a wide range of prior specifications, the conclusion is robust. If it changes materially, the inference is prior-sensitive and deserves careful discussion.

Some software implementations use automatic prior calibration rules (e.g., based on effect size or information content in the data) to reduce arbitrariness. While convenient, these methods still embed assumptions. Transparency about prior choice is always preferable to the illusion of objectivity.

5.7 Brief comparison with frequentist hypothesis testing

The frequentist approach to hypothesis testing is conceptually different. It treats parameters as fixed but unknown and focuses on controlling error rates across repeated hypothetical samples:

A test statistic \(T(D)\) is compared to a threshold derived from the null distribution \(P(T | H_0)\). The p-value is \(P(T(D) \geq t_{\text{obs}} | H_0)\), the probability of observing data as or more extreme than observed, if \(H_0\) were true. A p-value below \(\alpha\) (e.g., 0.05) triggers rejection of \(H_0\).

Key differences:

Feature Bayesian Frequentist
Assigns probability to hypotheses Yes No; tests hypothesis rejection rates
Uses prior information Yes; explicitly No; aims for objectivity through error control
Penalizes complexity Automatically via Occam factor Only through significance level (weak link)
Interpretation of p-value N/A Probability of data under \(H_0\), not probability \(H_0\) is true
Sample size effects Accounted for naturally Strong: small effects are rejected in large samples, regardless of effect size
Binary decision Unnecessary; provides probabilities Often yes/no; less flexible

Both frameworks are useful and address different questions. Frequentist tests excel at controlling false-positive rates in exploratory settings. Bayesian methods excel when you want to rank competing theories and make decisions balancing evidence and consequences. Modern practice often combines both: use frequentist tests for initial screening, then Bayesian analysis for focused inference.

5.8 Computing the marginal likelihood

Having seen what the marginal likelihood does for model comparison, we turn to how to compute it. The integral \(p(D \mid M) = \int p(D \mid \theta, M)\, p(\theta \mid M)\, d\theta\) is the primary computational bottleneck in Bayesian model comparison. For simple conjugate models — where the prior and likelihood are conjugate families — analytical solutions exist (the coin example above being one). In all other settings we must resort to numerical approximation. The four families of methods below cover the practical landscape; each is presented with its idea, strengths, and pitfalls.

  • Laplace approximation. The cheapest analytic estimator: assume the posterior is sharply peaked around its mode and expand the log-posterior to second order around the MAP estimate \(\hat{\theta}\). Strengths: requires only the MAP and Hessian; often accurate when the posterior is well-approximated by a high-dimensional Gaussian. Weaknesses: poor for multimodal or heavy-tailed posteriors and degrades in high dimensions.

\[p(D \mid M) \approx p(D \mid \hat{\theta}, M)\, p(\hat{\theta} \mid M)\, (2\pi)^{d/2}\, |\mathcal{H}|^{-1/2}\]

where \(d\) is the dimension of the parameter space and \(\mathcal{H}\) is the Hessian of the negative log-posterior evaluated at \(\hat{\theta}\).

For many applied problems, the Laplace approximation is a good starting point. Use it as a baseline and compare with more robust methods if possible.

  • Harmonic mean estimator. If posterior samples \(\theta^{(s)}\), \(s = 1, \ldots, S\) are already available from MCMC, one can write \[\hat{Z}_{\mathrm{HM}} = \left[\frac{1}{S}\sum_{s=1}^S \frac{1}{p(D \mid \theta^{(s)}, M)}\right]^{-1}.\] This arises as an importance-sampling estimator with the prior as proposal: posterior samples are reweighted by the reciprocal of the likelihood, inverting the usual likelihood weighting. Tempting in its simplicity, it is unreliable for the reasons made precise below.

Remark. Pitfall: catastrophic instability. The harmonic mean estimator has infinite variance whenever the prior has lighter tails than the posterior, which occurs in nearly every realistic setting. A single posterior sample in a region of low prior density can dominate the sum, causing enormous variance and unreliable estimates. Newton & Raftery (1994) and Wolpert & Schmidler (2012) have thoroughly documented this pathology. We strongly discourage the use of the harmonic mean estimator in production analyses. Its tempting simplicity belies a fundamental flaw.

  • Bridge sampling. Meng & Wong (1996) corrected the importance-sampling bias of the harmonic mean using the identity \[\frac{Z_1}{Z_2} = \frac{\mathbb{E}_{q_2}[\alpha(\theta)\,q_1(\theta)]}{\mathbb{E}_{q_1}[\alpha(\theta)\,q_2(\theta)]},\] for any two densities \(q_1\), \(q_2\) with tractable normalising constants \(Z_1\), \(Z_2\) and any “bridge” function \(\alpha\). The optimal (minimum-variance) \(\alpha\) has the geometric-mean form; setting \(q_1\) to the posterior and \(q_2\) to the prior yields a marginal-likelihood estimate. Strengths: much lower variance than the harmonic mean; scales to moderate-to-high dimensions; widely implemented (e.g., in the R package bridgesampling of Gronau et al. 2017). Weaknesses: requires posterior samples and careful selection of the bridge function and proposal.

Bridge sampling is the recommended default when posterior samples are already in hand (e.g., from an MCMC run) and model comparison is a secondary goal. The bridgesampling R package provides a robust, production-ready implementation.

  • Nested sampling. Skilling (2006) reformulated the evidence integral as a one-dimensional integration over enclosed prior mass. With \(X(\lambda) = \int_{\{L(\theta) > \lambda\}} p(\theta \mid M)\, d\theta\), \[Z = \int p(D \mid \theta, M)\, p(\theta \mid M)\, d\theta = \int_0^1 L(X)\, dX,\] where \(L(X)\) is the likelihood as a function of enclosed prior mass \(X\). The algorithm maintains a set of \(K\) “live points” drawn from the prior; at each iteration the lowest-likelihood point is removed (becoming a “dead point”) and replaced by a new draw from the prior constrained to higher likelihood. The evidence is accumulated as \(Z \approx \sum_{i} L_i \, \Delta X_i\), where \(L_i\) and \(\Delta X_i\) are the likelihoods and prior-mass increments of the dead points. Strengths: handles multimodal posteriors naturally; delivers both \(Z\) and (importance-reweighted) posterior samples; popular in astrophysics and cosmology. Weaknesses: more expensive than Laplace; requires tuning of live-point count and the constrained sampler.

Nested sampling is implemented in MultiNest (Feroz et al.), dynesty (Speagle), and PolyChord (Handley et al.). Choose nested sampling when the posterior is complex, multimodal, or when you need both evidence and posterior samples with high confidence.

  • Thermodynamic integration and annealed importance sampling. A fourth family interpolates between prior and posterior through the tempered family \[\pi_\beta(\theta) \propto p(D \mid \theta, M)^\beta\, p(\theta \mid M), \quad \beta \in [0, 1],\] with \(\beta = 0\) the prior and \(\beta = 1\) the posterior. The marginal likelihood then satisfies \[\log Z = \int_0^1 \mathbb{E}_{\pi_\beta}\!\big[\log p(D \mid \theta, M)\big]\, d\beta,\] the foundation of thermodynamic integration (Gelman & Meng 1998) and annealed importance sampling (Neal 2001). Strengths: robust in high dimensions and on multimodal problems; sits naturally inside parallel-tempering MCMC (Chapter 10). Weaknesses: requires careful choice of temperature schedule and numerical integration scheme.

The trade-offs are summarised in the table below.

Method When to use Strengths Weaknesses Cost
Laplace Quick baseline with smooth, unimodal posterior Fast; only MAP and Hessian needed Poor for multimodal or heavy-tailed posteriors Very low
Harmonic mean Avoid Simplicity; uses existing MCMC samples Catastrophic instability; infinite variance Very low (but unreliable)
Bridge sampling MCMC samples in hand; moderate-to-high dimensions Low variance; theoretically principled; well-implemented Requires tuning; sensitive to proposal Low-to-moderate
Nested sampling Multimodal or complex posteriors; want both \(Z\) and posterior Handles multimodality naturally; provides full uncertainty; robust Computationally expensive; requires tuning Moderate-to-high

Practical recommendations. For rapid Bayes-factor assessment with a well-conditioned, unimodal posterior, compute the Laplace approximation as a baseline and supplement it with uncertainty bounds. If posterior samples from MCMC are in hand and a robust point estimate is needed, use bridge sampling — the practical sweet spot of accuracy, interpretability, and implementation maturity. For complex, multimodal posteriors or when maximum confidence in the evidence is essential, employ nested sampling despite its higher cost. Never rely on the harmonic mean estimator in production. In all cases, report not just a point estimate but also an uncertainty band, and pair it with the sensitivity analysis introduced above whenever feasible.

5.9 Exercises

(Written test, 15 June 2026 — Q1.) A detector can operate in two mutually exclusive regimes, \(H_0\) and \(H_1\). Before seeing the data, the calibration team assigns \(P(H_1) = 0.35\). For the observed data \(D\), the likelihood ratio is \[\frac{P(D \mid H_1)}{P(D \mid H_0)} = 4.\] Compute the posterior probability \(P(H_1 \mid D)\). Is the Bayes factor alone sufficient to determine this posterior probability? Briefly explain.

Solution. The prior odds are \(\pi(H_1)/\pi(H_0) = 0.35/0.65 = 7/13\), so the posterior odds are \[\frac{P(H_1 \mid D)}{P(H_0 \mid D)} = \text{BF}_{10}\,\frac{\pi(H_1)}{\pi(H_0)} = 4 \cdot \frac{7}{13} = \frac{28}{13}.\] Therefore \(P(H_1 \mid D) = (28/13)/(1 + 28/13) = 28/41 \approx 0.683\). The Bayes factor alone is not sufficient: it only quantifies how the data update the odds, so one also needs the prior odds (here \(\pi(H_1) = 0.35\)) to recover a posterior probability.

Two mutually exclusive hypotheses \(H_0\), \(H_1\) are assigned equal prior probability. The Bayes factor in favor of \(H_1\) is \(\text{BF}_{10} = 9\). Compute the posterior odds and \(P(H_1 \mid D)\).

Solution. With equal priors, \(\pi(H_1)/\pi(H_0) = 1\), so the posterior odds equal the Bayes factor: \[\frac{P(H_1 \mid D)}{P(H_0 \mid D)} = \text{BF}_{10} = 9.\] Hence \(P(H_1 \mid D) = 9/(1+9) = 9/10 = 0.9\).

Three mutually exclusive models \(M_1, M_2, M_3\) have prior probabilities \((0.5,\, 0.3,\, 0.2)\). The likelihoods of the observed data are in the ratio \(P(D \mid M_1) : P(D \mid M_2) : P(D \mid M_3) = 2 : 1 : 4\). Compute the posterior probability of each model.

Solution. The unnormalised posterior weights are \(\pi(M_i)\,P(D \mid M_i)\): \[(0.5)(2) : (0.3)(1) : (0.2)(4) = 1.0 : 0.3 : 0.8.\] Normalising by \(1.0 + 0.3 + 0.8 = 2.1\) gives \[P(M_1 \mid D) = \tfrac{10}{21}, \quad P(M_2 \mid D) = \tfrac{3}{21} = \tfrac{1}{7}, \quad P(M_3 \mid D) = \tfrac{8}{21}.\]

A coin is flipped \(n = 5\) times and four heads are observed. Compare two simple hypotheses with equal prior probability: \(H_0\): the coin is fair (\(p = 1/2\)); \(H_1\): the coin has \(p = 3/4\). Compute \(\text{BF}_{10}\) exactly (no calculator — leave powers of \(2\), \(3\), \(4\) unsimplified) and give the posterior odds.

Solution. The binomial coefficient \(\binom{5}{4}\) cancels in the ratio, leaving \[\text{BF}_{10} = \frac{(3/4)^4 (1/4)^1}{(1/2)^5} = \frac{3^4/4^5}{1/2^5} = \frac{3^4}{4^5}\cdot 2^5 = \frac{3^4}{2^5} = \frac{81}{32}.\] With equal priors the posterior odds equal \(\text{BF}_{10} = 81/32 \approx 2.53\), so \(P(H_1 \mid D) = 81/113\).

Two Poisson models are compared on a single count \(k = 2\): \(M_0\) with rate \(\lambda_0 = 1\), \(M_1\) with rate \(\lambda_1 = 3\). Write \(\text{BF}_{10}\) in closed form using the Poisson PMF. Express it in terms of \(e\) (do not evaluate numerically).

Solution. The Poisson PMF is \(P(k \mid \lambda) = e^{-\lambda}\lambda^k/k!\). The factorial \(k! = 2!\) cancels: \[\text{BF}_{10} = \frac{e^{-3}\,3^2/2!}{e^{-1}\,1^2/2!} = 9\,e^{-2}.\]

A Gaussian observation \(x\) has known variance \(\sigma^2\). Compare \(M_0\): \(\mu = 0\) versus \(M_1\): \(\mu = \mu_1\) (a fixed non-zero value). Derive \[\text{BF}_{10}(x) = \exp\!\left( \frac{2 \mu_1 x - \mu_1^2}{2\sigma^2} \right).\] For which \(x\) does the data favor \(M_1\)?

Solution. The Gaussian densities at \(x\) give \[\text{BF}_{10}(x) = \frac{\exp[-(x-\mu_1)^2/(2\sigma^2)]}{\exp[-x^2/(2\sigma^2)]} = \exp\!\left(\frac{2\mu_1 x - \mu_1^2}{2\sigma^2}\right).\] The data favour \(M_1\) (i.e. \(\text{BF}_{10} > 1\)) iff \(2\mu_1 x - \mu_1^2 > 0\), i.e. \(x > \mu_1/2\) when \(\mu_1 > 0\) (and \(x < \mu_1/2\) when \(\mu_1 < 0\)): the cross-over is exactly the midpoint between the two competing means.

The prior odds against \(H_1\) are \(1 : 9\). What Bayes factor \(\text{BF}_{10}\) is required to push the posterior probability \(P(H_1 \mid D)\) above (a) \(1/2\) and (b) \(0.95\)?

Solution. Posterior odds \(R = P/(1-P)\) are obtained from prior odds \(1/9\) via \(R = \text{BF}_{10}\cdot(1/9)\), so \(\text{BF}_{10} = 9R\). (a) \(P = 1/2 \Rightarrow R = 1 \Rightarrow \text{BF}_{10} = 9\). (b) \(P = 0.95 \Rightarrow R = 0.95/0.05 = 19 \Rightarrow \text{BF}_{10} = 171\).

Repeat the calculation of Exercise 1 with the prior changed first to \(P(H_1) = 0.10\) and then to \(P(H_1) = 0.70\), keeping the same likelihood ratio. Comment on the sensitivity of the conclusion to the prior.

Solution. With \(\text{BF}_{10} = 4\) the posterior odds are \(R = 4\,\pi(H_1)/(1-\pi(H_1))\) and \(P(H_1 \mid D) = R/(1+R)\). For \(\pi(H_1) = 0.10\): \(R = 4 \cdot (1/9) = 4/9\), so \(P(H_1 \mid D) = 4/13 \approx 0.308\). For \(\pi(H_1) = 0.70\): \(R = 4 \cdot (7/3) = 28/3\), so \(P(H_1 \mid D) = 28/31 \approx 0.903\). The qualitative conclusion flips: a moderate likelihood ratio of 4 is not enough to overturn a sceptical prior, but easily reinforces an already-favourable one. The Bayes factor must always be reported with the prior used.

Show that when the two hypotheses are assigned equal prior probability, the posterior odds equal the Bayes factor. State the converse and discuss its practical meaning.

Solution. By definition, \[\frac{P(H_1 \mid D)}{P(H_0 \mid D)} = \text{BF}_{10}\,\frac{\pi(H_1)}{\pi(H_0)}.\] If \(\pi(H_1) = \pi(H_0) = 1/2\) the prior-odds factor is \(1\), so the posterior odds equal \(\text{BF}_{10}\). Converse: if the posterior odds equal the Bayes factor, then \(\pi(H_1) = \pi(H_0)\). Practically, quoting \(\text{BF}_{10}\) as if it were a probability tacitly assumes a 50/50 prior — perfectly fine if disclosed, misleading if not.

A binary outcome \(X \mid \theta \sim \text{Bernoulli}(\theta)\) is observed \(n\) times with \(s\) successes. Under the prior \(\theta \sim \text{Uniform}(0,1)\), show that the marginal likelihood is \[p(\text{data}) = \frac{s!\,(n-s)!}{(n+1)!}.\]

Solution. The likelihood for an ordered sequence with \(s\) successes is \(\theta^s(1-\theta)^{n-s}\). With \(\pi(\theta) = 1\) on \([0,1]\), \[p(\text{data}) = \int_0^1 \theta^s (1-\theta)^{n-s}\,d\theta = B(s+1, n-s+1) = \frac{s!\,(n-s)!}{(n+1)!},\] using \(B(a,b) = \Gamma(a)\Gamma(b)/\Gamma(a+b)\) and \(\Gamma(k+1) = k!\).

Same likelihood as in the previous exercise but with prior \(\theta \sim \text{Beta}(\alpha, \beta)\). Express the marginal likelihood in terms of the Beta function \(B(\cdot, \cdot)\). Use it to compute the Bayes factor between \(\text{Beta}(\alpha_1, \beta_1)\) and \(\text{Beta}(\alpha_0, \beta_0)\) priors.

Solution. With \(\pi(\theta) = \theta^{\alpha-1}(1-\theta)^{\beta-1}/B(\alpha,\beta)\), the marginal likelihood is \[p(\text{data} \mid \alpha,\beta) = \frac{1}{B(\alpha,\beta)}\int_0^1 \theta^{s+\alpha-1}(1-\theta)^{n-s+\beta-1}\,d\theta = \frac{B(s+\alpha,\,n-s+\beta)}{B(\alpha,\beta)}.\] The Bayes factor between two Beta priors is therefore \[\text{BF}_{10} = \frac{B(s+\alpha_1,\,n-s+\beta_1)/B(\alpha_1,\beta_1)}{B(s+\alpha_0,\,n-s+\beta_0)/B(\alpha_0,\beta_0)}.\]

A Gaussian observation \(x \mid \theta \sim \mathcal{N}(\theta, 1)\) is compared under two models: \(M_0\): \(\theta = 0\); \(M_1\): \(\theta \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \tau^2)\) with prior width \(\tau\). Derive \(\text{BF}_{10}\) as a function of \(x\) and \(\tau\). What happens to \(\text{BF}_{10}\) as \(\tau \to \infty\) with \(x\) fixed? Comment on Lindley’s paradox.

Solution. Marginalising the Gaussian likelihood \(x \mid \theta \sim \mathcal{N}(\theta,1)\) over the \(\mathcal{N}(0,\tau^2)\) prior on \(\theta\) gives \(x \mid M_1 \sim \mathcal{N}(0, 1+\tau^2)\), while \(x \mid M_0 \sim \mathcal{N}(0,1)\). Hence \[\text{BF}_{10}(x,\tau) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{1+\tau^2}}\,\exp\!\left[\frac{x^2}{2}\cdot\frac{\tau^2}{1+\tau^2}\right].\] As \(\tau \to \infty\) with \(x\) fixed, the exponential tends to \(e^{x^2/2}\) (finite) while the prefactor \(1/\sqrt{1+\tau^2} \to 0\), so \(\text{BF}_{10} \to 0\) and \(M_0\) always wins. This is Lindley’s paradox: an arbitrarily diffuse alternative is penalised by an unbounded Occam factor, so a single observation can never overturn the null no matter how far from zero.

Classify the strength of evidence on the (Jeffreys / Kass–Raftery) scale for each of the Bayes factors \(\text{BF}_{10} \in \{1,\, 3,\, 12,\, 150\}\). State the cut-offs you use.

Solution. Using Jeffreys’ cut-offs \((1,3]\) weak, \((3,10]\) moderate (substantial), \((10,30]\) strong, \((30,100]\) very strong, \(>100\) decisive: \(\text{BF}_{10} = 1\): no evidence (indifference between \(H_0\) and \(H_1\)). \(\text{BF}_{10} = 3\): at the boundary of weak/moderate — “bare mention” or just-substantial evidence for \(H_1\). \(\text{BF}_{10} = 12\): strong evidence for \(H_1\). \(\text{BF}_{10} = 150\): decisive evidence for \(H_1\).

Two models are retained after data analysis with posterior probabilities \(P(M_1 \mid D) = 0.4\), \(P(M_2 \mid D) = 0.6\). Their posterior-predictive densities for a future observation \(y\) are \(\mathcal{N}(0, 1)\) and \(\mathcal{N}(2, 1)\) respectively. Write the model-averaged predictive density of \(y\) and compute its mean and variance.

Solution. The model-averaged predictive is a mixture: \[p(y \mid D) = 0.4\,\phi(y;0,1) + 0.6\,\phi(y;2,1).\] Mean: \(\mathbb{E}[y] = 0.4\cdot 0 + 0.6\cdot 2 = 1.2\). Variance (law of total variance): \(\mathrm{Var}(y) = \mathbb{E}[\mathrm{Var}(y\mid M)] + \mathrm{Var}(\mathbb{E}[y\mid M]) = 1 + (0.4\cdot 0^2 + 0.6\cdot 2^2 - 1.2^2) = 1 + (2.4 - 1.44) = 1.96\).

For a Gaussian likelihood with known variance and a Gaussian prior of width \(\sigma_p \gg \sigma_{\text{post}}\), show that the marginal likelihood factorises as \[p(D) \approx p(D \mid \hat\theta_{\text{MAP}}) \cdot \frac{\sigma_{\text{post}}}{\sigma_p}.\] Identify the Occam factor and explain why broader priors are automatically penalised.

Solution. Around the MAP the likelihood is approximately \(p(D \mid \theta) \approx p(D \mid \hat\theta_{\text{MAP}})\exp[-(\theta-\hat\theta_{\text{MAP}})^2/(2\sigma_{\text{post}}^2)]\), while the prior on the scale of the peak is essentially flat: \(\pi(\theta) \approx 1/(\sqrt{2\pi}\,\sigma_p)\). Marginalising, \[p(D) \approx p(D \mid \hat\theta_{\text{MAP}})\cdot\frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}\,\sigma_p}\cdot\sqrt{2\pi}\,\sigma_{\text{post}} = p(D \mid \hat\theta_{\text{MAP}})\cdot\frac{\sigma_{\text{post}}}{\sigma_p}.\] The Occam factor is \(\sigma_{\text{post}}/\sigma_p < 1\): a broader prior \(\sigma_p\) spreads the prior over a region most of which is inconsistent with the data, multiplicatively shrinking the evidence and so penalising the model.

(Define and explain.) Define the Bayes factor in favour of \(H_1\) against \(H_0\), both for parameter-free models and for parametric models. Show how it relates prior odds and posterior odds, and explain the role of the marginal likelihood. (Brief; no calculation needed.)

Solution. The Bayes factor is the ratio of marginal likelihoods, \(\text{BF}_{10} = p(D \mid H_1)/p(D \mid H_0)\). For parameter-free models this is just the ratio of two ordinary likelihoods; for parametric models \(H_j\) with parameters \(\theta_j\), \[p(D \mid H_j) = \int p(D \mid \theta_j, H_j)\,\pi(\theta_j \mid H_j)\,d\theta_j,\] the prior-averaged likelihood. It multiplies prior odds into posterior odds, \[\frac{P(H_1 \mid D)}{P(H_0 \mid D)} = \text{BF}_{10}\,\frac{\pi(H_1)}{\pi(H_0)}.\] The marginal likelihood quantifies how well a model predicted the data before fitting; the integral automatically penalises models whose prior wastes mass on regions inconsistent with the data (Occam’s razor).

(Compare and contrast.) State two practical differences between a Bayesian Bayes-factor analysis and a frequentist \(p\)-value. Pick a concrete example (e.g. testing \(\mu = 0\) from \(n\) Gaussian samples) and describe the qualitative conclusion each framework draws when \(n\) is moderately large.

Solution. Two key differences: (i) the Bayes factor compares two models symmetrically and can support either \(H_0\) or \(H_1\), whereas a \(p\)-value only quantifies tail evidence against \(H_0\); (ii) the Bayes factor automatically embeds an Occam penalty against diffuse alternatives, while a \(p\)-value is independent of the alternative.

For \(\mu = 0\) vs. \(\mu \sim \mathcal{N}(0,\tau^2)\) on \(n\) samples with sample mean \(\bar x\), the \(p\)-value is \(2\,(1 - \Phi(\sqrt n\,|\bar x|/\sigma))\) and shrinks like \(1/\sqrt n\), so any non-zero \(\bar x\) is eventually “significant”. The Bayes factor \(\text{BF}_{10} \propto \sqrt{\sigma^2/(n\tau^2)}\,\exp(\cdots)\) instead carries a \(\sqrt{1/n}\) Occam prefactor, so for moderately large \(n\) and small \(\bar x\) it may favour \(H_0\) — Lindley’s paradox.

(Define and explain.) Define the Occam factor and explain why broader priors over a complex model are automatically penalised in the Bayes factor. Give a sketch using a one-dimensional parameter with width \(\sigma_p\) and posterior width \(\sigma_{\text{post}}\).

Solution. The Occam factor is the multiplicative term that appears in the Laplace decomposition of the evidence, \[p(D \mid H) \approx p(D \mid \hat\theta)\cdot\underbrace{\pi(\hat\theta)\cdot\sqrt{2\pi}\,\sigma_{\text{post}}}_{\text{Occam factor}} \;\approx\; p(D \mid \hat\theta)\cdot\frac{\sigma_{\text{post}}}{\sigma_p},\] i.e. the fraction of the prior volume that the posterior actually occupies. Doubling the prior width \(\sigma_p\) halves the Occam factor and hence the marginal likelihood, even though the best-fit likelihood does not change. Broader priors waste mass on regions the data exclude, so the Bayes factor penalises them automatically — no ad-hoc complexity term needed.

(Show that.) Show that the Bayes factor for two parametric models is invariant under bijective reparametrisation of either model’s parameters. (Hint: the marginal likelihood is an integral against the prior, so any Jacobian cancels.)

Solution. Let \(\phi = g(\theta)\) be a bijection with \(\theta = g^{-1}(\phi)\) and Jacobian \(|d\theta/d\phi|\). The transformed prior density is \(\tilde\pi(\phi) = \pi(g^{-1}(\phi))\,|d\theta/d\phi|\) and the likelihood becomes \(\tilde p(D \mid \phi) = p(D \mid g^{-1}(\phi))\). Then \[\int \tilde p(D \mid \phi)\,\tilde\pi(\phi)\,d\phi = \int p(D \mid g^{-1}(\phi))\,\pi(g^{-1}(\phi))\,\left|\frac{d\theta}{d\phi}\right|\,d\phi = \int p(D \mid \theta)\,\pi(\theta)\,d\theta,\] by change of variables. So each model’s marginal likelihood is unchanged under reparametrisation, and hence so is \(\text{BF}_{10}\).

## Introduction

When faced with multiple competing hypotheses or models, scientists need a principled way to compare them in light of observed data. The Bayesian framework provides an elegant and automatic way to do this: the posterior odds ratio, built from the Bayes factor and the prior odds. Unlike frequentist hypothesis tests that focus on rejecting a single null hypothesis, Bayesian model comparison directly assigns probabilities to models and penalizes unjustified complexity.

The key insight—Occam's razor—emerges automatically from the mathematics. Complex models that require fine-tuning of their parameters to fit data are penalized, while simple models that make robust predictions across a wide range of parameter values are favored. This chapter develops the theory and practice of Bayesian model selection, from the basic posterior odds formula to sophisticated applications in scientific inference.

::: {.example name="Bayes' theorem and the role of evidence (exam-style)"}
State Bayes' theorem for a continuous parameter $\theta$ and data $D$. Explain the meaning of likelihood, prior, posterior, and evidence. Why is the evidence often omitted in parameter estimation but essential in model comparison?

**Solution.** Bayes' theorem reads
$$p(\theta \mid D, H) = \frac{p(D \mid \theta, H)\,p(\theta \mid H)}{p(D \mid H)}, \qquad p(D \mid H) = \int p(D \mid \theta, H)\,p(\theta \mid H)\,d\theta.$$
Here $p(D \mid \theta, H)$ is the **likelihood**, $p(\theta \mid H)$ is the **prior**, $p(\theta \mid D, H)$ is the **posterior**, and $p(D \mid H)$ is the **evidence** (or marginal likelihood).

In parameter estimation within a fixed model $H$, the evidence is independent of $\theta$ and acts only as a normalisation constant, so one writes $p(\theta \mid D, H) \propto p(D \mid \theta, H)\,p(\theta \mid H)$. In model comparison, however, different models have different evidences, and the ratio of evidences is precisely the Bayes factor — so $p(D \mid H)$ cannot be dropped.
:::

## Posterior odds and the Bayes factor

Suppose we have two mutually exclusive and exhaustive hypotheses $H_0$ and $H_1$, and we observe data $D$. Bayes' theorem tells us:

$$P(H_i | D) = \frac{P(D | H_i) P(H_i)}{P(D)}$$

where $P(D) = P(D | H_0) P(H_0) + P(D | H_1) P(H_1)$ is the total probability of the data, obtained by marginalizing over both hypotheses.

::: {.definition name="Posterior odds ratio"}
The posterior odds ratio is the ratio of the posterior probabilities:

$$R = \frac{P(H_1 | D)}{P(H_0 | D)}$$

This directly quantifies which hypothesis is more likely given the data. Values $R > 1$ favor $H_1$, while $R < 1$ favor $H_0$.
:::

Now divide the posterior for $H_1$ by the posterior for $H_0$:

$$R = \frac{P(H_1 | D)}{P(H_0 | D)} = \frac{P(D | H_1)}{P(D | H_0)} \cdot \frac{P(H_1)}{P(H_0)}$$

The first ratio is the Bayes factor; the second is the prior odds.

::: {.definition name="Bayes factor"}
The Bayes factor is:

$$\text{BF}_{10} = \frac{P(D | H_1)}{P(D | H_0)}$$

It expresses how much the data shifts the odds between the two models, independent of prior beliefs.
:::

Thus we have the fundamental equation of Bayesian model comparison:

$$R = \text{BF}_{10} \times \frac{P(H_1)}{P(H_0)}$$

::: {.note}
Under uniform priors—when $P(H_1) = P(H_0) = 1/2$—the posterior odds equal the Bayes factor. This is why some practitioners use $\text{BF} > 1$ as a criterion for favoring $H_1$ "objectively," removing apparent subjectivity of the prior. However, this merely replaces one prior assumption (about models) with another (equal prior probability).
:::

::: {.example name="Bayes factor — definition and role (exam-style)"}
Consider two models $H_0$ and $H_1$. Define the Bayes factor in favour of $H_1$ against $H_0$, both for parameter-free models and for parametric models. Show how it relates prior odds and posterior odds, and explain the role of the marginal likelihood.

**Solution.** The Bayes factor is
$$\text{BF}_{10} = \frac{p(D \mid H_1)}{p(D \mid H_0)}.$$
By Bayes' theorem, the posterior odds satisfy
$$\frac{p(H_1 \mid D)}{p(H_0 \mid D)} = \frac{p(D \mid H_1)\,p(H_1)}{p(D \mid H_0)\,p(H_0)} = \text{BF}_{10}\,\frac{p(H_1)}{p(H_0)}.$$
The Bayes factor is therefore the multiplicative factor by which the data update prior odds into posterior odds. If $\text{BF}_{10} > 1$, the data favour $H_1$ over $H_0$ relative to the prior odds; if $\text{BF}_{10} < 1$, they favour $H_0$.

For parametric models $H_j$ with parameters $\theta_j$ and prior $p(\theta_j \mid H_j)$,
$$p(D \mid H_j) = \int p(D \mid \theta_j, H_j)\,p(\theta_j \mid H_j)\,d\theta_j,$$
so the evidence averages the likelihood over the prior. This integral automatically embeds an Occam penalty: models that spread prior mass over regions not supported by the data are penalised.
:::

Two quick consequences round off the basic machinery before we move to the parametric case.

- **Posterior probabilities from the odds.** Once $R$ is known, the model probabilities are recovered from $P(H_0 \mid D) = 1/(1+R)$ and $P(H_1 \mid D) = R/(1+R)$.
- **Jeffreys' interpretation scale.** Jeffreys (1961) proposed a rough scale for the magnitude of a Bayes factor: $\text{BF}_{10}$ in $(1,3]$ is weak evidence for $H_1$, $(3,10]$ moderate, $(10,30]$ strong, $(30,100]$ very strong, and above $100$ decisive (reverse the ratios for evidence against $H_1$). The cutoffs are conventional and context-dependent; report the magnitude alongside its uncertainty and the cost of being wrong rather than treating any threshold as binding.

::: {.note}
Jeffreys' scale is a communication device, not a decision rule. Combine it with a sensitivity analysis (see below) before reporting "strong" or "decisive" evidence.
:::

## The marginal likelihood and Occam's razor

The Bayes factor depends on the marginal likelihood (also called the model evidence), the probability of the data averaged over all possible parameter values under that model:

$$P(D | H_i) = \int P(D | \theta, H_i) \pi(\theta | H_i) \, d\theta$$

Here $\pi(\theta | H_i)$ is the prior distribution over parameters under hypothesis $H_i$, and $P(D | \theta, H_i)$ is the likelihood.

::: {.definition name="Occam's razor in Bayesian inference"}
Marginal likelihoods automatically encode a penalty for model complexity. A complex model with many parameters typically spreads its prior mass over a large region of parameter space. Only a small region of this parameter space will fit the data well. When we integrate over all parameters, the contribution from the region that fits data is small relative to the total prior volume, yielding a low marginal likelihood—even if the model achieves a higher best-fit likelihood than a simpler competitor.
:::

To see this concretely, consider two models:
- $H_0$: a simple model with a fixed parameter value $\theta_0$.
- $H_1$: a complex model with parameter $\theta$ uniformly distributed over the range $[a, b]$.

If both models fit the data equally well at their best-fit parameter values, then $P(D | \theta_0, H_0) \approx P(D | \hat{\theta}, H_1)$ where $\hat{\theta}$ is the MLE under $H_1$.

But:
$$P(D | H_0) = P(D | \theta_0, H_0)$$
$$P(D | H_1) \approx \frac{1}{b-a} \int_a^b P(D | \theta, H_1) \, d\theta \approx P(D | \hat{\theta}, H_1) \cdot \frac{\Delta\theta_{\text{eff}}}{b-a}$$

where $\Delta\theta_{\text{eff}}$ is the effective width of the likelihood peak. If $\Delta\theta_{\text{eff}} \ll b - a$, then $P(D | H_1) \ll P(D | H_0)$ despite $H_1$ fitting the data equally well locally. The Bayes factor penalizes $H_1$ for having a large prior parameter space most of which is inconsistent with the data.

This intuition becomes quantitative through Laplace's approximation, which isolates the penalty as a distinct *Occam factor*.

- **Laplace approximation of the evidence.** For continuous parameters, assume $P(D | \theta, H)$ is sharply peaked around its maximum $\hat{\theta}$ and the prior is smooth on the scale of that peak. Then $P(D | H) \approx P(D | \hat{\theta}, H) \cdot \pi(\hat{\theta}) \cdot \Delta\theta_{\text{eff}}$, factoring the evidence into a best-fit likelihood and a prior-volume term.
- **Occam factor.** The combination $\text{Occam} = \pi(\hat{\theta}) \cdot \Delta\theta_{\text{eff}}$ is the prior probability of the region of parameter space that actually fits the data. For a $d$-dimensional model with roughly uniform prior of range $L_i$ in each direction, $\text{Occam} \approx \prod_i (\Delta\theta_{\text{eff},i}/L_i)$: narrow likelihood peaks inside wide priors are heavily penalised.
- **Decomposed Bayes factor.** Combining the two ingredients gives
  $$\text{BF}_{10} = \frac{P(D | \hat{\theta}_1, H_1)}{P(D | \hat{\theta}_0, H_0)} \cdot \frac{\text{Occam}_1}{\text{Occam}_0}.$$
  The first ratio favours the more flexible model; the second penalises it for complexity. The balance — arising purely from integration, not from any ad-hoc penalty — determines the winner.

## Example: Testing coin fairness

This classic example from Sivia (2006) illustrates the full machinery. Suppose we flip a coin $n = 250$ times and observe $h = 140$ heads. We compare:

- $H_0$: The coin is fair ($\theta = 0.5$).
- $H_1$: The coin has unknown bias $\theta \sim \text{Beta}(1,1)$ (uniform on $[0,1]$).

The likelihood for $h$ heads in $n$ flips is:
$$P(D | \theta) = \binom{n}{h} \theta^h (1-\theta)^{n-h}$$

Under $H_0$:
$$P(D | H_0) = \binom{n}{h} (0.5)^n$$

Under $H_1$, with the Beta(1,1) prior:
$$P(D | H_1) = \int_0^1 \binom{n}{h} \theta^h (1-\theta)^{n-h} \, d\theta = \binom{n}{h} \cdot \frac{h! (n-h)!}{(n+1)!}$$

Therefore:
$$\text{BF}_{10} = \frac{P(D | H_1)}{P(D | H_0)} = \frac{h! (n-h)!}{(n+1)!} \cdot 2^n$$

With $n = 250$ and $h = 140$:
$$\text{BF}_{10} = \frac{140! \cdot 110!}{251!} \cdot 2^{250} \approx 0.044$$

This strong evidence against the coin being fair makes intuitive sense: observing 140 heads in 250 flips is notably different from 50-50 randomness.

::: {.example name="Sensitivity to prior width"}
Now suppose we use a wider prior for $H_1$: $\theta \sim \text{Beta}(\alpha, \alpha)$ for some $\alpha > 1$. This concentrates the prior more around $\theta = 0.5$. The Occam factor shrinks, raising the Bayes factor. Conversely, using $\alpha < 1$ (a U-shaped prior favoring extreme values) lowers it.

This illustrates a key insight: the Bayes factor is not prior-independent. However, it responds *sensibly*: wider priors (more agnostic) increase the Occam penalty, favoring simpler models; narrower priors (more peaked) decrease the penalty. A practitioner should explore this sensitivity and report it alongside point estimates.
:::

## Lindley's paradox

A famous puzzle arises when comparing a point null hypothesis (e.g., $\theta = 0$) to a broad alternative. As sample size increases, frequentist p-values eventually fall below any threshold (e.g., 0.05), calling for rejection of the null. Yet the Bayes factor may simultaneously favor the null, if the alternative prior is very diffuse.

::: {.example name="Lindley's paradox"}
Suppose we test $H_0: \mu = 0$ versus $H_1: \mu \sim N(0, \sigma_\mu^2)$ on data $x_1, \ldots, x_n \sim N(\mu, 1)$. With a very large sample and $\bar{x}$ only slightly away from zero, a frequentist test will reject $H_0$ (small p-value), but if $\sigma_\mu$ is very large (vague prior on $\mu$), the Occam penalty under $H_1$ keeps the Bayes factor favoring $H_0$.

The resolution is that the "paradox" reflects a real difference in how the two frameworks treat vague alternatives. Frequentist tests focus on the likelihood under $H_0$. Bayesian tests also account for where the alternative prior was placed. This is not an error but a feature: it discourages chasing phantom effects in vague parameter spaces.
:::

::: {.example name="Closed-form Bayes factor for a Gaussian mean (exam-style)"}
Let $D = \{x_1, \ldots, x_n\}$ be i.i.d.\ observations from $\mathcal{N}(\mu, \sigma^2)$ with known variance $\sigma^2$. Compare
$$H_0: \mu = 0, \qquad H_1: \mu \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \tau^2).$$
Compute $\text{BF}_{10}$ in terms of the sample mean $\bar x$.

**Solution.** The sample mean $\bar x$ is a sufficient statistic for $\mu$ with $\bar x \mid \mu \sim \mathcal{N}(\mu, \sigma^2/n)$. Under $H_0$,
$$\bar x \mid H_0 \sim \mathcal{N}\!\left(0, \frac{\sigma^2}{n}\right).$$
Under $H_1$, marginalising over the Gaussian prior on $\mu$ gives
$$\bar x \mid H_1 \sim \mathcal{N}\!\left(0,\, \tau^2 + \frac{\sigma^2}{n}\right).$$
Therefore
$$\text{BF}_{10} = \frac{p(\bar x \mid H_1)}{p(\bar x \mid H_0)} = \sqrt{\frac{\sigma^2/n}{\tau^2 + \sigma^2/n}}\,\exp\!\left[\frac{\bar x^2}{2}\!\left(\frac{n}{\sigma^2} - \frac{1}{\tau^2 + \sigma^2/n}\right)\right].$$
Large $|\bar x|$ favours the model with a free mean, while the prefactor $\sqrt{(\sigma^2/n)/(\tau^2 + \sigma^2/n)} \ll 1$ penalises the broader prior-predictive distribution under $H_1$ — the Occam factor. Letting $\tau \to \infty$ at fixed $\bar x$ drives the Bayes factor to favour $H_0$: this is Lindley's paradox.
:::

## Sensitivity analysis and recommendations

Because the Bayes factor depends on prior specification under each model, responsible practice demands a sensitivity analysis:

1. **Vary the prior width** under the complex model within a reasonable range.
2. **Plot or tabulate** the Bayes factor as a function of prior width.
3. **Report the range** of Bayes factors across plausible priors.
4. **Use multiple Bayes factor values** (e.g., the median and bounds) rather than a single point estimate.

If the Bayes factor remains in the same evidential category across a wide range of prior specifications, the conclusion is robust. If it changes materially, the inference is prior-sensitive and deserves careful discussion.

::: {.note}
Some software implementations use automatic prior calibration rules (e.g., based on effect size or information content in the data) to reduce arbitrariness. While convenient, these methods still embed assumptions. Transparency about prior choice is always preferable to the illusion of objectivity.
:::

## Brief comparison with frequentist hypothesis testing

The frequentist approach to hypothesis testing is conceptually different. It treats parameters as fixed but unknown and focuses on controlling error rates across repeated hypothetical samples:

::: {.definition name="Frequentist hypothesis test"}
A test statistic $T(D)$ is compared to a threshold derived from the null distribution $P(T | H_0)$. The p-value is $P(T(D) \geq t_{\text{obs}} | H_0)$, the probability of observing data as or more extreme than observed, if $H_0$ were true. A p-value below $\alpha$ (e.g., 0.05) triggers rejection of $H_0$.
:::

Key differences:

| Feature | Bayesian | Frequentist |
|:---|:---|:---|
| Assigns probability to hypotheses | Yes | No; tests hypothesis rejection rates |
| Uses prior information | Yes; explicitly | No; aims for objectivity through error control |
| Penalizes complexity | Automatically via Occam factor | Only through significance level (weak link) |
| Interpretation of p-value | N/A | Probability of data under $H_0$, not probability $H_0$ is true |
| Sample size effects | Accounted for naturally | Strong: small effects are rejected in large samples, regardless of effect size |
| Binary decision | Unnecessary; provides probabilities | Often yes/no; less flexible |

Both frameworks are useful and address different questions. Frequentist tests excel at controlling false-positive rates in exploratory settings. Bayesian methods excel when you want to rank competing theories and make decisions balancing evidence and consequences. Modern practice often combines both: use frequentist tests for initial screening, then Bayesian analysis for focused inference.

## Computing the marginal likelihood

Having seen *what* the marginal likelihood does for model comparison, we turn to *how* to compute it. The integral $p(D \mid M) = \int p(D \mid \theta, M)\, p(\theta \mid M)\, d\theta$ is the primary computational bottleneck in Bayesian model comparison. For simple conjugate models — where the prior and likelihood are conjugate families — analytical solutions exist (the coin example above being one). In all other settings we must resort to numerical approximation. The four families of methods below cover the practical landscape; each is presented with its idea, strengths, and pitfalls.

- **Laplace approximation.** The cheapest analytic estimator: assume the posterior is sharply peaked around its mode and expand the log-posterior to second order around the MAP estimate $\hat{\theta}$. *Strengths:* requires only the MAP and Hessian; often accurate when the posterior is well-approximated by a high-dimensional Gaussian. *Weaknesses:* poor for multimodal or heavy-tailed posteriors and degrades in high dimensions.

::: {.definition name="Laplace approximation for marginal likelihood"}
$$p(D \mid M) \approx p(D \mid \hat{\theta}, M)\, p(\hat{\theta} \mid M)\, (2\pi)^{d/2}\, |\mathcal{H}|^{-1/2}$$

where $d$ is the dimension of the parameter space and $\mathcal{H}$ is the Hessian of the negative log-posterior evaluated at $\hat{\theta}$.
:::

::: {.note}
For many applied problems, the Laplace approximation is a good starting point. Use it as a baseline and compare with more robust methods if possible.
:::

- **Harmonic mean estimator.** If posterior samples $\theta^{(s)}$, $s = 1, \ldots, S$ are already available from MCMC, one can write
  $$\hat{Z}_{\mathrm{HM}} = \left[\frac{1}{S}\sum_{s=1}^S \frac{1}{p(D \mid \theta^{(s)}, M)}\right]^{-1}.$$
  This arises as an importance-sampling estimator with the prior as proposal: posterior samples are reweighted by the reciprocal of the likelihood, inverting the usual likelihood weighting. Tempting in its simplicity, it is unreliable for the reasons made precise below.

::: {.remark}
**Pitfall: catastrophic instability.** The harmonic mean estimator has infinite variance whenever the prior has lighter tails than the posterior, which occurs in nearly every realistic setting. A single posterior sample in a region of low prior density can dominate the sum, causing enormous variance and unreliable estimates. Newton & Raftery (1994) and Wolpert & Schmidler (2012) have thoroughly documented this pathology. **We strongly discourage the use of the harmonic mean estimator in production analyses.** Its tempting simplicity belies a fundamental flaw.
:::

- **Bridge sampling.** Meng & Wong (1996) corrected the importance-sampling bias of the harmonic mean using the identity
  $$\frac{Z_1}{Z_2} = \frac{\mathbb{E}_{q_2}[\alpha(\theta)\,q_1(\theta)]}{\mathbb{E}_{q_1}[\alpha(\theta)\,q_2(\theta)]},$$
  for any two densities $q_1$, $q_2$ with tractable normalising constants $Z_1$, $Z_2$ and any "bridge" function $\alpha$. The optimal (minimum-variance) $\alpha$ has the geometric-mean form; setting $q_1$ to the posterior and $q_2$ to the prior yields a marginal-likelihood estimate. *Strengths:* much lower variance than the harmonic mean; scales to moderate-to-high dimensions; widely implemented (e.g., in the R package `bridgesampling` of Gronau et al. 2017). *Weaknesses:* requires posterior samples and careful selection of the bridge function and proposal.

::: {.note}
Bridge sampling is the recommended default when posterior samples are already in hand (e.g., from an MCMC run) and model comparison is a secondary goal. The `bridgesampling` R package provides a robust, production-ready implementation.
:::

- **Nested sampling.** Skilling (2006) reformulated the evidence integral as a one-dimensional integration over enclosed prior mass. With $X(\lambda) = \int_{\{L(\theta) > \lambda\}} p(\theta \mid M)\, d\theta$,
  $$Z = \int p(D \mid \theta, M)\, p(\theta \mid M)\, d\theta = \int_0^1 L(X)\, dX,$$
  where $L(X)$ is the likelihood as a function of enclosed prior mass $X$. The algorithm maintains a set of $K$ "live points" drawn from the prior; at each iteration the lowest-likelihood point is removed (becoming a "dead point") and replaced by a new draw from the prior constrained to higher likelihood. The evidence is accumulated as $Z \approx \sum_{i} L_i \, \Delta X_i$, where $L_i$ and $\Delta X_i$ are the likelihoods and prior-mass increments of the dead points. *Strengths:* handles multimodal posteriors naturally; delivers both $Z$ and (importance-reweighted) posterior samples; popular in astrophysics and cosmology. *Weaknesses:* more expensive than Laplace; requires tuning of live-point count and the constrained sampler.

::: {.note}
Nested sampling is implemented in `MultiNest` (Feroz et al.), `dynesty` (Speagle), and `PolyChord` (Handley et al.). Choose nested sampling when the posterior is complex, multimodal, or when you need both evidence and posterior samples with high confidence.
:::

- **Thermodynamic integration and annealed importance sampling.** A fourth family interpolates between prior and posterior through the tempered family
  $$\pi_\beta(\theta) \propto p(D \mid \theta, M)^\beta\, p(\theta \mid M), \quad \beta \in [0, 1],$$
  with $\beta = 0$ the prior and $\beta = 1$ the posterior. The marginal likelihood then satisfies
  $$\log Z = \int_0^1 \mathbb{E}_{\pi_\beta}\!\big[\log p(D \mid \theta, M)\big]\, d\beta,$$
  the foundation of thermodynamic integration (Gelman & Meng 1998) and annealed importance sampling (Neal 2001). *Strengths:* robust in high dimensions and on multimodal problems; sits naturally inside parallel-tempering MCMC (Chapter 10). *Weaknesses:* requires careful choice of temperature schedule and numerical integration scheme.

The trade-offs are summarised in the table below.

| Method | When to use | Strengths | Weaknesses | Cost |
|:---|:---|:---|:---|:---|
| **Laplace** | Quick baseline with smooth, unimodal posterior | Fast; only MAP and Hessian needed | Poor for multimodal or heavy-tailed posteriors | Very low |
| **Harmonic mean** | *Avoid* | Simplicity; uses existing MCMC samples | Catastrophic instability; infinite variance | Very low (but unreliable) |
| **Bridge sampling** | MCMC samples in hand; moderate-to-high dimensions | Low variance; theoretically principled; well-implemented | Requires tuning; sensitive to proposal | Low-to-moderate |
| **Nested sampling** | Multimodal or complex posteriors; want both $Z$ and posterior | Handles multimodality naturally; provides full uncertainty; robust | Computationally expensive; requires tuning | Moderate-to-high |
| **Thermodynamic integration** | High-dimensional problems; parallel tempering available | Robust; scalable to high dimensions | Needs temperature schedule; numerical integration error | Moderate-to-high |

**Practical recommendations.** For rapid Bayes-factor assessment with a well-conditioned, unimodal posterior, compute the Laplace approximation as a baseline and supplement it with uncertainty bounds. If posterior samples from MCMC are in hand and a robust point estimate is needed, **use bridge sampling** — the practical sweet spot of accuracy, interpretability, and implementation maturity. For complex, multimodal posteriors or when maximum confidence in the evidence is essential, **employ nested sampling** despite its higher cost. **Never rely on the harmonic mean estimator** in production. In all cases, report not just a point estimate but also an uncertainty band, and pair it with the sensitivity analysis introduced above whenever feasible.

::: {.exercise}

*(Written test, 15 June 2026 — Q1.)* A detector can operate in two mutually exclusive regimes, $H_0$ and $H_1$. Before seeing the data, the calibration team assigns $P(H_1) = 0.35$. For the observed data $D$, the likelihood ratio is
$$\frac{P(D \mid H_1)}{P(D \mid H_0)} = 4.$$
Compute the posterior probability $P(H_1 \mid D)$. Is the Bayes factor alone sufficient to determine this posterior probability? Briefly explain.
:::

::: {.solution}
The prior odds are $\pi(H_1)/\pi(H_0) = 0.35/0.65 = 7/13$, so the posterior odds are
$$\frac{P(H_1 \mid D)}{P(H_0 \mid D)} = \text{BF}_{10}\,\frac{\pi(H_1)}{\pi(H_0)} = 4 \cdot \frac{7}{13} = \frac{28}{13}.$$
Therefore $P(H_1 \mid D) = (28/13)/(1 + 28/13) = 28/41 \approx 0.683$. The Bayes factor alone is not sufficient: it only quantifies how the data update the odds, so one also needs the prior odds (here $\pi(H_1) = 0.35$) to recover a posterior probability.
:::

::: {.exercise}

Two mutually exclusive hypotheses $H_0$, $H_1$ are assigned equal prior probability. The Bayes factor in favor of $H_1$ is $\text{BF}_{10} = 9$. Compute the posterior odds and $P(H_1 \mid D)$.
:::

::: {.solution}
With equal priors, $\pi(H_1)/\pi(H_0) = 1$, so the posterior odds equal the Bayes factor:
$$\frac{P(H_1 \mid D)}{P(H_0 \mid D)} = \text{BF}_{10} = 9.$$
Hence $P(H_1 \mid D) = 9/(1+9) = 9/10 = 0.9$.
:::

::: {.exercise}

Three mutually exclusive models $M_1, M_2, M_3$ have prior probabilities $(0.5,\, 0.3,\, 0.2)$. The likelihoods of the observed data are in the ratio $P(D \mid M_1) : P(D \mid M_2) : P(D \mid M_3) = 2 : 1 : 4$. Compute the posterior probability of each model.
:::

::: {.solution}
The unnormalised posterior weights are $\pi(M_i)\,P(D \mid M_i)$:
$$(0.5)(2) : (0.3)(1) : (0.2)(4) = 1.0 : 0.3 : 0.8.$$
Normalising by $1.0 + 0.3 + 0.8 = 2.1$ gives
$$P(M_1 \mid D) = \tfrac{10}{21}, \quad P(M_2 \mid D) = \tfrac{3}{21} = \tfrac{1}{7}, \quad P(M_3 \mid D) = \tfrac{8}{21}.$$
:::

::: {.exercise}

A coin is flipped $n = 5$ times and four heads are observed. Compare two simple hypotheses with equal prior probability:
$H_0$: the coin is fair ($p = 1/2$); $H_1$: the coin has $p = 3/4$.
Compute $\text{BF}_{10}$ exactly (no calculator — leave powers of $2$, $3$, $4$ unsimplified) and give the posterior odds.
:::

::: {.solution}
The binomial coefficient $\binom{5}{4}$ cancels in the ratio, leaving
$$\text{BF}_{10} = \frac{(3/4)^4 (1/4)^1}{(1/2)^5} = \frac{3^4/4^5}{1/2^5} = \frac{3^4}{4^5}\cdot 2^5 = \frac{3^4}{2^5} = \frac{81}{32}.$$
With equal priors the posterior odds equal $\text{BF}_{10} = 81/32 \approx 2.53$, so $P(H_1 \mid D) = 81/113$.
:::

::: {.exercise}

Two Poisson models are compared on a single count $k = 2$: $M_0$ with rate $\lambda_0 = 1$, $M_1$ with rate $\lambda_1 = 3$. Write $\text{BF}_{10}$ in closed form using the Poisson PMF. Express it in terms of $e$ (do not evaluate numerically).
:::

::: {.solution}
The Poisson PMF is $P(k \mid \lambda) = e^{-\lambda}\lambda^k/k!$. The factorial $k! = 2!$ cancels:
$$\text{BF}_{10} = \frac{e^{-3}\,3^2/2!}{e^{-1}\,1^2/2!} = 9\,e^{-2}.$$
:::

::: {.exercise}

A Gaussian observation $x$ has known variance $\sigma^2$. Compare $M_0$: $\mu = 0$ versus $M_1$: $\mu = \mu_1$ (a fixed non-zero value). Derive
$$\text{BF}_{10}(x) = \exp\!\left( \frac{2 \mu_1 x - \mu_1^2}{2\sigma^2} \right).$$
For which $x$ does the data favor $M_1$?
:::

::: {.solution}
The Gaussian densities at $x$ give
$$\text{BF}_{10}(x) = \frac{\exp[-(x-\mu_1)^2/(2\sigma^2)]}{\exp[-x^2/(2\sigma^2)]} = \exp\!\left(\frac{2\mu_1 x - \mu_1^2}{2\sigma^2}\right).$$
The data favour $M_1$ (i.e.\ $\text{BF}_{10} > 1$) iff $2\mu_1 x - \mu_1^2 > 0$, i.e.\ $x > \mu_1/2$ when $\mu_1 > 0$ (and $x < \mu_1/2$ when $\mu_1 < 0$): the cross-over is exactly the midpoint between the two competing means.
:::

::: {.exercise}

The prior odds against $H_1$ are $1 : 9$. What Bayes factor $\text{BF}_{10}$ is required to push the posterior probability $P(H_1 \mid D)$ above (a) $1/2$ and (b) $0.95$?
:::

::: {.solution}
Posterior odds $R = P/(1-P)$ are obtained from prior odds $1/9$ via $R = \text{BF}_{10}\cdot(1/9)$, so $\text{BF}_{10} = 9R$.
(a) $P = 1/2 \Rightarrow R = 1 \Rightarrow \text{BF}_{10} = 9$.
(b) $P = 0.95 \Rightarrow R = 0.95/0.05 = 19 \Rightarrow \text{BF}_{10} = 171$.
:::

::: {.exercise}

Repeat the calculation of Exercise 1 with the prior changed first to $P(H_1) = 0.10$ and then to $P(H_1) = 0.70$, keeping the same likelihood ratio. Comment on the sensitivity of the conclusion to the prior.
:::

::: {.solution}
With $\text{BF}_{10} = 4$ the posterior odds are $R = 4\,\pi(H_1)/(1-\pi(H_1))$ and $P(H_1 \mid D) = R/(1+R)$.
For $\pi(H_1) = 0.10$: $R = 4 \cdot (1/9) = 4/9$, so $P(H_1 \mid D) = 4/13 \approx 0.308$.
For $\pi(H_1) = 0.70$: $R = 4 \cdot (7/3) = 28/3$, so $P(H_1 \mid D) = 28/31 \approx 0.903$.
The qualitative conclusion flips: a moderate likelihood ratio of 4 is not enough to overturn a sceptical prior, but easily reinforces an already-favourable one. The Bayes factor must always be reported with the prior used.
:::

::: {.exercise}

Show that when the two hypotheses are assigned equal prior probability, the posterior odds equal the Bayes factor. State the converse and discuss its practical meaning.
:::

::: {.solution}
By definition,
$$\frac{P(H_1 \mid D)}{P(H_0 \mid D)} = \text{BF}_{10}\,\frac{\pi(H_1)}{\pi(H_0)}.$$
If $\pi(H_1) = \pi(H_0) = 1/2$ the prior-odds factor is $1$, so the posterior odds equal $\text{BF}_{10}$. Converse: if the posterior odds equal the Bayes factor, then $\pi(H_1) = \pi(H_0)$. Practically, quoting $\text{BF}_{10}$ as if it were a probability tacitly assumes a 50/50 prior — perfectly fine if disclosed, misleading if not.
:::

::: {.exercise}

A binary outcome $X \mid \theta \sim \text{Bernoulli}(\theta)$ is observed $n$ times with $s$ successes. Under the prior $\theta \sim \text{Uniform}(0,1)$, show that the marginal likelihood is
$$p(\text{data}) = \frac{s!\,(n-s)!}{(n+1)!}.$$
:::

::: {.solution}
The likelihood for an ordered sequence with $s$ successes is $\theta^s(1-\theta)^{n-s}$. With $\pi(\theta) = 1$ on $[0,1]$,
$$p(\text{data}) = \int_0^1 \theta^s (1-\theta)^{n-s}\,d\theta = B(s+1, n-s+1) = \frac{s!\,(n-s)!}{(n+1)!},$$
using $B(a,b) = \Gamma(a)\Gamma(b)/\Gamma(a+b)$ and $\Gamma(k+1) = k!$.
:::

::: {.exercise}

Same likelihood as in the previous exercise but with prior $\theta \sim \text{Beta}(\alpha, \beta)$. Express the marginal likelihood in terms of the Beta function $B(\cdot, \cdot)$. Use it to compute the Bayes factor between $\text{Beta}(\alpha_1, \beta_1)$ and $\text{Beta}(\alpha_0, \beta_0)$ priors.
:::

::: {.solution}
With $\pi(\theta) = \theta^{\alpha-1}(1-\theta)^{\beta-1}/B(\alpha,\beta)$, the marginal likelihood is
$$p(\text{data} \mid \alpha,\beta) = \frac{1}{B(\alpha,\beta)}\int_0^1 \theta^{s+\alpha-1}(1-\theta)^{n-s+\beta-1}\,d\theta = \frac{B(s+\alpha,\,n-s+\beta)}{B(\alpha,\beta)}.$$
The Bayes factor between two Beta priors is therefore
$$\text{BF}_{10} = \frac{B(s+\alpha_1,\,n-s+\beta_1)/B(\alpha_1,\beta_1)}{B(s+\alpha_0,\,n-s+\beta_0)/B(\alpha_0,\beta_0)}.$$
:::

::: {.exercise}

A Gaussian observation $x \mid \theta \sim \mathcal{N}(\theta, 1)$ is compared under two models:
$M_0$: $\theta = 0$;
$M_1$: $\theta \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \tau^2)$ with prior width $\tau$.
Derive $\text{BF}_{10}$ as a function of $x$ and $\tau$. What happens to $\text{BF}_{10}$ as $\tau \to \infty$ with $x$ fixed? Comment on Lindley's paradox.
:::

::: {.solution}
Marginalising the Gaussian likelihood $x \mid \theta \sim \mathcal{N}(\theta,1)$ over the $\mathcal{N}(0,\tau^2)$ prior on $\theta$ gives $x \mid M_1 \sim \mathcal{N}(0, 1+\tau^2)$, while $x \mid M_0 \sim \mathcal{N}(0,1)$. Hence
$$\text{BF}_{10}(x,\tau) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{1+\tau^2}}\,\exp\!\left[\frac{x^2}{2}\cdot\frac{\tau^2}{1+\tau^2}\right].$$
As $\tau \to \infty$ with $x$ fixed, the exponential tends to $e^{x^2/2}$ (finite) while the prefactor $1/\sqrt{1+\tau^2} \to 0$, so $\text{BF}_{10} \to 0$ and $M_0$ always wins. This is Lindley's paradox: an arbitrarily diffuse alternative is penalised by an unbounded Occam factor, so a single observation can never overturn the null no matter how far from zero.
:::

Classify the strength of evidence on the (Jeffreys / Kass–Raftery) scale for each of the Bayes factors $\text{BF}_{10} \in \{1,\, 3,\, 12,\, 150\}$. State the cut-offs you use.
:::

::: {.solution}
Using Jeffreys' cut-offs $(1,3]$ weak, $(3,10]$ moderate (substantial), $(10,30]$ strong, $(30,100]$ very strong, $>100$ decisive:
$\text{BF}_{10} = 1$: no evidence (indifference between $H_0$ and $H_1$).
$\text{BF}_{10} = 3$: at the boundary of weak/moderate — "bare mention" or just-substantial evidence for $H_1$.
$\text{BF}_{10} = 12$: strong evidence for $H_1$.
$\text{BF}_{10} = 150$: decisive evidence for $H_1$.
:::

::: {.exercise}

Two models are retained after data analysis with posterior probabilities $P(M_1 \mid D) = 0.4$, $P(M_2 \mid D) = 0.6$. Their posterior-predictive densities for a future observation $y$ are $\mathcal{N}(0, 1)$ and $\mathcal{N}(2, 1)$ respectively. Write the model-averaged predictive density of $y$ and compute its mean and variance.
:::

::: {.solution}
The model-averaged predictive is a mixture:
$$p(y \mid D) = 0.4\,\phi(y;0,1) + 0.6\,\phi(y;2,1).$$
Mean: $\mathbb{E}[y] = 0.4\cdot 0 + 0.6\cdot 2 = 1.2$.
Variance (law of total variance): $\mathrm{Var}(y) = \mathbb{E}[\mathrm{Var}(y\mid M)] + \mathrm{Var}(\mathbb{E}[y\mid M]) = 1 + (0.4\cdot 0^2 + 0.6\cdot 2^2 - 1.2^2) = 1 + (2.4 - 1.44) = 1.96$.
:::

::: {.exercise}

For a Gaussian likelihood with known variance and a Gaussian prior of width $\sigma_p \gg \sigma_{\text{post}}$, show that the marginal likelihood factorises as
$$p(D) \approx p(D \mid \hat\theta_{\text{MAP}}) \cdot \frac{\sigma_{\text{post}}}{\sigma_p}.$$
Identify the Occam factor and explain why broader priors are automatically penalised.
:::

::: {.solution}
Around the MAP the likelihood is approximately $p(D \mid \theta) \approx p(D \mid \hat\theta_{\text{MAP}})\exp[-(\theta-\hat\theta_{\text{MAP}})^2/(2\sigma_{\text{post}}^2)]$, while the prior on the scale of the peak is essentially flat: $\pi(\theta) \approx 1/(\sqrt{2\pi}\,\sigma_p)$. Marginalising,
$$p(D) \approx p(D \mid \hat\theta_{\text{MAP}})\cdot\frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}\,\sigma_p}\cdot\sqrt{2\pi}\,\sigma_{\text{post}} = p(D \mid \hat\theta_{\text{MAP}})\cdot\frac{\sigma_{\text{post}}}{\sigma_p}.$$
The Occam factor is $\sigma_{\text{post}}/\sigma_p < 1$: a broader prior $\sigma_p$ spreads the prior over a region most of which is inconsistent with the data, multiplicatively shrinking the evidence and so penalising the model.
:::

::: {.exercise}

*(Define and explain.)* Define the Bayes factor in favour of $H_1$ against $H_0$, both for parameter-free models and for parametric models. Show how it relates prior odds and posterior odds, and explain the role of the marginal likelihood. (Brief; no calculation needed.)
:::

::: {.solution}
The Bayes factor is the ratio of marginal likelihoods, $\text{BF}_{10} = p(D \mid H_1)/p(D \mid H_0)$. For parameter-free models this is just the ratio of two ordinary likelihoods; for parametric models $H_j$ with parameters $\theta_j$,
$$p(D \mid H_j) = \int p(D \mid \theta_j, H_j)\,\pi(\theta_j \mid H_j)\,d\theta_j,$$
the prior-averaged likelihood. It multiplies prior odds into posterior odds,
$$\frac{P(H_1 \mid D)}{P(H_0 \mid D)} = \text{BF}_{10}\,\frac{\pi(H_1)}{\pi(H_0)}.$$
The marginal likelihood quantifies how well a model predicted the data *before* fitting; the integral automatically penalises models whose prior wastes mass on regions inconsistent with the data (Occam's razor).
:::

::: {.exercise}

*(Compare and contrast.)* State two practical differences between a Bayesian Bayes-factor analysis and a frequentist $p$-value. Pick a concrete example (e.g.\ testing $\mu = 0$ from $n$ Gaussian samples) and describe the qualitative conclusion each framework draws when $n$ is moderately large.
:::

::: {.solution}
Two key differences: (i) the Bayes factor compares two models symmetrically and can support either $H_0$ or $H_1$, whereas a $p$-value only quantifies tail evidence against $H_0$; (ii) the Bayes factor automatically embeds an Occam penalty against diffuse alternatives, while a $p$-value is independent of the alternative.

For $\mu = 0$ vs.\ $\mu \sim \mathcal{N}(0,\tau^2)$ on $n$ samples with sample mean $\bar x$, the $p$-value is $2\,(1 - \Phi(\sqrt n\,|\bar x|/\sigma))$ and shrinks like $1/\sqrt n$, so any non-zero $\bar x$ is eventually "significant". The Bayes factor $\text{BF}_{10} \propto \sqrt{\sigma^2/(n\tau^2)}\,\exp(\cdots)$ instead carries a $\sqrt{1/n}$ Occam prefactor, so for moderately large $n$ and small $\bar x$ it may favour $H_0$ — Lindley's paradox.
:::

::: {.exercise}

*(Define and explain.)* Define the *Occam factor* and explain why broader priors over a complex model are automatically penalised in the Bayes factor. Give a sketch using a one-dimensional parameter with width $\sigma_p$ and posterior width $\sigma_{\text{post}}$.
:::

::: {.solution}
The Occam factor is the multiplicative term that appears in the Laplace decomposition of the evidence,
$$p(D \mid H) \approx p(D \mid \hat\theta)\cdot\underbrace{\pi(\hat\theta)\cdot\sqrt{2\pi}\,\sigma_{\text{post}}}_{\text{Occam factor}} \;\approx\; p(D \mid \hat\theta)\cdot\frac{\sigma_{\text{post}}}{\sigma_p},$$
i.e.\ the fraction of the prior volume that the posterior actually occupies. Doubling the prior width $\sigma_p$ halves the Occam factor and hence the marginal likelihood, even though the best-fit likelihood does not change. Broader priors waste mass on regions the data exclude, so the Bayes factor penalises them automatically — no ad-hoc complexity term needed.
:::

::: {.exercise}

*(Show that.)* Show that the Bayes factor for two parametric models is invariant under bijective reparametrisation of either model's parameters. (Hint: the marginal likelihood is an integral against the prior, so any Jacobian cancels.)
:::

::: {.solution}
Let $\phi = g(\theta)$ be a bijection with $\theta = g^{-1}(\phi)$ and Jacobian $|d\theta/d\phi|$. The transformed prior density is $\tilde\pi(\phi) = \pi(g^{-1}(\phi))\,|d\theta/d\phi|$ and the likelihood becomes $\tilde p(D \mid \phi) = p(D \mid g^{-1}(\phi))$. Then
$$\int \tilde p(D \mid \phi)\,\tilde\pi(\phi)\,d\phi = \int p(D \mid g^{-1}(\phi))\,\pi(g^{-1}(\phi))\,\left|\frac{d\theta}{d\phi}\right|\,d\phi = \int p(D \mid \theta)\,\pi(\theta)\,d\theta,$$
by change of variables. So each model's marginal likelihood is unchanged under reparametrisation, and hence so is $\text{BF}_{10}$.
:::
