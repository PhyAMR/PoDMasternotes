7.1 Introduction

The Gaussian (or normal) distribution is the most frequently encountered model in statistical inference. Its ubiquity stems from the Central Limit Theorem, which guarantees that sample averages tend toward normality under weak conditions, and from its mathematical tractability—the Gaussian family is closed under many operations and admits conjugate priors. This chapter develops both Bayesian and frequentist approaches to inference of the mean \(\mu\) and variance \(\sigma^2\) of a Gaussian model, emphasizing the conjugate prior structure that yields closed-form posterior distributions.

We begin with the simplest case: inference of the mean with known variance. We then extend to variance estimation with known mean, and finally treat joint inference of both parameters using the Normal-Inverse-Gamma conjugate family. Throughout, we highlight credible intervals, hypothesis testing via the Bayes factor, and the Bayesian \(t\)-test, which naturally marginalizes over the unknown variance.

7.2 Gaussian Sampling Model and Sufficient Statistics

Let \(D = \{x_1, \ldots, x_n\}\) be \(n\) independent observations from a Gaussian distribution with pdf \[p(x_i \mid \mu, \sigma^2) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi\sigma^2}} \exp\left( -\frac{(x_i - \mu)^2}{2\sigma^2} \right).\]

The likelihood function for the data is the product \[p(D \mid \mu, \sigma^2) = \prod_{i=1}^n p(x_i \mid \mu, \sigma^2) \propto (\sigma^2)^{-n/2} \exp\left( -\frac{1}{2\sigma^2} \sum_{i=1}^n (x_i - \mu)^2 \right).\]

Using the key identity \[\sum_{i=1}^n (x_i - \mu)^2 = \sum_{i=1}^n (x_i - \bar{x})^2 + n(\bar{x} - \mu)^2 = S + n(\bar{x} - \mu)^2,\] where \(\bar{x} = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n x_i\) and \(S = \sum_{i=1}^n (x_i - \bar{x})^2\), the likelihood becomes \[p(D \mid \mu, \sigma^2) \propto (\sigma^2)^{-n/2} \exp\left( -\frac{S + n(\bar{x} - \mu)^2}{2\sigma^2} \right).\]

The statistics \(\bar{x}\) and \(S\) are sufficient for \((\mu, \sigma^2)\): they capture all information in the data relevant to parameter inference. Any inference on \((\mu, \sigma^2)\) depends on the data only through these two summaries.

7.3 Inference of the Mean with Known Variance

Assume \(\sigma^2\) is a known constant. The likelihood as a function of \(\mu\) is \[p(D \mid \mu, \sigma^2) \propto \exp\left( -\frac{n(\bar{x} - \mu)^2}{2\sigma^2} \right).\]

This is Gaussian in \(\mu\) centered at the sample mean \(\bar{x}\).

7.3.1 Uniform Prior and Posterior

Assign a uniform (improper) prior \(p(\mu) \propto 1\). This is also the Jeffreys prior for \(\mu\), since the Fisher information \(\mathcal{I}(\mu) = \frac{n}{\sigma^2}\) does not depend on \(\mu\).

By Bayes’ theorem, \[p(\mu \mid D, \sigma^2) \propto p(D \mid \mu, \sigma^2) \cdot p(\mu) \propto \exp\left( -\frac{n(\bar{x} - \mu)^2}{2\sigma^2} \right).\]

The posterior distribution of \(\mu\) is \[\mu \mid D, \sigma^2 \sim \mathcal{N}\left( \bar{x}, \frac{\sigma^2}{n} \right).\]

The posterior mean equals the maximum likelihood estimate (MLE) \(\hat{\mu}_{\text{MLE}} = \bar{x}\), and the posterior variance shrinks as \(1/n\), reflecting increased confidence with larger sample size.

7.3.2 Conjugate Normal-Normal Model

To incorporate prior knowledge, use a Gaussian prior \(\mu \sim \mathcal{N}(\mu_0, \sigma_0^2)\).

The posterior is obtained by multiplying the likelihood and prior: \[p(\mu \mid D, \sigma^2) \propto \exp\left( -\frac{n(\bar{x} - \mu)^2}{2\sigma^2} \right) \cdot \exp\left( -\frac{(\mu - \mu_0)^2}{2\sigma_0^2} \right).\]

Completing the square in the exponent: \[p(\mu \mid D, \sigma^2) \propto \exp\left( -\frac{\kappa_n (\mu - \mu_n)^2}{2} \right),\] where the posterior precision and mean are

Normal-Normal Conjugate Update: \[\begin{align} \kappa_n &= \frac{1}{\sigma_0^2} + \frac{n}{\sigma^2}, \\ \mu_n &= \frac{\mu_0/\sigma_0^2 + n\bar{x}/\sigma^2}{\kappa_n} = \frac{\mu_0/\sigma_0^2 + n\bar{x}/\sigma^2}{1/\sigma_0^2 + n/\sigma^2}. \end{align}\]

The posterior is \(\mu \mid D, \sigma^2 \sim \mathcal{N}(\mu_n, \kappa_n^{-1})\).

Interpretation: Precisions add; the posterior mean is a precision-weighted average of the prior mean and sample mean. As \(n \to \infty\), the posterior is dominated by the data (\(\mu_n \to \bar{x}\)). For a weak prior (\(\sigma_0^2 \to \infty\)), \(\kappa_n \to n/\sigma^2\) and \(\mu_n \to \bar{x}\), recovering the uniform prior result.

Measurements have known variance \(\sigma^2 = 4\). The sample mean from \(n = 9\) measurements is \(\bar x = 3\). A prior for the mean is \(\mu \sim \mathcal{N}(0, 1)\). Compute the posterior mean and variance, and compare with the flat-prior posterior mean.

Solution. With \(\sigma_0^2 = 1\) and \(n/\sigma^2 = 9/4\), the conjugate posterior precision and variance are \[\kappa_n = \frac{1}{\sigma_0^2} + \frac{n}{\sigma^2} = 1 + \frac{9}{4} = \frac{13}{4}, \qquad V_n = \kappa_n^{-1} = \frac{4}{13}.\] The posterior mean is the precision-weighted average \[\mu_n = V_n\!\left(\frac{\mu_0}{\sigma_0^2} + \frac{n\bar x}{\sigma^2}\right) = \frac{4}{13}\!\left(0 + \frac{9 \cdot 3}{4}\right) = \frac{27}{13} \approx 2.08.\] With the flat (improper) prior the posterior would be \(\mu \mid D \sim \mathcal{N}(\bar x, \sigma^2/n) = \mathcal{N}(3,\,4/9)\), so the posterior mean is just \(\bar x = 3\). The proper prior shrinks the estimate from \(3\) toward the prior mean \(0\), by a factor controlled by the ratio of prior to data precision. With only \(n = 9\) samples the prior carries non-trivial weight; with \(n \to \infty\) the shrinkage vanishes.

7.4 Inference of the Variance with Known Mean

Now assume \(\mu\) is known and infer \(\sigma^2\). The likelihood is \[p(D \mid \mu, \sigma^2) \propto (\sigma^2)^{-n/2} \exp\left( -\frac{\sum_i (x_i - \mu)^2}{2\sigma^2} \right).\]

Setting \(\nu = \sum_i (x_i - \mu)^2\), the likelihood depends on \(\sigma^2\) via \[p(D \mid \mu, \sigma^2) \propto (\sigma^2)^{-n/2} \exp\left( -\frac{\nu}{2\sigma^2} \right).\]

7.4.1 Jeffreys Prior for Scale Parameters

Variance is a scale parameter: if we rescale \(x \to ax\), then \(\sigma^2 \to a^2\sigma^2\). As shown in Chapter 5 (§ Fisher information, Cramér–Rao, and the Jeffreys prior), the Jeffreys prior \(p_J(\theta) \propto \sqrt{\det \mathcal{I}(\theta)}\) is reparametrization-invariant. For the Gaussian scale parameter \(\sigma^2\) with known mean, the Fisher information is \(\mathcal{I}(\sigma^2) = \frac{n}{2(\sigma^2)^2}\), yielding \(p_J(\sigma^2) \propto \frac{1}{\sigma^2}\) (or equivalently, \(p_J(\log\sigma^2) \propto 1\), uniform on log-scale). This prior ensures invariance under rescaling—critical for Bayes factors. Chapter 5 provides an extended example and reparametrization invariance proof; here we apply it directly to the Gaussian posterior.

7.4.2 Inverse-Gamma Conjugate Family

The Inverse-Gamma distribution is conjugate for variance estimation:

If \(X \sim \text{Gamma}(\alpha, \beta)\) with pdf \(f(x) = \frac{\beta^\alpha}{\Gamma(\alpha)} x^{\alpha-1} e^{-\beta x}\), then \(1/X \sim \text{Inv-Gamma}(\alpha, \beta)\) with pdf \[f(z) = \frac{\beta^\alpha}{\Gamma(\alpha)} (1/z)^{\alpha+1} \exp(-\beta/z).\]

A prior \(\sigma^2 \sim \text{Inv-Gamma}(\alpha_0, \beta_0)\) combined with the likelihood yields a posterior:

Inverse-Gamma Conjugate Update: \[\sigma^2 \mid D, \mu \sim \text{Inv-Gamma}\left( \alpha_0 + \frac{n}{2}, \beta_0 + \frac{\nu}{2} \right),\] where \(\nu = \sum_i (x_i - \mu)^2\).

The Jeffreys prior corresponds to \(\alpha_0 = 0\) and \(\beta_0 = 0\) (or \(\alpha_0 = -1/2\) to ensure integrability). The posterior mean is \(\mathbb{E}[\sigma^2 \mid D] = \frac{\beta_n}{\alpha_n - 1}\) for \(\alpha_n > 1\).

7.5 Joint Inference: Normal-Inverse-Gamma Model

Most realistic: both \(\mu\) and \(\sigma^2\) are unknown. Use a factorized prior \[p(\mu, \sigma^2) = p(\mu \mid \sigma^2) p(\sigma^2),\] with \(\mu \mid \sigma^2 \sim \mathcal{N}(\mu_0, \sigma^2/\kappa_0)\) and \(\sigma^2 \sim \text{Inv-Gamma}(\alpha_0, \beta_0)\).

This is the Normal-Inverse-Gamma (NIG) conjugate family with hyperparameters \((\mu_0, \kappa_0, \alpha_0, \beta_0)\).

7.5.1 Posterior Update

Combining likelihood with the NIG prior yields a posterior with hyperparameters:

Normal-Inverse-Gamma Conjugate Update: \[\begin{align} \kappa_n &= \kappa_0 + n, \\ \mu_n &= \frac{\kappa_0 \mu_0 + n\bar{x}}{\kappa_n}, \\ \alpha_n &= \alpha_0 + \frac{n}{2}, \\ \beta_n &= \beta_0 + \frac{S}{2} + \frac{\kappa_0 n}{2(\kappa_0 + n)} (\bar{x} - \mu_0)^2, \end{align}\] where \(S = \sum_i (x_i - \bar{x})^2\).

The joint posterior factorizes as \[p(\mu, \sigma^2 \mid D) \propto (\sigma^2)^{-(\alpha_n + 1)} \exp\left( -\frac{\beta_n}{\sigma^2} - \frac{\kappa_n(\mu - \mu_n)^2}{2\sigma^2} \right).\]

7.5.2 Marginal Posterior of the Mean

Marginalizing \(\sigma^2\) yields the marginal posterior of \(\mu\):

The posterior marginal distribution of \(\mu\) given \(D\) is \[\mu \mid D \sim t_{2\alpha_n}\left( \mu_n, \frac{\beta_n}{\alpha_n \kappa_n} \right),\] a Student-\(t\) distribution with \(\nu = 2\alpha_n\) degrees of freedom, location \(\mu_n\), and scale parameter \(\sqrt{\beta_n/(\alpha_n \kappa_n)}\).

Interpretation: The heavy tails of the \(t\)-distribution reflect additional uncertainty from not knowing \(\sigma^2\). For large \(n\), \(\nu = 2\alpha_n \approx n \to \infty\), so \(t\)-distribution converges to Gaussian, recovering asymptotic normality.

7.6 Credible Intervals and Regions

7.6.1 Central Credible Interval for the Mean

A central \((1-\alpha)\)% credible interval is \[\mu \pm t_{2\alpha_n, 1-\alpha/2} \sqrt{\frac{\beta_n}{\alpha_n \kappa_n}},\] where \(t_{\nu, p}\) denotes the \(p\)-quantile of a Student-\(t\) distribution with \(\nu\) degrees of freedom.

Frequentist analogue: In the large-sample limit, this recovers the familiar confidence interval \(\hat{\mu} \pm z_{1-\alpha/2} \sqrt{\hat{\sigma}^2/n}\).

7.6.2 Joint Credible Region

A joint \((1-\alpha)\)% credible region for \(\theta = (\mu, \sigma^2)\) is the ellipse \[(\theta - \hat{\theta})^T \mathcal{I}_n(\hat{\theta}) (\theta - \hat{\theta}) \le \chi^2_{2, 1-\alpha},\] where \(\mathcal{I}_n(\hat{\theta})\) is the Fisher information matrix evaluated at the MLE and \(\chi^2_{2, 1-\alpha}\) is the \((1-\alpha)\) quantile of the chi-squared distribution with 2 degrees of freedom.

The posterior asymptotically factorizes: the estimated correlation between \(\mu\) and \(\sigma^2\) vanishes as \(n \to \infty\). This convergence to Gaussian form and factorization is guaranteed by the Bernstein–von Mises theorem (Chapter 5, § Fisher information, Cramér–Rao, and the Jeffreys prior), which states that the posterior becomes approximately \(\mathcal{N}(\hat{\theta}_{\mathrm{ML}}, \mathcal{I}_n(\theta_*)^{-1})\) for large \(n\), independently of the prior. This is why Bayesian credible intervals and frequentist confidence intervals asymptotically agree.

7.7 Bayesian Hypothesis Testing

7.7.1 One-Sided Test for the Mean

Consider \(H_0: \mu \le \mu^* \text{ vs. } H_1: \mu > \mu^*\).

The posterior probability of \(H_1\) is \[P(H_1 \mid D) = 1 - F_t\left( \frac{\mu^* - \mu_n}{\sqrt{\beta_n/(\alpha_n \kappa_n)}} \right),\] where \(F_t\) is the CDF of a Student-\(t\) distribution with \(2\alpha_n\) degrees of freedom.

A decision rule: reject \(H_0\) if \(P(H_1 \mid D) > 1 - \alpha\). Equivalently, reject if \(\mu^*\) lies below the lower bound of the \((1-\alpha)\)% credible interval.

7.7.2 Two-Sided Test and Bayes Factors

For \(H_0: \mu = \mu^* \text{ vs. } H_1: \mu \ne \mu^*\), compare the marginal likelihoods:

The Bayes factor of \(H_1\) over \(H_0\) is \[\text{BF}_{10} = \frac{p(D \mid H_1)}{p(D \mid H_0)} = \frac{\int p(D \mid \mu, \sigma^2) p(\mu, \sigma^2 \mid H_1) d\mu d\sigma^2}{\int_{\mu=\mu^*} p(D \mid \mu^*, \sigma^2) p(\sigma^2 \mid H_0) d\sigma^2}.\]

Interpretation: - \(\text{BF}_{10} > 1\): evidence favors \(H_1\) (mean differs from \(\mu^*\)). - \(\text{BF}_{10} < 1\): evidence favors \(H_0\). - Magnitude quantifies strength of evidence. The Bayes factor, unlike frequentist \(p\)-values, is invariant under the choice of scale (Jeffreys prior ensures this).

7.7.3 Bayesian \(t\)-Test

The Bayesian \(t\)-test compares observed \(t = (\bar{x} - \mu^*) / \sqrt{S/(n(n-1))}\) to its posterior predictive distribution under \(H_0\). With the NIG conjugate prior, the marginal likelihood under \(H_0\) is a \(t\)-distribution, naturally accounting for variance uncertainty without ad-hoc degrees-of-freedom corrections.

7.8 Posterior Predictive Distribution

Given posterior \(p(\mu, \sigma^2 \mid D)\), the posterior predictive distribution for a new observation \(\tilde{x}\) is \[p(\tilde{x} \mid D) = \int p(\tilde{x} \mid \mu, \sigma^2) p(\mu, \sigma^2 \mid D) d\mu d\sigma^2.\]

For the NIG model, this marginalizes to a Student-\(t\) distribution with the same degrees of freedom and location as the posterior for \(\mu\), but with inflated scale (reflecting both parameter and observational uncertainty): \[\tilde{x} \mid D \sim t_{2\alpha_n}\left( \mu_n, \frac{\beta_n}{\alpha_n} \left(1 + \frac{1}{\kappa_n}\right) \right).\]

The posterior predictive intervals are wider than credible intervals for the mean, appropriately quantifying full predictive uncertainty.

7.9 Frequentist Contrast

The frequentist approach uses the sample \(t\)-statistic \[t = \frac{\bar{x} - \mu}{\sqrt{S / (n(n-1))}},\] which under \(H_0: \mu = \mu^*\) follows \(t_{n-1}\). Rejection is based on \(p\)-value: \(p = P(|T| \ge |t_{\text{obs}}| \mid H_0)\).

Key differences from Bayes:

  1. Frequentist \(t\)-test: assumes \(\mu^*\) exactly true under \(H_0\); \(p\)-value is the probability of observing data as extreme or more extreme under repeated sampling.

  2. Bayesian \(t\)-test: posterior probability that \(\mu > \mu^*\) (or any specified region); updates beliefs given observed data; allows flexible loss functions for decision-making.

  3. Sample size: Frequentist \(p\)-values can remain small for minor effects with large \(n\). Bayes factors adapt: as data accumulate under \(H_1\), they grow; but if data weakly support \(H_1\) relative to prediction, BF remains modest.

  4. Asymptotic equivalence: For fixed effect size and large \(n\), frequentist confidence intervals and Bayesian credible intervals converge; the posterior becomes approximately Gaussian by Bernstein–von Mises.

7.11 Exercises

Conjugate Normal–Normal model with known variance. Observe \(n = 4\) measurements with sample mean \(\bar x = 2\) from \(\mathcal{N}(\mu, \sigma^2 = 1)\), with prior \(\mu \sim \mathcal{N}(0, 1)\). Compute the posterior mean and variance.

Solution.

Same model as above. As \(n \to \infty\) with \(\bar x \to \mu_*\) fixed, what does the posterior tend to? State the result in terms of \(\mu_*\) and \(\sigma^2\).

Solution.

Inference of the variance with known mean. Observe \(n\) i.i.d. samples from \(\mathcal{N}(0, \sigma^2)\) with sum of squares \(S = \sum_i x_i^2\). Use the conjugate Inverse-Gamma prior \(\sigma^2 \sim \text{IG}(\alpha_0, \beta_0)\) and derive the posterior.

Solution.

Show that \((\bar x,\,s^2) = (\tfrac{1}{n}\sum_i x_i,\ \tfrac{1}{n-1}\sum_i (x_i - \bar x)^2)\) are sufficient statistics for \((\mu, \sigma^2)\) in the i.i.d. \(\mathcal{N}(\mu, \sigma^2)\) model, using the factorisation theorem.

Solution.

Conjugate Normal–Inverse-Gamma joint posterior. Under prior \((\mu, \sigma^2) \sim \text{NIG}(\mu_0, \kappa_0, \alpha_0, \beta_0)\), write the posterior parameters \((\mu_n, \kappa_n, \alpha_n, \beta_n)\) after observing \(n\) samples with sample mean \(\bar x\) and sum of squares from \(\bar x\) equal to \(S_{xx}\).

Solution.

For the NIG conjugate model, derive the marginal posterior \(p(\mu \mid D)\) and identify it as a Student-\(t\) distribution. State its location, scale, and degrees of freedom.

Solution.

Posterior predictive in the conjugate Normal model with known \(\sigma^2\). Derive \(p(x_{\text{new}} \mid D)\) when \(\mu \sim \mathcal{N}(\mu_0, \sigma_0^2)\) and the data have sample mean \(\bar x\).

Solution.

For the conjugate model with known \(\sigma^2\), derive the \(95\%\) equal-tailed credible interval for \(\mu\) using the conjugate posterior parameters \((\mu_n, V_n)\). Use \(z_{0.975} \approx 2\).

Solution.

Sequential updating. With known \(\sigma^2\) and prior \(\mu \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \tau^2)\), you observe data in two batches with sample means \(\bar x_1, \bar x_2\) and counts \(n_1, n_2\). Show that updating sequentially gives the same posterior as updating in one batch.

Solution.

Posterior predictive variance decomposition. Show that the posterior predictive variance in the conjugate Normal model is \(V_n + \sigma^2\), and identify which term is parameter uncertainty and which is irreducible noise.

Solution.

Multivariate conjugate update. For \(\mathbf x \sim \mathcal{N}(\boldsymbol\mu, \boldsymbol\Sigma)\) with \(\boldsymbol\Sigma\) known and prior \(\boldsymbol\mu \sim \mathcal{N}(\boldsymbol\mu_0, \boldsymbol\Sigma_0)\), write the posterior \(p(\boldsymbol\mu \mid D)\) after observing one sample \(\mathbf x\).

Solution.

Bayesian \(z\)-test. With prior \(\mu \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \tau^2)\) and known \(\sigma^2\), compute the Bayes factor between \(H_0:\mu = 0\) and \(H_1:\mu \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \tau^2)\) as a function of the standardised sample mean \(z = \bar x \sqrt{n}/\sigma\). (You can reuse the worked example “Closed-form Bayes factor for a Gaussian mean” in Chapter 4.)

Solution.

## Introduction

The Gaussian (or normal) distribution is the most frequently encountered model in statistical inference. Its ubiquity stems from the Central Limit Theorem, which guarantees that sample averages tend toward normality under weak conditions, and from its mathematical tractability—the Gaussian family is closed under many operations and admits conjugate priors. This chapter develops both Bayesian and frequentist approaches to inference of the mean $\mu$ and variance $\sigma^2$ of a Gaussian model, emphasizing the conjugate prior structure that yields closed-form posterior distributions.

We begin with the simplest case: inference of the mean with known variance. We then extend to variance estimation with known mean, and finally treat joint inference of both parameters using the Normal-Inverse-Gamma conjugate family. Throughout, we highlight credible intervals, hypothesis testing via the Bayes factor, and the Bayesian $t$-test, which naturally marginalizes over the unknown variance.

## Gaussian Sampling Model and Sufficient Statistics

:::{.definition}
Let $D = \{x_1, \ldots, x_n\}$ be $n$ independent observations from a Gaussian distribution with pdf
$$p(x_i \mid \mu, \sigma^2) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi\sigma^2}} \exp\left( -\frac{(x_i - \mu)^2}{2\sigma^2} \right).$$
:::

The likelihood function for the data is the product
$$p(D \mid \mu, \sigma^2) = \prod_{i=1}^n p(x_i \mid \mu, \sigma^2) \propto (\sigma^2)^{-n/2} \exp\left( -\frac{1}{2\sigma^2} \sum_{i=1}^n (x_i - \mu)^2 \right).$$

Using the key identity
$$\sum_{i=1}^n (x_i - \mu)^2 = \sum_{i=1}^n (x_i - \bar{x})^2 + n(\bar{x} - \mu)^2 = S + n(\bar{x} - \mu)^2,$$
where $\bar{x} = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n x_i$ and $S = \sum_{i=1}^n (x_i - \bar{x})^2$, the likelihood becomes
$$p(D \mid \mu, \sigma^2) \propto (\sigma^2)^{-n/2} \exp\left( -\frac{S + n(\bar{x} - \mu)^2}{2\sigma^2} \right).$$

:::{.definition}
The statistics $\bar{x}$ and $S$ are **sufficient** for $(\mu, \sigma^2)$: they capture all information in the data relevant to parameter inference. Any inference on $(\mu, \sigma^2)$ depends on the data only through these two summaries.
:::

## Inference of the Mean with Known Variance

Assume $\sigma^2$ is a known constant. The likelihood as a function of $\mu$ is
$$p(D \mid \mu, \sigma^2) \propto \exp\left( -\frac{n(\bar{x} - \mu)^2}{2\sigma^2} \right).$$

This is Gaussian in $\mu$ centered at the sample mean $\bar{x}$.

### Uniform Prior and Posterior

Assign a uniform (improper) prior $p(\mu) \propto 1$. This is also the Jeffreys prior for $\mu$, since the Fisher information $\mathcal{I}(\mu) = \frac{n}{\sigma^2}$ does not depend on $\mu$.

By Bayes' theorem,
$$p(\mu \mid D, \sigma^2) \propto p(D \mid \mu, \sigma^2) \cdot p(\mu) \propto \exp\left( -\frac{n(\bar{x} - \mu)^2}{2\sigma^2} \right).$$

:::{.theorem}
The posterior distribution of $\mu$ is
$$\mu \mid D, \sigma^2 \sim \mathcal{N}\left( \bar{x}, \frac{\sigma^2}{n} \right).$$
:::

The posterior mean equals the maximum likelihood estimate (MLE) $\hat{\mu}_{\text{MLE}} = \bar{x}$, and the posterior variance shrinks as $1/n$, reflecting increased confidence with larger sample size.

### Conjugate Normal-Normal Model

To incorporate prior knowledge, use a Gaussian prior $\mu \sim \mathcal{N}(\mu_0, \sigma_0^2)$.

The posterior is obtained by multiplying the likelihood and prior:
$$p(\mu \mid D, \sigma^2) \propto \exp\left( -\frac{n(\bar{x} - \mu)^2}{2\sigma^2} \right) \cdot \exp\left( -\frac{(\mu - \mu_0)^2}{2\sigma_0^2} \right).$$

Completing the square in the exponent:
$$p(\mu \mid D, \sigma^2) \propto \exp\left( -\frac{\kappa_n (\mu - \mu_n)^2}{2} \right),$$
where the posterior precision and mean are

:::{.definition}
**Normal-Normal Conjugate Update:**
\begin{align}
\kappa_n &= \frac{1}{\sigma_0^2} + \frac{n}{\sigma^2}, \\
\mu_n &= \frac{\mu_0/\sigma_0^2 + n\bar{x}/\sigma^2}{\kappa_n} = \frac{\mu_0/\sigma_0^2 + n\bar{x}/\sigma^2}{1/\sigma_0^2 + n/\sigma^2}.
\end{align}
:::

The posterior is $\mu \mid D, \sigma^2 \sim \mathcal{N}(\mu_n, \kappa_n^{-1})$.

**Interpretation:** Precisions add; the posterior mean is a precision-weighted average of the prior mean and sample mean. As $n \to \infty$, the posterior is dominated by the data ($\mu_n \to \bar{x}$). For a weak prior ($\sigma_0^2 \to \infty$), $\kappa_n \to n/\sigma^2$ and $\mu_n \to \bar{x}$, recovering the uniform prior result.

::: {.example name="Normal–Normal update: small sample with informative prior (exam-style)"}
Measurements have known variance $\sigma^2 = 4$. The sample mean from $n = 9$ measurements is $\bar x = 3$. A prior for the mean is $\mu \sim \mathcal{N}(0, 1)$. Compute the posterior mean and variance, and compare with the flat-prior posterior mean.

**Solution.** With $\sigma_0^2 = 1$ and $n/\sigma^2 = 9/4$, the conjugate posterior precision and variance are
$$\kappa_n = \frac{1}{\sigma_0^2} + \frac{n}{\sigma^2} = 1 + \frac{9}{4} = \frac{13}{4}, \qquad V_n = \kappa_n^{-1} = \frac{4}{13}.$$
The posterior mean is the precision-weighted average
$$\mu_n = V_n\!\left(\frac{\mu_0}{\sigma_0^2} + \frac{n\bar x}{\sigma^2}\right) = \frac{4}{13}\!\left(0 + \frac{9 \cdot 3}{4}\right) = \frac{27}{13} \approx 2.08.$$
With the flat (improper) prior the posterior would be $\mu \mid D \sim \mathcal{N}(\bar x, \sigma^2/n) = \mathcal{N}(3,\,4/9)$, so the posterior mean is just $\bar x = 3$. The proper prior shrinks the estimate from $3$ toward the prior mean $0$, by a factor controlled by the ratio of prior to data precision. With only $n = 9$ samples the prior carries non-trivial weight; with $n \to \infty$ the shrinkage vanishes.
:::

## Inference of the Variance with Known Mean

Now assume $\mu$ is known and infer $\sigma^2$. The likelihood is
$$p(D \mid \mu, \sigma^2) \propto (\sigma^2)^{-n/2} \exp\left( -\frac{\sum_i (x_i - \mu)^2}{2\sigma^2} \right).$$

Setting $\nu = \sum_i (x_i - \mu)^2$, the likelihood depends on $\sigma^2$ via
$$p(D \mid \mu, \sigma^2) \propto (\sigma^2)^{-n/2} \exp\left( -\frac{\nu}{2\sigma^2} \right).$$

### Jeffreys Prior for Scale Parameters

Variance is a **scale parameter**: if we rescale $x \to ax$, then $\sigma^2 \to a^2\sigma^2$. As shown in Chapter 5 (§ Fisher information, Cramér–Rao, and the Jeffreys prior), the Jeffreys prior $p_J(\theta) \propto \sqrt{\det \mathcal{I}(\theta)}$ is reparametrization-invariant. For the Gaussian scale parameter $\sigma^2$ with known mean, the Fisher information is $\mathcal{I}(\sigma^2) = \frac{n}{2(\sigma^2)^2}$, yielding $p_J(\sigma^2) \propto \frac{1}{\sigma^2}$ (or equivalently, $p_J(\log\sigma^2) \propto 1$, uniform on log-scale). This prior ensures invariance under rescaling—critical for Bayes factors. Chapter 5 provides an extended example and reparametrization invariance proof; here we apply it directly to the Gaussian posterior.

### Inverse-Gamma Conjugate Family

The Inverse-Gamma distribution is conjugate for variance estimation:

:::{.definition}
If $X \sim \text{Gamma}(\alpha, \beta)$ with pdf $f(x) = \frac{\beta^\alpha}{\Gamma(\alpha)} x^{\alpha-1} e^{-\beta x}$, then $1/X \sim \text{Inv-Gamma}(\alpha, \beta)$ with pdf
$$f(z) = \frac{\beta^\alpha}{\Gamma(\alpha)} (1/z)^{\alpha+1} \exp(-\beta/z).$$
:::

A prior $\sigma^2 \sim \text{Inv-Gamma}(\alpha_0, \beta_0)$ combined with the likelihood yields a posterior:

:::{.theorem}
**Inverse-Gamma Conjugate Update:**
$$\sigma^2 \mid D, \mu \sim \text{Inv-Gamma}\left( \alpha_0 + \frac{n}{2}, \beta_0 + \frac{\nu}{2} \right),$$
where $\nu = \sum_i (x_i - \mu)^2$.
:::

The Jeffreys prior corresponds to $\alpha_0 = 0$ and $\beta_0 = 0$ (or $\alpha_0 = -1/2$ to ensure integrability). The posterior mean is $\mathbb{E}[\sigma^2 \mid D] = \frac{\beta_n}{\alpha_n - 1}$ for $\alpha_n > 1$.

## Joint Inference: Normal-Inverse-Gamma Model

Most realistic: both $\mu$ and $\sigma^2$ are unknown. Use a factorized prior
$$p(\mu, \sigma^2) = p(\mu \mid \sigma^2) p(\sigma^2),$$
with $\mu \mid \sigma^2 \sim \mathcal{N}(\mu_0, \sigma^2/\kappa_0)$ and $\sigma^2 \sim \text{Inv-Gamma}(\alpha_0, \beta_0)$.

This is the **Normal-Inverse-Gamma (NIG)** conjugate family with hyperparameters $(\mu_0, \kappa_0, \alpha_0, \beta_0)$.

### Posterior Update

Combining likelihood with the NIG prior yields a posterior with hyperparameters:

:::{.definition}
**Normal-Inverse-Gamma Conjugate Update:**
\begin{align}
\kappa_n &= \kappa_0 + n, \\
\mu_n &= \frac{\kappa_0 \mu_0 + n\bar{x}}{\kappa_n}, \\
\alpha_n &= \alpha_0 + \frac{n}{2}, \\
\beta_n &= \beta_0 + \frac{S}{2} + \frac{\kappa_0 n}{2(\kappa_0 + n)} (\bar{x} - \mu_0)^2,
\end{align}
where $S = \sum_i (x_i - \bar{x})^2$.
:::

The joint posterior factorizes as
$$p(\mu, \sigma^2 \mid D) \propto (\sigma^2)^{-(\alpha_n + 1)} \exp\left( -\frac{\beta_n}{\sigma^2} - \frac{\kappa_n(\mu - \mu_n)^2}{2\sigma^2} \right).$$

### Marginal Posterior of the Mean

Marginalizing $\sigma^2$ yields the marginal posterior of $\mu$:

:::{.theorem}
The posterior marginal distribution of $\mu$ given $D$ is
$$\mu \mid D \sim t_{2\alpha_n}\left( \mu_n, \frac{\beta_n}{\alpha_n \kappa_n} \right),$$
a Student-$t$ distribution with $\nu = 2\alpha_n$ degrees of freedom, location $\mu_n$, and scale parameter $\sqrt{\beta_n/(\alpha_n \kappa_n)}$.
:::

**Interpretation:** The heavy tails of the $t$-distribution reflect additional uncertainty from not knowing $\sigma^2$. For large $n$, $\nu = 2\alpha_n \approx n \to \infty$, so $t$-distribution converges to Gaussian, recovering asymptotic normality.

## Credible Intervals and Regions

### Central Credible Interval for the Mean

A central $(1-\alpha)$% credible interval is
$$\mu \pm t_{2\alpha_n, 1-\alpha/2} \sqrt{\frac{\beta_n}{\alpha_n \kappa_n}},$$
where $t_{\nu, p}$ denotes the $p$-quantile of a Student-$t$ distribution with $\nu$ degrees of freedom.

**Frequentist analogue:** In the large-sample limit, this recovers the familiar confidence interval $\hat{\mu} \pm z_{1-\alpha/2} \sqrt{\hat{\sigma}^2/n}$.

### Joint Credible Region

A joint $(1-\alpha)$% credible region for $\theta = (\mu, \sigma^2)$ is the ellipse
$$(\theta - \hat{\theta})^T \mathcal{I}_n(\hat{\theta}) (\theta - \hat{\theta}) \le \chi^2_{2, 1-\alpha},$$
where $\mathcal{I}_n(\hat{\theta})$ is the Fisher information matrix evaluated at the MLE and $\chi^2_{2, 1-\alpha}$ is the $(1-\alpha)$ quantile of the chi-squared distribution with 2 degrees of freedom.

The posterior asymptotically factorizes: the estimated correlation between $\mu$ and $\sigma^2$ vanishes as $n \to \infty$. This convergence to Gaussian form and factorization is guaranteed by the **Bernstein–von Mises theorem** (Chapter 5, § Fisher information, Cramér–Rao, and the Jeffreys prior), which states that the posterior becomes approximately $\mathcal{N}(\hat{\theta}_{\mathrm{ML}}, \mathcal{I}_n(\theta_*)^{-1})$ for large $n$, independently of the prior. This is why Bayesian credible intervals and frequentist confidence intervals asymptotically agree.

## Bayesian Hypothesis Testing

### One-Sided Test for the Mean

Consider $H_0: \mu \le \mu^* \text{ vs. } H_1: \mu > \mu^*$.

The posterior probability of $H_1$ is
$$P(H_1 \mid D) = 1 - F_t\left( \frac{\mu^* - \mu_n}{\sqrt{\beta_n/(\alpha_n \kappa_n)}} \right),$$
where $F_t$ is the CDF of a Student-$t$ distribution with $2\alpha_n$ degrees of freedom.

A decision rule: reject $H_0$ if $P(H_1 \mid D) > 1 - \alpha$. Equivalently, reject if $\mu^*$ lies below the lower bound of the $(1-\alpha)$% credible interval.

### Two-Sided Test and Bayes Factors

For $H_0: \mu = \mu^* \text{ vs. } H_1: \mu \ne \mu^*$, compare the marginal likelihoods:

:::{.definition}
The **Bayes factor** of $H_1$ over $H_0$ is
$$\text{BF}_{10} = \frac{p(D \mid H_1)}{p(D \mid H_0)} = \frac{\int p(D \mid \mu, \sigma^2) p(\mu, \sigma^2 \mid H_1) d\mu d\sigma^2}{\int_{\mu=\mu^*} p(D \mid \mu^*, \sigma^2) p(\sigma^2 \mid H_0) d\sigma^2}.$$
:::

**Interpretation:**
- $\text{BF}_{10} > 1$: evidence favors $H_1$ (mean differs from $\mu^*$).
- $\text{BF}_{10} < 1$: evidence favors $H_0$.
- Magnitude quantifies strength of evidence. The Bayes factor, unlike frequentist $p$-values, is invariant under the choice of scale (Jeffreys prior ensures this).

### Bayesian $t$-Test

The Bayesian $t$-test compares observed $t = (\bar{x} - \mu^*) / \sqrt{S/(n(n-1))}$ to its posterior predictive distribution under $H_0$. With the NIG conjugate prior, the marginal likelihood under $H_0$ is a $t$-distribution, naturally accounting for variance uncertainty without ad-hoc degrees-of-freedom corrections.

## Posterior Predictive Distribution

Given posterior $p(\mu, \sigma^2 \mid D)$, the posterior predictive distribution for a new observation $\tilde{x}$ is
$$p(\tilde{x} \mid D) = \int p(\tilde{x} \mid \mu, \sigma^2) p(\mu, \sigma^2 \mid D) d\mu d\sigma^2.$$

For the NIG model, this marginalizes to a Student-$t$ distribution with the same degrees of freedom and location as the posterior for $\mu$, but with inflated scale (reflecting both parameter and observational uncertainty):
$$\tilde{x} \mid D \sim t_{2\alpha_n}\left( \mu_n, \frac{\beta_n}{\alpha_n} \left(1 + \frac{1}{\kappa_n}\right) \right).$$

The posterior predictive intervals are wider than credible intervals for the mean, appropriately quantifying full predictive uncertainty.

## Frequentist Contrast

The frequentist approach uses the **sample $t$-statistic**
$$t = \frac{\bar{x} - \mu}{\sqrt{S / (n(n-1))}},$$
which under $H_0: \mu = \mu^*$ follows $t_{n-1}$. Rejection is based on $p$-value: $p = P(|T| \ge |t_{\text{obs}}| \mid H_0)$.

**Key differences from Bayes:**

1. **Frequentist $t$-test:** assumes $\mu^*$ exactly true under $H_0$; $p$-value is the probability of observing data as extreme or more extreme under repeated sampling.

2. **Bayesian $t$-test:** posterior probability that $\mu > \mu^*$ (or any specified region); updates beliefs given observed data; allows flexible loss functions for decision-making.

3. **Sample size:** Frequentist $p$-values can remain small for minor effects with large $n$. Bayes factors adapt: as data accumulate under $H_1$, they grow; but if data weakly support $H_1$ relative to prediction, BF remains modest.

4. **Asymptotic equivalence:** For fixed effect size and large $n$, frequentist confidence intervals and Bayesian credible intervals converge; the posterior becomes approximately Gaussian by Bernstein–von Mises.
