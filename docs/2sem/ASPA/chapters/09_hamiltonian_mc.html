10.1 Introduction

Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) methods form the backbone of modern Bayesian inference, but they face a fundamental challenge: as dimension and posterior curvature increase, standard samplers become inefficient. Random-walk Metropolis–Hastings requires ever-smaller step sizes to maintain acceptance, leading to slow exploration and highly autocorrelated samples. Independence samplers, which propose large moves, suffer equally in high dimensions as their proposals become increasingly unlikely.

Hamiltonian Monte Carlo (HMC) breaks this curse of dimensionality by exploiting the geometry of the target distribution. Instead of proposing moves blindly, it uses gradient information to propose distant, geometrically informed steps that remain probable under the posterior. By augmenting the state space with auxiliary momentum variables, HMC transforms random exploration into ballistic motion guided by the log-posterior surface.

This chapter synthesizes the mechanics, practical implementation, and automatic tuning of HMC. We cover the theoretical foundation (Hamiltonian dynamics and energy conservation), the discrete leapfrog integrator, the Metropolis correction for discretization error, and modern variants like the No-U-Turn Sampler (NUTS) that remove manual tuning. We also discuss when HMC is preferable to alternatives and highlight its role in packages like Stan.

10.2 Why random-walk Metropolis fails in high dimensions

Remark. Standard random-walk Metropolis proposes local moves: \[ x' = x + \eta, \quad \eta \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \Sigma_{\text{prop}}). \]

Acceptance probability is \(\alpha = \min(1, e^{-\Delta \log \pi})\), where \(\Delta \log \pi = \log \pi(x') - \log \pi(x)\).

The curse: In \(d\) dimensions, for a fixed acceptance rate (typically \(\sim 0.23\) for the Metropolis algorithm in the limit), the optimal step size scales as \(O(d^{-1/2})\). This means: - To maintain constant acceptance as \(d\) grows, step sizes must shrink like \(d^{-1/2}\). - Distance explored per iteration scales as step size \(\times \sqrt{d} \sim O(1)\), regardless of dimension. - Autocorrelation grows linearly with \(d\), making the effective sample size collapse.

For correlated posteriors with high curvature (e.g., a narrow ridge), the problem is even worse: proposals must shrink to match the local geometry, requiring \(O(d)\) or \(O(d^2)\) steps to traverse the parameter space.

Bivariate Gaussian with strong correlation

Consider a target \(\pi(x) = \mathcal{N}(0, \Sigma)\) with \[ \Sigma = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & \rho \\ \rho & 1 \end{pmatrix}, \quad \rho = 0.95. \]

The ridge is elongated; random-walk MH must take tiny steps perpendicular to the ridge to avoid rejection, then many small steps along the ridge. For a \(d=100\)-dimensional elongated Gaussian, RW-MH becomes prohibitively slow. HMC, by following the natural flow of the posterior, explores this space in far fewer iterations.

10.3 Hamiltonian mechanics: the continuous story

Hamiltonian dynamics govern many physical systems. We repurpose them for sampling by augmenting the parameter space with momentum variables, then exploiting the conservation laws of the resulting flow.

  • Augmented state space. Let \(\mathbf{q} \in \mathbb{R}^d\) be the parameters of interest (confusingly called “position” in physics). We introduce momentum \(\mathbf{p} \in \mathbb{R}^d\), an auxiliary variable sampled from a Gaussian with covariance matrix \(M\) (the “mass matrix”).

The augmented target distribution is \[ \pi(\mathbf{q}, \mathbf{p}) = \pi(\mathbf{q}) \, f(\mathbf{p} \mid M), \] where \(f(\mathbf{p} \mid M) = \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{0}, M)\) is the momentum density.

Crucial observation: Marginalizing over \(\mathbf{p}\) recovers the original target: \[ \int \pi(\mathbf{q}, \mathbf{p}) \, d\mathbf{p} = \pi(\mathbf{q}) \int \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{0}, M) \, d\mathbf{p} = \pi(\mathbf{q}). \]

Thus, sampling \((\mathbf{q}, \mathbf{p})\) and discarding \(\mathbf{p}\) gives samples from \(\pi(\mathbf{q})\).

  • The Hamiltonian function. The joint density factorizes as \[ \pi(\mathbf{q}, \mathbf{p}) = \pi(\mathbf{q}) \, \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{0}, M) \propto \exp\left[-U(\mathbf{q}) - K(\mathbf{p})\right], \] where we define the Hamiltonian \(H(\mathbf{q}, \mathbf{p}) = U(\mathbf{q}) + K(\mathbf{p})\).
  • Potential energy: \(U(\mathbf{q}) = -\log \pi(\mathbf{q})\).
  • Kinetic energy: \(K(\mathbf{p}) = \frac{1}{2} \mathbf{p}^T M^{-1} \mathbf{p}\).

The Hamiltonian is \(H(\mathbf{q}, \mathbf{p}) = U(\mathbf{q}) + K(\mathbf{p})\).

The joint density becomes \(\pi(\mathbf{q}, \mathbf{p}) \propto \exp[-H(\mathbf{q}, \mathbf{p})]\).

The mass matrix \(M\) controls the geometry: a diagonal \(M\) with larger entries allows larger steps in those directions. The default is \(M = I\) (identity), but adaptive mass matrices can be tuned to match posterior covariance.

  • Hamilton’s equations. The dynamics of a Hamiltonian system are governed by the canonical equations of motion.

Hamilton’s equations of motion: \[ \frac{d\mathbf{q}}{dt} = \nabla_{\mathbf{p}} H = M^{-1} \mathbf{p}, \] \[ \frac{d\mathbf{p}}{dt} = -\nabla_{\mathbf{q}} H = -\nabla_{\mathbf{q}} U(\mathbf{q}) = \nabla_{\mathbf{q}} \log \pi(\mathbf{q}). \]

Interpretation: - Position evolves along the momentum direction, scaled by \(M^{-1}\). - Momentum evolves in the direction of the gradient (natural gradient of log-posterior). - Key property: These equations naturally navigate toward high-probability regions via \(\nabla \log \pi\).

  • Energy conservation and volume preservation. An exact Hamiltonian trajectory conserves total energy \(H(\mathbf{q}(t), \mathbf{p}(t)) = \text{const}\), with profound consequences for sampling.

Energy conservation: Along an exact Hamiltonian trajectory, \(\frac{dH}{dt} = 0\).

Proof: \[ \frac{dH}{dt} = \dot{\mathbf{q}}^T \nabla_{\mathbf{q}} H + \dot{\mathbf{p}}^T \nabla_{\mathbf{p}} H = (\nabla_{\mathbf{p}} H)^T \nabla_{\mathbf{q}} H + (-\nabla_{\mathbf{q}} H)^T \nabla_{\mathbf{p}} H = 0. \]

Exact trajectories move along level sets of the Hamiltonian, staying on surfaces of constant \(H\). Since \(\pi(\mathbf{q}, \mathbf{p}) \propto e^{-H}\), exact trajectories move along level sets of the joint density — they are ideal proposals! Additionally, Hamiltonian flow is volume-preserving (symplectic) in phase space.

Liouville’s theorem: The divergence of the Hamiltonian velocity field is zero: \[ \nabla \cdot \begin{pmatrix} \dot{\mathbf{q}} \\ \dot{\mathbf{p}} \end{pmatrix} = \nabla_{\mathbf{q}} \cdot (M^{-1} \mathbf{p}) + \nabla_{\mathbf{p}} \cdot (-\nabla_{\mathbf{q}} U) = 0 + 0 = 0. \]

Thus Hamiltonian flow preserves volume in phase space and leaves \(\pi(\mathbf{q}, \mathbf{p})\) invariant.

Together, energy conservation and volume preservation imply that Hamiltonian flow preserves \(\pi(\mathbf{q}, \mathbf{p})\). But running the dynamics alone would never leave the level set determined by the initial condition: to explore the whole space we must periodically jump between level sets of \(H\) by refreshing \(\mathbf{p}\) from its Gaussian marginal.

10.4 The leapfrog integrator

We cannot simulate Hamiltonian dynamics exactly on a computer. Instead, we discretize time using the leapfrog integrator, which maintains approximate energy conservation and reversibility while being computationally efficient.

  • Leapfrog discretization. Given current state \((\mathbf{q}_0, \mathbf{p}_0)\), step size \(\varepsilon > 0\), and number of steps \(L\), one leapfrog step is defined below. The name “leapfrog” reflects the staggered half-steps: momentum is updated at half-steps, position at full steps.

One leapfrog step (from \((\mathbf{q}_t, \mathbf{p}_t)\) to \((\mathbf{q}_{t+1}, \mathbf{p}_{t+1})\)):

\[ \begin{aligned} \mathbf{p}_{t+\varepsilon/2} &= \mathbf{p}_t - \frac{\varepsilon}{2} \nabla U(\mathbf{q}_t), \\ \mathbf{q}_{t+\varepsilon} &= \mathbf{q}_t + \varepsilon M^{-1} \mathbf{p}_{t+\varepsilon/2}, \\ \mathbf{p}_{t+\varepsilon} &= \mathbf{p}_{t+\varepsilon/2} - \frac{\varepsilon}{2} \nabla U(\mathbf{q}_{t+\varepsilon}). \end{aligned} \]

Repeating \(L\) times produces a trajectory of length \(T = L \varepsilon\).

  • Why leapfrog? The integrator has three essential properties that make it ideal for HMC.

The leapfrog integrator is:

  1. Reversible: If we run the integrator backward (reversing \(\varepsilon \to -\varepsilon\) and flipping momentum at the end), we recover the initial state exactly (up to numerical precision). This ensures the proposal \((\mathbf{q}', \mathbf{p}') = \text{Leapfrog}(\mathbf{q}, \mathbf{p}, \varepsilon, L)\) and its reverse satisfy \(q((\mathbf{q}', \mathbf{p}') \to (\mathbf{q}, \mathbf{p})) = q((\mathbf{q}, \mathbf{p}) \to (\mathbf{q}', \mathbf{p}'))\), so the forward and reverse probabilities cancel in the Metropolis ratio.

  2. Volume-preserving: The leapfrog map is a composition of volume-preserving transformations and preserves the Jacobian determinant. This reduces the Metropolis acceptance formula.

  3. Symplectic: The integrator is a symplectic integrator, meaning it preserves the symplectic structure \(\mathbf{q} \wedge \mathbf{p}\). The error in energy per step is \(O(\varepsilon^2)\), so total error over \(L\) steps is \(O(\varepsilon^2)\).

For small \(\varepsilon\), the discretization error is tiny, energy error \(\Delta H \approx O(\varepsilon^2)\) accumulates slowly, and acceptance rates remain high. If \(\varepsilon\) is too large, energy errors accumulate and the rejection rate increases.

10.4.1 Example: 2D leapfrog trajectory

Let us visualize a leapfrog trajectory in 2D phase space for the bivariate Gaussian example.

# Define target (log-posterior and gradient)
rho <- 0.95
Sigma_inv <- solve(matrix(c(1, rho, rho, 1), 2, 2))
log_pi <- function(q) {
  -0.5 * as.numeric(t(q) %*% Sigma_inv %*% q)
}
grad_U <- function(q) {
  Sigma_inv %*% q
}

# Leapfrog integrator
leapfrog <- function(q, p, epsilon, L) {
  p <- p - 0.5 * epsilon * grad_U(q)
  for (i in 1:L) {
    q <- q + epsilon * p
    if (i < L) {
      p <- p - epsilon * grad_U(q)
    }
  }
  p <- p - 0.5 * epsilon * grad_U(q)
  list(q = q, p = -p)
}

# Simulate trajectory and track states
q0 <- c(2, 2)
p0 <- c(1, 0)
epsilon <- 0.1
L <- 100

q_curr <- q0
p_curr <- p0
trajectory_q <- matrix(NA, L+1, 2)
trajectory_p <- matrix(NA, L+1, 2)
trajectory_q[1,] <- q0
trajectory_p[1,] <- p0

for (step in 1:L) {
  # Half-step momentum
  p_curr <- p_curr - 0.5 * epsilon * grad_U(q_curr)
  # Full-step position
  q_curr <- q_curr + epsilon * p_curr
  # Half-step momentum
  p_curr <- p_curr - 0.5 * epsilon * grad_U(q_curr)
  trajectory_q[step+1,] <- q_curr
  trajectory_p[step+1,] <- p_curr
}

# Plot phase space trajectory

10.5 The HMC algorithm

Combining leapfrog integration with Metropolis acceptance yields the complete HMC algorithm.

HMC Algorithm

Given current position \(\mathbf{q}_t\):

  1. Refresh momentum: Draw \(\mathbf{p} \sim \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{0}, M)\).
  2. Store current energy: Compute \(H_{\text{old}} = U(\mathbf{q}_t) + K(\mathbf{p})\).
  3. Integrate: Run leapfrog for \(L\) steps with step size \(\varepsilon\): \[ (\mathbf{q}', \mathbf{p}') = \text{Leapfrog}(\mathbf{q}_t, \mathbf{p}, \varepsilon, L). \]
  4. Compute proposal energy: \(H_{\text{new}} = U(\mathbf{q}') + K(\mathbf{p}')\).
  5. Metropolis correction: Accept with probability \[ \alpha = \min\left(1, \exp(-\Delta H)\right), \quad \Delta H = H_{\text{new}} - H_{\text{old}}. \]
  6. Move or reject:
    • If \(u \sim \text{Uniform}(0,1) < \alpha\): set \(\mathbf{q}_{t+1} = \mathbf{q}'\).
    • Else: set \(\mathbf{q}_{t+1} = \mathbf{q}_t\).

  • Why the Metropolis correction? If we could simulate Hamiltonian dynamics exactly, \(\Delta H = 0\) and every proposal would be accepted. However, leapfrog introduces discretization error \(\Delta H \approx O(L \varepsilon^2)\). The Metropolis step removes any bias introduced by discretization, restores exact invariance to the joint distribution \(\pi(\mathbf{q}, \mathbf{p})\), and ensures that marginalizing over \(\mathbf{p}\) gives exact samples from \(\pi(\mathbf{q})\). A smaller \(\varepsilon\) reduces discretization error and increases acceptance; a larger \(\varepsilon\) reduces computation but risks rejection.

  • Momentum refresh. In step 1, we draw a fresh momentum independently of the previous momentum. This is crucial: otherwise, the trajectory would continue indefinitely on level sets of \(H\), exploring only one mode. By resampling \(\mathbf{p}\) at each iteration, we jump between level sets of \(H\) (exploring different energies), allow exploration of the full parameter space, and introduce randomness necessary for ergodicity. Notably, resampling \(\mathbf{p} \sim \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{0}, M)\) is Gibbs sampling in the \((q, p)\) space.

10.6 Tuning and practical guidance

HMC’s efficiency depends critically on three tuning parameters — step size \(\varepsilon\), number of leapfrog steps \(L\), and mass matrix \(M\) — together with a warm-up phase that adapts them automatically.

  • Step size \(\varepsilon\). This controls discretization granularity, and is the parameter most often tuned automatically.

The step size \(\varepsilon\) controls discretization granularity.

  • Too small: \(\varepsilon \to 0\) gives accurate proposals, high acceptance, but slow exploration (many gradient evaluations).
  • Too large: \(\varepsilon\) large causes energy errors to accumulate; acceptance rate \(\alpha\) drops.

Typical rule: Target acceptance rate of 0.65–0.80 (varies slightly with \(d\)). Adjust \(\varepsilon\) to match this during warm-up.

  • Trajectory length and number of steps \(L\). The product \(T = L \varepsilon\) is the simulated time and determines how far each proposal travels.

The trajectory length is \(T = L \varepsilon\). A longer trajectory explores farther but at higher computational cost.

  • Too few steps: Behaves like random-walk MH (local exploration).
  • Too many steps: Trajectories double back on themselves (waste computation and may reject).

Effective range: Aim for \(L \in [20, 100]\) initially; tune via ESS per gradient evaluation.

  • Mass matrix \(M\). The mass matrix controls how momentum couples to position. With \(M = I\) (identity), kinetic energy is \(K(\mathbf{p}) = \frac{1}{2} \|\mathbf{p}\|^2\) — isotropic.

Remark. Adaptive mass matrix: If the posterior has vastly different scales or high correlations, an isotropic \(M\) is inefficient. Adaptive HMC (as implemented in Stan) estimates the posterior covariance \(\Sigma_{\text{post}}\) during warm-up and sets \(M = \Sigma_{\text{post}}^{-1}\). This makes the “momentum-space” geometry match the “parameter-space” geometry, enabling larger steps and better mixing.

Equivalently, we can work with a reparametrization or preconditioning. The improvement can be dramatic in correlated or high-dimensional problems.

  • Warm-up and adaptation. Most HMC implementations (especially Stan) use a warm-up (or “burn-in”) phase to (i) find a reasonable starting point and estimate posterior covariance, (ii) adapt \(\varepsilon\) to target a desired acceptance rate, (iii) adapt \(M\) to match posterior structure, and (iv) discard warm-up samples as they are not from the stationary distribution. After warm-up, \(\varepsilon\) and \(M\) are fixed, and the algorithm becomes a standard Markov chain with invariant distribution \(\pi(\mathbf{q})\).

10.7 Divergent transitions and energy issues

HMC can fail in subtle ways:

Remark. Divergent transitions occur when the numerical integration goes unstable, typically because: - Step size \(\varepsilon\) is too large and energy error \(|\Delta H|\) becomes very large, causing rejection. - Posterior has regions of high curvature that the integrator cannot resolve. - Posterior has heavy tails or mild multimodality.

Indicator: Many proposals have \(\Delta H \gg 10\) (large energy errors).

Solution: Reduce \(\varepsilon\), increase warm-up iterations, or use an adaptive mass matrix. Stan prints warnings about divergences; they suggest the sampler is not exploring properly and posterior inferences may be biased.

10.8 NUTS: No-U-Turn Sampler

Modern software like Stan uses the No-U-Turn Sampler (NUTS), which removes the need to manually tune \(L\). Instead of running a fixed number of steps \(L\), NUTS adaptively expands the trajectory until it would begin to double back.

Doubling criterion: A trajectory “doubles back” (makes a U-turn) when the “generalized velocity” \((\mathbf{q}' - \mathbf{q}_{\text{left}})^T \mathbf{p}\) becomes negative, indicating backward motion. More precisely, a U-turn is detected using the dot product: \[ (\mathbf{q}_{\text{right}} - \mathbf{q}_{\text{left}})^T \mathbf{p}_{\text{right}} < 0 \quad \text{or} \quad (\mathbf{q}_{\text{right}} - \mathbf{q}_{\text{left}})^T \mathbf{p}_{\text{left}} < 0. \]

NUTS extends the trajectory by “doubling” (adding steps in the same direction) until a U-turn is detected, then accepts from the entire trajectory.

The advantages of NUTS over fixed-\(L\) HMC are:

  • Eliminates manual tuning of \(L\): The algorithm automatically determines trajectory length.
  • Prevents wasted computation: Stops before the trajectory begins circling back.
  • Maintains properties: The randomized doubling ensures detailed balance is preserved (via Metropolis-from-trajectory).
  • Stan implementation: NUTS is the default sampler in Stan with automatic warm-up and adaptation.

10.9 Comparison: HMC vs random-walk MH vs Gibbs

Scaling with dimension \(d\):

RW-MH \(O(1)\) \(O(d)\) \(O(d^{-2})\)
Gibbs \(1\) \(O(d)\) \(O(d^{-1})\) if uncorrelated
HMC \(0.6\)–\(0.9\) \(O(\sqrt{d})\) or better \(O(1)\) to \(O(d^{-1})\)

(Rough asymptotics; constants matter.)

Practical implications: - RW-MH: Simple to implement, no gradients needed. But prohibitively slow for \(d > 20\) unless posterior is nearly isotropic. - Gibbs: Requires conditional distributions. Fast if draws from conditionals are cheap. Struggles with correlations. - HMC: Requires gradients but dramatically outperforms RW-MH in medium to high dimensions (\(d \geq 10\)). Excellent when gradients are available (automatic differentiation).

Comparison on correlated bivariate Gaussian

# Setup
rho <- 0.95
Sigma <- matrix(c(1, rho, rho, 1), 2, 2)
Sigma_inv <- solve(Sigma)

log_pi <- function(q) {
  -0.5 * as.numeric(t(q) %*% Sigma_inv %*% q)
}

grad_U <- function(q) {
  Sigma_inv %*% q
}

# RW-MH
rw_mh <- function(n_iter, step = 0.3) {
  q <- c(0, 0)
  samples <- matrix(NA, n_iter, 2)
  acc <- 0
  for (i in 1:n_iter) {
    q_prop <- q + rnorm(2, 0, step)
    log_alpha <- log_pi(q_prop) - log_pi(q)
    if (log(runif(1)) < log_alpha) {
      q <- q_prop
      acc <- acc + 1
    }
    samples[i, ] <- q
  }
  list(samples = samples, acc_rate = acc / n_iter)
}

# Leapfrog for HMC
leapfrog <- function(q, p, eps, L) {
  p <- p - 0.5 * eps * grad_U(q)
  for (i in 1:L) {
    q <- q + eps * p
    if (i < L) p <- p - eps * grad_U(q)
  }
  p <- p - 0.5 * eps * grad_U(q)
  list(q = q, p = -p)
}

# HMC
hmc <- function(n_iter, eps = 0.15, L = 20) {
  q <- c(0, 0)
  samples <- matrix(NA, n_iter, 2)
  acc <- 0
  for (i in 1:n_iter) {
    p <- rnorm(2)
    H_old <- 0.5 * as.numeric(t(q) %*% Sigma_inv %*% q) + 0.5 * sum(p^2)
    prop <- leapfrog(q, p, eps, L)
    q_prop <- prop$q
    p_prop <- prop$p
    H_new <- 0.5 * as.numeric(t(q_prop) %*% Sigma_inv %*% q_prop) + 0.5 * sum(p_prop^2)
    log_alpha <- -(H_new - H_old)
    if (log(runif(1)) < log_alpha) {
      q <- q_prop
      acc <- acc + 1
    }
    samples[i, ] <- q
  }
  list(samples = samples, acc_rate = acc / n_iter)
}

# Run samplers

par(mfrow = c(2, 2), mar = c(4, 4, 3, 1))

     xlab = "Iteration", ylab = "q1")
grid(col = "grey90")

plot(fit_hmc$samples[,1], type = "l", lwd = 1, col = "#d62728",

10.10 Practical recommendations

Remark. When to use HMC: - Posterior dimension \(d \geq 10\). - Gradient \(\nabla \log \pi\) is available (exact or via automatic differentiation). - Posterior is not highly multimodal with separated modes. - Computational budget permits gradient evaluations (modern autodiff is fast).

When HMC struggles: - Highly multimodal posterior with isolated modes (HMC gets trapped in one mode; consider parallel tempering or other global methods). - Discrete parameters (HMC requires continuous state; marginalize out discrete variables or use other samplers). - Gradient unavailable and too expensive to compute numerically.

When to prefer alternatives: - Gibbs sampling: If conditional distributions are easy to sample. Better mixing if conditionals are well-separated; worse if highly correlated. - Random-walk MH: Low-dimensional (\(d < 10\)), no gradients, simple implementation preferred. - Slice sampling or other adaptive methods: If gradient unavailable but adaptive exploration is desired.

10.11 Stan and modern practice

The probabilistic programming language Stan uses NUTS with automatic warm-up by default. This means:

  1. Users specify the log-posterior (via a model statement).
  2. Stan automatically differentiates to obtain \(\nabla \log \pi\) via the reverse-mode autodiff.
  3. During warm-up, step size \(\varepsilon\) is tuned to target 0.80 acceptance; mass matrix \(M\) is adapted.
  4. NUTS extends trajectories adaptively without manual specification of \(L\).
  5. Convergence diagnostics (\(\hat{R}\), effective sample size) are computed automatically.

This makes HMC accessible to practitioners without deep knowledge of tuning. Stan is now the most widely used tool for general-purpose Bayesian inference, precisely because it combines HMC’s superior performance with ease of use.

10.12 Exercises

For a target \(\pi(\theta) \propto \exp(-U(\theta))\) with \(U(\theta) = \tfrac{1}{2}\theta^\top\theta\) (standard Gaussian), write the Hamiltonian \(H(\theta, p)\) with a unit mass matrix, the canonical equations of motion, and the analytic solution for \((\theta(t), p(t))\).

Solution. With \(M = I\) the Hamiltonian is \[H(\theta, p) = \tfrac{1}{2}\theta^\top\theta + \tfrac{1}{2}p^\top p,\] and Hamilton’s equations give \(\dot\theta = p\), \(\dot p = -\theta\). This is the harmonic oscillator, whose closed-form solution is \[\theta(t) = \theta_0\cos t + p_0\sin t, \qquad p(t) = -\theta_0\sin t + p_0\cos t,\] i.e. a rigid rotation of phase space, so the trajectory stays exactly on the level set \(H = \tfrac{1}{2}(\|\theta_0\|^2+\|p_0\|^2)\).

Show that for a separable Hamiltonian \(H(\theta, p) = U(\theta) + K(p)\) with \(K(p) = \tfrac{1}{2}p^\top M^{-1}p\), the canonical equations decouple into \(\dot\theta = M^{-1}p\) and \(\dot p = -\nabla U(\theta)\).

Solution. Because \(U\) depends only on \(\theta\) and \(K\) only on \(p\), the gradients separate: \(\nabla_p H = \nabla_p K(p)\) and \(\nabla_\theta H = \nabla_\theta U(\theta)\). Hamilton’s equations therefore become \[\dot\theta = \nabla_p H = M^{-1}p, \qquad \dot p = -\nabla_\theta H = -\nabla U(\theta),\] so position is driven only by \(p\) (through \(M^{-1}\)) and momentum only by the potential gradient — the coupling between \(\theta\) and \(p\) is gone, which is exactly what lets leapfrog use alternating explicit half-steps.

Energy conservation. Differentiate \(H(\theta(t), p(t))\) along an exact trajectory and show that \(dH/dt = 0\). State why the leapfrog integrator does not exactly conserve \(H\) but conserves a modified shadow Hamiltonian.

Solution. Along an exact trajectory, \[\frac{dH}{dt} = \nabla_\theta H \cdot \dot\theta + \nabla_p H \cdot \dot p = \nabla_\theta H \cdot \nabla_p H + \nabla_p H \cdot (-\nabla_\theta H) = 0.\] Leapfrog is a discrete symplectic map, not the exact flow, so \(H\) oscillates by \(O(\varepsilon^2)\). By backward-error analysis it instead conserves a shadow Hamiltonian \(\tilde H = H + \varepsilon^2 H_2 + \dots\) exactly, which is why \(\Delta H\) stays bounded over long trajectories instead of drifting.

Write out one full step of the leapfrog integrator for a separable Hamiltonian with step size \(\varepsilon\). Show that the update is symplectic by checking that the Jacobian determinant equals one.

Solution. The three sub-steps are \[p_{1/2} = p_0 - \tfrac{\varepsilon}{2}\nabla U(\theta_0), \quad \theta_1 = \theta_0 + \varepsilon M^{-1}p_{1/2}, \quad p_1 = p_{1/2} - \tfrac{\varepsilon}{2}\nabla U(\theta_1).\] Each sub-step is a shear in either \(\theta\) or \(p\), so its Jacobian is upper- or lower-triangular with unit diagonal, e.g. the half kick has Jacobian \(\begin{pmatrix} I & 0 \\ -\tfrac{\varepsilon}{2}\nabla^2 U & I\end{pmatrix}\) with determinant \(1\). The full step is the composition, hence \(\det J = 1\cdot 1\cdot 1 = 1\), so leapfrog preserves phase-space volume and the Metropolis ratio reduces to \(\exp(-\Delta H)\).

Why random-walk Metropolis fails in high dimensions. Argue that for an isotropic Gaussian target in \(d\) dimensions, the optimal step size scales as \(\varepsilon \sim d^{-1/2}\) and the number of steps to traverse the typical set scales as \(d^{1/2}\), giving a quadratic cost in \(d\). Contrast with HMC’s \(d^{1/4}\) cost.

Solution. A proposal \(\theta' = \theta + \varepsilon\xi\) with \(\xi\sim\mathcal{N}(0,I)\) produces \(\Delta\log\pi\) with mean \(-\tfrac{1}{2}d\varepsilon^2\) and variance \(\sim d\varepsilon^2\), so keeping a non-vanishing acceptance forces \(d\varepsilon^2 = O(1)\), i.e. \(\varepsilon \sim d^{-1/2}\). The displacement per accepted step is then \(\|\Delta\theta\|\sim\varepsilon\sqrt{d}\sim 1\), and crossing the typical set of radius \(\sqrt{d}\) as a diffusion requires \[N_{\text{RW-MH}} \sim d,\qquad \text{i.e. total cost}\propto d,\] which becomes \(d^2\) once correlation length is included. HMC instead tolerates \(\varepsilon\sim d^{-1/4}\) (the leapfrog energy error is \(O(\varepsilon^2)\) summed coherently), so trajectories of length \(T = O(1)\) need \(L\sim d^{1/4}\) gradient evaluations — exponentially better in high \(d\).

HMC acceptance probability. Show that the acceptance probability of a proposed move \((\theta_0, p_0) \to (\theta_L, p_L)\) generated by leapfrog is \[\alpha = \min\!\left\{1,\ \exp\!\big(-[H(\theta_L, p_L) - H(\theta_0, p_0)]\big)\right\}.\]

Solution. The augmented target is \(\pi(\theta,p) \propto e^{-H(\theta,p)}\). Leapfrog combined with the final momentum flip \(p\mapsto -p\) gives a deterministic, volume-preserving, self-inverse proposal, so the forward and reverse proposal densities are equal and their Jacobian is \(1\). The Metropolis–Hastings ratio collapses to \[\alpha = \min\!\left\{1, \frac{e^{-H(\theta_L,p_L)}}{e^{-H(\theta_0,p_0)}}\right\} = \min\{1,\exp(-\Delta H)\}.\] Because exact dynamics would give \(\Delta H = 0\), the only source of rejection is leapfrog’s discretization error.

Optimal acceptance rate. State the result (Beskos et al., 2013) that the optimal HMC acceptance for isotropic Gaussian targets is approximately \(0.65\). Sketch the argument in words: too-low acceptance wastes proposals, too-high acceptance means \(\varepsilon\) is too small.

Solution. For separable Gaussian targets the leapfrog energy error is asymptotically Gaussian, \(\Delta H \sim \mathcal{N}(\mu, 2\mu)\) with \(\mu \propto d\varepsilon^4\), so the expected squared jumping distance per gradient evaluation is maximised at \[\alpha^\star = \mathbb{E}[\min\{1,e^{-\Delta H}\}] \approx 0.651.\] Heuristically, very low acceptance means most leapfrog work is thrown away on rejected trajectories, while very high acceptance means \(\varepsilon\) is so small that each accepted trajectory barely moves; the sweet spot trades these costs.

Mass-matrix reparametrisation. Show that running HMC with mass matrix \(M\) on parameter \(\theta\) is equivalent to running HMC with identity mass matrix on the reparametrised variable \(\tilde\theta = M^{1/2}\theta\). State the practical implication for posteriors with correlated parameters.

Solution. Let \(\tilde\theta = M^{1/2}\theta\) and \(\tilde p = M^{-1/2}p\). Then the kinetic energy becomes \[K(p) = \tfrac{1}{2}p^\top M^{-1}p = \tfrac{1}{2}\tilde p^\top\tilde p,\] so in \((\tilde\theta,\tilde p)\) coordinates the kinetic energy uses the identity mass matrix, while the Jacobian \(|\det M^{1/2}|\) is constant and cancels in the acceptance ratio. Practically, choosing \(M\approx \Sigma_{\text{post}}^{-1}\) whitens correlated posteriors: HMC sees an approximately isotropic problem in \(\tilde\theta\), so a single step size works in all directions and step length need not shrink to the smallest eigendirection.

Divergent transitions. Describe when leapfrog energy errors blow up. Give one example posterior geometry (e.g. a “funnel” in hierarchical models) where divergences are common, and the standard remedy (non-centered reparametrisation).

Solution. Leapfrog energy error scales as \(\Delta H = O(\varepsilon^2\|\nabla^2 U\|)\), so wherever the local curvature spikes, \(|\Delta H|\) explodes and Stan flags a divergence (typically \(|\Delta H|\gtrsim 10^3\)). Neal’s funnel \(\theta_i \mid \tau \sim \mathcal{N}(0,e^\tau)\), \(\tau\sim\mathcal{N}(0,9)\) is the canonical example: near \(\tau\ll 0\) the funnel neck is so narrow that no single \(\varepsilon\) works for both the neck and the mouth. The remedy is the non-centred reparametrisation \[\theta_i = e^{\tau/2}z_i, \qquad z_i\sim\mathcal{N}(0,1),\] which decouples scale from location and gives an approximately isotropic posterior in \((z,\tau)\).

NUTS termination criterion. Describe Hoffman & Gelman’s no-U-turn condition: stop doubling the trajectory once the proposal starts curling back on itself. Why does this remove the need to tune the trajectory length \(L\) manually?

Solution. NUTS keeps doubling the trajectory in random forward/backward directions and terminates once the displacement vector \(\Delta\theta = \theta_{\text{right}} - \theta_{\text{left}}\) stops aligning with the momentum at either endpoint, i.e. \[\Delta\theta^\top p_{\text{right}} < 0 \quad\text{or}\quad \Delta\theta^\top p_{\text{left}} < 0.\] At that instant any further integration would curve the trajectory back on itself, wasting gradient evaluations. Because the criterion is local and computed on the fly, the user no longer has to pick a fixed \(L\): NUTS adapts the trajectory length per iteration to the local geometry while preserving detailed balance through a slice/multinomial sampler over the built tree.

Time reversibility. Show that the leapfrog integrator is reversible: if we apply it for \(L\) steps to go from \((\theta_0, p_0)\) to \((\theta_L, p_L)\), then a single negation \(p_L \mapsto -p_L\) followed by \(L\) leapfrog steps returns to \((\theta_0, -p_0)\).

Solution. The kinetic energy is even in \(p\), so under \(p\mapsto -p\) the equations \(\dot\theta = M^{-1}p\), \(\dot p = -\nabla U\) become \(\dot\theta = -M^{-1}p\), \(\dot p = \nabla U\) — exactly the time-reversed dynamics. For one leapfrog step, applying the three sub-steps with negated momentum literally undoes the original substeps in reverse order: \[\Phi_\varepsilon\circ R\circ\Phi_\varepsilon = R, \qquad R: (\theta,p)\mapsto(\theta,-p).\] Composing \(L\) such involutions gives \(\Phi_\varepsilon^L\circ R\circ\Phi_\varepsilon^L(\theta_0,p_0) = (\theta_0,-p_0)\), which is what makes the leapfrog proposal symmetric so the MH ratio depends only on \(\Delta H\).

Effect of step size on energy error. For leapfrog applied to a quadratic Hamiltonian, show that the per-step energy error scales as \(O(\varepsilon^3)\) but the total error over a trajectory of fixed length scales as \(O(\varepsilon^2)\). Why does this argue for keeping \(\varepsilon\) moderately small but not vanishingly small?

Solution. Leapfrog is a second-order symplectic method, so its local truncation error in \(H\) per step is \(O(\varepsilon^3)\). Over a trajectory of fixed length \(T = L\varepsilon\), the number of steps is \(L = T/\varepsilon\), so the global energy error scales as \[\Delta H_{\text{total}} = L\cdot O(\varepsilon^3) = O(\varepsilon^2).\] Shrinking \(\varepsilon\) further keeps \(\Delta H\) tiny and acceptance near \(1\), but doubles the number of gradient evaluations: cost scales like \(1/\varepsilon\) while ESS gain is sub-linear, so the sweet spot is a moderate \(\varepsilon\) targeting ~65–80% acceptance, not \(\varepsilon\to 0\).

HMC vs random-walk Metropolis. Sketch on a single plot: target trace from random-walk MH with poorly tuned step size, target trace from HMC with reasonable \((\varepsilon, L)\). Identify the integrated autocorrelation time of each visually and explain the gap.

Solution. The RW-MH trace looks like a slow diffusive walk with long flat plateaus (frequent rejections) and visible drift over many iterations, so its integrated autocorrelation time \(\tau_{\text{RW}}\) is large; the HMC trace looks like nearly independent draws hopping across the support, with \(\tau_{\text{HMC}}\approx 1\)–\(5\). The ESS ratio scales as \[\frac{\text{ESS}_{\text{HMC}}}{\text{ESS}_{\text{RW}}} = \frac{\tau_{\text{RW}}}{\tau_{\text{HMC}}} \sim \frac{d}{d^{1/2}} = d^{1/2},\] because each HMC trajectory traverses an \(O(1)\) fraction of the typical set in one move, while RW-MH only takes a step of size \(O(d^{-1/2})\).

Define the typical set of a \(d\)-dimensional posterior. Argue why HMC, which conserves energy and follows gradient flow, stays inside the typical set, while random-walk MH wastes most of its time outside it in high dimensions.

Solution. The typical set \(\mathcal{T}_\delta\) is the thin shell of states whose log-density equals its expectation up to \(\delta\): \[\mathcal{T}_\delta = \{\theta : |\log\pi(\theta) - \mathbb{E}_\pi[\log\pi]| < \delta\},\] which in high \(d\) concentrates near a \((d{-}1)\)-dimensional surface far from the mode (e.g. a thin shell of radius \(\sqrt{d}\) for \(\mathcal{N}(0,I)\)). HMC trajectories conserve \(H = U + K\), and on average \(U(\theta)\approx \mathbb{E}_\pi[U]\), so the dynamics flow along this level set — they orbit inside \(\mathcal{T}_\delta\) by construction. RW-MH proposes isotropically in \(\mathbb{R}^d\), where almost all directions point off the thin shell, so most proposals leave the typical set, get rejected, and the chain barely moves.

## Introduction

Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) methods form the backbone of modern Bayesian inference, but they face a fundamental challenge: as dimension and posterior curvature increase, standard samplers become inefficient. Random-walk Metropolis–Hastings requires ever-smaller step sizes to maintain acceptance, leading to slow exploration and highly autocorrelated samples. Independence samplers, which propose large moves, suffer equally in high dimensions as their proposals become increasingly unlikely.

Hamiltonian Monte Carlo (HMC) breaks this curse of dimensionality by exploiting the geometry of the target distribution. Instead of proposing moves blindly, it uses gradient information to propose distant, geometrically informed steps that remain probable under the posterior. By augmenting the state space with auxiliary momentum variables, HMC transforms random exploration into ballistic motion guided by the log-posterior surface.

This chapter synthesizes the mechanics, practical implementation, and automatic tuning of HMC. We cover the theoretical foundation (Hamiltonian dynamics and energy conservation), the discrete leapfrog integrator, the Metropolis correction for discretization error, and modern variants like the No-U-Turn Sampler (NUTS) that remove manual tuning. We also discuss when HMC is preferable to alternatives and highlight its role in packages like Stan.

## Why random-walk Metropolis fails in high dimensions

::: {.remark}
Standard random-walk Metropolis proposes local moves:
$$
x' = x + \eta, \quad \eta \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \Sigma_{\text{prop}}).
$$

Acceptance probability is $\alpha = \min(1, e^{-\Delta \log \pi})$, where $\Delta \log \pi = \log \pi(x') - \log \pi(x)$.

**The curse:** In $d$ dimensions, for a fixed acceptance rate (typically $\sim 0.23$ for the Metropolis algorithm in the limit), the optimal step size scales as $O(d^{-1/2})$. This means:
- To maintain constant acceptance as $d$ grows, step sizes must shrink like $d^{-1/2}$.
- Distance explored per iteration scales as step size $\times \sqrt{d} \sim O(1)$, regardless of dimension.
- **Autocorrelation grows linearly with $d$**, making the effective sample size collapse.

For correlated posteriors with high curvature (e.g., a narrow ridge), the problem is even worse: proposals must shrink to match the local geometry, requiring $O(d)$ or $O(d^2)$ steps to traverse the parameter space.
:::

::: {.example}
**Bivariate Gaussian with strong correlation**

Consider a target $\pi(x) = \mathcal{N}(0, \Sigma)$ with
$$
\Sigma = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & \rho \\ \rho & 1 \end{pmatrix}, \quad \rho = 0.95.
$$

The ridge is elongated; random-walk MH must take tiny steps perpendicular to the ridge to avoid rejection, then many small steps along the ridge. For a $d=100$-dimensional elongated Gaussian, RW-MH becomes prohibitively slow. HMC, by following the natural flow of the posterior, explores this space in far fewer iterations.
:::

## Hamiltonian mechanics: the continuous story

Hamiltonian dynamics govern many physical systems. We repurpose them for sampling by augmenting the parameter space with momentum variables, then exploiting the conservation laws of the resulting flow.

- **Augmented state space.** Let $\mathbf{q} \in \mathbb{R}^d$ be the parameters of interest (confusingly called "position" in physics). We introduce **momentum** $\mathbf{p} \in \mathbb{R}^d$, an auxiliary variable sampled from a Gaussian with covariance matrix $M$ (the "mass matrix").

::: {.definition}
The **augmented target distribution** is
$$
\pi(\mathbf{q}, \mathbf{p}) = \pi(\mathbf{q}) \, f(\mathbf{p} \mid M),
$$
where $f(\mathbf{p} \mid M) = \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{0}, M)$ is the momentum density.

**Crucial observation:** Marginalizing over $\mathbf{p}$ recovers the original target:
$$
\int \pi(\mathbf{q}, \mathbf{p}) \, d\mathbf{p} = \pi(\mathbf{q}) \int \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{0}, M) \, d\mathbf{p} = \pi(\mathbf{q}).
$$

Thus, sampling $(\mathbf{q}, \mathbf{p})$ and discarding $\mathbf{p}$ gives samples from $\pi(\mathbf{q})$.
:::

- **The Hamiltonian function.** The joint density factorizes as
$$
\pi(\mathbf{q}, \mathbf{p}) = \pi(\mathbf{q}) \, \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{0}, M) \propto \exp\left[-U(\mathbf{q}) - K(\mathbf{p})\right],
$$
where we define the **Hamiltonian** $H(\mathbf{q}, \mathbf{p}) = U(\mathbf{q}) + K(\mathbf{p})$.

::: {.definition}
- **Potential energy:** $U(\mathbf{q}) = -\log \pi(\mathbf{q})$.
- **Kinetic energy:** $K(\mathbf{p}) = \frac{1}{2} \mathbf{p}^T M^{-1} \mathbf{p}$.

The **Hamiltonian** is $H(\mathbf{q}, \mathbf{p}) = U(\mathbf{q}) + K(\mathbf{p})$.

The joint density becomes $\pi(\mathbf{q}, \mathbf{p}) \propto \exp[-H(\mathbf{q}, \mathbf{p})]$.
:::

The mass matrix $M$ controls the geometry: a diagonal $M$ with larger entries allows larger steps in those directions. The default is $M = I$ (identity), but adaptive mass matrices can be tuned to match posterior covariance.

- **Hamilton's equations.** The dynamics of a Hamiltonian system are governed by the canonical equations of motion.

::: {.theorem}
**Hamilton's equations of motion:**
$$
\frac{d\mathbf{q}}{dt} = \nabla_{\mathbf{p}} H = M^{-1} \mathbf{p},
$$
$$
\frac{d\mathbf{p}}{dt} = -\nabla_{\mathbf{q}} H = -\nabla_{\mathbf{q}} U(\mathbf{q}) = \nabla_{\mathbf{q}} \log \pi(\mathbf{q}).
$$

**Interpretation:**
- Position evolves along the momentum direction, scaled by $M^{-1}$.
- Momentum evolves in the direction of the gradient (natural gradient of log-posterior).
- **Key property:** These equations naturally navigate toward high-probability regions via $\nabla \log \pi$.
:::

- **Energy conservation and volume preservation.** An exact Hamiltonian trajectory conserves total energy $H(\mathbf{q}(t), \mathbf{p}(t)) = \text{const}$, with profound consequences for sampling.

::: {.proposition}
**Energy conservation:** Along an exact Hamiltonian trajectory, $\frac{dH}{dt} = 0$.

**Proof:**
$$
\frac{dH}{dt} = \dot{\mathbf{q}}^T \nabla_{\mathbf{q}} H + \dot{\mathbf{p}}^T \nabla_{\mathbf{p}} H = (\nabla_{\mathbf{p}} H)^T \nabla_{\mathbf{q}} H + (-\nabla_{\mathbf{q}} H)^T \nabla_{\mathbf{p}} H = 0.
$$
:::

Exact trajectories move along **level sets** of the Hamiltonian, staying on surfaces of constant $H$. Since $\pi(\mathbf{q}, \mathbf{p}) \propto e^{-H}$, exact trajectories move along level sets of the joint density — they are ideal proposals! Additionally, Hamiltonian flow is **volume-preserving** (symplectic) in phase space.

::: {.proposition}
**Liouville's theorem:** The divergence of the Hamiltonian velocity field is zero:
$$
\nabla \cdot \begin{pmatrix} \dot{\mathbf{q}} \\ \dot{\mathbf{p}} \end{pmatrix} = \nabla_{\mathbf{q}} \cdot (M^{-1} \mathbf{p}) + \nabla_{\mathbf{p}} \cdot (-\nabla_{\mathbf{q}} U) = 0 + 0 = 0.
$$

Thus Hamiltonian flow preserves volume in phase space and leaves $\pi(\mathbf{q}, \mathbf{p})$ invariant.
:::

Together, energy conservation and volume preservation imply that Hamiltonian flow preserves $\pi(\mathbf{q}, \mathbf{p})$. But running the dynamics alone would never leave the level set determined by the initial condition: to explore the whole space we must periodically jump between level sets of $H$ by **refreshing** $\mathbf{p}$ from its Gaussian marginal.

## The leapfrog integrator

We cannot simulate Hamiltonian dynamics exactly on a computer. Instead, we discretize time using the **leapfrog integrator**, which maintains approximate energy conservation and reversibility while being computationally efficient.

- **Leapfrog discretization.** Given current state $(\mathbf{q}_0, \mathbf{p}_0)$, step size $\varepsilon > 0$, and number of steps $L$, one leapfrog step is defined below. The name "leapfrog" reflects the staggered half-steps: momentum is updated at half-steps, position at full steps.

::: {.note}
**One leapfrog step** (from $(\mathbf{q}_t, \mathbf{p}_t)$ to $(\mathbf{q}_{t+1}, \mathbf{p}_{t+1})$):

$$
\begin{aligned}
\mathbf{p}_{t+\varepsilon/2} &= \mathbf{p}_t - \frac{\varepsilon}{2} \nabla U(\mathbf{q}_t), \\
\mathbf{q}_{t+\varepsilon} &= \mathbf{q}_t + \varepsilon M^{-1} \mathbf{p}_{t+\varepsilon/2}, \\
\mathbf{p}_{t+\varepsilon} &= \mathbf{p}_{t+\varepsilon/2} - \frac{\varepsilon}{2} \nabla U(\mathbf{q}_{t+\varepsilon}).
\end{aligned}
$$

Repeating $L$ times produces a trajectory of length $T = L \varepsilon$.
:::

- **Why leapfrog?** The integrator has three essential properties that make it ideal for HMC.

::: {.theorem}
The leapfrog integrator is:

1. **Reversible:** If we run the integrator backward (reversing $\varepsilon \to -\varepsilon$ and flipping momentum at the end), we recover the initial state exactly (up to numerical precision). This ensures the proposal $(\mathbf{q}', \mathbf{p}') = \text{Leapfrog}(\mathbf{q}, \mathbf{p}, \varepsilon, L)$ and its reverse satisfy $q((\mathbf{q}', \mathbf{p}') \to (\mathbf{q}, \mathbf{p})) = q((\mathbf{q}, \mathbf{p}) \to (\mathbf{q}', \mathbf{p}'))$, so the forward and reverse probabilities cancel in the Metropolis ratio.

2. **Volume-preserving:** The leapfrog map is a composition of volume-preserving transformations and preserves the Jacobian determinant. This reduces the Metropolis acceptance formula.

3. **Symplectic:** The integrator is a symplectic integrator, meaning it preserves the symplectic structure $\mathbf{q} \wedge \mathbf{p}$. The error in energy per step is $O(\varepsilon^2)$, so total error over $L$ steps is $O(\varepsilon^2)$.
:::

For small $\varepsilon$, the discretization error is tiny, energy error $\Delta H \approx O(\varepsilon^2)$ accumulates slowly, and acceptance rates remain high. If $\varepsilon$ is too large, energy errors accumulate and the rejection rate increases.

### Example: 2D leapfrog trajectory

Let us visualize a leapfrog trajectory in 2D phase space for the bivariate Gaussian example.

#| label: lst-leapfrog-trajectory
#| lst-cap: "Leapfrog trajectory"
#| fig-cap: "Leapfrog trajectory (red spiral) in 2D phase space for a correlated bivariate Gaussian, starting from q=(2,2), p=(1,0). The trajectory spirals inward toward the mode due to energy dissipation in the discretization, then the Metropolis correction either accepts or rejects."

# Define target (log-posterior and gradient)
rho <- 0.95
Sigma_inv <- solve(matrix(c(1, rho, rho, 1), 2, 2))
log_pi <- function(q) {
  -0.5 * as.numeric(t(q) %*% Sigma_inv %*% q)
}
grad_U <- function(q) {
  Sigma_inv %*% q
}

# Leapfrog integrator
leapfrog <- function(q, p, epsilon, L) {
  p <- p - 0.5 * epsilon * grad_U(q)
  for (i in 1:L) {
    q <- q + epsilon * p
    if (i < L) {
      p <- p - epsilon * grad_U(q)
    }
  }
  p <- p - 0.5 * epsilon * grad_U(q)
  list(q = q, p = -p)
}

# Simulate trajectory and track states
q0 <- c(2, 2)
p0 <- c(1, 0)
epsilon <- 0.1
L <- 100

q_curr <- q0
p_curr <- p0
trajectory_q <- matrix(NA, L+1, 2)
trajectory_p <- matrix(NA, L+1, 2)
trajectory_q[1,] <- q0
trajectory_p[1,] <- p0

for (step in 1:L) {
  # Half-step momentum
  p_curr <- p_curr - 0.5 * epsilon * grad_U(q_curr)
  # Full-step position
  q_curr <- q_curr + epsilon * p_curr
  # Half-step momentum
  p_curr <- p_curr - 0.5 * epsilon * grad_U(q_curr)
  trajectory_q[step+1,] <- q_curr
  trajectory_p[step+1,] <- p_curr
}

# Plot phase space trajectory
plot(trajectory_q[,1], trajectory_p[,1],
     type = "l", lwd = 1.5, col = "#d62728",
     main = "Leapfrog Trajectory in Phase Space (q1, p1)",
     xlab = "q1 (position)", ylab = "p1 (momentum)")
points(q0[1], p0[1], pch = 19, cex = 1.5, col = "#2ca02c")
points(trajectory_q[L+1, 1], trajectory_p[L+1, 1], pch = 19, cex = 1.5, col = "#1f77b4")
legend("topright", legend = c("Trajectory", "Start", "End"),

## The HMC algorithm

Combining leapfrog integration with Metropolis acceptance yields the complete HMC algorithm.

::: {.note}
**HMC Algorithm**

Given current position $\mathbf{q}_t$:

1. **Refresh momentum:** Draw $\mathbf{p} \sim \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{0}, M)$.
2. **Store current energy:** Compute $H_{\text{old}} = U(\mathbf{q}_t) + K(\mathbf{p})$.
3. **Integrate:** Run leapfrog for $L$ steps with step size $\varepsilon$:
   $$
   (\mathbf{q}', \mathbf{p}') = \text{Leapfrog}(\mathbf{q}_t, \mathbf{p}, \varepsilon, L).
   $$
4. **Compute proposal energy:** $H_{\text{new}} = U(\mathbf{q}') + K(\mathbf{p}')$.
5. **Metropolis correction:** Accept with probability
   $$
   \alpha = \min\left(1, \exp(-\Delta H)\right), \quad \Delta H = H_{\text{new}} - H_{\text{old}}.
   $$
6. **Move or reject:**
   - If $u \sim \text{Uniform}(0,1) < \alpha$: set $\mathbf{q}_{t+1} = \mathbf{q}'$.
   - Else: set $\mathbf{q}_{t+1} = \mathbf{q}_t$.
:::

- **Why the Metropolis correction?** If we could simulate Hamiltonian dynamics exactly, $\Delta H = 0$ and every proposal would be accepted. However, leapfrog introduces discretization error $\Delta H \approx O(L \varepsilon^2)$. The Metropolis step removes any bias introduced by discretization, restores exact invariance to the joint distribution $\pi(\mathbf{q}, \mathbf{p})$, and ensures that marginalizing over $\mathbf{p}$ gives exact samples from $\pi(\mathbf{q})$. A smaller $\varepsilon$ reduces discretization error and increases acceptance; a larger $\varepsilon$ reduces computation but risks rejection.

- **Momentum refresh.** In step 1, we draw a fresh momentum independently of the previous momentum. This is crucial: otherwise, the trajectory would continue indefinitely on level sets of $H$, exploring only one mode. By resampling $\mathbf{p}$ at each iteration, we jump between level sets of $H$ (exploring different energies), allow exploration of the full parameter space, and introduce randomness necessary for ergodicity. Notably, resampling $\mathbf{p} \sim \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{0}, M)$ is **Gibbs sampling** in the $(q, p)$ space.

## Tuning and practical guidance

HMC's efficiency depends critically on three tuning parameters — step size $\varepsilon$, number of leapfrog steps $L$, and mass matrix $M$ — together with a warm-up phase that adapts them automatically.

- **Step size $\varepsilon$.** This controls discretization granularity, and is the parameter most often tuned automatically.

::: {.definition}
The **step size** $\varepsilon$ controls discretization granularity.

- **Too small:** $\varepsilon \to 0$ gives accurate proposals, high acceptance, but slow exploration (many gradient evaluations).
- **Too large:** $\varepsilon$ large causes energy errors to accumulate; acceptance rate $\alpha$ drops.

**Typical rule:** Target acceptance rate of 0.65–0.80 (varies slightly with $d$). Adjust $\varepsilon$ to match this during warm-up.
:::

- **Trajectory length and number of steps $L$.** The product $T = L \varepsilon$ is the simulated time and determines how far each proposal travels.

::: {.definition}
The **trajectory length** is $T = L \varepsilon$. A longer trajectory explores farther but at higher computational cost.

- **Too few steps:** Behaves like random-walk MH (local exploration).
- **Too many steps:** Trajectories double back on themselves (waste computation and may reject).

**Effective range:** Aim for $L \in [20, 100]$ initially; tune via ESS per gradient evaluation.
:::

- **Mass matrix $M$.** The mass matrix controls how momentum couples to position. With $M = I$ (identity), kinetic energy is $K(\mathbf{p}) = \frac{1}{2} \|\mathbf{p}\|^2$ — isotropic.

::: {.remark}
**Adaptive mass matrix:** If the posterior has vastly different scales or high correlations, an isotropic $M$ is inefficient. Adaptive HMC (as implemented in Stan) estimates the posterior covariance $\Sigma_{\text{post}}$ during warm-up and sets $M = \Sigma_{\text{post}}^{-1}$. This makes the "momentum-space" geometry match the "parameter-space" geometry, enabling larger steps and better mixing.

Equivalently, we can work with a reparametrization or preconditioning. The improvement can be dramatic in correlated or high-dimensional problems.
:::

- **Warm-up and adaptation.** Most HMC implementations (especially Stan) use a **warm-up** (or "burn-in") phase to (i) find a reasonable starting point and estimate posterior covariance, (ii) adapt $\varepsilon$ to target a desired acceptance rate, (iii) adapt $M$ to match posterior structure, and (iv) discard warm-up samples as they are not from the stationary distribution. After warm-up, $\varepsilon$ and $M$ are fixed, and the algorithm becomes a standard Markov chain with invariant distribution $\pi(\mathbf{q})$.

## Divergent transitions and energy issues

HMC can fail in subtle ways:

::: {.remark}
**Divergent transitions** occur when the numerical integration goes unstable, typically because:
- Step size $\varepsilon$ is too large and energy error $|\Delta H|$ becomes very large, causing rejection.
- Posterior has regions of high curvature that the integrator cannot resolve.
- Posterior has heavy tails or mild multimodality.

**Indicator:** Many proposals have $\Delta H \gg 10$ (large energy errors).

**Solution:** Reduce $\varepsilon$, increase warm-up iterations, or use an adaptive mass matrix. Stan prints warnings about divergences; they suggest the sampler is not exploring properly and posterior inferences may be biased.
:::

## NUTS: No-U-Turn Sampler

Modern software like Stan uses the **No-U-Turn Sampler (NUTS)**, which removes the need to manually tune $L$. Instead of running a fixed number of steps $L$, NUTS adaptively expands the trajectory until it would begin to double back.

::: {.definition}
**Doubling criterion:** A trajectory "doubles back" (makes a U-turn) when the "generalized velocity" $(\mathbf{q}' - \mathbf{q}_{\text{left}})^T \mathbf{p}$ becomes negative, indicating backward motion. More precisely, a U-turn is detected using the dot product:
$$
(\mathbf{q}_{\text{right}} - \mathbf{q}_{\text{left}})^T \mathbf{p}_{\text{right}} < 0 \quad \text{or} \quad (\mathbf{q}_{\text{right}} - \mathbf{q}_{\text{left}})^T \mathbf{p}_{\text{left}} < 0.
$$

NUTS extends the trajectory by "doubling" (adding steps in the same direction) until a U-turn is detected, then accepts from the entire trajectory.
:::

The advantages of NUTS over fixed-$L$ HMC are:

- **Eliminates manual tuning of $L$:** The algorithm automatically determines trajectory length.
- **Prevents wasted computation:** Stops before the trajectory begins circling back.
- **Maintains properties:** The randomized doubling ensures detailed balance is preserved (via Metropolis-from-trajectory).
- **Stan implementation:** NUTS is the default sampler in Stan with automatic warm-up and adaptation.

## Comparison: HMC vs random-walk MH vs Gibbs

::: {.proposition}
**Scaling with dimension $d$:**

| Sampler | Acceptance | Auto-correlation | ESS per gradient |
|---------|-----------|-----------------|-----------------|
| RW-MH | $O(1)$ | $O(d)$ | $O(d^{-2})$ |
| Gibbs | $1$ | $O(d)$ | $O(d^{-1})$ if uncorrelated |
| HMC | $0.6$–$0.9$ | $O(\sqrt{d})$ or better | $O(1)$ to $O(d^{-1})$ |

(Rough asymptotics; constants matter.)

**Practical implications:**
- **RW-MH:** Simple to implement, no gradients needed. But prohibitively slow for $d > 20$ unless posterior is nearly isotropic.
- **Gibbs:** Requires conditional distributions. Fast if draws from conditionals are cheap. Struggles with correlations.
- **HMC:** Requires gradients but dramatically outperforms RW-MH in medium to high dimensions ($d \geq 10$). Excellent when gradients are available (automatic differentiation).
:::

::: {.example}
**Comparison on correlated bivariate Gaussian**

We sample from $\mathcal{N}(0, \Sigma)$ with $\Sigma = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0.95 \\ 0.95 & 1 \end{pmatrix}$ using:
1. Random-walk MH with $\text{step} = 0.3$.
2. HMC with $\varepsilon = 0.15$, $L = 20$.

```{r}
#| label: lst-hmc-vs-rwmh
#| lst-cap: "Hmc vs rwmh"
#| fig-cap: "Trace plots (top) and autocorrelation (bottom) for RW-MH (left) vs HMC (right) on correlated 2D Gaussian. HMC explores the ridge efficiently; RW-MH gets stuck."

# Setup
rho <- 0.95
Sigma <- matrix(c(1, rho, rho, 1), 2, 2)
Sigma_inv <- solve(Sigma)

log_pi <- function(q) {
  -0.5 * as.numeric(t(q) %*% Sigma_inv %*% q)
}

grad_U <- function(q) {
  Sigma_inv %*% q
}

# RW-MH
rw_mh <- function(n_iter, step = 0.3) {
  q <- c(0, 0)
  samples <- matrix(NA, n_iter, 2)
  acc <- 0
  for (i in 1:n_iter) {
    q_prop <- q + rnorm(2, 0, step)
    log_alpha <- log_pi(q_prop) - log_pi(q)
    if (log(runif(1)) < log_alpha) {
      q <- q_prop
      acc <- acc + 1
    }
    samples[i, ] <- q
  }
  list(samples = samples, acc_rate = acc / n_iter)
}

# Leapfrog for HMC
leapfrog <- function(q, p, eps, L) {
  p <- p - 0.5 * eps * grad_U(q)
  for (i in 1:L) {
    q <- q + eps * p
    if (i < L) p <- p - eps * grad_U(q)
  }
  p <- p - 0.5 * eps * grad_U(q)
  list(q = q, p = -p)
}

# HMC
hmc <- function(n_iter, eps = 0.15, L = 20) {
  q <- c(0, 0)
  samples <- matrix(NA, n_iter, 2)
  acc <- 0
  for (i in 1:n_iter) {
    p <- rnorm(2)
    H_old <- 0.5 * as.numeric(t(q) %*% Sigma_inv %*% q) + 0.5 * sum(p^2)
    prop <- leapfrog(q, p, eps, L)
    q_prop <- prop$q
    p_prop <- prop$p
    H_new <- 0.5 * as.numeric(t(q_prop) %*% Sigma_inv %*% q_prop) + 0.5 * sum(p_prop^2)
    log_alpha <- -(H_new - H_old)
    if (log(runif(1)) < log_alpha) {
      q <- q_prop
      acc <- acc + 1
    }
    samples[i, ] <- q
  }
  list(samples = samples, acc_rate = acc / n_iter)
}

# Run samplers
set.seed(42)
n_iter <- 2000
fit_mh <- rw_mh(n_iter, step = 0.3)
fit_hmc <- hmc(n_iter, eps = 0.15, L = 20)

# Plot traces
par(mfrow = c(2, 2), mar = c(4, 4, 3, 1))

plot(fit_mh$samples[,1], type = "l", lwd = 1, col = "#1f77b4",
     main = paste0("RW-MH Trace (acc=", round(fit_mh$acc_rate, 3), ")"),
     xlab = "Iteration", ylab = "q1")
grid(col = "grey90")

plot(fit_hmc$samples[,1], type = "l", lwd = 1, col = "#d62728",
     main = paste0("HMC Trace (acc=", round(fit_hmc$acc_rate, 3), ")"),
     xlab = "Iteration", ylab = "q1")
grid(col = "grey90")

# Plot ACF
acf(fit_mh$samples[,1], main = "RW-MH Autocorr", lag.max = 50, col = "#1f77b4")
acf(fit_hmc$samples[,1], main = "HMC Autocorr", lag.max = 50, col = "#d62728")
```

RW-MH has acceptance $\approx 0.61$ and poor mixing; autocorrelation decays slowly. HMC has acceptance $\approx 0.96$ and autocorrelation decays in $\sim 10$ lags, yielding much larger effective sample size per gradient evaluation.
:::

## Practical recommendations

::: {.remark}
**When to use HMC:**
- Posterior dimension $d \geq 10$.
- Gradient $\nabla \log \pi$ is available (exact or via automatic differentiation).
- Posterior is not highly multimodal with separated modes.
- Computational budget permits gradient evaluations (modern autodiff is fast).

**When HMC struggles:**
- Highly multimodal posterior with isolated modes (HMC gets trapped in one mode; consider parallel tempering or other global methods).
- Discrete parameters (HMC requires continuous state; marginalize out discrete variables or use other samplers).
- Gradient unavailable and too expensive to compute numerically.

**When to prefer alternatives:**
- **Gibbs sampling:** If conditional distributions are easy to sample. Better mixing if conditionals are well-separated; worse if highly correlated.
- **Random-walk MH:** Low-dimensional ($d < 10$), no gradients, simple implementation preferred.
- **Slice sampling or other adaptive methods:** If gradient unavailable but adaptive exploration is desired.
:::

## Stan and modern practice

The probabilistic programming language **Stan** uses NUTS with automatic warm-up by default. This means:

1. Users specify the log-posterior (via a model statement).
2. Stan automatically differentiates to obtain $\nabla \log \pi$ via the reverse-mode autodiff.
3. During warm-up, step size $\varepsilon$ is tuned to target 0.80 acceptance; mass matrix $M$ is adapted.
4. NUTS extends trajectories adaptively without manual specification of $L$.
5. Convergence diagnostics ($\hat{R}$, effective sample size) are computed automatically.

This makes HMC accessible to practitioners without deep knowledge of tuning. **Stan is now the most widely used tool for general-purpose Bayesian inference**, precisely because it combines HMC's superior performance with ease of use.

## Exercises

::: {.exercise}

For a target $\pi(\theta) \propto \exp(-U(\theta))$ with $U(\theta) = \tfrac{1}{2}\theta^\top\theta$ (standard Gaussian), write the Hamiltonian $H(\theta, p)$ with a unit mass matrix, the canonical equations of motion, and the analytic solution for $(\theta(t), p(t))$.
:::

::: {.solution}
With $M = I$ the Hamiltonian is
$$H(\theta, p) = \tfrac{1}{2}\theta^\top\theta + \tfrac{1}{2}p^\top p,$$
and Hamilton's equations give $\dot\theta = p$, $\dot p = -\theta$. This is the harmonic oscillator, whose closed-form solution is
$$\theta(t) = \theta_0\cos t + p_0\sin t, \qquad p(t) = -\theta_0\sin t + p_0\cos t,$$
i.e. a rigid rotation of phase space, so the trajectory stays exactly on the level set $H = \tfrac{1}{2}(\|\theta_0\|^2+\|p_0\|^2)$.
:::

::: {.exercise}

Show that for a separable Hamiltonian $H(\theta, p) = U(\theta) + K(p)$ with $K(p) = \tfrac{1}{2}p^\top M^{-1}p$, the canonical equations decouple into $\dot\theta = M^{-1}p$ and $\dot p = -\nabla U(\theta)$.
:::

::: {.solution}
Because $U$ depends only on $\theta$ and $K$ only on $p$, the gradients separate: $\nabla_p H = \nabla_p K(p)$ and $\nabla_\theta H = \nabla_\theta U(\theta)$. Hamilton's equations therefore become
$$\dot\theta = \nabla_p H = M^{-1}p, \qquad \dot p = -\nabla_\theta H = -\nabla U(\theta),$$
so position is driven only by $p$ (through $M^{-1}$) and momentum only by the potential gradient — the coupling between $\theta$ and $p$ is gone, which is exactly what lets leapfrog use alternating explicit half-steps.
:::

::: {.exercise}

Energy conservation. Differentiate $H(\theta(t), p(t))$ along an exact trajectory and show that $dH/dt = 0$. State why the leapfrog integrator does not exactly conserve $H$ but conserves a *modified* shadow Hamiltonian.
:::

::: {.solution}
Along an exact trajectory,
$$\frac{dH}{dt} = \nabla_\theta H \cdot \dot\theta + \nabla_p H \cdot \dot p = \nabla_\theta H \cdot \nabla_p H + \nabla_p H \cdot (-\nabla_\theta H) = 0.$$
Leapfrog is a discrete symplectic map, not the exact flow, so $H$ oscillates by $O(\varepsilon^2)$. By backward-error analysis it instead conserves a shadow Hamiltonian $\tilde H = H + \varepsilon^2 H_2 + \dots$ exactly, which is why $\Delta H$ stays bounded over long trajectories instead of drifting.
:::

::: {.exercise}

Write out one full step of the leapfrog integrator for a separable Hamiltonian with step size $\varepsilon$. Show that the update is symplectic by checking that the Jacobian determinant equals one.
:::

::: {.solution}
The three sub-steps are
$$p_{1/2} = p_0 - \tfrac{\varepsilon}{2}\nabla U(\theta_0), \quad \theta_1 = \theta_0 + \varepsilon M^{-1}p_{1/2}, \quad p_1 = p_{1/2} - \tfrac{\varepsilon}{2}\nabla U(\theta_1).$$
Each sub-step is a shear in either $\theta$ or $p$, so its Jacobian is upper- or lower-triangular with unit diagonal, e.g. the half kick has Jacobian $\begin{pmatrix} I & 0 \\ -\tfrac{\varepsilon}{2}\nabla^2 U & I\end{pmatrix}$ with determinant $1$. The full step is the composition, hence $\det J = 1\cdot 1\cdot 1 = 1$, so leapfrog preserves phase-space volume and the Metropolis ratio reduces to $\exp(-\Delta H)$.
:::

::: {.exercise}

Why random-walk Metropolis fails in high dimensions. Argue that for an isotropic Gaussian target in $d$ dimensions, the optimal step size scales as $\varepsilon \sim d^{-1/2}$ and the number of steps to traverse the typical set scales as $d^{1/2}$, giving a *quadratic* cost in $d$. Contrast with HMC's $d^{1/4}$ cost.
:::

::: {.solution}
A proposal $\theta' = \theta + \varepsilon\xi$ with $\xi\sim\mathcal{N}(0,I)$ produces $\Delta\log\pi$ with mean $-\tfrac{1}{2}d\varepsilon^2$ and variance $\sim d\varepsilon^2$, so keeping a non-vanishing acceptance forces $d\varepsilon^2 = O(1)$, i.e. $\varepsilon \sim d^{-1/2}$. The displacement per accepted step is then $\|\Delta\theta\|\sim\varepsilon\sqrt{d}\sim 1$, and crossing the typical set of radius $\sqrt{d}$ as a diffusion requires
$$N_{\text{RW-MH}} \sim d,\qquad \text{i.e. total cost}\propto d,$$
which becomes $d^2$ once correlation length is included. HMC instead tolerates $\varepsilon\sim d^{-1/4}$ (the leapfrog energy error is $O(\varepsilon^2)$ summed coherently), so trajectories of length $T = O(1)$ need $L\sim d^{1/4}$ gradient evaluations — exponentially better in high $d$.
:::

::: {.exercise}

HMC acceptance probability. Show that the acceptance probability of a proposed move $(\theta_0, p_0) \to (\theta_L, p_L)$ generated by leapfrog is
$$\alpha = \min\!\left\{1,\ \exp\!\big(-[H(\theta_L, p_L) - H(\theta_0, p_0)]\big)\right\}.$$
:::

::: {.solution}
The augmented target is $\pi(\theta,p) \propto e^{-H(\theta,p)}$. Leapfrog combined with the final momentum flip $p\mapsto -p$ gives a deterministic, volume-preserving, self-inverse proposal, so the forward and reverse proposal densities are equal and their Jacobian is $1$. The Metropolis–Hastings ratio collapses to
$$\alpha = \min\!\left\{1, \frac{e^{-H(\theta_L,p_L)}}{e^{-H(\theta_0,p_0)}}\right\} = \min\{1,\exp(-\Delta H)\}.$$
Because exact dynamics would give $\Delta H = 0$, the only source of rejection is leapfrog's discretization error.
:::

::: {.exercise}

Optimal acceptance rate. State the result (Beskos et al., 2013) that the optimal HMC acceptance for isotropic Gaussian targets is approximately $0.65$. Sketch the argument in words: too-low acceptance wastes proposals, too-high acceptance means $\varepsilon$ is too small.
:::

::: {.solution}
For separable Gaussian targets the leapfrog energy error is asymptotically Gaussian, $\Delta H \sim \mathcal{N}(\mu, 2\mu)$ with $\mu \propto d\varepsilon^4$, so the expected squared jumping distance per gradient evaluation is maximised at
$$\alpha^\star = \mathbb{E}[\min\{1,e^{-\Delta H}\}] \approx 0.651.$$
Heuristically, very low acceptance means most leapfrog work is thrown away on rejected trajectories, while very high acceptance means $\varepsilon$ is so small that each accepted trajectory barely moves; the sweet spot trades these costs.
:::

::: {.exercise}

Mass-matrix reparametrisation. Show that running HMC with mass matrix $M$ on parameter $\theta$ is equivalent to running HMC with identity mass matrix on the reparametrised variable $\tilde\theta = M^{1/2}\theta$. State the practical implication for posteriors with correlated parameters.
:::

::: {.solution}
Let $\tilde\theta = M^{1/2}\theta$ and $\tilde p = M^{-1/2}p$. Then the kinetic energy becomes
$$K(p) = \tfrac{1}{2}p^\top M^{-1}p = \tfrac{1}{2}\tilde p^\top\tilde p,$$
so in $(\tilde\theta,\tilde p)$ coordinates the kinetic energy uses the identity mass matrix, while the Jacobian $|\det M^{1/2}|$ is constant and cancels in the acceptance ratio. Practically, choosing $M\approx \Sigma_{\text{post}}^{-1}$ whitens correlated posteriors: HMC sees an approximately isotropic problem in $\tilde\theta$, so a single step size works in all directions and step length need not shrink to the smallest eigendirection.
:::

::: {.exercise}

Divergent transitions. Describe when leapfrog energy errors blow up. Give one example posterior geometry (e.g.\ a "funnel" in hierarchical models) where divergences are common, and the standard remedy (non-centered reparametrisation).
:::

::: {.solution}
Leapfrog energy error scales as $\Delta H = O(\varepsilon^2\|\nabla^2 U\|)$, so wherever the local curvature spikes, $|\Delta H|$ explodes and Stan flags a *divergence* (typically $|\Delta H|\gtrsim 10^3$). Neal's funnel $\theta_i \mid \tau \sim \mathcal{N}(0,e^\tau)$, $\tau\sim\mathcal{N}(0,9)$ is the canonical example: near $\tau\ll 0$ the funnel neck is so narrow that no single $\varepsilon$ works for both the neck and the mouth. The remedy is the non-centred reparametrisation
$$\theta_i = e^{\tau/2}z_i, \qquad z_i\sim\mathcal{N}(0,1),$$
which decouples scale from location and gives an approximately isotropic posterior in $(z,\tau)$.
:::

::: {.exercise}

NUTS termination criterion. Describe Hoffman & Gelman's no-U-turn condition: stop doubling the trajectory once the proposal starts curling back on itself. Why does this remove the need to tune the trajectory length $L$ manually?
:::

::: {.solution}
NUTS keeps doubling the trajectory in random forward/backward directions and terminates once the displacement vector $\Delta\theta = \theta_{\text{right}} - \theta_{\text{left}}$ stops aligning with the momentum at either endpoint, i.e.
$$\Delta\theta^\top p_{\text{right}} < 0 \quad\text{or}\quad \Delta\theta^\top p_{\text{left}} < 0.$$
At that instant any further integration would curve the trajectory back on itself, wasting gradient evaluations. Because the criterion is *local* and computed on the fly, the user no longer has to pick a fixed $L$: NUTS adapts the trajectory length per iteration to the local geometry while preserving detailed balance through a slice/multinomial sampler over the built tree.
:::

::: {.exercise}

Time reversibility. Show that the leapfrog integrator is reversible: if we apply it for $L$ steps to go from $(\theta_0, p_0)$ to $(\theta_L, p_L)$, then a single negation $p_L \mapsto -p_L$ followed by $L$ leapfrog steps returns to $(\theta_0, -p_0)$.
:::

::: {.solution}
The kinetic energy is even in $p$, so under $p\mapsto -p$ the equations $\dot\theta = M^{-1}p$, $\dot p = -\nabla U$ become $\dot\theta = -M^{-1}p$, $\dot p = \nabla U$ — exactly the time-reversed dynamics. For one leapfrog step, applying the three sub-steps with negated momentum literally undoes the original substeps in reverse order:
$$\Phi_\varepsilon\circ R\circ\Phi_\varepsilon = R, \qquad R: (\theta,p)\mapsto(\theta,-p).$$
Composing $L$ such involutions gives $\Phi_\varepsilon^L\circ R\circ\Phi_\varepsilon^L(\theta_0,p_0) = (\theta_0,-p_0)$, which is what makes the leapfrog proposal symmetric so the MH ratio depends only on $\Delta H$.
:::

::: {.exercise}

Effect of step size on energy error. For leapfrog applied to a quadratic Hamiltonian, show that the per-step energy error scales as $O(\varepsilon^3)$ but the total error over a trajectory of fixed length scales as $O(\varepsilon^2)$. Why does this argue for keeping $\varepsilon$ moderately small but not vanishingly small?
:::

::: {.solution}
Leapfrog is a second-order symplectic method, so its *local* truncation error in $H$ per step is $O(\varepsilon^3)$. Over a trajectory of fixed length $T = L\varepsilon$, the number of steps is $L = T/\varepsilon$, so the global energy error scales as
$$\Delta H_{\text{total}} = L\cdot O(\varepsilon^3) = O(\varepsilon^2).$$
Shrinking $\varepsilon$ further keeps $\Delta H$ tiny and acceptance near $1$, but doubles the number of gradient evaluations: cost scales like $1/\varepsilon$ while ESS gain is sub-linear, so the sweet spot is a moderate $\varepsilon$ targeting ~65–80% acceptance, not $\varepsilon\to 0$.
:::

::: {.exercise}

HMC vs random-walk Metropolis. Sketch on a single plot: target trace from random-walk MH with poorly tuned step size, target trace from HMC with reasonable $(\varepsilon, L)$. Identify the integrated autocorrelation time of each visually and explain the gap.
:::

::: {.solution}
The RW-MH trace looks like a slow diffusive walk with long flat plateaus (frequent rejections) and visible drift over many iterations, so its integrated autocorrelation time $\tau_{\text{RW}}$ is large; the HMC trace looks like nearly independent draws hopping across the support, with $\tau_{\text{HMC}}\approx 1$–$5$. The ESS ratio scales as
$$\frac{\text{ESS}_{\text{HMC}}}{\text{ESS}_{\text{RW}}} = \frac{\tau_{\text{RW}}}{\tau_{\text{HMC}}} \sim \frac{d}{d^{1/2}} = d^{1/2},$$
because each HMC trajectory traverses an $O(1)$ fraction of the typical set in one move, while RW-MH only takes a step of size $O(d^{-1/2})$.
:::

::: {.exercise}

Define the *typical set* of a $d$-dimensional posterior. Argue why HMC, which conserves energy and follows gradient flow, stays inside the typical set, while random-walk MH wastes most of its time outside it in high dimensions.
:::

::: {.solution}
The typical set $\mathcal{T}_\delta$ is the thin shell of states whose log-density equals its expectation up to $\delta$:
$$\mathcal{T}_\delta = \{\theta : |\log\pi(\theta) - \mathbb{E}_\pi[\log\pi]| < \delta\},$$
which in high $d$ concentrates near a $(d{-}1)$-dimensional surface far from the mode (e.g. a thin shell of radius $\sqrt{d}$ for $\mathcal{N}(0,I)$). HMC trajectories conserve $H = U + K$, and on average $U(\theta)\approx \mathbb{E}_\pi[U]$, so the dynamics flow *along* this level set — they orbit inside $\mathcal{T}_\delta$ by construction. RW-MH proposes isotropically in $\mathbb{R}^d$, where almost all directions point *off* the thin shell, so most proposals leave the typical set, get rejected, and the chain barely moves.
:::
