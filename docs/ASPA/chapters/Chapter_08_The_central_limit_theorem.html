8.1 The theorem

In many applications sums of independent and identically distributed random variables occur. Quite generally, we consider random variables \(x_{n}, n=1, \ldots, N\), all drawn independently from the same probability density. Then the ‘central limit theorem’ (CLT) claims that the PDF of weighted sums of these variables converges towards a Gaussian for \(N \rightarrow \infty\).

Central limit theorem

Define the random variable

\[ S=\sum_{n=1}^{N} c_{n} x_{n} \]

with \(x_{n}\) i.i.d. random variables with mean \(\mu_{x}\) and variance \(\sigma_{x}^{2}<\infty\). The weights \(c_{n}\) fulfil the following conditions:

\[ \begin{equation*} \lim _{N \rightarrow \infty} \frac{1}{N} \sum_{n=1}^{N} c_{n}^{v}<\infty, \quad v \in \mathbb{Z} \tag{8.1} \end{equation*} \]

Then

\[ \lim _{N \rightarrow \infty} p(S \mid N, \mathcal{I})=\mathcal{N}\left(S \mid \mu_{S}, \sigma_{S}\right) \]

\[ \begin{align*} \mu_{S} & :=\langle S\rangle=\mu_{x} \sum_{n=1}^{N} c_{n}, \tag{8.2}\\ \sigma_{S}^{2} & :=\operatorname{var}(S)=\sigma_{x}^{2} \sum_{n=1}^{N} c_{n}^{2} . \end{align*} \]

Central limit theorem for the sample mean

\[ \begin{align*} S & =\frac{1}{N} \sum_{n=1}^{N} x_{n}, \tag{8.3a}\\ \lim _{N \rightarrow \infty} p(S \mid N, \mathcal{I}) & =\mathcal{N}\left(S \mid \mu_{S}, \sigma_{S}\right), \tag{8.3b}\\ \mu_{S} & :=\mu_{x}, \tag{8.3c}\\ \sigma_{S}^{2} & :=\frac{\sigma_{x}^{2}}{N} . \tag{8.3d} \end{align*} \]

As a matter of fact, the relation for mean and variance of \(S\), given in equation (8.3c) and (8.3d), is valid for all values of \(N\) (see Section 19.1 [p. 284]), not just for large \(N\), but the PDF in general approaches a Gaussian only for \(N \rightarrow \infty\). There is an almost obvious exception: the sum of Gaussian random variables always has a Gaussian PDF, as outlined in Section 19.2 [p. 294]. Another interesting special case is the PDF of the sum of \(N\) Poissonian random variables. In Section 2.1.3 [p. 22] we have proved that it is Poissonian for \(N=2\). Then, repeatedly adding another Poissonian random variable to the sum reveals that the sum of any number \(N\) of Poissonian random variables \(n_{i}\) is always Poisson distributed, with mean \(\mu=\sum_{i=1}^{N} \mu_{i}\), where \(\mu_{i}\) is the mean of the individual Poisson random variables. So it is always a Poisson distribution. Is that a contradiction to the central limit theorem? The answer is ‘no’, as the Poisson distribution approaches a Gaussian one for \(\mu \rightarrow \infty\). The limiting Gaussian PDF for \(N \rightarrow \infty\) is not really of great interest for real-world applications, where \(N\) is mostly finite. We will therefore study what happens for moderate \(N\) guided by two examples, before we take a quick glance at the proof of the CLT.

8.1.1 Example I: Sum of exponential random numbers

First we consider the sample mean \(S\) of exponentially distributed random variables. In this case it is quite simple to determine the PDF for any \(N\) analytically. The PDF of the sum \(\bar{S}\) of exponentially distributed random variables has been determined in equation (7.68) [p. 130]:

\[ \begin{equation*} p(\bar{S} \mid N, \mathcal{I})=\frac{\beta^{N}}{\Gamma(N)} \bar{S}^{N-1} e^{-\beta \bar{S}} \tag{8.4} \end{equation*} \]

Then the PDF for the sample mean follows from the transformation \(\bar{S}=N S\), i.e.

\[ p(S \mid N, \mathcal{I})=N \frac{\beta^{N}}{\Gamma(N)} N^{N-1} S^{N-1} e^{-\beta N S} . \]

8.1.2 Example II: Sum of uniform random numbers

A case frequently occurring in practice is the sum \(\bar{S}\) of i.i.d. uniform random numbers from the unit interval \([0,1]\). Also in this case the PDF for \(\bar{S}\) can be computed analytically for arbitrary \(N\). First we determine the characteristic function of the uniform distribution

\[ p(x)=\boldsymbol{I}(0 \leq x \leq 1) . \]

With equation (7.53) [p. 124] we get

\[ \begin{equation*} \Phi(\omega)=\int_{0}^{1} e^{i \omega x} d x=\frac{e^{i \omega}-1}{i \omega} . \tag{8.5} \end{equation*} \]

The characteristic function of the sum \(\bar{S}=\sum_{i=1}^{N} x_{i}\) is the \(N\)-power of equation (8.5) (see equation (7.66) [p. 130]). The inverse transformation yields

\[ \begin{aligned} p(\bar{S} \mid N, \mathcal{I}) & =\frac{1}{2 \pi} \int_{-\infty}^{\infty}\left(\frac{e^{i \omega}-1}{i \omega}\right)^{N} e^{-i \omega \bar{S}} d \omega \\ & =\frac{(-i)^{N}}{2 \pi} \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} \omega^{-N}\left(\sum_{k=0}^{N}\binom{N}{k} e^{i k \omega}(-1)^{N-k}\right) e^{-i \omega \bar{S}} d \omega \\ & =\frac{i^{N}}{2 \pi} \sum_{k=0}^{N}\binom{N}{k}(-1)^{k} \underbrace{\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} \omega^{-N} e^{i \omega(k-\bar{S})} d \omega}_{:=I} \end{aligned} \]

The integral \(I\) can be computed using Cauchy’s integral theorem and we end up with

\[ I=i^{N} \pi \frac{(k-\bar{S})^{N-1}}{(N-1)!} \operatorname{sign}(k-\bar{S}) . \]

The PDF of \(\bar{S}\) is then

\[ p(\bar{S} \mid N, \mathcal{I})=\frac{(-1)^{N}}{2} \sum_{k=0}^{N}\binom{N}{k}(-1)^{k} \frac{(k-\bar{S})^{N-1}}{(N-1)!} \operatorname{sign}(k-\bar{S}) . \]

Therefore, the density \(p(\bar{S} \mid N, \mathcal{I})\) is a piecewise polynomial. The first few \((N=1,2,3)\) are given by

\[ \begin{aligned} & p(\bar{S} \mid N=1, \mathcal{I})= \begin{cases}0 & \text { if } \bar{S} \leq 0 \\ 1 & \text { if } 0<\bar{S} \leq 1 \\ 0 & \text { if } 1<\bar{S}\end{cases} \\ & p(\bar{S} \mid N=2, \mathcal{I})= \begin{cases}0 & \text { if } \bar{S} \leq 0 \\ \bar{S} & \text { if } 0<\bar{S} \leq 1 \\ -\bar{S}+2 & \text { if } 1<\bar{S} \leq 2 \\ 0 & \text { if } 2<\bar{S} .\end{cases} \end{aligned} \]

In Figure 8.2 we compare the exact PDF of the sample mean \(S\) with the Gaussian approximation given by the CLT for different values of \(N\). We observe that the convergence towards the Gaussian distribution is even faster than in the exponential case.

8.2 Stable distributions

As we have seen, the sum of i.i.d. random variables with finite variance converges towards a Gaussian distribution. But what happens if the random variables violate the finite variance condition? There is an important class of random distributions, the stable distributions, which act as ‘attractors’ for the sum of i.i.d. random variables. Roughly speaking,

Examples for stable distributions are the normal distribution, the Cauchy distribution, the Levy distribution and the Landau distribution. Stable distributions can be considered as a generalization of the central limit theorem to random variables without finite variance. A closed form of the probability density for a general stable distribution does not exist, but the density can be expressed using its specific characteristic function [101]

\[ \begin{equation*} \phi(z)=\exp \left[\left(-c_{0}+i(z /|z|) c_{1}\right)|z|^{\alpha}\right] . \tag{8.6} \end{equation*} \]

The parameter \(0<\alpha \leq 2\) is called the exponent (or index) of the stable distribution. Stable distributions with \(\alpha=1\) are the Cauchy distributions, and stable distributions with \(\alpha=2\) are the Gaussian distributions. The probability density follows from \(\phi(z)\) via

\[ \begin{equation*} p(x)=\frac{1}{2 \pi} \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} \phi(z) e^{-i x z} d z \tag{8.7} \end{equation*} \]

8.3 Proof of the central limit theorem

Here we will give a rough idea of the proof. A mathematically rigorous treatment can be found in [160]. To understand the principal reason for the CLT it suffices to consider the sample mean with equal weights \(c_{n}=1 / N\). Moreover, we can subtract the mean of the individual random variables and introduce new random variables by

\[ y_{i}=\frac{x_{i}-\mu_{x}}{N} . \]

Then the sum \(S_{y}=\sum_{i} y_{i}\) of the random variables \(y_{i}\) is related to the original sample mean via

\[ \begin{equation*} S_{x}=\frac{1}{N} \sum_{i} x_{i}=S_{y}+\mu_{x} . \tag{8.8} \end{equation*} \]

The variables have \(\left\langle y_{i}\right\rangle=0\) and \(\sigma_{y}^{2}:=\operatorname{var}\left(y_{i}\right)=\sigma_{x}^{2} / N^{2}\). Let the characteristic function of \(y\) be \(\Phi(\omega)\). According to equation (7.58) [p. 125] we have

\[ \begin{equation*} \left.\Phi(\omega)\right|_{\omega=0}=1,\left.\quad \Phi^{(1)}(\omega)\right|_{\omega=0}=0,\left.\quad \Phi^{(2)}(\omega)\right|_{\omega=0}=-\frac{\sigma_{x}^{2}}{N^{2}} . \tag{8.9} \end{equation*} \]

The characteristic function \(\Phi_{y}(\omega)\) of \(S_{y}\) is, according to equation (7.66) [p. 130], simply

\[ \Phi_{S_{y}}(\omega)=\Phi(\omega)^{N}=e^{N \ln (\Phi(\omega))} . \]

Next we expand \(\ln (\Phi(\omega))\) about \(\omega=0\) up to second order. Owing to equation (8.9) the result is simply

\[ \ln (\Phi(\omega))=-\frac{\sigma_{x}^{2}}{2 N^{2}} \omega^{2} . \]

Up to second order in the exponent the characteristic function reads

\[ \Phi_{S_{y}}(\omega)=e^{-\frac{\sigma_{x}^{2} \omega^{2}}{N}} \]

The inverse Fourier transform of the Gaussian has been given in equation (7.62) [p. 127] and yields

\[ p\left(S_{y} \mid N, \mathcal{I}\right)=e^{-\frac{S_{y}^{2}}{2 \sigma_{x}^{2} / N}} \]

Along with equation (8.8) we find the sought-for result

\[ p\left(S_{x} \mid N, \mathcal{I}\right)=\frac{1}{\sqrt{2 \pi \sigma_{S}^{2}}} e^{-\frac{\left(S_{x}-\mu_{x}\right)^{2}}{2 \sigma_{S}^{2}}}, \]

with \(\sigma_{S}^{2}=\sigma_{x}^{2} / N\), in agreement with equation (8.3d).

8.4 Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC)

The central limit theorem is the basis of one of the most important algorithms in the area of computer simulations, the Monte Carlo algorithm. There are a plethora of different problems in physics (statistical physics as well as quantum mechanics), information theory and engineering, which can be tackled with Monte Carlo algorithms. Within classical statistical physics the computation of thermodynamic expectation values in the canonical ensemble is one of the key tasks:

\[ \begin{equation*} \langle O\rangle_{T}=\int d^{m} x O(\boldsymbol{x}) p(\boldsymbol{x}) \tag{8.10} \end{equation*} \]

In the case of discrete degrees of freedom, the integration has to be replaced by a summation. The probability density \(p(\boldsymbol{x})\) depends only on the energy \(\boldsymbol{E}(\boldsymbol{x})\) and is given by the normalized Boltzmann factor

\[ p(\boldsymbol{x})=\frac{1}{Z} e^{-E(\boldsymbol{x}) / k T} \]

The challenge lies in the very high dimensionality of the integrals or sums, which can hardly ever be accessed by analytical means. In order to employ the central limit theorem, we can map the high-dimensional integral to a one-dimensional integral, as outlined in Section 7.3 [p. 96]. To this end we introduce the probability density of \(O\) :

\[ p(O)=\int d^{m} x \delta(O-O(\boldsymbol{x})) p(\boldsymbol{x}) \]

The mean and variance of \(O\) can be derived easily:

\[ \begin{align*} \langle O\rangle & =\int d O O p(O)=\int d^{m} x O(\boldsymbol{x}) p(\boldsymbol{x}) \tag{8.11a}\\ \operatorname{var}(O) & =\int d O(O-\langle O\rangle)^{2} p(O)=\int d^{m} x(O(\boldsymbol{x})-\langle O\rangle)^{2} p(\boldsymbol{x}) \tag{8.11b} \end{align*} \]

Therefore the mean of \(O\) is the desired integral. Furthermore, as guaranteed by the central limit theorem, the sample mean

\[ \mathcal{O}=\frac{1}{N} \sum_{n=1}^{N} O\left(x_{n}\right) \]

of the random numbers \(O\left(\boldsymbol{x}_{n}\right)\) is distributed as a Gaussian distribution for sufficiently large \(N\), with

\[ \operatorname{var}(\mathcal{O})=\frac{\operatorname{var}(\mathcal{O})}{\mathcal{N}} \]

This implies that high-dimensional integrals or sums of type can be estimated by the arithmetic mean of a sample \(\left\{x_{1}, \ldots, x_{N}\right\}\) of random vectors distributed as \(p(\boldsymbol{x})\), and

\[ \begin{equation*} \langle O\rangle=\frac{1}{N} \sum_{n=1}^{N} O\left(x_{n}\right) \pm \sqrt{\frac{\operatorname{var}(O)}{N}} . \tag{8.12} \end{equation*} \]

This also reveals why it is beneficial to repeat measurements of a quantity distorted by statistical fluctuations. The uncertainty of the arithmetic mean is given by

Standard error

\[ \begin{equation*} \mathrm{SE}=\frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{N}} \tag{8.13} \end{equation*} \]

We shall return to MC integration and MCMC in particular in Chapter 30 [p. 537].

8.5 The multivariate case

The CLT can be generalized to the multivariate case. Then it reads, for the sample mean of i.i.d. vectors of random variables,

Central limit theorem in \(\boldsymbol{m}\) dimensions

Let \(\boldsymbol{S} \in \mathbb{R}^{m}\) be a vector of random variables, defined by

\[ S=\frac{1}{N} \sum_{n=1}^{N} x_{n} \]

with \(\boldsymbol{x}_{n}\) i.i.d. random variables with mean \(\boldsymbol{\mu} \in \mathbb{R}^{m}\) and covariance \(\mathbf{C}\). Then

\[ \begin{equation*} \lim _{N \rightarrow \infty} p(\mathbf{S} \mid N, \mathcal{I})=\mathcal{N}(\mathbf{S} \mid \boldsymbol{\mu}, \mathbf{C} / N) \tag{8.14} \end{equation*} \]

$$
p\left(S_{x} \mid N, \mathcal{I}\right)=\frac{1}{\sqrt{2 \pi \sigma_{S}^{2}}} e^{-\frac{\left(S_{x}-\mu_{x}\right)^{2}}{2 \sigma_{S}^{2}}},
$$

with $\sigma_{S}^{2}=\sigma_{x}^{2} / N$, in agreement with equation (8.3d).

### 8.4 Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC)

The central limit theorem is the basis of one of the most important algorithms in the area of computer simulations, the Monte Carlo algorithm. There are a plethora of different problems in physics (statistical physics as well as quantum mechanics), information theory and engineering, which can be tackled with Monte Carlo algorithms. Within classical statistical
physics the computation of thermodynamic expectation values in the canonical ensemble is one of the key tasks:

$$
\begin{equation*}
\langle O\rangle_{T}=\int d^{m} x O(\boldsymbol{x}) p(\boldsymbol{x}) \tag{8.10}
\end{equation*}
$$

In the case of discrete degrees of freedom, the integration has to be replaced by a summation. The probability density $p(\boldsymbol{x})$ depends only on the energy $\boldsymbol{E}(\boldsymbol{x})$ and is given by the normalized Boltzmann factor

$$
p(\boldsymbol{x})=\frac{1}{Z} e^{-E(\boldsymbol{x}) / k T}
$$

The challenge lies in the very high dimensionality of the integrals or sums, which can hardly ever be accessed by analytical means. In order to employ the central limit theorem, we can map the high-dimensional integral to a one-dimensional integral, as outlined in Section 7.3 [p. 96]. To this end we introduce the probability density of $O$ :

$$
p(O)=\int d^{m} x \delta(O-O(\boldsymbol{x})) p(\boldsymbol{x})
$$

The mean and variance of $O$ can be derived easily:

$$
\begin{align*}
\langle O\rangle & =\int d O O p(O)=\int d^{m} x O(\boldsymbol{x}) p(\boldsymbol{x})  \tag{8.11a}\\
\operatorname{var}(O) & =\int d O(O-\langle O\rangle)^{2} p(O)=\int d^{m} x(O(\boldsymbol{x})-\langle O\rangle)^{2} p(\boldsymbol{x}) \tag{8.11b}
\end{align*}
$$

Therefore the mean of $O$ is the desired integral. Furthermore, as guaranteed by the central limit theorem, the sample mean

$$
\mathcal{O}=\frac{1}{N} \sum_{n=1}^{N} O\left(x_{n}\right)
$$

of the random numbers $O\left(\boldsymbol{x}_{n}\right)$ is distributed as a Gaussian distribution for sufficiently large $N$, with

$$
\operatorname{var}(\mathcal{O})=\frac{\operatorname{var}(\mathcal{O})}{\mathcal{N}}
$$

This implies that high-dimensional integrals or sums of type can be estimated by the arithmetic mean of a sample $\left\{x_{1}, \ldots, x_{N}\right\}$ of random vectors distributed as $p(\boldsymbol{x})$, and

$$
\begin{equation*}
\langle O\rangle=\frac{1}{N} \sum_{n=1}^{N} O\left(x_{n}\right) \pm \sqrt{\frac{\operatorname{var}(O)}{N}} . \tag{8.12}
\end{equation*}
$$

This also reveals why it is beneficial to repeat measurements of a quantity distorted by statistical fluctuations. The uncertainty of the arithmetic mean is given by

## Standard error

$$
\begin{equation*}
\mathrm{SE}=\frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{N}} \tag{8.13}
\end{equation*}
$$

We shall return to MC integration and MCMC in particular in Chapter 30 [p. 537].

### 8.5 The multivariate case

The CLT can be generalized to the multivariate case. Then it reads, for the sample mean of i.i.d. vectors of random variables,

## Central limit theorem in $\boldsymbol{m}$ dimensions

Let $\boldsymbol{S} \in \mathbb{R}^{m}$ be a vector of random variables, defined by

$$
S=\frac{1}{N} \sum_{n=1}^{N} x_{n}
$$

with $\boldsymbol{x}_{n}$ i.i.d. random variables with mean $\boldsymbol{\mu} \in \mathbb{R}^{m}$ and covariance $\mathbf{C}$. Then

$$
\begin{equation*}
\lim _{N \rightarrow \infty} p(\mathbf{S} \mid N, \mathcal{I})=\mathcal{N}(\mathbf{S} \mid \boldsymbol{\mu}, \mathbf{C} / N) \tag{8.14}
\end{equation*}
$$
