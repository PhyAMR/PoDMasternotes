19 Sampling distributions and common hypothesis tests

In this chapter we derive the probability distributions of common test statistics of finite samples of random variables and apply them to common hypothesis tests.

19.1 Mean and median of i.i.d. random variables

We start out with the mean and median of a sample \(\left\{x_{1}, \ldots, x_{L}\right\}\) of size \(L\) of i.i.d. random numbers drawn from a PDF \(\rho(x)\). The mean and variance are as usual

\[ \begin{aligned} \langle x\rangle & =\int x \rho(x) d x \\ \operatorname{var}(x) & =\int(x-\langle x\rangle)^{2} \rho(x) d x \end{aligned} \]

19.1.1 Distribution of the sample mean

Definition (Sample mean) We define

Sample mean

\[ \begin{equation*} \bar{x}:=\frac{1}{L} \sum_{i=1}^{L} x_{i} . \tag{19.1} \end{equation*} \]

The actual value of \(\bar{x}\) depends on the sample and is therefore a random variable as well. The corresponding PDF \(p(\bar{x} \mid L, \rho, \mathcal{I})\) can be determined by the marginalization rule

\[ p(\bar{x} \mid L, \rho, \mathcal{I})=\int \underbrace{p\left(\bar{x} \mid x_{1}, \ldots, x_{L}, L, \rho, \mathcal{I}\right)}_{\delta\left(\bar{x}-\frac{1}{L} \sum_{i=1}^{L} x_{i}\right)} p\left(x_{1}, \ldots, x_{L} \mid L, \rho, \mathcal{I}\right) d x_{1} \ldots d x_{L} \]

As the elements of the sample are i.i.d., we can simplify the expression:

\[ p(\bar{x} \mid L, \rho, \mathcal{I})=\int \delta\left(\bar{x}-\frac{1}{L} \sum_{i=1}^{L} x_{i}\right) \rho\left(x_{1}\right) \ldots \rho\left(x_{L}\right) d x_{1} \ldots d x_{L} \]

Without specifying the underlying PDF \(\rho(x)\), we cannot proceed further with the general expression. We either have to consider the limit \(L \rightarrow \infty\), in order to use the central limit theorem, or we have to specify the PDF.

However, we can derive general results for the lowest moments. The zeroth moment (normalization) is apparently equal to 1 , as long as \(\rho(x)\) is properly normalized. The first moment, the expectation value of the sample mean, yields

\[ \begin{aligned} \langle\bar{x}\rangle & =\int \bar{x} p(\bar{x} \mid L, \rho, \mathcal{I}) d \bar{x} \\ & =\int\left(\frac{1}{L} \sum_{i=1}^{L} x_{i}\right) \rho\left(x_{1}\right) \ldots \rho\left(x_{L}\right) d x_{1} \ldots d x_{L} \\ & =\frac{1}{L} \sum_{i=1}^{L} \int x_{i} \rho\left(x_{1}\right) \ldots \rho\left(x_{L}\right) d x_{1} \ldots d x_{L} \\ & =\frac{1}{L} \sum_{i=1}^{L}(\underbrace{\int x_{i} \rho\left(x_{i}\right) d x_{i}}_{=\langle x\rangle} \prod_{j \neq i}^{\int \rho\left(x_{j}\right) d x_{j}}) \\ \langle\bar{x}\rangle & =\langle x\rangle \end{aligned} \]

That is to say, the sample mean is an unbiased estimator for the underlying (true) mean of \(\rho(x)\).

19.1.2 Variance of the sample mean

Next we will determine the variance of the sample mean. To this end we need the second moment

\[ \begin{aligned} \left\langle\bar{x}^{2}\right\rangle & =\int \bar{x}^{2} p(\bar{x} \mid L, \rho, \mathcal{I}) d \bar{x} \\ & =\int\left(\frac{1}{L} \sum_{i=1}^{L} x_{i}\right)^{2} \rho\left(x_{1}\right) \ldots \rho\left(x_{L}\right) d x_{1} \ldots d x_{L} \\ & =\frac{1}{L^{2}} \sum_{i, j=1}^{L} \int x_{i} x_{j} \rho\left(x_{1}\right) \ldots \rho\left(x_{L}\right) d x_{1} \ldots d x_{L} \end{aligned} \]

We need to distinguish the cases \(i=j\) and \(i \neq j\) :

\[ \begin{aligned} \left\langle\bar{x}^{2}\right\rangle= & \frac{1}{L^{2}} \sum_{i=1}^{L} \int x_{i}^{2} \rho\left(x_{1}\right) \ldots \rho\left(x_{L}\right) d x_{1} \ldots d x_{L} \\ & +\frac{1}{L^{2}} \sum_{\substack{i, j=1 \\ i \neq j}}^{L} \int x_{i} x_{j} \rho\left(x_{1}\right) \ldots \rho\left(x_{L}\right) d x_{1} \ldots d x_{L} \\ = & \frac{1}{L^{2}} \sum_{i=1}^{L}\left\langle x^{2}\right\rangle+\frac{1}{L^{2}} \sum_{\substack{i, j=1 \\ i \neq j}}^{L}\langle x\rangle\langle x\rangle \\ = & \frac{1}{L}\left\langle x^{2}\right\rangle+\frac{L(L-1)}{L^{2}}\langle x\rangle^{2}=\frac{1}{L}\left(\left\langle x^{2}\right\rangle-\langle x\rangle^{2}\right)+\langle x\rangle^{2} \\ = & \frac{\left\langle(\Delta x)^{2}\right\rangle}{L}+\langle x\rangle^{2}=\frac{\operatorname{var}(x)}{L}+\langle x\rangle^{2} . \end{aligned} \]

Because \(\langle\bar{x}\rangle=\langle x\rangle\), the variance is

Variance of the sample mean

\[ \begin{equation*} \operatorname{var}(\bar{x})=\frac{\operatorname{var}(x)}{L} . \tag{19.2} \end{equation*} \]

In other words, the root mean square deviation of the sample mean from the true mean (statistical error of the mean) is given by

Standard error

\[ \begin{equation*} \Delta \bar{x}:=\mathrm{SE}:=\frac{\sqrt{\operatorname{var}(x)}}{\sqrt{L}} . \tag{19.3} \end{equation*} \]

It differs from the standard deviation by the factor \(1 / \sqrt{L}\). The standard deviation is a measure for the scattering of the individual elements of the sample, while the standard error is a measure for the scattering of the sample mean. This is the main advantage of repeated measurements, which suffer from statistical noise. The standard error implies that it is possible to reduce the uncertainty below a desired limit. Another advantage of computing sample means is provided by the central limit theorem. It guarantees that the sample mean for large samples is normally distributed, irrespective of the underlying PDF \(\rho(x)\), if the variance is finite, see Section 19.1.3. Needless to say, knowledge of the PDF is important for data analysis and inferences in general.

Up to now, a small deficiency still exists: we use the sample mean in order to estimate the true mean of \(\rho(x)\). The uncertainty of the estimator depends on the variance of \(\rho(x)\), which we will not know. Where do we get \(\operatorname{var}(x)\) from? One is prompted to estimate it from the sample, similarly to the sample mean, by

\[ v=\frac{1}{L} \sum_{i=1}^{L}\left(x_{i}-\bar{x}\right)^{2} \]

We have replaced the unknown true mean by the sample mean. The estimate for the variance can be rewritten as

\[ v=\frac{1}{L} \sum_{i=1}^{L} x_{i}^{2}-2 \bar{x} \underbrace{\frac{1}{L} \sum_{i=1}^{L} x_{i}}_{\bar{x}}+\frac{1}{L} \sum_{i=1}^{L} \bar{x}^{2}=\frac{1}{L} \sum_{i=1}^{L} x_{i}^{2}-\bar{x}^{2} . \]

Also, this estimator is a random variable. Its mean is

\[ \langle v\rangle=\underbrace{\frac{1}{L} \sum_{i=1}^{L}\left\langle x_{i}^{2}\right\rangle}_{\left\langle x^{2}\right\rangle}-\left\langle\bar{x}^{2}\right\rangle \stackrel{(19.2)}{=} \underbrace{\left\langle x^{2}\right\rangle-\langle x\rangle^{2}}_{=\operatorname{var}(x)}-\frac{\operatorname{var}(x)}{L}=\frac{L-1}{L} \operatorname{var}(x) . \]

Apparently, this estimator exhibits a bias, which can easily be eliminated by introducing a slightly different estimator:

UNBIASED ESTIMATOR FOR THE VARIANCE

\[ \begin{equation*} \sigma_{\mathrm{est}}^{2}=\frac{1}{L-1} \sum_{i=1}^{L}\left(x_{i}-\bar{x}\right)^{2} \tag{19.4} \end{equation*} \]

The modified prefactor \(1 /(L-1)\) is understood as follows. The quantities \(\Delta_{i}=x_{i}-\bar{x}\) are not independent, since \(\sum_{i} \Delta_{i}=0\). There are only \(L-1\) independent variables \(\xi_{i}\) that can be formed from the \(x_{i}\) by linear combination, the so-called Helmert transformation, as discussed in Section 7.10 [p. 136]. The number of degrees of freedom is thereby reduced by one.

19.1.3 Distribution of the sample median

The sample mean has a severe drawback. Its variance is proportional to the variance \(\operatorname{var}(x)\) of the sample elements. Even though it is reduced by \(1 / L\), it will be infinite if the underlying PDF has an infinite variance. This is for instance the case if \(\rho(x)\) is a Cauchy distribution, which occurs quite often in physics. In this case, the sample mean is meaningless and it is advisable to use the median instead.

In this section we will, therefore, consider the properties of the sample median. ::: {.callout-note appearance=“simple”} ## Definition (Median) The median, or central value, of a continuous distribution is defined as

:::

Median of a continuous distribution \(\boldsymbol{\rho}(\boldsymbol{x})\) with CDF \(\boldsymbol{F}(\boldsymbol{x})\)

\[ \begin{align*} F(\hat{x}) & =\int_{-\infty}^{\hat{x}} \rho(t) d t \stackrel{!}{=} \frac{1}{2} \\ \hat{x} & =F^{-1}\left(\frac{1}{2}\right) \tag{19.5} \end{align*} \]

Definition (Sample median) Given a sample \(\left\{s_{1}, \ldots, s_{L}\right\}\) of size \(L\) drawn from a probability density \(\rho(x)\) whose elements are sorted in ascending order \(\left\{s_{1}, \leq s_{2} \ldots \leq s_{L}\right\}\), the sample median is defined by

Sample median

\[ \breve{x}= \begin{cases}s_{n+1} & \text { for } L=2 n+1, \tag{19.6}\\ \frac{s_{n}+s_{n+1}}{2} & \text { for } L=2 n .\end{cases} \]

We are now interested in the sampling distribution of the sample median, i.e. in \(p(\breve{x} \mid \rho, L, \mathcal{I})\), of a sample of size \(L\) drawn from \(\rho(x)\). We confine the discussion to the case of odd sample size \(L=2 n+1\). The sample median is then the element \(n+1\) of the ordered list. The sought-for PDF is nothing but the corresponding ( \(n+1\) )th-order statistic. According to equation (7.42) [p. 119], the PDF in the present context reads

\[ \begin{equation*} p(\breve{x} \mid \rho, L=2 n+1, \mathcal{I})=\frac{1}{B(n+1, n+1)} F(\breve{x})^{n}[1-F(\breve{x})]^{n} \rho(\breve{x}) . \tag{19.7} \end{equation*} \]

The average sample median has already been derived in equation (7.43) [p. 120]:

\[ \begin{equation*} \langle\bar{x}\rangle_{n}=\int_{0}^{1} F^{-1}(f) \quad p_{\beta}(f \mid n+1, n+1) d f=\left\langle F^{-1}\right\rangle_{n}, \tag{19.8} \end{equation*} \]

where the latter mean is that of the associated beta PDF. We restrict the discussion to PDFs with singly connected support, such that \(F^{-1}\) is unique. The mean and variance of the beta PDF \(p_{\beta}(q \mid n+1, n+1)\) are

\[ \begin{align*} \mu_{\beta} & =\frac{1}{2} \tag{19.9}\\ \sigma_{\beta}^{2} & =\frac{1}{4(L+2)} \tag{19.10} \end{align*} \]

If the density is symmetric, i.e. \(\exists x_{0}: \rho\left(x_{0}-\Delta x\right)=\rho\left(x_{0}+\Delta x\right)\), then the mean and median of \(\rho(x)\) are the same: \(\langle x\rangle=\hat{x}=x_{0}\). Moreover, the CDF \(F(x)\) is antisymmetric w.r.t. point reflection about \(\left(\hat{x}, \frac{1}{2}\right)\). Apparently, since it is the same curve merely rotated by 90 degrees, the same holds true for \(F^{-1}(x)\). Consequently, in case of a symmetric density, \(F^{-1}(x)\) can be decomposed into

\[ F^{-1}(x)=\underbrace{F^{-1}\left(\frac{1}{2}\right)}_{=\hat{x}}+G(x), \]

where \(G(x)\) is an antisymmetric function about \(x=\frac{1}{2}\) and the average sample median simplifies to

\[ \langle\breve{x}\rangle_{n}=\left\langle F^{-1}\right\rangle_{n}=\hat{x}+\langle G\rangle_{n}=\hat{x}, \]

since \(p_{\beta}(f \mid n+1, n+1)\) entering the definition (equation (19.8)) is symmetric.

MEAN OF THE SAMPLE MEDIAN FOR SYMMETRIC DENSITY

\[ \begin{equation*} \langle\bar{x}\rangle_{n}=\hat{x} . \tag{19.11} \end{equation*} \]

In other words, iff the density is symmetric, the average sample median is equal to both the median and the mean of \(\rho(x)\). Consequently, the sample median is an unbiased estimator. In case of an asymmetric density we expand \(F^{-1}(f)\) about the mean of the beta PDF, \(\mu_{\beta}=\frac{1}{2}\), as it is equivalent to an expansion in \(\frac{1}{L}\). Starting from equation (19.8), we obtain

\[ \begin{aligned} \langle\bar{x}\rangle_{n}= & \underbrace{\left\langle F^{-1}\left(\frac{1}{2}\right)\right\rangle}_{=\hat{x}}+\left.\frac{d F^{-1}(f)}{d f}\right|_{f=\frac{1}{2}} \underbrace{\left\langle\left(f-\frac{1}{2}\right)\right\rangle}_{0} \\ & +\left.\frac{1}{2} \frac{d^{2} F^{-1}(q)}{d q^{2}}\right|_{q=\frac{1}{2}} \underbrace{\left\langle\left(q-\frac{1}{2}\right)^{2}\right\rangle+O\left(\sigma_{\beta}^{4}\right)}_{\sigma_{\beta}^{2}} \\ = & \hat{x}+\left.\frac{1}{2} \frac{d^{2} F^{-1}(q)}{d q^{2}}\right|_{q=\frac{1}{2}} \sigma_{\beta}^{2}+O\left(\sigma_{\beta}^{4}\right) . \end{aligned} \]

According to equation (19.10), \(\sigma_{\beta}^{2}=\frac{1}{4(L+2)}=\frac{1}{4 L}+O\left(\frac{1}{L^{2}}\right)\). That is, on average, the sample median provides the true median of the underlying probability density only up to a systematic deviation of order \(O\left(\frac{1}{L}\right)\) :

\[ \langle\bar{x}\rangle=\hat{x}+\left.\frac{1}{8 L} \frac{d^{2} F^{-1}(q)}{d q^{2}}\right|_{q=\frac{1}{2}}+O\left(\frac{1}{L^{2}}\right) . \]

For the prefactor we still need the derivatives of \(F^{-1}(f)\). By means of the chain rule we obtain

\[ \frac{d F^{-1}(f)}{d f}=\frac{1}{\rho\left(F^{-1}(f)\right)}, \]

and for the second derivative

\[ \frac{d^{2} F^{-1}(f)}{d f^{2}}=-\left.\frac{1}{\rho\left(F^{-1}(f)\right)^{3}} \frac{d \rho(x)}{d x}\right|_{x=F^{-1}(f)} . \]

We merely need these derivatives for \(f=\frac{1}{2}\) :

\[ \begin{align*} \left.\frac{d F^{-1}(q)}{d q}\right|_{q=\frac{1}{2}} & =\frac{1}{\rho(\hat{x})} \tag{19.12a}\\ \left.\frac{d^{2} F^{-1}(q)}{d q^{2}}\right|_{q=\frac{1}{2}} & =-\left.\frac{1}{\rho(\hat{x})^{3}} \frac{d \rho(x)}{d x}\right|_{x=\hat{x}} . \tag{19.12b} \end{align*} \]

The final result reads

Average sample median IN CASE OF ASYMMETRIC DENSITIES

\[ \begin{equation*} \langle\hat{x}\rangle=\hat{x}-\left.\frac{1}{8 L \rho(\hat{x})^{3}} \frac{d \rho(x)}{d x}\right|_{x=\hat{x}}+O\left(L^{-2}\right) . \tag{19.13} \end{equation*} \]

19.1.4 Variance of the sample median

Next we determine the variance of the sample median, or rather that of \(p \breve{x} \mid \rho, L= 2 n+1, \mathcal{I}\) ), given in equation (19.7):

\[ \begin{equation*} \operatorname{var}(\breve{x})=\left\langle\left[F^{-1}(f)-\langle\breve{x}\rangle\right]^{2}\right\rangle . \tag{19.14} \end{equation*} \]

We use the same expansion as before:

\[ \begin{aligned} \operatorname{var}(\breve{x}) & =\left\langle\left[\not x+\left.\frac{d F^{-1}(f)}{d f}\right|_{f=\frac{1}{2}}\left(f-\frac{1}{2}\right)+O\left(\sigma_{\beta}^{2}\right)-\not x-O\left(\sigma_{\beta}^{2}\right)\right]^{2}\right\rangle \\ & =\left[\left.\frac{d F^{-1}(f)}{d f}\right|_{f=\frac{1}{2}}\right]^{2} \underbrace{\left.\left[f-\frac{1}{2}\right]^{2}\right\rangle}_{\sigma_{\beta}^{2}}+O\left(\sigma_{\beta}^{4}\right) \end{aligned} \]

and obtain

\[ \operatorname{var}(\breve{x})=\left(\left.\frac{d F^{-1}(f)}{d f}\right|_{f=\frac{1}{2}}\right)^{2} \frac{1}{4 L}+O\left(\frac{1}{L^{2}}\right) . \]

Hence the scattering of the sample median is of order \(1 / \sqrt{L}\). It therefore predominates the uncertainty of the result for large \(L\), as the bias decreases like \(1 / L\). Along with equation (19.12), the result is

Variance of the sample median FOR A SAMPLE OF SIZE \(\boldsymbol{L} \boldsymbol{=} \mathbf{2} \boldsymbol{n} \boldsymbol{+} \mathbf{1}\) DRAWN FROM \(\boldsymbol{\rho} \boldsymbol{(} \boldsymbol{x} \boldsymbol{)}\)

\[ \begin{equation*} \operatorname{var}(\breve{x})=\frac{1}{4 L \rho(\hat{x})^{2}}+O\left(L^{-2}\right) . \tag{19.15} \end{equation*} \]

We have seen that in case of a symmetric density, the average sample median agrees with both the true median and the mean. This is corroborated by the above series expansion, as the derivative of \(\rho(x)\) vanishes at \(\hat{x}\). Interestingly, the variance of the sample median is independent of the variance of the underlying density \(\rho(x)\).

Consequently, the sample median is more robust than the sample mean when it comes to experiments with large scattering in the individual values, in other words, when the variance of \(\rho(x)\) is large or even infinite.

We want to close the discussion about the sample median with a few examples.

Example: Cauchy distribution

The Cauchy distribution is particularly interesting as it occurs quite frequently in physics and has an infinite variance. Here we use the PDF

\[ \rho_{C}(x)=\frac{1}{\pi\left(1+x^{2}\right)}, \]

which has zero mean. It is symmetric and the mean and median are equal. The CDF can be computed analytically:

\[ F_{C}(x)=\frac{1}{2}+\frac{\arctan (x)}{\pi}, \]

as well as its inverse:

\[ \begin{equation*} F_{C}^{-1}(f)=\tan \left[\pi\left(f-\frac{1}{2}\right)\right] . \tag{19.16} \end{equation*} \]

It is apparently antisymmetric and the median is \(\hat{x}=F_{C}^{-1}\left(\frac{1}{2}\right)=0\). The sample median is unbiased and the leading-order approximation of the variance reads, according to equation (19.15),

\[ \begin{equation*} \operatorname{var}(\breve{x})=\frac{\pi^{2}}{4 L} . \tag{19.17} \end{equation*} \]

The results are depicted in Figure 19.1. The variance diverges for \(L=1\), as it corresponds to that of the original PDF, but it is finite for \(L \geq 3\) and approaches rapidly the leadingorder approximation of equation (19.17). In addition to the analytic results, Figure 19.1 also contains the data obtained by a straightforward computer simulation. Random Cauchy samples of size \(L\) were generated and the corresponding medians determined. The variance of such a list of \(10^{6}\) median values is included in the figure. The perfect agreement corroborates the correctness of the analytic result.

Example: Exponential distribution

As another example we study the sample median of random numbers drawn from an exponential PDF

\[ \rho(x)=e^{-x} . \]

In this case, the sample median is biased, as the exponential function is obviously not symmetric. According to equation (7.17b) [p. 104], the CDF of the exponential PDF reads

\[ F_{e}(x \mid \lambda)=1-e^{-x}, \]

which has the inverse

\[ F_{e}^{-1}(f \mid \lambda)=-\ln (1-f) . \]

The median is therefore

\[ \hat{x}=F_{e}^{-1}\left(\frac{1}{2}\right)=\ln (2) . \]

At the location of the median the density is \(\rho_{e}(\hat{x})=\frac{1}{2}\) and the derivative reads \(\rho_{e}^{\prime}(\hat{x})=-\frac{1}{2}\). So, according to equation (19.15), the leading-order approximation of the variance of the sample median is

\[ \operatorname{var}(\breve{x})=\frac{1}{L} \]

As already mentioned, the sample median is biased and based on equation (19.13) it yields on average

\[ \langle\bar{x}\rangle=\hat{x}-\frac{-\frac{1}{2}}{8 L\left(\frac{1}{2}\right)^{3}}=\ln (2)+\frac{1}{2 L} . \]

Again, higher-order contributions in \(1 / L\) have been ignored. The results for the average sample median and its variance are depicted in Figure 19.2. For samples of finite size there is a systematic deviation from the intrinsic value of the median, which, however, disappears as \(1 / L\), while the standard error decreases only as \(1 / \sqrt{L}\).

19.2 Mean and variance of Gaussian samples

In the previous sections, we did not specify the PDF from which the independent random numbers are drawn. Owning to the omnipresence of Gaussian random numbers, in particular in conjunction with the central limit theorem, several hypothesis tests are based on normal distributions. The nice thing about this is that the sampling distribution can be given analytically.

Suppose we have samples of size \(N\) of i.i.n.d. random variables \(\left\{x_{n}\right\}\) with mean \(x_{0}\) and variance \(\sigma^{2}\). For these samples, we determine the sample mean

\[ s=\frac{1}{N} \sum_{n=1}^{N} x_{n} \]

and

\[ v=\sum_{n=1}^{N}\left(x_{n}-\bar{x}\right)^{2}, \]

which is proportional to the sample variance. We want to determine the distribution \(p\left(s, v \mid N, x_{0}, \sigma, \mathcal{I}\right)\) of \(s\) and \(v\). Instead of marginalizing over the samples \(\boldsymbol{x}\), we use the Helmert transform (see Section 7.10 [p. 136])

\[ \begin{equation*} p\left(s, v \mid x_{0}, \sigma, N, \mathcal{I}\right)=\int d^{N} \xi p\left(s, v \mid \xi, x_{0}, \sigma, N, \mathcal{I}\right) p\left(\xi \mid x_{0}, \sigma, N, \mathcal{I}\right) \tag{19.18} \end{equation*} \]

Given the Helmert-transformed sample \(\boldsymbol{\xi}, s\) and \(v\) are uniquely fixed and therefore expressed in terms of Dirac delta functions as

\[ p(s, v \mid \xi, \sigma, \mathcal{I})=\delta\left(s-\frac{\xi_{N}}{\sqrt{N}}\right) \delta\left(v-\eta^{2}\right) . \]

Apart from irrelevant prefactors, which we will determine at the end via normalization, we have

\[ \begin{aligned} p\left(s, v \mid x_{0}, \sigma, N, \mathcal{I}\right) \propto & \int d \xi_{N} d \eta^{N-1} \mathcal{N}(\eta \mid 0, \sigma) \mathcal{N}\left(\xi_{N} \mid \sqrt{N} x_{0}, \sigma\right) \ldots \\ & \ldots \delta\left(\xi_{N}-s \sqrt{N}\right) \delta\left(v-\eta^{2}\right) \\ \propto & \mathcal{N}\left(\sqrt{N} s \mid \sqrt{N} x_{0}, \sigma\right) \int d \eta \eta^{N-2} e^{-\frac{\eta^{2}}{\sigma^{2}}} \frac{\delta(\eta-\sqrt{v})}{2 \eta} \end{aligned} \]

For the last step we have introduced \((N-1)\)-dimensional spherical coordinates. The remaining integral yields

\[ \begin{align*} p\left(s, v \mid x_{0}, \sigma, N, \mathcal{I}\right) & =\mathcal{N}\left(s \mid x_{0}, \frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{N}}\right) \cdot \frac{1}{Z} v^{\frac{N-3}{2}} e^{-\frac{v}{\sigma^{2}}} \\ & =\mathcal{N}\left(s \mid x_{0}, \frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{N}}\right) \cdot p_{\Gamma}\left(v \left\lvert\, \alpha=\frac{N-1}{2}\right., \beta=\frac{1}{2 \sigma^{2}}\right) . \tag{19.19} \end{align*} \]

19.2.1 PDF of the sample mean

The PDF for the sample mean is given by the first factor of equation (19.19).

PDF FOR THE SAMPLE MEAN
OF I.I.N.D. RANDOM NUMBERS

sample: \(\quad\left\{x_{1}, \ldots, x_{N}\right\}\) drawn from \(\mathcal{N}\left(x \mid x_{0}, \sigma\right)\) parameters: \(\quad N, x_{0}, \sigma\) random variable: \(\quad s=\frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^{N} x_{i}\)

\[ \begin{equation*} p\left(s \mid x_{0}, \sigma, N, \mathcal{I}\right)=\frac{1}{\sqrt{2 \pi \sigma^{2} / N}} e^{-\frac{\left(s-x_{0}\right)^{2}}{2 \sigma^{2} / N}}=\mathcal{N}\left(s \mid x_{0}, \frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{N}}\right) . \tag{19.20} \end{equation*} \]

19.2.2 PDF of the sample variance, chi-squared statistic

The probability density function for the sample variance is obtained from the properly normalized first factor in equation (19.19).

PDF for the sample variance
FOR I.I.N.D. RANDOM VARIABLES

sample: \(\left\{x_{1}, \ldots, x_{N}\right\}\) drawn from \(\mathcal{N}\left(x \mid x_{0}, \sigma\right)\) parameters: \(N, x_{0}, \sigma\) random variable: \(\quad v=\sum_{n=1}^{N}\left(x_{n}-\bar{x}\right)^{2}\)

\[ \begin{align*} p\left(v \mid x_{0}, \sigma, N, \mathcal{I}\right) & =\frac{\left(2 \sigma^{2}\right)^{-\frac{N-1}{2}}}{\Gamma\left(\frac{N-1}{2}\right)} v^{\left(\frac{N-1}{2}-1\right)} e^{-\frac{v}{2 \sigma^{2}}} \\ & =p_{\Gamma}\left(v \left\lvert\, \alpha=\frac{N-1}{2}\right., \beta=\frac{1}{2 \sigma^{2}}\right) \tag{19.21} \end{align*} \]

Apparently, it is a Gamma distribution. The mean \(\langle v\rangle\) is of particular interest. It follows from equation (7.14c) [p. 102]:

\[ \langle v\rangle=\frac{\alpha}{\beta}=(N-1) \sigma^{2} . \]

The result corroborates that the unbiased estimator for the variance is normalized by \(N-1\), as discussed in Section 19.1.2 [p. 285]. In connection with the chi-squared distribution, it is common to use normalized random variables

\[ \tilde{x}_{n}=\frac{x_{n}}{\sigma} . \]

The sample variance \(v\) is transformed into

\[ z=\frac{v}{\sigma^{2}} . \]

The sampling distribution of the variance can be considered as a chi-squared distribution equation (7.16a) [p. 104] with \(N-1\) degrees of freedom:

\[ \begin{equation*} p\left(z \mid N, x_{0}, \sigma, \mathcal{I}\right)=p_{\chi^{2}}(z \mid N-1) . \tag{19.22} \end{equation*} \]

We started out with \(N\) degrees of freedom \(\left\{x_{n}\right\}\), but lost one as the definition of the sample variance contains the sample mean \(\bar{x}\) which had to be determined from the data.

In the above derivation we have assumed i.i.n.d. random numbers, that is, they were all drawn from the same normal PDF. This can easily be generalized, in order to make the result applicable to a wider range of problems. We assume that the random numbers are still independent and normally distributed, but no longer identically. In other words, each random variable is Gaussian with its own mean and variance. Next we define new shifted and scaled random variables

\[ \tilde{x}_{n}=\frac{x_{n}-x_{0, n}}{\sigma_{n}}, \]

which now all have one and the same normal \(\operatorname{PDF} \mathcal{N}\left(\tilde{x}_{n} \mid 0,1\right)\). The previous consideration implies that

\[ \begin{equation*} \chi^{2}=\sum_{n=1}^{N} \frac{\left(x_{n}-x_{0, n}\right)^{2}}{\sigma_{n}^{2}} \tag{19.23} \end{equation*} \]

has a chi-squared distribution. It is therefore called a chi-squared statistic.

Sampling distribution of the \(\boldsymbol{\chi}^{\mathbf{2}}\)-statistic

\[ \begin{align*} & \text { sample: } \\ & \text { parameters: } \\ & \text { test statistic: } \quad \begin{aligned} & \left\{x_{1}, \ldots, x_{N}\right\}, x_{i} \text { drawn from } \mathcal{N}\left(x_{i} \mid x_{0}^{i}, \sigma_{i}\right) \\ p\left(\chi^{2} \mid N, x_{0}^{1}, \ldots, x_{0}^{N}, \sigma_{1}, \ldots, \sigma_{N}, \sigma_{1}, \ldots, \sigma_{N}, \mathcal{I}\right) & =\frac{(2)^{-\frac{N}{2}}}{\Gamma\left(\frac{N}{2}\right)}\left(\chi^{2}\right)^{\left(\frac{N}{2}-1\right)} e^{-\chi^{2} / 2} \\ & =p_{\chi^{2}}\left(\chi^{2} \mid N\right) \end{aligned} \end{align*} \]

\(19.3 z\)-Statistic

We consider the situation with two samples \(X_{1}\) and \(X_{2}\) of size \(N_{1}\) and \(N_{2}\), respectively. The elements of the two samples are drawn independently from two normal distributions with individual variances \(\sigma_{1}^{2}\) and \(\sigma_{2}^{2}\). A null hypothesis in this context might be \(H\) : The two samples have the same mean. The \(z\)-statistic in this case is defined as \(\boldsymbol{z}\)-Statistic FOR THE DIFFERENCE IN MEANS

\[ \begin{align*} z & =\frac{\text { sample mean } 1-\text { sample mean } 2}{\text { standard error of difference of sample means }}, \\ z & =\frac{\bar{x}_{2}-\bar{x}_{1}}{\mathrm{SE}}, \\ \mathrm{SE}^{2} & =\frac{\sigma_{1}^{2}}{N_{1}}+\frac{\sigma_{2}^{2}}{N_{2}} . \tag{19.25} \end{align*} \]

This is precisely the sufficient statistic provided by the Bayesian approach to the question ‘Are the true means of two normal samples of sizes \(N_{1}\) and \(N_{2}\) the same, provided the corresponding variances are \(\sigma_{1}^{2}\) and \(\sigma_{2}^{2}\) ?’ We leave the proof as an exercise for the reader. The relevant quantities are the individual standard errors \(\mathrm{SE}_{\alpha}:=\frac{\sigma_{\alpha}}{\sqrt{N_{\alpha}}}\) and the resulting standard error of the difference of sample means of i.i.n.d. random variables, i.e. \(\mathrm{SE}^{2}= \mathrm{SE}_{1}^{2}+\mathrm{SE}_{2}^{2}\). For notational ease we use the abbreviations \(\mathbf{N}=\left\{N_{1}, N_{2}\right\}\) and \(\boldsymbol{\sigma}=\left\{\sigma_{1}, \sigma_{2}\right\}\). The PDF for \(z\) is obtained via the marginalization rule upon introducing the sample means:

\[ \begin{aligned} p(z \mid \mathbf{N}, \boldsymbol{\sigma}, \mathcal{I}) & =\int d \bar{x}_{1} d \bar{x}_{2} p\left(z \mid \bar{x}_{1}, \bar{x}_{2}, \mathbf{N}, \boldsymbol{\sigma}, \mathcal{I}\right) p\left(\bar{x}_{1}, \bar{x}_{2} \mid \mathbf{N}, \boldsymbol{\sigma}, \mathcal{I}\right) \\ & =\int d \bar{x}_{1} d \bar{x}_{2} \delta\left(z-\frac{\bar{x}_{2}-\bar{x}_{1}}{\mathrm{SE}}\right) p\left(\bar{x}_{1} \mid N_{1}, \sigma_{1}, \mathcal{I}\right) p\left(\bar{x}_{2} \mid N_{2}, \sigma_{2}, \mathcal{I}\right) \\ & =\sigma \int d \bar{x}_{1} p\left(\bar{x}_{1} \mid N_{1}, \sigma_{1}, \mathcal{I}\right) p\left(\bar{x}_{2}=\bar{x}_{1}+z \mathrm{SE} \mid N_{2}, \sigma_{2}, \mathcal{I}\right) \end{aligned} \]

Now we introduce the common mean \(x_{0}\) of the underlying normal PDFs. Furthermore, we suppress the prefactor and adjust it at the end. Moreover, we use equation (19.20) for the PDF of a sample mean:

\[ \begin{aligned} p(z \mid \mathbf{N}, \boldsymbol{\sigma}, \mathcal{I}) \propto & \int d x_{0} p\left(x_{0} \mid \mathcal{I}\right) \cdots \\ & \times \int d \bar{x}_{1 p} p\left(\bar{x}_{1} \mid N_{1}, \sigma_{1}, x_{0}, \mathcal{I}\right) p\left(\bar{x}_{2}=\bar{x}_{1}+z \mathrm{SE} \mid N_{2}, \sigma_{2}, x_{0}, \mathcal{I}\right) \\ \propto & \int d x_{0} p\left(x_{0} \mid \mathcal{I}\right) \int d \bar{x}_{1} e^{-\frac{1}{2}\left(\frac{\left(\bar{x}_{1}-x_{0}\right)^{2}}{\sigma_{1}^{2} / N_{1}}+\frac{\left(\bar{x}_{1}+z \mathrm{SE}-x_{0}\right)^{2}}{\sigma_{2}^{2} / N_{2}}\right)} \\ \propto & \underbrace{\int d x_{0} p\left(x_{0} \mid \mathcal{I}\right)}_{=1} \int d s_{1} e^{-\frac{1}{2}\left(\frac{s_{1}^{2}}{\sigma_{1}^{2} / N_{1}}+\frac{\left(s_{1}+z \mathrm{SE}\right)^{2}}{\sigma_{2}^{2} / N_{2}}\right)} \end{aligned} \]

In the remaining integral the argument of the exponential by completing the square w.r.t. \(s_{1}\) results in an expression of the form

\[ \left(\frac{s_{1}^{2}}{\sigma_{1}^{2} / N_{1}}+\frac{\left(s_{1}+z \mathrm{SE}\right)^{2}}{\sigma_{2}^{2} / N_{2}}\right)=C\left(s_{1}-s_{1}^{*}\right)^{2}+z^{2} . \]

The integral over \(s_{1}\) just modifies the prefactor and we obtain

\[ p(z \mid \mathbf{N}, \boldsymbol{\sigma}, \mathcal{I}) \propto e^{-\frac{1}{2} z^{2}}, \]

and the sought-for result is \(\boldsymbol{z}\)-Statistic FOR THE DIFFERENCE IN MEANS

\[ \begin{array}{ll} \text { sample 1: } & \left\{x_{1}^{1}, \ldots, x_{N_{1}}^{1}\right\} \text { drawn from } \mathcal{N}\left(x \mid x_{0}, \sigma_{1}\right) \\ \text { sample 2: } & \left\{x_{1}^{2}, \ldots, x_{N_{2}}^{2}\right\} \text { drawn from } \mathcal{N}\left(x \mid x_{0}, \sigma_{2}\right) \\ \text { given: } & N_{1}, N_{2}, \sigma_{1}, \sigma_{2} \\ \text { test statistic: } & z=\frac{\bar{x}_{2}-\bar{x}_{1}}{\mathrm{SE}} ; \quad \mathrm{SE}^{2}=\frac{\sigma_{1}^{2}}{N_{1}}+\frac{\sigma_{2}^{2}}{N_{2}} \\ & p_{z}(z)=\frac{1}{\sqrt{2 \pi}} e^{-z^{2} / 2}=\mathcal{N}(z \mid \mu=0, \sigma=1) . \tag{19.26} \end{array} \]

It appears that the quantities \(N_{\alpha}\) and \(\sigma_{\alpha}\) do not enter the PDF of \(z\), they are merely required for the calculation of \(z\). Moreover, the test statistic in equation (19.25) as well as the corresponding PDF are independent of \(x_{0}\) and we never had to specify the prior PDF for \(x_{0}\), merely its normalization mattered. It corresponds to a null hypothesis: \(H_{0}\) : The means of two normal samples of given variances are the same. An alternative hypothesis, also leading to a \(z\)-statistic, is \(H_{0}\) : The true mean \(\mu\) of a normal sample of given variance is \(\mu_{0}\). \(\boldsymbol{z}\)-Statistic FOR A PREDICTED \(\boldsymbol{\mu}_{\mathbf{0}}\) sample: \(\quad\left\{x_{1}, \ldots, x_{N}\right\}\) drawn from \(\mathcal{N}\left(x \mid x_{0}, \sigma\right)\) given: \(\quad N, \sigma\) test statistic: \(\quad z=\frac{\bar{x}-\mu_{0}}{\mathrm{SE}} ; \quad \mathrm{SE}^{2}=\frac{\sigma^{2}}{N}\)

\[ \begin{equation*} p_{z}(z)=\frac{1}{\sqrt{2 \pi}} e^{-z^{2} / 2}=\mathcal{N}(z \mid \mu=0, \sigma=1) . \tag{19.27} \end{equation*} \]

This is a special version of the previous case for \(\sigma_{2} \rightarrow 0\). It implies \(\bar{x}_{2}=\mu_{0}\). The rest is obvious and consequently \(z\) in this case is also distributed as \(\mathcal{N}(z \mid \mu=0, \sigma=1)\).

19.4 Student’s \(t\)-statistic

Next we generalize the previous discussion towards an unknown variance. The random variables of one sample are still independently and normally distributed with common mean and variance, but the variance is not at our disposal.

19.4.1 Student’s \(\boldsymbol{t}\)-test for a predicted mean

We start out with the situation where a Gaussian sample is given of unknown variance. This situation has already been examined in the Bayesian framework in Section 17.2.3 [p. 266]. The null hypothesis in this case reads \(H_{0}\) : The true mean \(\mu\) of a normal sample of unknown variance is \(\mu_{0}\). The suitable test statistic as depicted in equation (17.11) [p. 266] reads

\(\boldsymbol{t}\)-Statistic
FOR A PREDICTED \(\boldsymbol{\mu}_{\mathbf{0}}\)

\[ \begin{align*} t & :=\frac{\bar{x}-\mu_{0}}{\mathrm{SE}^{*}} \tag{19.28}\\ \mathrm{SE}^{* 2} & :=\frac{\overline{(\Delta x)^{2}}}{(N-1)} \end{align*} \]

In order to derive the sampling distribution we introduce - via the marginalization rule - the missing values of the sample mean, the sample variance \(v:=N \overline{(\Delta x)^{2}}\) and the intrinsic standard deviation \(\sigma\). As several times before, we suppress prefactors that can be determined easily afterwards:

\[ \begin{aligned} p\left(t \mid N, \mu_{0}, \mathcal{I}\right)= & \iiint d \sigma d \bar{x} d v p\left(t \mid \bar{x}, v, \sigma, N, \mu_{0}, \mathcal{I}\right) p\left(\bar{x}, v, \sigma \mid N, \mu_{0}, \mathcal{I}\right) \\ = & \iiint d \sigma d \bar{x} d v \delta\left(t-\frac{\sqrt{N(N-1)}\left(\bar{x}-\mu_{0}\right)}{\sqrt{v}}\right) \ldots \\ & \ldots \underbrace{p\left(\bar{x} \mid \sigma, N, \mu_{0}, \mathcal{I}\right)}_{=\mathcal{N}\left(\bar{x} \mid \mu_{0}, \frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{N}}\right)} \underbrace{p\left(v \mid \sigma, N, \mu_{0}, \mathcal{I}\right)}_{=p_{\Gamma}\left(v \left\lvert\, \frac{N-1}{2}\right., \frac{1}{2 \sigma^{2}}\right)} p\left(\sigma \mid N, \mu_{0}, \mathcal{I}\right) \end{aligned} \]

Next it is expedient to introduce new variables \(s:=\left(\bar{x}-\mu_{0}\right) / \sigma\) and \(w:=v / \sigma^{2}\) in which the PDFs are

\[ \begin{align*} \mathcal{N}\left(\bar{x} \mid \mu_{0}, \frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{N}}\right) \stackrel{(19.20)}{=} \mathcal{N}\left(s \mid 0, \frac{1}{\sqrt{N}}\right) \tag{19.29}\\ p_{\Gamma}\left(v \left\lvert\, \frac{N-1}{2}\right., \frac{1}{2 \sigma^{2}}\right) d v \stackrel{(19.21)}{=} p_{\Gamma}\left(w \left\lvert\, \frac{N-1}{2}\right., \frac{1}{2}\right) d w \tag{19.30} \end{align*} \]

We obtain

\[ \begin{aligned} p\left(t \mid N, \mu_{0}, \mathcal{I}\right)= & \iint d s d w \delta\left(t-\frac{\sqrt{N(N-1)} s}{\sqrt{w}}\right) \cdots \\ & \ldots \mathcal{N}\left(s \mid 0, \frac{1}{\sqrt{N}}\right) p_{\Gamma}\left(w \left\lvert\, \frac{N-1}{2}\right., \frac{1}{2}\right) \underbrace{\int d \sigma p\left(\sigma \mid N, \mu_{0}, \mathcal{I}\right)}_{=1} \\ \propto & \iint d s d w \sqrt{w} \delta\left(s-\sqrt{w} \frac{t}{\sqrt{N(N-1)}}\right) \cdots \\ & \ldots \mathcal{N}\left(s \mid 0, \frac{1}{\sqrt{N}}\right) p_{\Gamma}\left(w \left\lvert\, \frac{N-1}{2}\right., \frac{1}{2}\right) \\ \propto & \int d w \sqrt{w} \mathcal{N}\left(\left.\sqrt{w} \frac{t}{\sqrt{N(N-1)}} \right\rvert\, 0, \frac{1}{\sqrt{N}}\right) p_{\Gamma}\left(w \left\lvert\, \frac{N-1}{2}\right., \frac{1}{2}\right) \end{aligned} \]

\[ \begin{aligned} & \propto \int d w \sqrt{w} e^{-\frac{1}{2} \frac{w t^{2}}{(N-1)}} w^{\frac{N-3}{2}} e^{-\frac{w}{2}} \\ & \propto \int \frac{d w}{w} w^{\frac{N}{2}} e^{-\frac{w}{2}\left(1+\frac{t^{2}}{N-1}\right)} \end{aligned} \]

Finally, the substitution \(w\left(1+\frac{t^{2}}{N-1}\right)=y\) yields the desired result

\[ p\left(t \mid N, \mu_{0}, \mathcal{I}\right) \propto \int \frac{d y}{y} y^{\frac{N}{2}} e^{-\frac{y}{2}}\left(1+\frac{t^{2}}{N-1}\right)^{-\frac{N}{2}} \propto\left(1+\frac{t^{2}}{N-1}\right)^{-\frac{N}{2}} \]

in the form of the Student \(t\)-distribution, already introduced in equation (7.31a) [p. 109].

Sampling distribution of Student’s \(\boldsymbol{t}\)-statistic FOR PREDICTED MEAN \(\boldsymbol{\mu}_{\mathbf{0}}\)

sample: \(\quad\left\{x_{1}, \ldots, x_{N}\right\} \quad\) drawn from \(\mathcal{N}\left(x \mid \mu_{0}, \sigma\right)\) given: \(\quad N, \mu_{0}\) test statistic: \(\quad t=\frac{\bar{x}-\mu_{0}}{\mathrm{SE}^{*}} ; \quad \mathrm{SE}^{* 2}:=\frac{\overline{(\Delta x)^{2}}}{(N-1)}\)

\[ \begin{align*} p\left(t \mid N, \mu_{0}, \mathcal{I}\right) & =p_{t}(t \mid v=N-1) \\ p_{t}(t \mid v) & :=\frac{\Gamma\left(\frac{v+1}{2}\right)}{\Gamma\left(\frac{v}{2}\right) \sqrt{\pi v}}\left(1+\frac{t^{2}}{v}\right)^{-\frac{v+1}{2}} \tag{19.31} \end{align*} \]

19.4.2 Student’s \(\boldsymbol{t}\)-test for the difference in sample means

We generalize the previous analysis to test the hypothesis \(H_{0}\) : The means of two normal samples of common yet unknown variance are the same. According to equation (17.20) [p. 270] and equation (17.19) [p. 269], a sensible test statistic is

\(\boldsymbol{t}\)-Statistic

FOR THE DIFFERENCE IN MEANS

\[ \begin{align*} t & :=\frac{\bar{x}_{2}-\bar{x}_{1}}{\mathrm{SE}_{\mathrm{diff}}^{*}} \\ \mathrm{SE}_{\mathrm{diff}}^{*} & :=\frac{N}{(N-2) N_{1} N_{2}} \sum_{\alpha} N_{\alpha} \overline{\left(\Delta x_{\alpha}\right)^{2}} . \tag{19.32} \end{align*} \]

The derivation of the sampling distribution is outlined in Appendix B. 3 [p. 615]. The result is that \(t\) is distributed as \(p_{\Gamma}(t \mid v)\), with \(v=N-2\). Remarkably, the result is again independent of the prior PDF for the true \(\sigma\).

19.5 Fisher-Snedecor \(F\)-statistic

The last test statistic we want to investigate is the so-called Fisher-Snedecor \(F\)-statistic. As before we start out with two independent samples of size \(N_{1}\) and \(N_{2}\), respectively, which are drawn from individual normal distributions. The means of the two distributions are not specified and we wish to analyse whether the variances are the same.

In the Bayesian analysis equation (17.29) [p. 275] we have already encountered the sufficient statistic for this problem, namely

\[ \begin{align*} f & =\frac{{\tilde{\sigma_{1}}}^{2}}{{\tilde{\sigma_{2}}}^{2}}=\frac{n_{2}}{n_{1}} \frac{v_{1}}{v_{2}} \tag{19.33}\\ \text { with: } \quad n_{\alpha} & :=N_{\alpha}-1 . \tag{19.34} \end{align*} \]

The sought-for sampling distribution again follows from the marginalization rule:

\[ \begin{aligned} p\left(f \mid N_{1}, N_{2}, \mathcal{I}\right)= & \int d \sigma d v_{1} d v_{2} p\left(f \mid v_{1}, v_{2}, N_{1}, N_{2}, \mathcal{I}\right) p\left(v_{1}, v_{2}, \sigma \mid N_{1}, N_{2}, \mathcal{I}\right) \\ = & \int d \sigma p\left(\sigma \mid N_{1}, N_{2}, \mathcal{I}\right) \\ & \ldots \iint d v_{1} d v_{2} \delta\left(f-\frac{n_{2}}{n_{1}} \frac{v_{1}}{v_{2}}\right) \underbrace{p\left(v_{1} \mid \sigma, N_{1}, \mathcal{I}\right)}_{p_{\Gamma}\left(v_{1} \left\lvert\, \frac{n_{1}}{2}\right., \frac{1}{2 \sigma^{2}}\right)} \underbrace{p\left(v_{2} \mid \sigma, N_{2}, \mathcal{I}\right)}_{p_{\Gamma}\left(v_{2} \left\lvert\, \frac{n_{2}}{2}\right., \frac{1}{2 \sigma^{2}}\right)} . \end{aligned} \]

As before, we introduce the new variables \(w_{\alpha}:=\frac{v_{\alpha}}{\sigma^{2}}\). Together with equation (19.30) we obtain

\[ \begin{aligned} & p\left(f \mid N_{1}, N_{2}, \mathcal{I}\right) \propto \overbrace{\int d \sigma p\left(\sigma \mid N_{1}, N_{2}, \mathcal{I}\right)}^{=1} \iint d w_{1} d w_{2} w_{2} \delta\left(w_{1}-\frac{n_{1}}{n_{2}} f w_{2}\right) \\ & \ldots \cdot p_{\Gamma}\left(w_{1} \left\lvert\, \frac{n_{1}}{2}\right., \frac{1}{2}\right) p_{\Gamma}\left(w_{2} \left\lvert\, \frac{n_{2}}{2}\right., \frac{1}{2}\right) \\ & \propto \int d w_{2} w_{2} p_{\Gamma}\left(\left.\frac{n_{1}}{n_{2}} f w_{2} \right\rvert\, \frac{n_{1}}{2}, \frac{1}{2}\right) p_{\Gamma}\left(w_{2} \left\lvert\, \frac{n_{2}}{2}\right., \frac{1}{2}\right) \\ & \propto \int d w_{2} w_{2}\left(\left(\frac{n_{1}}{n_{2}} f w_{2}\right)^{\frac{n_{1}-2}{2}} e^{-\frac{1}{2} \frac{n_{1}}{n_{2}} f w_{2}}\right)\left(w_{2}^{\frac{n_{2}-2}{2}} e^{-\frac{w_{2}}{2}}\right) \\ & \propto f^{\frac{n_{1}-2}{2}} \int \frac{d w_{2}}{w_{2}} w_{2}^{\frac{n_{1}+n_{2}}{2}} e^{-\frac{w_{2}}{2}\left(1+\frac{n_{1}}{n_{2}} f\right)} \end{aligned} \]

The remaining result is solved by the substitution \(w_{2}\left(1+\frac{n_{1}}{n_{2}} f\right):=y\) :

\[ \begin{aligned} p\left(f \mid N_{1}, N_{2}, \mathcal{I}\right) & \propto f^{\frac{n_{1}-2}{2}}\left(1+\frac{n_{1}}{n_{2}} f\right)^{-\frac{n_{1}+n_{2}}{2}}\left[\int \frac{d y}{y} y^{\frac{n_{1}+n_{2}}{2}} e^{-\frac{y}{2}}\right] \\ & \propto f^{\frac{n_{2}-2}{2}}\left(n_{2}+n_{1} f\right)^{-\frac{n_{1}+n_{2}}{2}} \end{aligned} \]

Together with the correct normalization which follows from elementary integration, we obtain

\[ \begin{equation*} p_{F}\left(f \mid n_{1}, n_{2}\right)=\frac{n_{1}^{n_{1}} n_{2}^{n_{2}}}{B\left(\frac{n_{1}}{2} \frac{n_{2}}{2}\right)} f^{\frac{n_{1}}{2}-1}\left(n_{2}+n_{1} f\right)^{-\frac{n_{1}+n_{2}}{2}} \tag{19.35} \end{equation*} \]

We see again that the prior for \(\sigma\) is not needed for the sampling distribution of the \(F\)-statistic. Qualitatively, the curves of the \(F\)-distribution are bell-shaped like the chi-squared distribution. The mean and variance have already been discussed in equation (19.35). In particular, the mean is

\[ \langle f\rangle=\frac{N_{2}-1}{N_{2}-3} \underset{N_{2} \rightarrow \infty}{\longrightarrow} 1 . \]

It is always greater than 1 and does not depend on \(N_{1}\), which might appear odd at first glance. On second thoughts, however, it appears quite reasonable, since the sample variances \(v_{\alpha}\) are independently chi-squared distributed:

\[ \langle f\rangle=\frac{n_{2}}{n_{1}}\left\langle\frac{v_{1}}{v_{2}}\right\rangle=\frac{\left\langle v_{1}\right\rangle}{n_{1}}\left\langle\frac{n_{2}}{v_{2}}\right\rangle . \]

The chi-squared or rather \(\Gamma\) distribution of \(v_{\sigma}\) is, according to equation (19.21) [p. 295], \(p_{\Gamma}\left(v \left\lvert\, \frac{n_{\alpha}}{2}\right., \frac{1}{2 \sigma^{2}}\right)\) and its mean is given by equation (7.14c) [p. 102], i.e.

\[ \frac{\left\langle v_{\alpha}\right\rangle}{n_{\alpha}}=\sigma^{2} . \]

Thus the mean of \(v_{1} / n_{1}\) yields the true variance. However, the nonlinear transformation \(1 / v_{2}\) introduces a bias since

\[ \left\langle\frac{1}{v_{2}}\right\rangle \neq \frac{1}{\left\langle v_{2}\right\rangle}, \]

except for \(N_{2} \rightarrow \infty\). The sampling distribution of the \(F\)-statistic is depicted in Figure 19.4 for different numbers of degrees of freedom. We can observe that the PDF gets ever narrower with increasing \(n_{2}\) and will finally end as a delta peak at \(f=1\).

19.6 Chi-squared in case of missing parameters

The chi-squared statistic has already been touched upon in Section 19.2.2 [p. 295], where all sample elements had a common mean. Here we want to generalize this to prepare the ground for hypothesis testing. To this end we consider a linear model

\[ \begin{equation*} \boldsymbol{g}^{(0)}=\mathbf{M} \boldsymbol{a}^{(0)}, \tag{19.37} \end{equation*} \]

where \(\boldsymbol{a}\) is an \(N\)-component column vector comprising the model parameters. The \(N_{d} \times N\) matrix \(\mathbf{M}\) represents the model, and \(\boldsymbol{g}\) stands for the \(N_{d}\)-component column vector of data values. The superscript ( 0 ) in \(\boldsymbol{g}^{(0)}\) indicates the underlying true data vector. The basic assumption (null hypothesis) is that the model correctly describes the data. Hence, there is an underlying true set of parameters, denoted by \(\boldsymbol{a}^{(0)}\). The actually measured values \(\boldsymbol{g}\), however, are distorted by a noise vector \(\boldsymbol{\eta}\) :

\[ g=g^{(0)}+\eta . \]

Needless to say that the noise vector is unknown, but we suppose that it is distributed as a multivariate normal with zero mean:

\[ p(\boldsymbol{\eta} \mid \mathbf{C})=\frac{1}{Z} \exp \left\{\boldsymbol{\eta}^{T} \mathbf{C}^{-1} \boldsymbol{\eta}\right\} . \]

The normalization \(Z\) is immaterial for the ensuing discussion. Here we even allow for correlations in the measurements, described by the covariance matrix \(\mathbf{C}\). The likelihood therefore reads

\[ \begin{aligned} p(\boldsymbol{g} \mid \boldsymbol{a}, \mathbf{M}, \mathbf{C}) & =\frac{e^{\chi^{2}}}{Z} \\ \chi^{2} & :=(\boldsymbol{g}-\mathbf{M} \boldsymbol{a})^{T} \mathbf{C}^{-1}(\boldsymbol{g}-\mathbf{M} \boldsymbol{a}) . \end{aligned} \]

The notation simplifies considerably if we introduce the following transformations upfront:

\[ \begin{aligned} \boldsymbol{g} & \rightarrow \tilde{\mathbf{g}}:=\mathbf{C}^{-1 / 2} \boldsymbol{g}, \\ \mathbf{M} & \rightarrow \tilde{\mathbf{M}}:=\mathbf{C}^{-1 / 2} \mathbf{M}, \\ \mathbf{C} & \rightarrow \mathbf{1} . \end{aligned} \]

Henceforward, we suppress the tilde. Then equation (19.37) still holds and the loglikelihood turns into

\[ \begin{equation*} \chi^{2}=(g-\mathbf{M} a)^{T}(g-\mathbf{M} a) . \tag{19.38} \end{equation*} \]

As an example, the model shall be an expansion

\[ g_{v}^{(0)}=\sum_{n=1}^{N} a_{n} \Phi_{n}\left(x_{v}\right) \]

in a suitable set of basis functions \(\Phi_{n}(x)\), not necessarily orthonormal. It is not even required that the functions are linearly independent. Then \(M_{v n}=\Phi_{n}\left(x_{v}\right)\). A simple common example would be a quadratic function

\[ g_{v}=a_{1}+a_{2} x_{v}+a_{3} x_{v}^{2}, \]

and \(x_{\nu}\) is the value of a control parameter of the experiment. Usually, some or all model parameters are unknown and have to be inferred from the data. Firstly, we split off that part of the model that contains predetermined parameters and subtract it from the data. This defines a new equation of the form of equation (19.37) in which now all model parameters are unknown. If all parameters are predetermined, the right-hand side is \(\mathbf{0}\). Formally, we can use an \(N_{d} \times 1\) model matrix \(\mathbf{M}=\mathbf{0}\).

In the frame of frequentist statistics, unknown model parameters are fixed by maximum likelihood (see Chapter 15 [p. 236]). The root of the gradient of equation (19.38) yields the ML estimate for the parameters

\[ \begin{equation*} a^{\mathrm{ML}}=\left(\mathbf{M}^{T} \mathbf{M}\right)^{-1} \mathbf{M}^{T} g . \tag{19.39} \end{equation*} \]

As a matter of fact, this is the solution of the linear regression model that will be discussed in more detail in Chapter 21 [p. 333]. The data vector corresponding to the ML solution is then

\[ \boldsymbol{g}^{\mathbf{M L}}:=\boldsymbol{M} \boldsymbol{a}^{\mathbf{M L}}=\underbrace{\boldsymbol{M}\left(\mathbf{M}^{T} \mathbf{M}\right)^{-1} \mathbf{M}^{T}}_{:=\mathbb{P}} \boldsymbol{g}=\mathbb{P} \boldsymbol{g} . \]

We have introduced the matrix \(\mathbb{P}\), which is a projection matrix/operator, because \(\mathbb{P}^{2}=\mathbb{P}\). Applying the projection operator to the true data vector,

\[ \begin{equation*} \mathbb{P} g^{(0)}=\mathbb{P} \mathbf{M} a^{(0)}=M\left(\mathbf{M}^{T} \mathbf{M}\right)^{-1} \mathbf{M}^{T} \mathbf{M} a^{(0)}=\mathbf{M} a^{(0)}=g^{(0)} \tag{19.40} \end{equation*} \]

reveals that \(\mathbb{P}\) projects into the subspace

\[ \begin{equation*} V_{\mathbb{P}}=\{\boldsymbol{g} \mid \boldsymbol{g}=\mathbf{M} \boldsymbol{a} ; \forall \boldsymbol{a}\} \tag{19.41} \end{equation*} \]

spanned by the data vectors accessible by the model. So \(\boldsymbol{g}^{\mathrm{ML}}\) can be understood as a projection of the measured data into \(V_{\mathbb{P}}\). The dimension \(E\) of \(V_{\mathbb{P}}\) is given by the number of linearly independent vectors in \(\mathbf{M}\). Next we compute the difference vector that enters the misfit:

\[ \boldsymbol{g}-\mathbf{M} a^{\mathrm{ML}}=(\mathbf{1}-\mathbb{P}) \boldsymbol{g}:=\mathbb{Q} \boldsymbol{g} . \]

It depends on the projection operator \(\mathbb{Q}=\mathbf{1}-\mathbb{P}\), which projects into the subspace \(V_{\mathbb{Q}}\) orthogonal to \(V_{\mathbb{P}}\). The misfit

\[ \chi^{2}(g):=g^{T} \mathbf{Q}^{T} \mathbf{Q} g \]

is apparently determined by the components of the data vector that cannot be reached by the model. The measured data are distributed as

\[ p\left(\boldsymbol{g} \mid \boldsymbol{g}^{(0)}, \mathbf{C}, \mathcal{I}\right)=\frac{1}{Z^{\prime \prime}} \exp \left\{\left(\boldsymbol{g}-\boldsymbol{g}^{(0)}\right)^{T}\left(\boldsymbol{g}-\boldsymbol{g}^{(0)}\right)\right\} . \]

Once again, \(Z^{\prime \prime}\) is an unimportant normalization factor. The noise vector \(\boldsymbol{\eta}=\boldsymbol{g}-\boldsymbol{g}^{(0)}\) shall be split into the subspaces \(V_{\mathbb{P}}\) and \(V_{\mathbb{Q}}\) as follows:

\[ \boldsymbol{g}-\boldsymbol{g}^{(0)}=(\mathbb{Q}+\mathbb{P})\left(\boldsymbol{g}-\boldsymbol{g}^{(0)}\right)=\mathbb{Q} \boldsymbol{g}+\mathbb{P}\left(\boldsymbol{g}-\boldsymbol{g}^{(0)}\right) \]

In the last step we have used equation (19.40). Moreover, we have the following orthogonality:

\[ \mathbb{Q}^{T} \mathbb{P}=\mathbb{P}-\mathbf{M}\left(\mathbf{M}^{T} \mathbf{M}\right)^{-1} \mathbf{M}^{T} \mathbf{M}\left(\mathbf{M}^{T} \mathbf{M}\right)^{-1} \mathbf{M}^{T}=\mathbb{P}-\mathbb{P}=0 \]

Together with equation (19.40), it allows us to write

\[ \left(\boldsymbol{g}-\boldsymbol{g}^{(0)}\right)^{T}\left(\boldsymbol{g}-\boldsymbol{g}^{(0)}\right)=(\mathbb{Q} \boldsymbol{g})^{T}(\mathbb{Q} \boldsymbol{g})+\left(\mathbb{P} \boldsymbol{g}-\boldsymbol{g}^{(0)}\right)^{T}\left(\mathbb{P} \boldsymbol{g}-\boldsymbol{g}^{(0)}\right) \]

Now we are prepared to compute the sampling distribution for the misfit through the marginalization rule

\[ p\left(\chi^{2} \mid \mathbf{C}, \boldsymbol{g}^{(0)}\right) \propto \int d \boldsymbol{g}^{N_{d}} \delta\left(\chi^{2}-\chi^{2}(\boldsymbol{g})\right) e^{-\frac{1}{2}(\mathbb{Q} \boldsymbol{g})^{T}(\mathbb{Q} \boldsymbol{g})} e^{-\frac{1}{2}\left(\mathbb{P} \boldsymbol{g}-\boldsymbol{g}^{(0)}\right)^{T}\left(\mathbb{P} \boldsymbol{g}-\boldsymbol{g}^{(0)}\right)} \]

The integration factors into independent integrals over \(V_{\mathbb{P}}\) and \(V_{\mathbb{Q}}\), respectively. The former just modifies the prefactor. It also shows that the sampling distribution is independent of the true data vector \(\boldsymbol{d}^{(0)}\). The remaining integral over the vectors in \(V_{\mathbb{Q}}\) of dimension \(n:=N_{d}-E\) has the structure

\[ p\left(\chi^{2} \mid \mathbf{C}, \boldsymbol{g}^{(0)}\right) \propto \int d x^{n} \delta\left(\chi^{2}-|\boldsymbol{x}|^{2}\right) e^{-\frac{1}{2}|\boldsymbol{x}|^{2}} \propto\left(\chi^{2}\right)^{\frac{n}{2}-1} e^{-\frac{1}{2} \chi^{2}} . \]

We summarize the result:

Distribution of the CHi-Squared statistic IN CASE OF MISSING PARAMETERS OF A LINEAR MODEL

\[ \begin{array}{ll} \text { model: } & \boldsymbol{g}=\mathbf{M} \boldsymbol{a} ; \quad \mathbf{M}: N_{d} \times N \text { matrix } \\ \text { additive noise: } & \boldsymbol{\eta} \text { drawn from } \mathcal{N}(\boldsymbol{\eta} \mid \mathbf{0}, \mathbf{C}) \\ \text { statistic: } & \chi^{2}:=(\boldsymbol{g}-\mathbf{M} \boldsymbol{a})^{T} \mathbf{C}^{-1}(\boldsymbol{g}-\mathbf{M} \boldsymbol{a}) \\ E: & \begin{array}{l} \text { number of linearly independent columns } \\ \text { in } \mathbf{M} \text {, associated with unknown parameters } \end{array} \\ & p\left(\chi^{2}\right)=p_{\chi^{2}}\left(\chi^{2} \mid n=N_{d}-E\right) . \end{array} \]

In view of equation (19.39), each parameter corresponds to a particular linear combination of the data. The latter in turn does not contribute to the misfit. That is why each linearly ‘independent model parameter’ reduces the number of degrees of freedom by one.

19.7 Common hypothesis tests

19.7.1 General procedure

In the introductory part we have outlined the ideas of the two frequentist approaches to hypothesis testing. In this part we limit the discussion to Fisherian significance tests. Up to now we have only considered simple hypotheses in which all parameters are fixed by the null hypothesis. In the case of a compound hypothesis, there are still open parameters. For example, when investigating some crystals with regard to their conductivity, the null hypothesis could be

\[ H_{0}: \text { The material has an ohmic resistance. } \]

In other words, the voltage is proportional to the current:

\[ U \propto I . \]

The proportionality constant (resistance) is the unknown parameter. The standard approach in dealing with such nuisance parameters in the frequentist approach is to fix the unknown parameters by the maximum likelihood estimator. The number of degrees of freedom, originally the number of data, is reduced by the number of estimated parameters, as these are inferred from the sample.

General procedure for significance tests

  1. Define the null hypothesis.
  2. Choose the test statistic \(x\) with sampling distribution \(p_{x}(x)\).
  3. Determine the ML estimator for the nuisance parameters \(\theta\).
  4. Define the significance level (e.g. \(\alpha=0.05\) ).
  5. Determine the rejection region \(\mathcal{R}_{\alpha}\), either one- or two-sided:

\[ \int_{\mathcal{R}_{\alpha}} p_{x}(x \mid \boldsymbol{\theta}) d x \]

  1. Evaluate the test statistic \(x=x_{0}\) for the measured sample.
  2. If \(x_{0} \in \mathcal{R}_{\alpha}\) reject the null hypothesis.

Next we will discuss the most common significance test in more detail and give some basic examples.

19.7.2 z-Tests

Is the mass correctly predicted?

Presumably, one of the simplest types of problem for significance testing is the following: The mass of a particle is measured repeatedly and the data are taken from a sample \(\boldsymbol{m}= \left\{m_{1}, \ldots, m_{N}\right\}\). The statistical errors of the weighing process are additive, uncorrelated and Gaussian with variance \(\sigma^{2}\).

The null hypothesis claims the true value of the mass is \(m_{0}\). So we are dealing with a simple null hypothesis and \(z\) is again the appropriate test statistic, with

\[ \begin{aligned} z & =\frac{\bar{m}-m_{0}}{\mathrm{SE}}, \\ \mathrm{SE} & =\frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{N}} \end{aligned} \]

The sampling distribution is the centred, unit variance normal distribution, i.e. \(\mathcal{N}(z, 0,1)\). We decide on a two-tailed test as large deviations from \(z=0\) in both directions indicate a discrepancy between null hypothesis and measurement. We choose a significance level \(\alpha\) and adjust the rejection region such that the probability mass in both tails is \(\alpha / 2\), i.e.

\[ \int_{z_{1}^{\mathrm{cr}}}^{\infty} \mathcal{N}(z, 0,1)=\int_{-\infty}^{z_{2}^{\mathrm{cr}}} \mathcal{N}(z, 0,1)=\alpha / 2 \]

By symmetry, \(z_{2}^{\mathrm{cr}}=-z_{2}^{\mathrm{cr}}:=-z^{\mathrm{cr}}\) and

\[ \int_{-\infty}^{-z^{\mathrm{cr}}} \mathcal{N}(z, 0,1)=\frac{1}{2} \operatorname{Erfc}\left(\frac{z^{\mathrm{cr}}}{\sqrt{2}}\right) \stackrel{!}{=} \frac{\alpha}{2} . \]

Hence, \(z^{\mathrm{cr}}=\sqrt{2} \operatorname{InvErfc}(\alpha)\), with ‘Erfc’ being the complementary error function and ‘InvErfc’ its inverse. For the two values \(\alpha=0.05(\alpha=0.01)\), recommended by Fisher, we obtain \(z^{\mathrm{cr}}=1.960\left(z^{\mathrm{cr}}=2.575\right)\). If the difference \(\left|\bar{m}-m_{0}\right|\) is greater than \(z^{\mathrm{cr}}\). SE the data are called significant, since the measurement differs significantly from the null hypothesis by an amount that can hardly be explained by chance alone. And therefore the hypothesis is to be rejected.

Does the control variable have an impact?

We claim that a control parameter \(S\) of an experiment does not affect the measurement of quantity \(X\). This assertion is our null hypothesis \(H_{0}\). In order to test the null hypothesis we perform a series of experiments for a specific value of the control variable \(S=S_{1}\). For this data set we determine the sample mean \(\bar{x}_{1}\). Then we repeat the experiment for a second value of the control parameter \(S_{2}\) and compute the corresponding sample mean \(\bar{x}_{2}\). If the hypothesis is correct, the difference between the two sample means should only be due to statistical fluctuations and they should be small on an appropriate scale. The latter is reasonably defined by the standard error (SE), which for simplicity shall be the same for both samples. According to equation (19.26) [p. 298], the relevant measure for the discrepancy of the two samples is

\[ z:=\frac{\bar{d}_{2}-\bar{d}_{1}}{\mathrm{SE}} . \]

For simplicity, we assume identical sample sizes \(N_{\alpha}=N\) and variances \(\sigma_{\alpha}=\sigma\). Then

\[ \mathrm{SE}^{2}=\frac{2 \sigma^{2}}{N} . \]

The rest of the test is the same as in the previous example.

19.7.3 Chi-squared test

The chi-squared test, sometimes also called the goodness of fit test, was first introduced by Karl Pearson (1900). It is presumably the most frequently used statistical test. In physics it is commonly utilized for testing whether a theoretical model describes experimental data or whether a frequency distribution fits a predicted probability distribution.

Testing a theoretical model

One class of problems to which the chi-squared test is routinely applied is model testing. Suppose we have a set of values \(\boldsymbol{s}=\left\{s_{1}, \ldots, s_{N}\right\}\) of a control parameter and the corresponding measured values \(\boldsymbol{y}=\left\{y_{1}, \ldots, y_{N}\right\}\) of a physical quantity \(Y\). The data are believed to be describable by a theoretical model

\[ y_{i}=f\left(s_{i} \mid \boldsymbol{\theta}\right), \]

which may or may not depend on additional known or unknown parameters \(\boldsymbol{\theta} \in \mathbb{R}^{N_{p}}\). The possible null hypothesis could be \(H_{0}:\) The data obey \(y_{i}=f\left(s_{i} \mid \boldsymbol{\theta}\right)\), with unknown parameters \(\boldsymbol{\theta}\).

If the hypothesis and hence the model is correct, the measured data differ from the model only by the noise values

\[ y_{i}=f\left(s_{i} \mid \boldsymbol{\theta}\right)+\eta_{i} . \]

The noise is supposed to be additive and i.n.d. with individual known standard deviation \(\sigma_{i}\) for each data point. The error distribution and its parameter are part of our background knowledge and not to be questioned. \({ }^{1}\) If part or all of the model parameters are unknown, they have to be determined by the maximum likelihood estimator (see Section 15.2 [p. 237 ]). As a result, the number of degrees of freedom (dof) is reduced to \(v=N-r\), with \(r\) being the number of unknown parameters.

The chi-squared test for this problem is thus

Chi-squared test for model evaluation

test statistic:

\[ \begin{equation*} x=\sum_{i=1}^{N} \frac{\left(y_{i}-f\left(s_{i} \mid \theta\right)\right)^{2}}{\sigma_{i}^{2}}, \tag{19.43} \end{equation*} \]

PDF: \(p_{\chi^{2}}(x \mid \nu)=\frac{2^{-\frac{\nu}{2}}}{\Gamma\left(\frac{\nu}{2}\right)} \quad x^{\frac{\nu}{2}-1} e^{-\frac{x}{2}}\), number of dof:

\[ v=N-r . \]

\(r\) parameters have been fixed by their ML estimate.

In the present context a two-tailed test is appropriate, since deviations of \(x_{0}\) from the mean \(\langle x\rangle_{\nu}=\nu\) on both sides are equally inconsistent with the null hypothesis. The rejection region is therefore composed of two intervals, with two critical values for the test statistic \(x_{l}^{\mathrm{cr}}\) and \(x_{r}^{\mathrm{cr}}\), respectively:

[^9]\[ \begin{aligned} \mathcal{R} & =\mathcal{R}_{\text {left }} \cup \mathcal{R}_{\text {right }} \\ \mathcal{R}_{\text {left }} & =\left\{x \mid x<x_{l}^{\mathrm{cr}}:=F_{\chi^{2}}^{-1}\left(\left.\frac{\alpha}{2} \right\rvert\, N-r\right)\right\} \\ \mathcal{R}_{\text {right }} & =\left\{x \mid x>x_{r}^{\mathrm{cr}}:=F_{\chi^{2}}^{-1}\left(\left.1-\frac{\alpha}{2} \right\rvert\, N-r\right)\right\} \end{aligned} \]

By way of an example, we consider the ohmic resistor problem outlined before. Given a set of values for the current (control parameter) \(\left\{I_{1}, \ldots, I_{N}\right\}\) the associated values for the voltage \(\left\{U_{1}, \ldots, U_{N}\right\}\) are determined experimentally. The statistical errors of the voltage measurement are additive, uncorrelated and Gaussian. The variance may depend on \(I\) so we have a set of individual standard deviations \(\left\{\sigma_{1}, \ldots, \sigma_{N}\right\}\). The theoretical function in this case is \(f(U \mid R)=R \cdot I\). The nuisance parameter \(R\) is fixed by its ML estimator. To this end we minimize the misfit

\[ x=\sum_{i=1}^{N}\left(\frac{U_{i}-R I_{i}}{\sigma_{i}}\right)^{2}=\sum_{i=1}^{N}\left(\frac{U_{i}}{\sigma_{i}}\right)^{2}-2 R \sum_{i=1}^{N}\left(\frac{U_{i} I_{i}}{\sigma_{i}^{2}}\right)+R^{2} \sum_{i=1}^{N}\left(\frac{I_{i}}{\sigma_{i}}\right)^{2} . \]

In terms of weighted sample means, defined by

\[ \langle O\rangle_{\sigma}:=\frac{\sum_{i=1}^{N} O_{i} \sigma_{i}^{-2}}{\sum_{i=1}^{N} \sigma_{i}^{-2}}, \]

the misfit reads

\[ \begin{equation*} x=\left(\sum_{i=1}^{N} \sigma_{i}^{-2}\right)\left(\left\langle U^{2}\right\rangle_{\sigma}-2 R\langle U \cdot I\rangle_{\sigma}+R^{2}\left\langle I^{2}\right\rangle_{\sigma}\right) . \tag{19.46} \end{equation*} \]

It is minimized by

\[ R^{\mathrm{ML}}=\frac{\langle U \cdot I\rangle_{\sigma}}{\left\langle I^{2}\right\rangle_{\sigma}} \]

The number of degrees of freedom is reduced by one \((v=N-1)\). The value of the test statistic based on the data is obtained by inserting \(R^{\mathrm{ML}}\) in equation (19.46). In Figure 19.5 mock data are presented for this problem. The sample size is \(N=21\) so that \(v=20\). The ML estimate for the resistance yields \(R^{\mathrm{ML}}=25.31\). The corresponding \(U-I\) curve is also depicted in the figure and fits the data reasonably well. The mean value of the chi-squared distribution with \(\nu\) degrees of freedom is generally \(\nu\). So in the present example on average we expect \(\langle x\rangle=20\). The numerical example depicted in the figure yields \(x_{0}=48.33\), which appears to be in the rightmost part of the chi-squared distribution. From Table 19.1 we read off that \(x_{0}\) is much greater than \(x_{r}^{\mathrm{cr}}\) for both \(\alpha=0.05\) and \(\alpha=0.01\). Consequently, the null hypothesis can safely be rejected. In the mock example we are in the privileged position that we know the true answer, which is usually not the case. The truth is that the data have been generated based on a different theoretical model \(U=R \cdot I+20 \cdot I^{2}\) that also included an \(I^{2}\) term. So, rejecting the null hypothesis was the correct decision.

For example, are the measured radioactive decays really Poisson distributed? The distribution to be tested can either be discrete or continuous. be illustrated in terms of a comprehensive example. Let a random variable assume all positive real values \(x \in[0, \infty)\). Let the underlying PDF be \(\rho(x)\). We discretize the \(x\)-axis as

\[ 0=x_{0}<x_{1}<x_{2}<\ldots<\infty . \]

The partitioning may, but need not, be equidistant. Next we introduce the probability that \(x\) falls into the interval \(I_{i}:=\left[x_{i}, x_{i+1}\right)\) :

\[ P_{i}=P\left(x \in I_{i} \mid \mathcal{I}\right)=\int_{x_{i}}^{x_{i+1}} \rho(x) d x, \quad i=0,1, \ldots \]

After discretization the continuous problems can be treated like a discrete problem. There are events \(E_{i}\), for \(i=1,2, \ldots\); the total number can be finite or infinite. Typical events are: \(x\) lies in \(I_{i}\), and the number of decays in a fixed-energy window is \(n_{i}\). The total count is \(N=\sum_{i} n_{i}\). The chi-squared test statistic in this case assumes the form

\[ \begin{aligned} x & =\sum_{i=1}^{L} \frac{\left(n_{i}-\left\langle n_{i}\right\rangle\right)^{2}}{\left\langle n_{i}\right\rangle}, \\ \left\langle n_{i}\right\rangle & :=N P_{i} . \end{aligned} \]

Needless to say, impossible events (those with \(P_{i}=0\) ) are discarded right from the start. Now the occupation numbers \(n_{i}\) are correlated ( \(\sum_{i} n_{i}=N\) ) and they obey a multinomial distribution. In other words, the key conditions for the chi-squared test are not fulfilled, namely i.i.n.d. random variables. But we prove in Appendix B. 2 [p. 612] that based on the central limit theorem the sampling distribution of \(x\) converges for large \(N\) towards the chi-squared distribution with \(L-1\) degrees of freedom. The reduction by one is due to the fact that the counts \(n_{i}\) sum up to \(N\).

constraint: \(\sum_{i=1}^{L} n_{i}=N\)
given: \(\left\{P_{i}\right\}\)
test statistic: \(x=\sum_{i=1}^{L} \frac{\left(n_{i}-\left\langle n_{i}\right\rangle\right)^{2}}{\left\langle n_{i}\right\rangle} ; \quad\left\langle n_{i}\right\rangle=N P_{i}\).
\(p\left(x \mid N, L,\left\{P_{i}\right\}, \mathcal{I}\right)=p_{\chi^{2}}(x \mid L-1)\). (19.47)

We will consider two representative examples.

Example: Biased coin

The simplest example for this application of a chi-squared test is the coin experiment. The null hypothesis claims the coin is fair, i.e. \(P_{\text {heads }}=P_{\text {tails }}=\frac{1}{2}\). The coin is tossed \(N\) times and heads occurs \(n_{1}\) and tails \(n_{2}\) times. In total \(N=n_{1}+n_{2}\). The chi-squared value is

\[ x_{0}=\frac{\left(n_{1}-\frac{N}{2}\right)^{2}}{\frac{N}{2}}+\frac{\left(n_{2}-\frac{N}{2}\right)^{2}}{\frac{N}{2}}=\frac{\left(2 n_{1}-N\right)^{2}}{N} . \]

The number of degrees of freedom is \(v=2-1=1\). In this example a right-tailed test in \(x_{0}\) is presumably adequate; we would also accept small values of \(x_{0}\) as it would be difficult to find a sensible alternative hypothesis that could explain small deviations from the theoretical value \(n=\frac{N}{2}\) better than the null hypothesis. Hence the data will be rejected if \(x_{0}>x^{\mathrm{cr}}\), or alternatively if

\[ n_{1}<\frac{1}{2}\left(N-\sqrt{x^{\mathrm{cr}} N}\right) \quad \text { or } \quad n_{1}>\frac{1}{2}\left(N+\sqrt{x^{\mathrm{cr}} N}\right) . \]

According to Table 19.2 the corresponding critical value for e.g. \(\alpha=0.1\) is \(x_{1 \%}^{\mathrm{cr}}=6.63\). For a sample of size \(N\) that means the hypothesis is rejected if

\[ n_{1}>62 \text { or } n_{1}<38 \]

Tests with undetermined parameters

Now we want to generalize the test on distributions if none or not all parameters are specified. As outlined before, the unspecified parameters are replaced by their ML estimates. We use the ML values to determine the theoretical mean occupation

\[ \left\langle n_{l}\right\rangle=N p_{l}\left(a^{\mathrm{ML}}\right) \]

and compute the chi-squared value as before. The number of degrees of freedom is reduced by the number \(N_{p}\) of ML-estimated parameters

\[ v=L-1-N_{p} . \]

One degree of freedom is lost by the sum rule \(N=\sum_{i} n_{i}=\sum_{i}\left\langle n_{i}\right\rangle\).

Example: Poisson distribution

Let us assume that an experiment is performed in which the number of radioactive decays during a fixed time window is repeatedly determined. The experiment delivers a list of absolute frequencies \(n_{l}\) for the occurrence of \(l\) decays, for \(l=0,1, \ldots\) The data are depicted in Table 19.3. Since they stem from radioactive decays, they should come from a Poisson distribution. In order to test this assumption we formulate the null hypothesis

\(H\) : The data sample is Poissonian

Contingency tables

Another scope of application of the chi-squared test are the so-called contingency tables. The issue covered is as follows. We measure two properties \(x\) and \(y\), which, for simplicity, shall only assume discrete values \(x_{1}, x_{2}, \ldots, x_{l}\), or rather \(y_{1}, y_{2}, \ldots, y_{k}\). The experiment is repeated \(N\) times. The number of experiments that yield the data pair \(\left(x_{i}, y_{j}\right)\) is \(n_{i j}\).

We fill the contingency table with these numbers, as illustrated in Table 19.4. We have introduced the following abbreviations:

\[ \begin{aligned} n_{i, .} & =\sum_{j} n_{i j}, \\ n_{., j} & =\sum_{i} n_{i j}, \\ N & =\sum_{i j} n_{i j} . \end{aligned} \]

We denote the probability for the occurrence of the value \(x_{i}\) by \(p_{i}\) and that for the value \(y_{j}\) by \(q_{j}\). The goal is to test whether there are correlations between the \(x\) and \(y\) values.

To this end the null hypothesis is \(H\) : The data are uncorrelated.

That means

\[ P\left(x_{i}, y_{j}\right)=p_{i} q_{j} . \]

Table 19.4 Contingency table | | \(y_{1}\) | \(y_{2}\) | … | \(y_{k}\) | Sum | | :— | :— | :— | :— | :— | :— | | \(x_{1}\) | \(n_{11}\) | \(n_{12}\) | … | \(n_{1 k}\) | \(n_{1, .}\) | | \(x_{2}\) | \(n_{21}\) | \(n_{22}\) | ⋯ | \(n_{2 k}\) | \(n_{2, .}\) | | ⋮ | ⋮ | ⋮ | ⋯ | ⋮ | ⋮ | | \(x_{l}\) | \(n_{l 1}\) | \(n_{l 2}\) | ⋯ | \(n_{l k}\) | \(n_{l, .}\) | | Sum | \(n_{., 1}\) | \(n_{., 2}\) | ⋯ | \(n_{., k}\) | \(N\) |

If the hypothesis is correct, the average entry in the contingency table for the pair ( \(x_{i} y_{j}\) ) should be \(N p_{i} q_{j}\). Unfortunately, we don’t know the probabilities \(p_{i}\) and \(q_{j}\) and we have to determine them via maximum likelihood. The likelihood function is

\[ p(\boldsymbol{n} \mid \boldsymbol{p}, \boldsymbol{q}, N) \propto \prod_{i j}\left(p_{i} q_{j}\right)^{n_{i j}}, \]

where the bold symbols stand for the set of corresponding values. We obtain the ML solution by the zeros of the derivative of the log-likelihood subject to the normalization constraints

\[ \sum_{i=1}^{l} p_{i}=1 \quad \text { and } \quad \sum_{j=1}^{k} q_{j}=1 . \]

The constraints are incorporated via the Lagrange method and the condition for the ML solution is

\[ \frac{\partial}{\partial p_{\nu}}\left(\sum_{i j} n_{i j} \ln \left(p_{i} \cdot q_{j}\right)-\lambda_{1} \sum_{i} p_{i}-\lambda_{2} \sum_{j} q_{j}\right)=\frac{\sum_{j} n_{\nu j}}{p_{\nu}}-\lambda_{1} \stackrel{!}{=} 0 . \]

Repeating the calculation for \(q_{\mu}\) and utilizing the normalization condition leads to the desired result

\[ \begin{aligned} & p_{\nu}^{\mathrm{ML}}=\frac{1}{N} n_{\nu, .}, \\ & q_{\mu}^{\mathrm{ML}}=\frac{1}{N} n_{., \mu} . \end{aligned} \]

The chi-squared statistic therefore reads

\[ x=\sum_{i j} \frac{\left(n_{i j}-N p_{i}^{\mathrm{ML}} q_{j}^{\mathrm{ML}}\right)^{2}}{N\left(p_{i}^{\mathrm{ML}} q_{j}^{\mathrm{ML}}\right)} . \]

The number of degrees of freedom is reduced from \(L-1\), as we have determined the probabilities \(p_{i}\) and \(q_{j}\). In principle, there are in total \(k+l\) unknown parameters, but since both sets of probabilities add up to one, the reduction is only \(k+l-2\). In total we have

\[ v=l \cdot k-1-k-l+2=(l-1)(k-1) . \]

In summary, the chi-squared test for contingency tables is

The basic assumptions underlying the \(t\)-test are the same as for the \(z\)-test, i.e. a sample of i.i.n.d. random numbers. The only difference is that the variance is unknown. Possible applications are the same as for the \(z\)-test.

19.7.5 F-Test

As we have seen in Section 19.5 [p. 302], the F-statistic is invoked to test the null hypothesis \(H_{0}\) : Two samples have the same variance based on two i.i.n.d. samples \(\left\{d_{1}^{(\alpha)}, \ldots, d_{N_{\alpha}}^{(\alpha)}\right\}\) which have a common variance. The intrinsic variance is not given, nor are the individual means further specified. The test statistic of the \(F\)-test is the ratio of the unbiased sample variances

\[ \begin{align*} f & =\frac{\left(\sigma_{1}^{*}\right)^{2}}{\left(\sigma_{2}^{*}\right)^{2}}=\frac{n_{2} v_{1}}{n_{1} v_{2}} \tag{19.50}\\ v_{\alpha} & =\sum_{i=1}^{N_{\alpha}}\left(d_{i}^{(\alpha)}-\bar{d}_{\alpha}\right)^{2} \end{align*} \]

We have shown in Section 19.5 [p. 302] that \(f\) is distributed as \(p_{F}\left(f \mid n_{1}, n_{2}\right)\), where \(n_{\alpha}= N_{\alpha}-1\) is the number of degrees of freedom in sample \(\alpha\). One degree of freedom is lost in order to fix the unknown mean. Otherwise, the means do not enter the analysis.

Example: Is an instrument still calibrated?

The \(F\)-test shall be applied to the question outlined in Section 17.2.4 [p. 275], of whether an instrument is still calibrated, evidenced by a variance ratio of 1 . We used two series of measurements of sizes \(N_{1}=N_{2}=20\). The null hypothesis claimed \(H\) : The variance of the experimental noise is the same in both data sets. The estimates for the standard deviations were \(\sigma_{1}^{*}=2.5\) and \(\sigma_{2}^{*}=4.4\), which implied \(f_{0}=3.1\). In this context it is reasonable to use a two-tailed test as deviations in both directions from \(f=1\) indicate likewise that the hypothesis is incorrect.

Since the \(F\)-distribution is invariant against the interchange of the sample variances, the \(P\) value for the two-sided test is

\[ P=2 \cdot \int_{f_{0}}^{\infty} p_{F}\left(f \mid n_{1}, n_{2}\right) d f=0.018 \]

Hence, based on \(\alpha=5 \%\) the data are significant and based on \(\alpha=1 \%\) the data are not significant. So the result is almost balanced and a decision in either direction is risky. Consequently, in this example the significance of the \(F\)-test is marginal.

This is in stark contrast to the Bayesian result, derived in Section 17.2.4 [p. 275], which highly favours the hypothesis \((P(H \mid D, \mathcal{I})=0.99)\) leading to the conclusion that the measurement apparatus is still working perfectly well.

Comparison of Bayesian and frequentist hypothesis tests

In previous chapters we have applied the Bayesian and the frequentist approach to some basic problems. The key differences of the two approaches are summarized in Table 20.1. The last two rows deserve additional remarks, which will be given in the following sections.

20.1 Prior knowledge is prior data

In scientific problems the present data are certainly not the only information that is known for the problem under consideration. Usually, there exists a wealth of knowledge in the form of previous experimental data and theoretical facts, such as positivity constraints, sum rules, asymptotic behaviour. A scientist is never in the situation that only the current data count. The Bayesian approach allows us to exploit all this information consistently. It has been criticized that priors are a subjective element of the theory. This is not really correct, as the Bayesian approach is internally consistent and deterministic. The only part that could be described as subjective is the knowledge that goes into the prior probabilities. But this degree of subjectivity is actually the foundation of all science. It is the expertise that exists in the respective discipline. The generation of experimental data is based on the same subjectivity, as it is generally motivated by prior knowledge in the form of previous data or theoretical models.

A great part of the prior knowledge is a summary of a conglomeration of previous measurements, with completely different meanings and sources of statistical errors. The Bayesian approach is proved to be consistent and it is no surprise that it allows a systematic and consistent treatment of these data in the form of prior probabilities. Suppose there is a new data set \(D\) and a collection of old data sets \(\left\{D_{i}\right\}\) collected over the years. It is just simply a matter of applying the product rule to determine the odds ratio for the null hypothesis:

\[ \begin{aligned} o\left(H_{0} \mid D, D_{1}, \ldots, D_{N}, \mathcal{I}\right) & =o\left(D \mid H_{0}, D_{1}, \ldots, D_{N}, \mathcal{I}\right) o\left(H_{0} \mid D_{1}, \ldots, D_{N}, \mathcal{I}\right) \\ & =o\left(D \mid H_{0}, \mathcal{I}\right) o\left(H_{0} \mid D_{1}, \ldots, D_{N}, \mathcal{I}\right) \end{aligned} \]

The previous data sets are immaterial in the first factor as it is a crucial feature of the null hypothesis that it uniquely fixes the likelihood. The second factor is nothing but

\[ o\left(H_{0} \mid D, D_{1}, \ldots, D_{N}, \mathcal{I}\right)=\prod_{i=\nu}^{N} o\left(D \mid H_{\nu}, \mathcal{I}\right) \cdot o_{\mathrm{P}}\left(H_{0}\right) . \]

The nice thing about the Bayesian approach is that it puts all prior data on the same footing, even if the various data samples have different error statistics and would have to be treated in frequentist tests with different test statistics. The last factor is the prior odds, which is not based on data information but rather on purely theoretical knowledge. If all data samples are properly accounted for they will predominate the result, unless there is a contradiction (e.g. if the data favour a negative value for a model parameter, while the theory tells us that it has to be positive).

20.2 Dependence on the stopping criterion

We have already addressed the impact of the stopping criterion on the likelihood estimator in Section 15.4 [p. 241]. Here we will deepen the discussion and study the impact of the stopping rule on hypothesis testing. The original idea, outlined here, traces back to L. J. Savage [60] and is discussed in more detail in [13]. We use a similar problem based on Bernoulli experiments. Let \(H_{0}\) be the hypothesis that the probability for an individual trial is \(p_{0}\). Based on the data pair ( \(n, N\) ), which is the number of successes \(n\) and total trials \(N\), we want to scrutinize the validity of the null hypothesis \(H_{0}\).

So far it sounds very familiar, but this time the data shall be generated based on different stopping conditions: the experiment was stopped (a) after \(N\) trials, or (b) when the \(n\)th success was observed.

20.2.1 Bayesian analysis

The Bayesian approach starts in both cases with the odds ratio

\[ o=\frac{P\left(n, N \mid H_{0}, \mathcal{I}_{\alpha}\right)}{P\left(n, N \mid \bar{H}_{0}, \mathcal{I}_{\alpha}\right)} \frac{P\left(H_{0} \mid \mathcal{I}_{\alpha}\right)}{P\left(\overline{H_{0}} \mid \mathcal{I}_{\alpha}\right)}, \]

where the background information \(\mathcal{I}_{\alpha}\) for \(\alpha \in\{a, b\}\) encodes the information about the stopping criterion. First of all, the choice of the stopping condition definitely has no influence on our prior knowledge about \(H_{0}\). In other words, the odds ratio is independent of \(H_{0}\). For the Bayes factor we investigate the two cases separately.

Predefined sample size

The likelihood conditional on \(H_{0}\) is

\[ P\left(n, N \mid H_{0}, B_{a}\right)=P\left(n \mid N, H_{0}, B_{a}\right) \underbrace{P\left(N \mid H_{0}, B_{a}\right)}_{=1} . \]

The second factor is equal to 1 , as for the predefined sample size \(N\) it is the true proposition. The first factor is simply the binomial distribution, from which we merely need the \(p_{0}\) dependence. We therefore write

\[ P\left(n, N \mid H_{0}, B_{a}\right)=C_{a} p_{0}^{n}\left(1-p_{0}\right)^{N-n} . \]

For the marginal likelihood, conditioned on the complementary hypothesis \(\bar{H}_{0}\), we employ the marginalization rule in order to introduce the unknown success probability \(p\), resulting in

\[ P\left(n, N \mid \bar{H}_{0}, B_{a}\right)=C_{a} \underbrace{\int p^{n}(1-p)^{N-n} p(p \mid B) d p}_{:=\mathcal{I}} . \]

The Bayes factor is therefore

\[ \begin{align*} o_{\mathrm{BF}, a} & =\frac{p_{0}^{n}\left(1-p_{0}\right)^{N-n}}{\mathcal{I}} \tag{20.1a}\\ \mathcal{I} & =\int p^{n}(1-p)^{N-n} p(p \mid B) d p \tag{20.1b} \end{align*} \]

Predefined number of successes

In this case the likelihood is

\[ P\left(n, N \mid H_{0}, B_{a}\right)=P\left(N \mid n, H_{0}, B_{b}\right) \underbrace{P\left(n \mid H_{0}, B_{b}\right)}_{=1} . \]

Here \(n\) is the true proposition, as the number of successes is predefined. The first factor is the probability distribution for the stopping time, discussed in Section 15.4 [p. 241]. The probability is, according to equation (15.11a) [p. 243],

\[ P\left(N \mid n, H_{0}, B_{b}\right)=C_{b} p_{0}^{n}\left(1-p_{0}\right)^{N-n} . \]

As a matter of fact, it has the same \(p_{0}\) dependence. The marginal likelihood required in case of \(\overline{H_{0}}\) therefore reads

\[ P\left(N \mid n, \overline{H_{0}}, B_{b}\right)=C_{b} \mathcal{I} . \]

So the Bayes factor is the same as in the first case (equation (20.1)):

\[ o_{\mathrm{BF}, b}=\frac{C_{b} p_{0}^{n}\left(1-p_{0}\right)^{N-n}}{C_{b} \mathcal{I}}=o_{\mathrm{BF}, a} . \]

We see that the Bayesian inference does not care about the stopping criterion used. What is the underlying deeper reason why the stopping criterion is irrelevant in the Bayesian approach? It is the fact that the parameter dependence of the likelihood is independent of the choice of the stopping condition. Generally speaking, the likelihood for some data \(\boldsymbol{D}\), given all required parameters \(\boldsymbol{a}\), has the form

\[ p\left(\boldsymbol{D} \mid \boldsymbol{a}, \mathcal{I}_{\alpha}\right)=f\left(\boldsymbol{D}, \mathcal{I}_{a}\right) g(\boldsymbol{a}, \boldsymbol{D}) \]

Consequently, the posterior probability for the parameter(s) is also not affected by the stopping condition, as the prefactor \(f\) drops out by the normalization. Similarly, in hypothesis testing, \(f\) drops out of the odds ratio, as it occurs both in \(p\left(\boldsymbol{D} \mid H_{0}, \mathcal{I}_{\alpha}\right)\) and \(p\left(\boldsymbol{D} \mid \overline{H_{0}}, \mathcal{I}_{\alpha}\right)\).

20.2.2 Frequentist hypothesis testing

Now let’s see how the frequentist approach deals with the stopping criterion. Typically, hypothesis testing is based on the \(P\) value, which we will determine for the two cases. The point null hypothesis is: the single-trial probability is \(p_{0}\).

\(P\) value if the sample size is predefined

If the sample size \(N\) is predefined and the null hypothesis specifies \(p_{0}\), then the expected number of successes and their variance is known. A reasonable measure for the discrepancy between measurement and theory (test statistic) is therefore

\[ \begin{aligned} \xi(n) & :=\frac{|n-\langle n\rangle|}{\sigma_{n}} \\ \langle n\rangle & =N p \\ \sigma_{n}^{2} & =N p_{0}\left(1-p_{0}\right) \end{aligned} \]

We use the modulus since a two-tailed test appears adequate. The PDF for \(\xi\) (sampling distribution) is

\[ \begin{aligned} p\left(\xi \mid p_{0}, N, \mathcal{I}\right) & =\sum_{n=0}^{N} p\left(\xi \mid n, p_{0}, N, \mathcal{I}\right) P(n \mid p, N, \mathcal{I}) \\ & =\sum_{n=0}^{N} \delta[\xi-\xi(n)] P\left(n \mid p_{0}, N, \mathcal{I}\right) \end{aligned} \]

Given the experimental count \(n^{*}\), the value of the test statistic is \(\xi^{*}=\xi\left(n^{*}\right)\) and the \(P\) value becomes

\[ P=\int_{\xi^{*}}^{\infty} p(\xi \mid p, N, \mathcal{I}) d \xi=\sum_{n=0}^{N} P(n \mid p, N, \mathcal{I}) \int_{\xi^{*}}^{\infty} \delta(\xi-\xi(n)) d \xi \]

The inequality \(\xi \geq \xi^{*}\) corresponds to \(|n-\langle n\rangle| \geq\left|n^{*}-\langle n\rangle\right|:=\Delta n^{*}\). The sum over \(n\) is therefore restricted to either \(n \geq\langle n\rangle+\Delta n^{*}\) or \(n \geq\langle n\rangle-\Delta n^{*}\), resulting in

\[ P=\sum_{n=0}^{\langle n\rangle-\Delta n^{*}} P(n \mid p, N, n, \mathcal{I})+\sum_{n=\langle n\rangle+\Delta n^{*}}^{N} P(n \mid p, N, n, \mathcal{I}) \]

As a numerical example we use \(N=100, n^{*}=5\) and \(p_{0}=0.1\). Then \(\langle n\rangle=10\) and \(\sigma_{n}=3\). The resulting \(P\) value is \(P=0.025\). Based on a significance level of \(5 \%\), say, we would reject the hypothesis. At first glance, the decision appears reasonable, as the measured value \(n^{*}\) lies clearly outside the \(1-\sigma\) interval.

\(P\) value if the success count is predefined

Now we repeat the analysis for the other stopping criterion in which \(N\) is the random variable. It obeys the negative binomial distribution with \(\langle N\rangle=n / p_{0}\) and \(\sigma_{N}^{2}=n(1- \left.p_{0}\right) / p_{0}^{2}\), according to equation (4.35) [p. 70]. In this case, a reasonable test statistic is

\[ \eta(N):=\frac{|N-\langle N\rangle|}{\sigma_{N}^{2}} \]

The corresponding sampling distribution is

\[ p(\eta \mid p, n, \mathcal{I})=\sum_{N=n}^{\infty} \delta(\eta-\eta(n)) P(N \mid p, n, \mathcal{I}) \]

For the experimental count \(N^{*}\) the value of the test statistic is \(\eta^{*}=\eta\left(N^{*}\right)\), and the \(P\) value becomes

\[ p=\int_{\eta^{*}}^{\infty} p(\eta \mid p, n, \mathcal{I}) d \eta=\sum_{N=n}^{\infty} P(N \mid p, n, \mathcal{I}) \int_{\eta^{*}}^{\infty} \delta(\eta-\eta(N)) d \eta \]

As before, the interval \(\eta \geq \eta^{*}\) corresponds to either \(N \geq\langle N\rangle+\Delta N^{*}\) or \(N \leq\langle N\rangle-\Delta N^{*}\), with \(\Delta N^{*}=\left|N^{*}-\langle N\rangle\right|\). The resulting \(P\) value is given by

\[ P=\sum_{N=n}^{\langle N\rangle-\Delta N^{*}} P(N \mid p, n, \mathcal{I})+\sum_{n=\langle N\rangle+\Delta N^{*}}^{\infty} P(N \mid p, n, \mathcal{I}) . \]

For the same set of parameters as before, i.e. \(n=5, N^{*}=100\) and \(p_{0}=0.1\), we obtain \(\langle N\rangle=5 / 0.1=50\) and \(\Delta N^{*}=50\). Consequently, the first sum does not contribute and the second sum yields \(P=0.13\). Based on the \(5 \%\) significance level, the hypothesis would clearly not be ruled out. Depending on the stopping criterion, we come to contradictory conclusions about the validity of the null hypothesis \(H_{0}\), although one and the same set of data is used. The dependence on the stopping condition is rather disturbing, particularly as in most publications the stopping condition is not quoted and presumably the experimenter did not even pay attention to it and might have used different criteria for different experiments.

20.2.3 Yet another stopping criterion

Let us consider an extreme but still legitimate stopping criterion. Suppose somebody is performing quality control and wants to determine the percentage of intact parts in a production, or in case of pharmaceutical research the percentage of successful treatments with a new drug. Generically, we are speaking of a Bernoulli experiment of \(N\) repetitions and the outcome consists of \(n_{s}\) successes and \(n_{f}\) failures. The goal is to assess the hypothesis that the failure rate \(\frac{n_{f}}{N}\) is below a certain threshold.

The experimenter could, for whatever reason, decide to stop the experiment when the number of successes exceeds the number of failures (for the first time). However, if that does not happen within a reasonable number of trials, \(N_{\text {max }}\) say, he stops in any case.

The reported output is given by \(\left\{n_{s}, n_{f}\right\}\), i.e. the number of successes and failures. In case of the first stopping condition \(n_{s}=n_{f}+1\), otherwise \(n_{s}+N_{f}=N_{\max }\). The likelihood \(P\left(n_{s}, n_{f} \mid A, \mathcal{I}\right)(A=H\) or \(A=\bar{H})\) entering the Bayes factor can be determined upon marginalization over the success probability \(p\) :

\[ \begin{aligned} P\left(n_{s}, n_{f} \mid A, N^{*}, \mathcal{I}\right) & =\int_{0}^{1} P\left(n_{s}, n_{f} \mid p, A, N^{*}, \mathcal{I}\right) p\left(p \mid A, \mathcal{N}^{*}, \mathcal{I}\right) d p \\ & =\int_{0}^{1} P\left(n_{s}, n_{f} \mid p, N^{*}, \mathcal{I}\right) p(p \mid A, \mathcal{I}) d p \end{aligned} \]

The first part of the integrand \(P\left(n_{s}, n_{f} \mid p, N^{*}, \mathcal{I}\right)\) has already been determined in the context of the first lead problem in Section 5.3 [p. 80], where we studied the Brownian motion in the presence of absorbing walls. ## 19 Sampling distributions and common hypothesis tests

In this chapter we derive the probability distributions of common test statistics of finite samples of random variables and apply them to common hypothesis tests.

### 19.1 Mean and median of i.i.d. random variables

We start out with the mean and median of a sample $\left\{x_{1}, \ldots, x_{L}\right\}$ of size $L$ of i.i.d. random numbers drawn from a PDF $\rho(x)$. The mean and variance are as usual

$$
\begin{aligned}
\langle x\rangle & =\int x \rho(x) d x \\
\operatorname{var}(x) & =\int(x-\langle x\rangle)^{2} \rho(x) d x
\end{aligned}
$$

### 19.1.1 Distribution of the sample mean

::: {.callout-note appearance="simple"}
## Definition (Sample mean) We define


:::

Sample mean

$$
\begin{equation*}
\bar{x}:=\frac{1}{L} \sum_{i=1}^{L} x_{i} . \tag{19.1}
\end{equation*}
$$

The actual value of $\bar{x}$ depends on the sample and is therefore a random variable as well. The corresponding PDF $p(\bar{x} \mid L, \rho, \mathcal{I})$ can be determined by the marginalization rule

$$
p(\bar{x} \mid L, \rho, \mathcal{I})=\int \underbrace{p\left(\bar{x} \mid x_{1}, \ldots, x_{L}, L, \rho, \mathcal{I}\right)}_{\delta\left(\bar{x}-\frac{1}{L} \sum_{i=1}^{L} x_{i}\right)} p\left(x_{1}, \ldots, x_{L} \mid L, \rho, \mathcal{I}\right) d x_{1} \ldots d x_{L}
$$

As the elements of the sample are i.i.d., we can simplify the expression:

$$
p(\bar{x} \mid L, \rho, \mathcal{I})=\int \delta\left(\bar{x}-\frac{1}{L} \sum_{i=1}^{L} x_{i}\right) \rho\left(x_{1}\right) \ldots \rho\left(x_{L}\right) d x_{1} \ldots d x_{L}
$$

Without specifying the underlying PDF $\rho(x)$, we cannot proceed further with the general expression. We either have to consider the limit $L \rightarrow \infty$, in order to use the central limit theorem, or we have to specify the PDF.

However, we can derive general results for the lowest moments. The zeroth moment (normalization) is apparently equal to 1 , as long as $\rho(x)$ is properly normalized. The first moment, the expectation value of the sample mean, yields

$$
\begin{aligned}
\langle\bar{x}\rangle & =\int \bar{x} p(\bar{x} \mid L, \rho, \mathcal{I}) d \bar{x} \\
& =\int\left(\frac{1}{L} \sum_{i=1}^{L} x_{i}\right) \rho\left(x_{1}\right) \ldots \rho\left(x_{L}\right) d x_{1} \ldots d x_{L} \\
& =\frac{1}{L} \sum_{i=1}^{L} \int x_{i} \rho\left(x_{1}\right) \ldots \rho\left(x_{L}\right) d x_{1} \ldots d x_{L} \\
& =\frac{1}{L} \sum_{i=1}^{L}(\underbrace{\int x_{i} \rho\left(x_{i}\right) d x_{i}}_{=\langle x\rangle} \prod_{j \neq i}^{\int \rho\left(x_{j}\right) d x_{j}}) \\
\langle\bar{x}\rangle & =\langle x\rangle
\end{aligned}
$$

That is to say, the sample mean is an unbiased estimator for the underlying (true) mean of $\rho(x)$.

### 19.1.2 Variance of the sample mean

Next we will determine the variance of the sample mean. To this end we need the second moment

$$
\begin{aligned}
\left\langle\bar{x}^{2}\right\rangle & =\int \bar{x}^{2} p(\bar{x} \mid L, \rho, \mathcal{I}) d \bar{x} \\
& =\int\left(\frac{1}{L} \sum_{i=1}^{L} x_{i}\right)^{2} \rho\left(x_{1}\right) \ldots \rho\left(x_{L}\right) d x_{1} \ldots d x_{L} \\
& =\frac{1}{L^{2}} \sum_{i, j=1}^{L} \int x_{i} x_{j} \rho\left(x_{1}\right) \ldots \rho\left(x_{L}\right) d x_{1} \ldots d x_{L}
\end{aligned}
$$

We need to distinguish the cases $i=j$ and $i \neq j$ :

$$
\begin{aligned}
\left\langle\bar{x}^{2}\right\rangle= & \frac{1}{L^{2}} \sum_{i=1}^{L} \int x_{i}^{2} \rho\left(x_{1}\right) \ldots \rho\left(x_{L}\right) d x_{1} \ldots d x_{L} \\
& +\frac{1}{L^{2}} \sum_{\substack{i, j=1 \\
i \neq j}}^{L} \int x_{i} x_{j} \rho\left(x_{1}\right) \ldots \rho\left(x_{L}\right) d x_{1} \ldots d x_{L} \\
= & \frac{1}{L^{2}} \sum_{i=1}^{L}\left\langle x^{2}\right\rangle+\frac{1}{L^{2}} \sum_{\substack{i, j=1 \\
i \neq j}}^{L}\langle x\rangle\langle x\rangle \\
= & \frac{1}{L}\left\langle x^{2}\right\rangle+\frac{L(L-1)}{L^{2}}\langle x\rangle^{2}=\frac{1}{L}\left(\left\langle x^{2}\right\rangle-\langle x\rangle^{2}\right)+\langle x\rangle^{2} \\
= & \frac{\left\langle(\Delta x)^{2}\right\rangle}{L}+\langle x\rangle^{2}=\frac{\operatorname{var}(x)}{L}+\langle x\rangle^{2} .
\end{aligned}
$$

Because $\langle\bar{x}\rangle=\langle x\rangle$, the variance is

## Variance of the sample mean

$$
\begin{equation*}
\operatorname{var}(\bar{x})=\frac{\operatorname{var}(x)}{L} . \tag{19.2}
\end{equation*}
$$

In other words, the root mean square deviation of the sample mean from the true mean (statistical error of the mean) is given by

## Standard error

$$
\begin{equation*}
\Delta \bar{x}:=\mathrm{SE}:=\frac{\sqrt{\operatorname{var}(x)}}{\sqrt{L}} . \tag{19.3}
\end{equation*}
$$

It differs from the standard deviation by the factor $1 / \sqrt{L}$. The standard deviation is a measure for the scattering of the individual elements of the sample, while the standard error is a measure for the scattering of the sample mean. This is the main advantage of repeated measurements, which suffer from statistical noise. The standard error implies that it is possible to reduce the uncertainty below a desired limit. Another advantage of computing sample means is provided by the central limit theorem. It guarantees that the sample mean for large samples is normally distributed, irrespective of the underlying PDF $\rho(x)$, if the variance is finite, see Section 19.1.3. Needless to say, knowledge of the PDF is important for data analysis and inferences in general.

Up to now, a small deficiency still exists: we use the sample mean in order to estimate the true mean of $\rho(x)$. The uncertainty of the estimator depends on the variance of $\rho(x)$, which we will not know. Where do we get $\operatorname{var}(x)$ from? One is prompted to estimate it from the sample, similarly to the sample mean, by

$$
v=\frac{1}{L} \sum_{i=1}^{L}\left(x_{i}-\bar{x}\right)^{2}
$$

We have replaced the unknown true mean by the sample mean. The estimate for the variance can be rewritten as

$$
v=\frac{1}{L} \sum_{i=1}^{L} x_{i}^{2}-2 \bar{x} \underbrace{\frac{1}{L} \sum_{i=1}^{L} x_{i}}_{\bar{x}}+\frac{1}{L} \sum_{i=1}^{L} \bar{x}^{2}=\frac{1}{L} \sum_{i=1}^{L} x_{i}^{2}-\bar{x}^{2} .
$$

Also, this estimator is a random variable. Its mean is

$$
\langle v\rangle=\underbrace{\frac{1}{L} \sum_{i=1}^{L}\left\langle x_{i}^{2}\right\rangle}_{\left\langle x^{2}\right\rangle}-\left\langle\bar{x}^{2}\right\rangle \stackrel{(19.2)}{=} \underbrace{\left\langle x^{2}\right\rangle-\langle x\rangle^{2}}_{=\operatorname{var}(x)}-\frac{\operatorname{var}(x)}{L}=\frac{L-1}{L} \operatorname{var}(x) .
$$

Apparently, this estimator exhibits a bias, which can easily be eliminated by introducing a slightly different estimator:

## UNBIASED ESTIMATOR FOR THE VARIANCE

$$
\begin{equation*}
\sigma_{\mathrm{est}}^{2}=\frac{1}{L-1} \sum_{i=1}^{L}\left(x_{i}-\bar{x}\right)^{2} \tag{19.4}
\end{equation*}
$$

The modified prefactor $1 /(L-1)$ is understood as follows. The quantities $\Delta_{i}=x_{i}-\bar{x}$ are not independent, since $\sum_{i} \Delta_{i}=0$. There are only $L-1$ independent variables $\xi_{i}$ that can be formed from the $x_{i}$ by linear combination, the so-called Helmert transformation, as discussed in Section 7.10 [p. 136]. The number of degrees of freedom is thereby reduced by one.

### 19.1.3 Distribution of the sample median

The sample mean has a severe drawback. Its variance is proportional to the variance $\operatorname{var}(x)$ of the sample elements. Even though it is reduced by $1 / L$, it will be infinite if the underlying PDF has an infinite variance. This is for instance the case if $\rho(x)$ is a Cauchy distribution, which occurs quite often in physics. In this case, the sample mean is meaningless and it is advisable to use the median instead.

In this section we will, therefore, consider the properties of the sample median.
::: {.callout-note appearance="simple"}
## Definition (Median) The median, or central value, of a continuous distribution is defined as


:::

## Median of a continuous distribution $\boldsymbol{\rho}(\boldsymbol{x})$ with CDF $\boldsymbol{F}(\boldsymbol{x})$

$$
\begin{align*}
F(\hat{x}) & =\int_{-\infty}^{\hat{x}} \rho(t) d t \stackrel{!}{=} \frac{1}{2} \\
\hat{x} & =F^{-1}\left(\frac{1}{2}\right) \tag{19.5}
\end{align*}
$$

::: {.callout-note appearance="simple"}
## Definition (Sample median) Given a sample $\left\{s_{1}, \ldots, s_{L}\right\}$ of size $L$ drawn from a probability density $\rho(x)$ whose elements are sorted in ascending order $\left\{s_{1}, \leq s_{2} \ldots \leq s_{L}\right\}$, the sample median is defined by


:::

Sample median

$$
\breve{x}= \begin{cases}s_{n+1} & \text { for } L=2 n+1,  \tag{19.6}\\ \frac{s_{n}+s_{n+1}}{2} & \text { for } L=2 n .\end{cases}
$$

We are now interested in the sampling distribution of the sample median, i.e. in $p(\breve{x} \mid \rho, L, \mathcal{I})$, of a sample of size $L$ drawn from $\rho(x)$. We confine the discussion to the case of odd sample size $L=2 n+1$. The sample median is then the element $n+1$ of the ordered list. The sought-for PDF is nothing but the corresponding ( $n+1$ )th-order statistic. According to equation (7.42) [p. 119], the PDF in the present context reads

$$
\begin{equation*}
p(\breve{x} \mid \rho, L=2 n+1, \mathcal{I})=\frac{1}{B(n+1, n+1)} F(\breve{x})^{n}[1-F(\breve{x})]^{n} \rho(\breve{x}) . \tag{19.7}
\end{equation*}
$$

The average sample median has already been derived in equation (7.43) [p. 120]:

$$
\begin{equation*}
\langle\bar{x}\rangle_{n}=\int_{0}^{1} F^{-1}(f) \quad p_{\beta}(f \mid n+1, n+1) d f=\left\langle F^{-1}\right\rangle_{n}, \tag{19.8}
\end{equation*}
$$

where the latter mean is that of the associated beta PDF. We restrict the discussion to PDFs with singly connected support, such that $F^{-1}$ is unique. The mean and variance of the beta PDF $p_{\beta}(q \mid n+1, n+1)$ are

$$
\begin{align*}
\mu_{\beta} & =\frac{1}{2}  \tag{19.9}\\
\sigma_{\beta}^{2} & =\frac{1}{4(L+2)} \tag{19.10}
\end{align*}
$$

If the density is symmetric, i.e. $\exists x_{0}: \rho\left(x_{0}-\Delta x\right)=\rho\left(x_{0}+\Delta x\right)$, then the mean and median of $\rho(x)$ are the same: $\langle x\rangle=\hat{x}=x_{0}$. Moreover, the CDF $F(x)$ is antisymmetric w.r.t. point reflection about $\left(\hat{x}, \frac{1}{2}\right)$. Apparently, since it is the same curve merely rotated by 90 degrees, the same holds true for $F^{-1}(x)$. Consequently, in case of a symmetric density, $F^{-1}(x)$ can be decomposed into

$$
F^{-1}(x)=\underbrace{F^{-1}\left(\frac{1}{2}\right)}_{=\hat{x}}+G(x),
$$

where $G(x)$ is an antisymmetric function about $x=\frac{1}{2}$ and the average sample median simplifies to

$$
\langle\breve{x}\rangle_{n}=\left\langle F^{-1}\right\rangle_{n}=\hat{x}+\langle G\rangle_{n}=\hat{x},
$$

since $p_{\beta}(f \mid n+1, n+1)$ entering the definition (equation (19.8)) is symmetric.

## MEAN OF THE SAMPLE MEDIAN FOR SYMMETRIC DENSITY

$$
\begin{equation*}
\langle\bar{x}\rangle_{n}=\hat{x} . \tag{19.11}
\end{equation*}
$$

In other words, iff the density is symmetric, the average sample median is equal to both the median and the mean of $\rho(x)$. Consequently, the sample median is an unbiased estimator. In case of an asymmetric density we expand $F^{-1}(f)$ about the mean of the beta PDF, $\mu_{\beta}=\frac{1}{2}$, as it is equivalent to an expansion in $\frac{1}{L}$. Starting from equation (19.8), we obtain

$$
\begin{aligned}
\langle\bar{x}\rangle_{n}= & \underbrace{\left\langle F^{-1}\left(\frac{1}{2}\right)\right\rangle}_{=\hat{x}}+\left.\frac{d F^{-1}(f)}{d f}\right|_{f=\frac{1}{2}} \underbrace{\left\langle\left(f-\frac{1}{2}\right)\right\rangle}_{0} \\
& +\left.\frac{1}{2} \frac{d^{2} F^{-1}(q)}{d q^{2}}\right|_{q=\frac{1}{2}} \underbrace{\left\langle\left(q-\frac{1}{2}\right)^{2}\right\rangle+O\left(\sigma_{\beta}^{4}\right)}_{\sigma_{\beta}^{2}} \\
= & \hat{x}+\left.\frac{1}{2} \frac{d^{2} F^{-1}(q)}{d q^{2}}\right|_{q=\frac{1}{2}} \sigma_{\beta}^{2}+O\left(\sigma_{\beta}^{4}\right) .
\end{aligned}
$$

According to equation (19.10), $\sigma_{\beta}^{2}=\frac{1}{4(L+2)}=\frac{1}{4 L}+O\left(\frac{1}{L^{2}}\right)$. That is, on average, the sample median provides the true median of the underlying probability density only up to a systematic deviation of order $O\left(\frac{1}{L}\right)$ :

$$
\langle\bar{x}\rangle=\hat{x}+\left.\frac{1}{8 L} \frac{d^{2} F^{-1}(q)}{d q^{2}}\right|_{q=\frac{1}{2}}+O\left(\frac{1}{L^{2}}\right) .
$$

For the prefactor we still need the derivatives of $F^{-1}(f)$. By means of the chain rule we obtain

$$
\frac{d F^{-1}(f)}{d f}=\frac{1}{\rho\left(F^{-1}(f)\right)},
$$

and for the second derivative

$$
\frac{d^{2} F^{-1}(f)}{d f^{2}}=-\left.\frac{1}{\rho\left(F^{-1}(f)\right)^{3}} \frac{d \rho(x)}{d x}\right|_{x=F^{-1}(f)} .
$$

We merely need these derivatives for $f=\frac{1}{2}$ :

$$
\begin{align*}
\left.\frac{d F^{-1}(q)}{d q}\right|_{q=\frac{1}{2}} & =\frac{1}{\rho(\hat{x})}  \tag{19.12a}\\
\left.\frac{d^{2} F^{-1}(q)}{d q^{2}}\right|_{q=\frac{1}{2}} & =-\left.\frac{1}{\rho(\hat{x})^{3}} \frac{d \rho(x)}{d x}\right|_{x=\hat{x}} . \tag{19.12b}
\end{align*}
$$

The final result reads

Average sample median IN CASE OF ASYMMETRIC DENSITIES

$$
\begin{equation*}
\langle\hat{x}\rangle=\hat{x}-\left.\frac{1}{8 L \rho(\hat{x})^{3}} \frac{d \rho(x)}{d x}\right|_{x=\hat{x}}+O\left(L^{-2}\right) . \tag{19.13}
\end{equation*}
$$

### 19.1.4 Variance of the sample median

Next we determine the variance of the sample median, or rather that of $p \breve{x} \mid \rho, L= 2 n+1, \mathcal{I}$ ), given in equation (19.7):

$$
\begin{equation*}
\operatorname{var}(\breve{x})=\left\langle\left[F^{-1}(f)-\langle\breve{x}\rangle\right]^{2}\right\rangle . \tag{19.14}
\end{equation*}
$$

We use the same expansion as before:

$$
\begin{aligned}
\operatorname{var}(\breve{x}) & =\left\langle\left[\not x+\left.\frac{d F^{-1}(f)}{d f}\right|_{f=\frac{1}{2}}\left(f-\frac{1}{2}\right)+O\left(\sigma_{\beta}^{2}\right)-\not x-O\left(\sigma_{\beta}^{2}\right)\right]^{2}\right\rangle \\
& =\left[\left.\frac{d F^{-1}(f)}{d f}\right|_{f=\frac{1}{2}}\right]^{2} \underbrace{\left.\left[f-\frac{1}{2}\right]^{2}\right\rangle}_{\sigma_{\beta}^{2}}+O\left(\sigma_{\beta}^{4}\right)
\end{aligned}
$$

and obtain

$$
\operatorname{var}(\breve{x})=\left(\left.\frac{d F^{-1}(f)}{d f}\right|_{f=\frac{1}{2}}\right)^{2} \frac{1}{4 L}+O\left(\frac{1}{L^{2}}\right) .
$$

Hence the scattering of the sample median is of order $1 / \sqrt{L}$. It therefore predominates the uncertainty of the result for large $L$, as the bias decreases like $1 / L$. Along with equation (19.12), the result is

> Variance of the sample median FOR A SAMPLE OF SIZE $\boldsymbol{L} \boldsymbol{=} \mathbf{2} \boldsymbol{n} \boldsymbol{+} \mathbf{1}$ DRAWN FROM $\boldsymbol{\rho} \boldsymbol{(} \boldsymbol{x} \boldsymbol{)}$

$$
\begin{equation*}
\operatorname{var}(\breve{x})=\frac{1}{4 L \rho(\hat{x})^{2}}+O\left(L^{-2}\right) . \tag{19.15}
\end{equation*}
$$

We have seen that in case of a symmetric density, the average sample median agrees with both the true median and the mean. This is corroborated by the above series expansion, as the derivative of $\rho(x)$ vanishes at $\hat{x}$. Interestingly, the variance of the sample median is independent of the variance of the underlying density $\rho(x)$.

Consequently, the sample median is more robust than the sample mean when it comes to experiments with large scattering in the individual values, in other words, when the variance of $\rho(x)$ is large or even infinite.

We want to close the discussion about the sample median with a few examples.

## Example: Cauchy distribution

The Cauchy distribution is particularly interesting as it occurs quite frequently in physics and has an infinite variance. Here we use the PDF

$$
\rho_{C}(x)=\frac{1}{\pi\left(1+x^{2}\right)},
$$

which has zero mean. It is symmetric and the mean and median are equal. The CDF can be computed analytically:

$$
F_{C}(x)=\frac{1}{2}+\frac{\arctan (x)}{\pi},
$$

as well as its inverse:

$$
\begin{equation*}
F_{C}^{-1}(f)=\tan \left[\pi\left(f-\frac{1}{2}\right)\right] . \tag{19.16}
\end{equation*}
$$

It is apparently antisymmetric and the median is $\hat{x}=F_{C}^{-1}\left(\frac{1}{2}\right)=0$. The sample median is unbiased and the leading-order approximation of the variance reads, according to equation (19.15),

$$
\begin{equation*}
\operatorname{var}(\breve{x})=\frac{\pi^{2}}{4 L} . \tag{19.17}
\end{equation*}
$$

The results are depicted in Figure 19.1. The variance diverges for $L=1$, as it corresponds to that of the original PDF, but it is finite for $L \geq 3$ and approaches rapidly the leadingorder approximation of equation (19.17). In addition to the analytic results, Figure 19.1 also contains the data obtained by a straightforward computer simulation. Random Cauchy samples of size $L$ were generated and the corresponding medians determined. The variance of such a list of $10^{6}$ median values is included in the figure. ## Example: Exponential distribution

As another example we study the sample median of random numbers drawn from an exponential PDF

$$
\rho(x)=e^{-x} .
$$

In this case, the sample median is biased, as the exponential function is obviously not symmetric. According to equation (7.17b) [p. 104], the CDF of the exponential PDF reads

$$
F_{e}(x \mid \lambda)=1-e^{-x},
$$

which has the inverse

$$
F_{e}^{-1}(f \mid \lambda)=-\ln (1-f) .
$$

The median is therefore

$$
\hat{x}=F_{e}^{-1}\left(\frac{1}{2}\right)=\ln (2) .
$$

At the location of the median the density is $\rho_{e}(\hat{x})=\frac{1}{2}$ and the derivative reads $\rho_{e}^{\prime}(\hat{x})=-\frac{1}{2}$. So, according to equation (19.15), the leading-order approximation of the variance of the sample median is

$$
\operatorname{var}(\breve{x})=\frac{1}{L}
$$

As already mentioned, the sample median is biased and based on equation (19.13) it yields on average

$$
\langle\bar{x}\rangle=\hat{x}-\frac{-\frac{1}{2}}{8 L\left(\frac{1}{2}\right)^{3}}=\ln (2)+\frac{1}{2 L} .
$$

Again, higher-order contributions in $1 / L$ have been ignored.

### 19.2 Mean and variance of Gaussian samples

In the previous sections, we did not specify the PDF from which the independent random numbers are drawn. Owning to the omnipresence of Gaussian random numbers, in particular in conjunction with the central limit theorem, several hypothesis tests are based on normal distributions. The nice thing about this is that the sampling distribution can be given analytically.

Suppose we have samples of size $N$ of i.i.n.d. random variables $\left\{x_{n}\right\}$ with mean $x_{0}$ and variance $\sigma^{2}$. For these samples, we determine the sample mean

$$
s=\frac{1}{N} \sum_{n=1}^{N} x_{n}
$$

and

$$
v=\sum_{n=1}^{N}\left(x_{n}-\bar{x}\right)^{2},
$$

which is proportional to the sample variance. We want to determine the distribution $p\left(s, v \mid N, x_{0}, \sigma, \mathcal{I}\right)$ of $s$ and $v$. Instead of marginalizing over the samples $\boldsymbol{x}$, we use the Helmert transform (see Section 7.10 [p. 136])

$$
\begin{equation*}
p\left(s, v \mid x_{0}, \sigma, N, \mathcal{I}\right)=\int d^{N} \xi p\left(s, v \mid \xi, x_{0}, \sigma, N, \mathcal{I}\right) p\left(\xi \mid x_{0}, \sigma, N, \mathcal{I}\right) \tag{19.18}
\end{equation*}
$$

Given the Helmert-transformed sample $\boldsymbol{\xi}, s$ and $v$ are uniquely fixed and therefore expressed in terms of Dirac delta functions as

$$
p(s, v \mid \xi, \sigma, \mathcal{I})=\delta\left(s-\frac{\xi_{N}}{\sqrt{N}}\right) \delta\left(v-\eta^{2}\right) .
$$

Apart from irrelevant prefactors, which we will determine at the end via normalization, we have

$$
\begin{aligned}
p\left(s, v \mid x_{0}, \sigma, N, \mathcal{I}\right) \propto & \int d \xi_{N} d \eta^{N-1} \mathcal{N}(\eta \mid 0, \sigma) \mathcal{N}\left(\xi_{N} \mid \sqrt{N} x_{0}, \sigma\right) \ldots \\
& \ldots \delta\left(\xi_{N}-s \sqrt{N}\right) \delta\left(v-\eta^{2}\right) \\
\propto & \mathcal{N}\left(\sqrt{N} s \mid \sqrt{N} x_{0}, \sigma\right) \int d \eta \eta^{N-2} e^{-\frac{\eta^{2}}{\sigma^{2}}} \frac{\delta(\eta-\sqrt{v})}{2 \eta}
\end{aligned}
$$

For the last step we have introduced $(N-1)$-dimensional spherical coordinates. The remaining integral yields

$$
\begin{align*}
p\left(s, v \mid x_{0}, \sigma, N, \mathcal{I}\right) & =\mathcal{N}\left(s \mid x_{0}, \frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{N}}\right) \cdot \frac{1}{Z} v^{\frac{N-3}{2}} e^{-\frac{v}{\sigma^{2}}} \\
& =\mathcal{N}\left(s \mid x_{0}, \frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{N}}\right) \cdot p_{\Gamma}\left(v \left\lvert\, \alpha=\frac{N-1}{2}\right., \beta=\frac{1}{2 \sigma^{2}}\right) . \tag{19.19}
\end{align*}
$$

### 19.2.1 PDF of the sample mean

The PDF for the sample mean is given by the first factor of equation (19.19).

## PDF FOR THE SAMPLE MEAN <br> OF I.I.N.D. RANDOM NUMBERS

sample: $\quad\left\{x_{1}, \ldots, x_{N}\right\}$ drawn from $\mathcal{N}\left(x \mid x_{0}, \sigma\right)$
parameters: $\quad N, x_{0}, \sigma$
random variable: $\quad s=\frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^{N} x_{i}$

$$
\begin{equation*}
p\left(s \mid x_{0}, \sigma, N, \mathcal{I}\right)=\frac{1}{\sqrt{2 \pi \sigma^{2} / N}} e^{-\frac{\left(s-x_{0}\right)^{2}}{2 \sigma^{2} / N}}=\mathcal{N}\left(s \mid x_{0}, \frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{N}}\right) . \tag{19.20}
\end{equation*}
$$

### 19.2.2 PDF of the sample variance, chi-squared statistic

The probability density function for the sample variance is obtained from the properly normalized first factor in equation (19.19).

## PDF for the sample variance <br> FOR I.I.N.D. RANDOM VARIABLES

sample:
$\left\{x_{1}, \ldots, x_{N}\right\}$ drawn from $\mathcal{N}\left(x \mid x_{0}, \sigma\right)$
parameters:
$N, x_{0}, \sigma$
random variable: $\quad v=\sum_{n=1}^{N}\left(x_{n}-\bar{x}\right)^{2}$

$$
\begin{align*}
p\left(v \mid x_{0}, \sigma, N, \mathcal{I}\right) & =\frac{\left(2 \sigma^{2}\right)^{-\frac{N-1}{2}}}{\Gamma\left(\frac{N-1}{2}\right)} v^{\left(\frac{N-1}{2}-1\right)} e^{-\frac{v}{2 \sigma^{2}}} \\
& =p_{\Gamma}\left(v \left\lvert\, \alpha=\frac{N-1}{2}\right., \beta=\frac{1}{2 \sigma^{2}}\right) \tag{19.21}
\end{align*}
$$

Apparently, it is a Gamma distribution. The mean $\langle v\rangle$ is of particular interest. It follows from equation (7.14c) [p. 102]:

$$
\langle v\rangle=\frac{\alpha}{\beta}=(N-1) \sigma^{2} .
$$

The result corroborates that the unbiased estimator for the variance is normalized by $N-1$, as discussed in Section 19.1.2 [p. 285]. In connection with the chi-squared distribution, it is common to use normalized random variables

$$
\tilde{x}_{n}=\frac{x_{n}}{\sigma} .
$$

The sample variance $v$ is transformed into

$$
z=\frac{v}{\sigma^{2}} .
$$

The sampling distribution of the variance can be considered as a chi-squared distribution equation (7.16a) [p. 104] with $N-1$ degrees of freedom:

$$
\begin{equation*}
p\left(z \mid N, x_{0}, \sigma, \mathcal{I}\right)=p_{\chi^{2}}(z \mid N-1) . \tag{19.22}
\end{equation*}
$$

We started out with $N$ degrees of freedom $\left\{x_{n}\right\}$, but lost one as the definition of the sample variance contains the sample mean $\bar{x}$ which had to be determined from the data.

In the above derivation we have assumed i.i.n.d. random numbers, that is, they were all drawn from the same normal PDF. This can easily be generalized, in order to make the result applicable to a wider range of problems. We assume that the random numbers are still independent and normally distributed, but no longer identically. In other words, each random variable is Gaussian with its own mean and variance. Next we define new shifted and scaled random variables

$$
\tilde{x}_{n}=\frac{x_{n}-x_{0, n}}{\sigma_{n}},
$$

which now all have one and the same normal $\operatorname{PDF} \mathcal{N}\left(\tilde{x}_{n} \mid 0,1\right)$. The previous consideration implies that

$$
\begin{equation*}
\chi^{2}=\sum_{n=1}^{N} \frac{\left(x_{n}-x_{0, n}\right)^{2}}{\sigma_{n}^{2}} \tag{19.23}
\end{equation*}
$$

has a chi-squared distribution. It is therefore called a chi-squared statistic.

# Sampling distribution of the $\boldsymbol{\chi}^{\mathbf{2}}$-statistic 

$$
\begin{align*}
& \text { sample: } \\
& \text { parameters: } \\
& \text { test statistic: } \quad \begin{aligned}
& \left\{x_{1}, \ldots, x_{N}\right\}, x_{i} \text { drawn from } \mathcal{N}\left(x_{i} \mid x_{0}^{i}, \sigma_{i}\right) \\
p\left(\chi^{2} \mid N, x_{0}^{1}, \ldots, x_{0}^{N}, \sigma_{1}, \ldots, \sigma_{N}, \sigma_{1}, \ldots, \sigma_{N}, \mathcal{I}\right) & =\frac{(2)^{-\frac{N}{2}}}{\Gamma\left(\frac{N}{2}\right)}\left(\chi^{2}\right)^{\left(\frac{N}{2}-1\right)} e^{-\chi^{2} / 2} \\
& =p_{\chi^{2}}\left(\chi^{2} \mid N\right)
\end{aligned}
\end{align*}
$$

## $19.3 z$-Statistic

We consider the situation with two samples $X_{1}$ and $X_{2}$ of size $N_{1}$ and $N_{2}$, respectively. The elements of the two samples are drawn independently from two normal distributions with individual variances $\sigma_{1}^{2}$ and $\sigma_{2}^{2}$. A null hypothesis in this context might be
$H$ : The two samples have the same mean.
The $z$-statistic in this case is defined as
$\boldsymbol{z}$-Statistic
FOR THE DIFFERENCE IN MEANS

$$
\begin{align*}
z & =\frac{\text { sample mean } 1-\text { sample mean } 2}{\text { standard error of difference of sample means }}, \\
z & =\frac{\bar{x}_{2}-\bar{x}_{1}}{\mathrm{SE}}, \\
\mathrm{SE}^{2} & =\frac{\sigma_{1}^{2}}{N_{1}}+\frac{\sigma_{2}^{2}}{N_{2}} . \tag{19.25}
\end{align*}
$$

This is precisely the sufficient statistic provided by the Bayesian approach to the question 'Are the true means of two normal samples of sizes $N_{1}$ and $N_{2}$ the same, provided the corresponding variances are $\sigma_{1}^{2}$ and $\sigma_{2}^{2}$ ?' We leave the proof as an exercise for the reader. The relevant quantities are the individual standard errors $\mathrm{SE}_{\alpha}:=\frac{\sigma_{\alpha}}{\sqrt{N_{\alpha}}}$ and the resulting standard error of the difference of sample means of i.i.n.d. random variables, i.e. $\mathrm{SE}^{2}= \mathrm{SE}_{1}^{2}+\mathrm{SE}_{2}^{2}$. For notational ease we use the abbreviations $\mathbf{N}=\left\{N_{1}, N_{2}\right\}$ and $\boldsymbol{\sigma}=\left\{\sigma_{1}, \sigma_{2}\right\}$. The PDF for $z$ is obtained via the marginalization rule upon introducing the sample means:

$$
\begin{aligned}
p(z \mid \mathbf{N}, \boldsymbol{\sigma}, \mathcal{I}) & =\int d \bar{x}_{1} d \bar{x}_{2} p\left(z \mid \bar{x}_{1}, \bar{x}_{2}, \mathbf{N}, \boldsymbol{\sigma}, \mathcal{I}\right) p\left(\bar{x}_{1}, \bar{x}_{2} \mid \mathbf{N}, \boldsymbol{\sigma}, \mathcal{I}\right) \\
& =\int d \bar{x}_{1} d \bar{x}_{2} \delta\left(z-\frac{\bar{x}_{2}-\bar{x}_{1}}{\mathrm{SE}}\right) p\left(\bar{x}_{1} \mid N_{1}, \sigma_{1}, \mathcal{I}\right) p\left(\bar{x}_{2} \mid N_{2}, \sigma_{2}, \mathcal{I}\right) \\
& =\sigma \int d \bar{x}_{1} p\left(\bar{x}_{1} \mid N_{1}, \sigma_{1}, \mathcal{I}\right) p\left(\bar{x}_{2}=\bar{x}_{1}+z \mathrm{SE} \mid N_{2}, \sigma_{2}, \mathcal{I}\right)
\end{aligned}
$$

Now we introduce the common mean $x_{0}$ of the underlying normal PDFs. Furthermore, we suppress the prefactor and adjust it at the end. Moreover, we use equation (19.20) for the PDF of a sample mean:

$$
\begin{aligned}
p(z \mid \mathbf{N}, \boldsymbol{\sigma}, \mathcal{I}) \propto & \int d x_{0} p\left(x_{0} \mid \mathcal{I}\right) \cdots \\
& \times \int d \bar{x}_{1 p} p\left(\bar{x}_{1} \mid N_{1}, \sigma_{1}, x_{0}, \mathcal{I}\right) p\left(\bar{x}_{2}=\bar{x}_{1}+z \mathrm{SE} \mid N_{2}, \sigma_{2}, x_{0}, \mathcal{I}\right) \\
\propto & \int d x_{0} p\left(x_{0} \mid \mathcal{I}\right) \int d \bar{x}_{1} e^{-\frac{1}{2}\left(\frac{\left(\bar{x}_{1}-x_{0}\right)^{2}}{\sigma_{1}^{2} / N_{1}}+\frac{\left(\bar{x}_{1}+z \mathrm{SE}-x_{0}\right)^{2}}{\sigma_{2}^{2} / N_{2}}\right)} \\
\propto & \underbrace{\int d x_{0} p\left(x_{0} \mid \mathcal{I}\right)}_{=1} \int d s_{1} e^{-\frac{1}{2}\left(\frac{s_{1}^{2}}{\sigma_{1}^{2} / N_{1}}+\frac{\left(s_{1}+z \mathrm{SE}\right)^{2}}{\sigma_{2}^{2} / N_{2}}\right)}
\end{aligned}
$$

In the remaining integral the argument of the exponential by completing the square w.r.t. $s_{1}$ results in an expression of the form

$$
\left(\frac{s_{1}^{2}}{\sigma_{1}^{2} / N_{1}}+\frac{\left(s_{1}+z \mathrm{SE}\right)^{2}}{\sigma_{2}^{2} / N_{2}}\right)=C\left(s_{1}-s_{1}^{*}\right)^{2}+z^{2} .
$$

The integral over $s_{1}$ just modifies the prefactor and we obtain

$$
p(z \mid \mathbf{N}, \boldsymbol{\sigma}, \mathcal{I}) \propto e^{-\frac{1}{2} z^{2}},
$$

and the sought-for result is
$\boldsymbol{z}$-Statistic
FOR THE DIFFERENCE IN MEANS

$$
\begin{array}{ll}
\text { sample 1: } & \left\{x_{1}^{1}, \ldots, x_{N_{1}}^{1}\right\} \text { drawn from } \mathcal{N}\left(x \mid x_{0}, \sigma_{1}\right) \\
\text { sample 2: } & \left\{x_{1}^{2}, \ldots, x_{N_{2}}^{2}\right\} \text { drawn from } \mathcal{N}\left(x \mid x_{0}, \sigma_{2}\right) \\
\text { given: } & N_{1}, N_{2}, \sigma_{1}, \sigma_{2} \\
\text { test statistic: } & z=\frac{\bar{x}_{2}-\bar{x}_{1}}{\mathrm{SE}} ; \quad \mathrm{SE}^{2}=\frac{\sigma_{1}^{2}}{N_{1}}+\frac{\sigma_{2}^{2}}{N_{2}} \\
& p_{z}(z)=\frac{1}{\sqrt{2 \pi}} e^{-z^{2} / 2}=\mathcal{N}(z \mid \mu=0, \sigma=1) . \tag{19.26}
\end{array}
$$

It appears that the quantities $N_{\alpha}$ and $\sigma_{\alpha}$ do not enter the PDF of $z$, they are merely required for the calculation of $z$. Moreover, the test statistic in equation (19.25) as well as the corresponding PDF are independent of $x_{0}$ and we never had to specify the prior PDF for $x_{0}$, merely its normalization mattered. It corresponds to a null hypothesis:
$H_{0}$ : The means of two normal samples of given variances are the same.
An alternative hypothesis, also leading to a $z$-statistic, is
$H_{0}$ : The true mean $\mu$ of a normal sample of given variance is $\mu_{0}$.
$\boldsymbol{z}$-Statistic FOR A PREDICTED $\boldsymbol{\mu}_{\mathbf{0}}$
sample: $\quad\left\{x_{1}, \ldots, x_{N}\right\}$ drawn from $\mathcal{N}\left(x \mid x_{0}, \sigma\right)$
given: $\quad N, \sigma$
test statistic: $\quad z=\frac{\bar{x}-\mu_{0}}{\mathrm{SE}} ; \quad \mathrm{SE}^{2}=\frac{\sigma^{2}}{N}$

$$
\begin{equation*}
p_{z}(z)=\frac{1}{\sqrt{2 \pi}} e^{-z^{2} / 2}=\mathcal{N}(z \mid \mu=0, \sigma=1) . \tag{19.27}
\end{equation*}
$$

This is a special version of the previous case for $\sigma_{2} \rightarrow 0$. It implies $\bar{x}_{2}=\mu_{0}$. The rest is obvious and consequently $z$ in this case is also distributed as $\mathcal{N}(z \mid \mu=0, \sigma=1)$.

### 19.4 Student's $t$-statistic

Next we generalize the previous discussion towards an unknown variance. The random variables of one sample are still independently and normally distributed with common mean and variance, but the variance is not at our disposal.

### 19.4.1 Student's $\boldsymbol{t}$-test for a predicted mean

We start out with the situation where a Gaussian sample is given of unknown variance. This situation has already been examined in the Bayesian framework in Section 17.2.3 [p. 266]. The null hypothesis in this case reads
$H_{0}$ : The true mean $\mu$ of a normal sample of unknown variance is $\mu_{0}$.
The suitable test statistic as depicted in equation (17.11) [p. 266] reads

## $\boldsymbol{t}$-Statistic <br> FOR A PREDICTED $\boldsymbol{\mu}_{\mathbf{0}}$

$$
\begin{align*}
t & :=\frac{\bar{x}-\mu_{0}}{\mathrm{SE}^{*}}  \tag{19.28}\\
\mathrm{SE}^{* 2} & :=\frac{\overline{(\Delta x)^{2}}}{(N-1)}
\end{align*}
$$

In order to derive the sampling distribution we introduce - via the marginalization rule - the missing values of the sample mean, the sample variance $v:=N \overline{(\Delta x)^{2}}$ and the intrinsic standard deviation $\sigma$. As several times before, we suppress prefactors that can be determined easily afterwards:

$$
\begin{aligned}
p\left(t \mid N, \mu_{0}, \mathcal{I}\right)= & \iiint d \sigma d \bar{x} d v p\left(t \mid \bar{x}, v, \sigma, N, \mu_{0}, \mathcal{I}\right) p\left(\bar{x}, v, \sigma \mid N, \mu_{0}, \mathcal{I}\right) \\
= & \iiint d \sigma d \bar{x} d v \delta\left(t-\frac{\sqrt{N(N-1)}\left(\bar{x}-\mu_{0}\right)}{\sqrt{v}}\right) \ldots \\
& \ldots \underbrace{p\left(\bar{x} \mid \sigma, N, \mu_{0}, \mathcal{I}\right)}_{=\mathcal{N}\left(\bar{x} \mid \mu_{0}, \frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{N}}\right)} \underbrace{p\left(v \mid \sigma, N, \mu_{0}, \mathcal{I}\right)}_{=p_{\Gamma}\left(v \left\lvert\, \frac{N-1}{2}\right., \frac{1}{2 \sigma^{2}}\right)} p\left(\sigma \mid N, \mu_{0}, \mathcal{I}\right)
\end{aligned}
$$

Next it is expedient to introduce new variables $s:=\left(\bar{x}-\mu_{0}\right) / \sigma$ and $w:=v / \sigma^{2}$ in which the PDFs are

$$
\begin{align*}
\mathcal{N}\left(\bar{x} \mid \mu_{0}, \frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{N}}\right) \stackrel{(19.20)}{=} \mathcal{N}\left(s \mid 0, \frac{1}{\sqrt{N}}\right)  \tag{19.29}\\
p_{\Gamma}\left(v \left\lvert\, \frac{N-1}{2}\right., \frac{1}{2 \sigma^{2}}\right) d v \stackrel{(19.21)}{=} p_{\Gamma}\left(w \left\lvert\, \frac{N-1}{2}\right., \frac{1}{2}\right) d w \tag{19.30}
\end{align*}
$$

We obtain

$$
\begin{aligned}
p\left(t \mid N, \mu_{0}, \mathcal{I}\right)= & \iint d s d w \delta\left(t-\frac{\sqrt{N(N-1)} s}{\sqrt{w}}\right) \cdots \\
& \ldots \mathcal{N}\left(s \mid 0, \frac{1}{\sqrt{N}}\right) p_{\Gamma}\left(w \left\lvert\, \frac{N-1}{2}\right., \frac{1}{2}\right) \underbrace{\int d \sigma p\left(\sigma \mid N, \mu_{0}, \mathcal{I}\right)}_{=1} \\
\propto & \iint d s d w \sqrt{w} \delta\left(s-\sqrt{w} \frac{t}{\sqrt{N(N-1)}}\right) \cdots \\
& \ldots \mathcal{N}\left(s \mid 0, \frac{1}{\sqrt{N}}\right) p_{\Gamma}\left(w \left\lvert\, \frac{N-1}{2}\right., \frac{1}{2}\right) \\
\propto & \int d w \sqrt{w} \mathcal{N}\left(\left.\sqrt{w} \frac{t}{\sqrt{N(N-1)}} \right\rvert\, 0, \frac{1}{\sqrt{N}}\right) p_{\Gamma}\left(w \left\lvert\, \frac{N-1}{2}\right., \frac{1}{2}\right)
\end{aligned}
$$

$$
\begin{aligned}
& \propto \int d w \sqrt{w} e^{-\frac{1}{2} \frac{w t^{2}}{(N-1)}} w^{\frac{N-3}{2}} e^{-\frac{w}{2}} \\
& \propto \int \frac{d w}{w} w^{\frac{N}{2}} e^{-\frac{w}{2}\left(1+\frac{t^{2}}{N-1}\right)}
\end{aligned}
$$

Finally, the substitution $w\left(1+\frac{t^{2}}{N-1}\right)=y$ yields the desired result

$$
p\left(t \mid N, \mu_{0}, \mathcal{I}\right) \propto \int \frac{d y}{y} y^{\frac{N}{2}} e^{-\frac{y}{2}}\left(1+\frac{t^{2}}{N-1}\right)^{-\frac{N}{2}} \propto\left(1+\frac{t^{2}}{N-1}\right)^{-\frac{N}{2}}
$$

in the form of the Student $t$-distribution, already introduced in equation (7.31a) [p. 109].

## Sampling distribution of Student's $\boldsymbol{t}$-statistic FOR PREDICTED MEAN $\boldsymbol{\mu}_{\mathbf{0}}$

sample: $\quad\left\{x_{1}, \ldots, x_{N}\right\} \quad$ drawn from $\mathcal{N}\left(x \mid \mu_{0}, \sigma\right)$
given: $\quad N, \mu_{0}$
test statistic: $\quad t=\frac{\bar{x}-\mu_{0}}{\mathrm{SE}^{*}} ; \quad \mathrm{SE}^{* 2}:=\frac{\overline{(\Delta x)^{2}}}{(N-1)}$

$$
\begin{align*}
p\left(t \mid N, \mu_{0}, \mathcal{I}\right) & =p_{t}(t \mid v=N-1) \\
p_{t}(t \mid v) & :=\frac{\Gamma\left(\frac{v+1}{2}\right)}{\Gamma\left(\frac{v}{2}\right) \sqrt{\pi v}}\left(1+\frac{t^{2}}{v}\right)^{-\frac{v+1}{2}} \tag{19.31}
\end{align*}
$$

### 19.4.2 Student's $\boldsymbol{t}$-test for the difference in sample means

We generalize the previous analysis to test the hypothesis
$H_{0}$ : The means of two normal samples of common yet unknown variance are the same.
According to equation (17.20) [p. 270] and equation (17.19) [p. 269], a sensible test statistic is

## $\boldsymbol{t}$-Statistic

FOR THE DIFFERENCE IN MEANS

$$
\begin{align*}
t & :=\frac{\bar{x}_{2}-\bar{x}_{1}}{\mathrm{SE}_{\mathrm{diff}}^{*}} \\
\mathrm{SE}_{\mathrm{diff}}^{*} & :=\frac{N}{(N-2) N_{1} N_{2}} \sum_{\alpha} N_{\alpha} \overline{\left(\Delta x_{\alpha}\right)^{2}} . \tag{19.32}
\end{align*}
$$

The derivation of the sampling distribution is outlined in Appendix B. 3 [p. 615]. The result is that $t$ is distributed as $p_{\Gamma}(t \mid v)$, with $v=N-2$. Remarkably, the result is again independent of the prior PDF for the true $\sigma$.

### 19.5 Fisher-Snedecor $F$-statistic

The last test statistic we want to investigate is the so-called Fisher-Snedecor $F$-statistic. As before we start out with two independent samples of size $N_{1}$ and $N_{2}$, respectively, which are drawn from individual normal distributions. The means of the two distributions are not specified and we wish to analyse whether the variances are the same.

In the Bayesian analysis equation (17.29) [p. 275] we have already encountered the sufficient statistic for this problem, namely

$$
\begin{align*}
f & =\frac{{\tilde{\sigma_{1}}}^{2}}{{\tilde{\sigma_{2}}}^{2}}=\frac{n_{2}}{n_{1}} \frac{v_{1}}{v_{2}}  \tag{19.33}\\
\text { with: } \quad n_{\alpha} & :=N_{\alpha}-1 . \tag{19.34}
\end{align*}
$$

The sought-for sampling distribution again follows from the marginalization rule:

$$
\begin{aligned}
p\left(f \mid N_{1}, N_{2}, \mathcal{I}\right)= & \int d \sigma d v_{1} d v_{2} p\left(f \mid v_{1}, v_{2}, N_{1}, N_{2}, \mathcal{I}\right) p\left(v_{1}, v_{2}, \sigma \mid N_{1}, N_{2}, \mathcal{I}\right) \\
= & \int d \sigma p\left(\sigma \mid N_{1}, N_{2}, \mathcal{I}\right) \\
& \ldots \iint d v_{1} d v_{2} \delta\left(f-\frac{n_{2}}{n_{1}} \frac{v_{1}}{v_{2}}\right) \underbrace{p\left(v_{1} \mid \sigma, N_{1}, \mathcal{I}\right)}_{p_{\Gamma}\left(v_{1} \left\lvert\, \frac{n_{1}}{2}\right., \frac{1}{2 \sigma^{2}}\right)} \underbrace{p\left(v_{2} \mid \sigma, N_{2}, \mathcal{I}\right)}_{p_{\Gamma}\left(v_{2} \left\lvert\, \frac{n_{2}}{2}\right., \frac{1}{2 \sigma^{2}}\right)} .
\end{aligned}
$$

As before, we introduce the new variables $w_{\alpha}:=\frac{v_{\alpha}}{\sigma^{2}}$. Together with equation (19.30) we obtain

$$
\begin{aligned}
& p\left(f \mid N_{1}, N_{2}, \mathcal{I}\right) \propto \overbrace{\int d \sigma p\left(\sigma \mid N_{1}, N_{2}, \mathcal{I}\right)}^{=1} \iint d w_{1} d w_{2} w_{2} \delta\left(w_{1}-\frac{n_{1}}{n_{2}} f w_{2}\right) \\
& \ldots \cdot p_{\Gamma}\left(w_{1} \left\lvert\, \frac{n_{1}}{2}\right., \frac{1}{2}\right) p_{\Gamma}\left(w_{2} \left\lvert\, \frac{n_{2}}{2}\right., \frac{1}{2}\right) \\
& \propto \int d w_{2} w_{2} p_{\Gamma}\left(\left.\frac{n_{1}}{n_{2}} f w_{2} \right\rvert\, \frac{n_{1}}{2}, \frac{1}{2}\right) p_{\Gamma}\left(w_{2} \left\lvert\, \frac{n_{2}}{2}\right., \frac{1}{2}\right) \\
& \propto \int d w_{2} w_{2}\left(\left(\frac{n_{1}}{n_{2}} f w_{2}\right)^{\frac{n_{1}-2}{2}} e^{-\frac{1}{2} \frac{n_{1}}{n_{2}} f w_{2}}\right)\left(w_{2}^{\frac{n_{2}-2}{2}} e^{-\frac{w_{2}}{2}}\right) \\
& \propto f^{\frac{n_{1}-2}{2}} \int \frac{d w_{2}}{w_{2}} w_{2}^{\frac{n_{1}+n_{2}}{2}} e^{-\frac{w_{2}}{2}\left(1+\frac{n_{1}}{n_{2}} f\right)}
\end{aligned}
$$

The remaining result is solved by the substitution $w_{2}\left(1+\frac{n_{1}}{n_{2}} f\right):=y$ :

$$
\begin{aligned}
p\left(f \mid N_{1}, N_{2}, \mathcal{I}\right) & \propto f^{\frac{n_{1}-2}{2}}\left(1+\frac{n_{1}}{n_{2}} f\right)^{-\frac{n_{1}+n_{2}}{2}}\left[\int \frac{d y}{y} y^{\frac{n_{1}+n_{2}}{2}} e^{-\frac{y}{2}}\right] \\
& \propto f^{\frac{n_{2}-2}{2}}\left(n_{2}+n_{1} f\right)^{-\frac{n_{1}+n_{2}}{2}}
\end{aligned}
$$

Together with the correct normalization which follows from elementary integration, we obtain

$$
\begin{equation*}
p_{F}\left(f \mid n_{1}, n_{2}\right)=\frac{n_{1}^{n_{1}} n_{2}^{n_{2}}}{B\left(\frac{n_{1}}{2} \frac{n_{2}}{2}\right)} f^{\frac{n_{1}}{2}-1}\left(n_{2}+n_{1} f\right)^{-\frac{n_{1}+n_{2}}{2}} \tag{19.35}
\end{equation*}
$$

We see again that the prior for $\sigma$ is not needed for the sampling distribution of the $F$-statistic. Qualitatively, the curves of the $F$-distribution are bell-shaped like the chi-squared distribution. The mean and variance have already been discussed in equation (19.35). In particular, the mean is

$$
\langle f\rangle=\frac{N_{2}-1}{N_{2}-3} \underset{N_{2} \rightarrow \infty}{\longrightarrow} 1 .
$$

It is always greater than 1 and does not depend on $N_{1}$, which might appear odd at first glance. On second thoughts, however, it appears quite reasonable, since the sample variances $v_{\alpha}$ are independently chi-squared distributed:

$$
\langle f\rangle=\frac{n_{2}}{n_{1}}\left\langle\frac{v_{1}}{v_{2}}\right\rangle=\frac{\left\langle v_{1}\right\rangle}{n_{1}}\left\langle\frac{n_{2}}{v_{2}}\right\rangle .
$$

The chi-squared or rather $\Gamma$ distribution of $v_{\sigma}$ is, according to equation (19.21) [p. 295], $p_{\Gamma}\left(v \left\lvert\, \frac{n_{\alpha}}{2}\right., \frac{1}{2 \sigma^{2}}\right)$ and its mean is given by equation (7.14c) [p. 102], i.e.

$$
\frac{\left\langle v_{\alpha}\right\rangle}{n_{\alpha}}=\sigma^{2} .
$$

Thus the mean of $v_{1} / n_{1}$ yields the true variance. However, the nonlinear transformation $1 / v_{2}$ introduces a bias since

$$
\left\langle\frac{1}{v_{2}}\right\rangle \neq \frac{1}{\left\langle v_{2}\right\rangle},
$$

except for $N_{2} \rightarrow \infty$. The sampling distribution of the $F$-statistic is depicted in Figure 19.4 for different numbers of degrees of freedom. We can observe that the PDF gets ever narrower with increasing $n_{2}$ and will finally end as a delta peak at $f=1$.

### 19.6 Chi-squared in case of missing parameters

The chi-squared statistic has already been touched upon in Section 19.2.2 [p. 295], where all sample elements had a common mean. Here we want to generalize this to prepare the ground for hypothesis testing. To this end we consider a linear model

$$
\begin{equation*}
\boldsymbol{g}^{(0)}=\mathbf{M} \boldsymbol{a}^{(0)}, \tag{19.37}
\end{equation*}
$$

where $\boldsymbol{a}$ is an $N$-component column vector comprising the model parameters. The $N_{d} \times N$ matrix $\mathbf{M}$ represents the model, and $\boldsymbol{g}$ stands for the $N_{d}$-component column vector of data values. The superscript ( 0 ) in $\boldsymbol{g}^{(0)}$ indicates the underlying true data vector. The basic assumption (null hypothesis) is that the model correctly describes the data. Hence, there is an underlying true set of parameters, denoted by $\boldsymbol{a}^{(0)}$. The actually measured values $\boldsymbol{g}$, however, are distorted by a noise vector $\boldsymbol{\eta}$ :

$$
g=g^{(0)}+\eta .
$$

Needless to say that the noise vector is unknown, but we suppose that it is distributed as a multivariate normal with zero mean:

$$
p(\boldsymbol{\eta} \mid \mathbf{C})=\frac{1}{Z} \exp \left\{\boldsymbol{\eta}^{T} \mathbf{C}^{-1} \boldsymbol{\eta}\right\} .
$$

The normalization $Z$ is immaterial for the ensuing discussion. Here we even allow for correlations in the measurements, described by the covariance matrix $\mathbf{C}$. The likelihood therefore reads

$$
\begin{aligned}
p(\boldsymbol{g} \mid \boldsymbol{a}, \mathbf{M}, \mathbf{C}) & =\frac{e^{\chi^{2}}}{Z} \\
\chi^{2} & :=(\boldsymbol{g}-\mathbf{M} \boldsymbol{a})^{T} \mathbf{C}^{-1}(\boldsymbol{g}-\mathbf{M} \boldsymbol{a}) .
\end{aligned}
$$

The notation simplifies considerably if we introduce the following transformations upfront:

$$
\begin{aligned}
\boldsymbol{g} & \rightarrow \tilde{\mathbf{g}}:=\mathbf{C}^{-1 / 2} \boldsymbol{g}, \\
\mathbf{M} & \rightarrow \tilde{\mathbf{M}}:=\mathbf{C}^{-1 / 2} \mathbf{M}, \\
\mathbf{C} & \rightarrow \mathbf{1} .
\end{aligned}
$$

Henceforward, we suppress the tilde. Then equation (19.37) still holds and the loglikelihood turns into

$$
\begin{equation*}
\chi^{2}=(g-\mathbf{M} a)^{T}(g-\mathbf{M} a) . \tag{19.38}
\end{equation*}
$$

As an example, the model shall be an expansion

$$
g_{v}^{(0)}=\sum_{n=1}^{N} a_{n} \Phi_{n}\left(x_{v}\right)
$$

in a suitable set of basis functions $\Phi_{n}(x)$, not necessarily orthonormal. It is not even required that the functions are linearly independent. Then $M_{v n}=\Phi_{n}\left(x_{v}\right)$. A simple common example would be a quadratic function

$$
g_{v}=a_{1}+a_{2} x_{v}+a_{3} x_{v}^{2},
$$

and $x_{\nu}$ is the value of a control parameter of the experiment. Usually, some or all model parameters are unknown and have to be inferred from the data. Firstly, we split off that part of the model that contains predetermined parameters and subtract it from the data. This defines a new equation of the form of equation (19.37) in which now all model parameters are unknown. If all parameters are predetermined, the right-hand side is $\mathbf{0}$. Formally, we can use an $N_{d} \times 1$ model matrix $\mathbf{M}=\mathbf{0}$.

In the frame of frequentist statistics, unknown model parameters are fixed by maximum likelihood (see Chapter 15 [p. 236]). The root of the gradient of equation (19.38) yields the ML estimate for the parameters

$$
\begin{equation*}
a^{\mathrm{ML}}=\left(\mathbf{M}^{T} \mathbf{M}\right)^{-1} \mathbf{M}^{T} g . \tag{19.39}
\end{equation*}
$$

As a matter of fact, this is the solution of the linear regression model that will be discussed in more detail in Chapter 21 [p. 333]. The data vector corresponding to the ML solution is then

$$
\boldsymbol{g}^{\mathbf{M L}}:=\boldsymbol{M} \boldsymbol{a}^{\mathbf{M L}}=\underbrace{\boldsymbol{M}\left(\mathbf{M}^{T} \mathbf{M}\right)^{-1} \mathbf{M}^{T}}_{:=\mathbb{P}} \boldsymbol{g}=\mathbb{P} \boldsymbol{g} .
$$

We have introduced the matrix $\mathbb{P}$, which is a projection matrix/operator, because $\mathbb{P}^{2}=\mathbb{P}$. Applying the projection operator to the true data vector,

$$
\begin{equation*}
\mathbb{P} g^{(0)}=\mathbb{P} \mathbf{M} a^{(0)}=M\left(\mathbf{M}^{T} \mathbf{M}\right)^{-1} \mathbf{M}^{T} \mathbf{M} a^{(0)}=\mathbf{M} a^{(0)}=g^{(0)} \tag{19.40}
\end{equation*}
$$

reveals that $\mathbb{P}$ projects into the subspace

$$
\begin{equation*}
V_{\mathbb{P}}=\{\boldsymbol{g} \mid \boldsymbol{g}=\mathbf{M} \boldsymbol{a} ; \forall \boldsymbol{a}\} \tag{19.41}
\end{equation*}
$$

spanned by the data vectors accessible by the model. So $\boldsymbol{g}^{\mathrm{ML}}$ can be understood as a projection of the measured data into $V_{\mathbb{P}}$. The dimension $E$ of $V_{\mathbb{P}}$ is given by the number of linearly independent vectors in $\mathbf{M}$. Next we compute the difference vector that enters the misfit:

$$
\boldsymbol{g}-\mathbf{M} a^{\mathrm{ML}}=(\mathbf{1}-\mathbb{P}) \boldsymbol{g}:=\mathbb{Q} \boldsymbol{g} .
$$

It depends on the projection operator $\mathbb{Q}=\mathbf{1}-\mathbb{P}$, which projects into the subspace $V_{\mathbb{Q}}$ orthogonal to $V_{\mathbb{P}}$. The misfit

$$
\chi^{2}(g):=g^{T} \mathbf{Q}^{T} \mathbf{Q} g
$$

is apparently determined by the components of the data vector that cannot be reached by the model. The measured data are distributed as

$$
p\left(\boldsymbol{g} \mid \boldsymbol{g}^{(0)}, \mathbf{C}, \mathcal{I}\right)=\frac{1}{Z^{\prime \prime}} \exp \left\{\left(\boldsymbol{g}-\boldsymbol{g}^{(0)}\right)^{T}\left(\boldsymbol{g}-\boldsymbol{g}^{(0)}\right)\right\} .
$$

Once again, $Z^{\prime \prime}$ is an unimportant normalization factor. The noise vector $\boldsymbol{\eta}=\boldsymbol{g}-\boldsymbol{g}^{(0)}$ shall be split into the subspaces $V_{\mathbb{P}}$ and $V_{\mathbb{Q}}$ as follows:

$$
\boldsymbol{g}-\boldsymbol{g}^{(0)}=(\mathbb{Q}+\mathbb{P})\left(\boldsymbol{g}-\boldsymbol{g}^{(0)}\right)=\mathbb{Q} \boldsymbol{g}+\mathbb{P}\left(\boldsymbol{g}-\boldsymbol{g}^{(0)}\right)
$$

In the last step we have used equation (19.40). Moreover, we have the following orthogonality:

$$
\mathbb{Q}^{T} \mathbb{P}=\mathbb{P}-\mathbf{M}\left(\mathbf{M}^{T} \mathbf{M}\right)^{-1} \mathbf{M}^{T} \mathbf{M}\left(\mathbf{M}^{T} \mathbf{M}\right)^{-1} \mathbf{M}^{T}=\mathbb{P}-\mathbb{P}=0
$$

Together with equation (19.40), it allows us to write

$$
\left(\boldsymbol{g}-\boldsymbol{g}^{(0)}\right)^{T}\left(\boldsymbol{g}-\boldsymbol{g}^{(0)}\right)=(\mathbb{Q} \boldsymbol{g})^{T}(\mathbb{Q} \boldsymbol{g})+\left(\mathbb{P} \boldsymbol{g}-\boldsymbol{g}^{(0)}\right)^{T}\left(\mathbb{P} \boldsymbol{g}-\boldsymbol{g}^{(0)}\right)
$$

Now we are prepared to compute the sampling distribution for the misfit through the marginalization rule

$$
p\left(\chi^{2} \mid \mathbf{C}, \boldsymbol{g}^{(0)}\right) \propto \int d \boldsymbol{g}^{N_{d}} \delta\left(\chi^{2}-\chi^{2}(\boldsymbol{g})\right) e^{-\frac{1}{2}(\mathbb{Q} \boldsymbol{g})^{T}(\mathbb{Q} \boldsymbol{g})} e^{-\frac{1}{2}\left(\mathbb{P} \boldsymbol{g}-\boldsymbol{g}^{(0)}\right)^{T}\left(\mathbb{P} \boldsymbol{g}-\boldsymbol{g}^{(0)}\right)}
$$

The integration factors into independent integrals over $V_{\mathbb{P}}$ and $V_{\mathbb{Q}}$, respectively. The former just modifies the prefactor. It also shows that the sampling distribution is independent
of the true data vector $\boldsymbol{d}^{(0)}$. The remaining integral over the vectors in $V_{\mathbb{Q}}$ of dimension $n:=N_{d}-E$ has the structure

$$
p\left(\chi^{2} \mid \mathbf{C}, \boldsymbol{g}^{(0)}\right) \propto \int d x^{n} \delta\left(\chi^{2}-|\boldsymbol{x}|^{2}\right) e^{-\frac{1}{2}|\boldsymbol{x}|^{2}} \propto\left(\chi^{2}\right)^{\frac{n}{2}-1} e^{-\frac{1}{2} \chi^{2}} .
$$

We summarize the result:

## Distribution of the CHi-Squared statistic IN CASE OF MISSING PARAMETERS OF A LINEAR MODEL

$$
\begin{array}{ll}
\text { model: } & \boldsymbol{g}=\mathbf{M} \boldsymbol{a} ; \quad \mathbf{M}: N_{d} \times N \text { matrix } \\
\text { additive noise: } & \boldsymbol{\eta} \text { drawn from } \mathcal{N}(\boldsymbol{\eta} \mid \mathbf{0}, \mathbf{C}) \\
\text { statistic: } & \chi^{2}:=(\boldsymbol{g}-\mathbf{M} \boldsymbol{a})^{T} \mathbf{C}^{-1}(\boldsymbol{g}-\mathbf{M} \boldsymbol{a}) \\
E: & \begin{array}{l}
\text { number of linearly independent columns } \\
\text { in } \mathbf{M} \text {, associated with unknown parameters }
\end{array} \\
& p\left(\chi^{2}\right)=p_{\chi^{2}}\left(\chi^{2} \mid n=N_{d}-E\right) .
\end{array}
$$

In view of equation (19.39), each parameter corresponds to a particular linear combination of the data. The latter in turn does not contribute to the misfit. That is why each linearly 'independent model parameter' reduces the number of degrees of freedom by one.

### 19.7 Common hypothesis tests

### 19.7.1 General procedure

In the introductory part we have outlined the ideas of the two frequentist approaches to hypothesis testing. In this part we limit the discussion to Fisherian significance tests. Up to now we have only considered simple hypotheses in which all parameters are fixed by the null hypothesis. In the case of a compound hypothesis, there are still open parameters. For example, when investigating some crystals with regard to their conductivity, the null hypothesis could be

$$
H_{0}: \text { The material has an ohmic resistance. }
$$

In other words, the voltage is proportional to the current:

$$
U \propto I .
$$

The proportionality constant (resistance) is the unknown parameter. The standard approach in dealing with such nuisance parameters in the frequentist approach is to fix the unknown
parameters by the maximum likelihood estimator. The number of degrees of freedom, originally the number of data, is reduced by the number of estimated parameters, as these are inferred from the sample.

## General procedure for significance tests

1. Define the null hypothesis.
2. Choose the test statistic $x$ with sampling distribution $p_{x}(x)$.
3. Determine the ML estimator for the nuisance parameters $\theta$.
4. Define the significance level (e.g. $\alpha=0.05$ ).
5. Determine the rejection region $\mathcal{R}_{\alpha}$, either one- or two-sided:

$$
\int_{\mathcal{R}_{\alpha}} p_{x}(x \mid \boldsymbol{\theta}) d x
$$

6. Evaluate the test statistic $x=x_{0}$ for the measured sample.
7. If $x_{0} \in \mathcal{R}_{\alpha}$ reject the null hypothesis.

Next we will discuss the most common significance test in more detail and give some basic examples.

### 19.7.2 z-Tests

## Is the mass correctly predicted?

Presumably, one of the simplest types of problem for significance testing is the following: The mass of a particle is measured repeatedly and the data are taken from a sample $\boldsymbol{m}= \left\{m_{1}, \ldots, m_{N}\right\}$. The statistical errors of the weighing process are additive, uncorrelated and Gaussian with variance $\sigma^{2}$.

The null hypothesis claims the true value of the mass is $m_{0}$. So we are dealing with a simple null hypothesis and $z$ is again the appropriate test statistic, with

$$
\begin{aligned}
z & =\frac{\bar{m}-m_{0}}{\mathrm{SE}}, \\
\mathrm{SE} & =\frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{N}}
\end{aligned}
$$

The sampling distribution is the centred, unit variance normal distribution, i.e. $\mathcal{N}(z, 0,1)$. We decide on a two-tailed test as large deviations from $z=0$ in both directions indicate a discrepancy between null hypothesis and measurement. We choose a significance level $\alpha$ and adjust the rejection region such that the probability mass in both tails is $\alpha / 2$, i.e.

$$
\int_{z_{1}^{\mathrm{cr}}}^{\infty} \mathcal{N}(z, 0,1)=\int_{-\infty}^{z_{2}^{\mathrm{cr}}} \mathcal{N}(z, 0,1)=\alpha / 2
$$

By symmetry, $z_{2}^{\mathrm{cr}}=-z_{2}^{\mathrm{cr}}:=-z^{\mathrm{cr}}$ and

$$
\int_{-\infty}^{-z^{\mathrm{cr}}} \mathcal{N}(z, 0,1)=\frac{1}{2} \operatorname{Erfc}\left(\frac{z^{\mathrm{cr}}}{\sqrt{2}}\right) \stackrel{!}{=} \frac{\alpha}{2} .
$$

Hence, $z^{\mathrm{cr}}=\sqrt{2} \operatorname{InvErfc}(\alpha)$, with 'Erfc' being the complementary error function and 'InvErfc' its inverse. For the two values $\alpha=0.05(\alpha=0.01)$, recommended by Fisher, we obtain $z^{\mathrm{cr}}=1.960\left(z^{\mathrm{cr}}=2.575\right)$. If the difference $\left|\bar{m}-m_{0}\right|$ is greater than $z^{\mathrm{cr}}$. SE the data are called significant, since the measurement differs significantly from the null hypothesis by an amount that can hardly be explained by chance alone. And therefore the hypothesis is to be rejected.

## Does the control variable have an impact?

We claim that a control parameter $S$ of an experiment does not affect the measurement of quantity $X$. This assertion is our null hypothesis $H_{0}$. In order to test the null hypothesis we perform a series of experiments for a specific value of the control variable $S=S_{1}$. For this data set we determine the sample mean $\bar{x}_{1}$. Then we repeat the experiment for a second value of the control parameter $S_{2}$ and compute the corresponding sample mean $\bar{x}_{2}$. If the hypothesis is correct, the difference between the two sample means should only be due to statistical fluctuations and they should be small on an appropriate scale. The latter is reasonably defined by the standard error (SE), which for simplicity shall be the same for both samples. According to equation (19.26) [p. 298], the relevant measure for the discrepancy of the two samples is

$$
z:=\frac{\bar{d}_{2}-\bar{d}_{1}}{\mathrm{SE}} .
$$

For simplicity, we assume identical sample sizes $N_{\alpha}=N$ and variances $\sigma_{\alpha}=\sigma$. Then

$$
\mathrm{SE}^{2}=\frac{2 \sigma^{2}}{N} .
$$

The rest of the test is the same as in the previous example.

### 19.7.3 Chi-squared test

The chi-squared test, sometimes also called the goodness of fit test, was first introduced by Karl Pearson (1900). It is presumably the most frequently used statistical test. In physics it is commonly utilized for testing whether a theoretical model describes experimental data or whether a frequency distribution fits a predicted probability distribution.

## Testing a theoretical model

One class of problems to which the chi-squared test is routinely applied is model testing. Suppose we have a set of values $\boldsymbol{s}=\left\{s_{1}, \ldots, s_{N}\right\}$ of a control parameter and the
corresponding measured values $\boldsymbol{y}=\left\{y_{1}, \ldots, y_{N}\right\}$ of a physical quantity $Y$. The data are believed to be describable by a theoretical model

$$
y_{i}=f\left(s_{i} \mid \boldsymbol{\theta}\right),
$$

which may or may not depend on additional known or unknown parameters $\boldsymbol{\theta} \in \mathbb{R}^{N_{p}}$. The possible null hypothesis could be
$H_{0}:$ The data obey $y_{i}=f\left(s_{i} \mid \boldsymbol{\theta}\right)$, with unknown parameters $\boldsymbol{\theta}$.

If the hypothesis and hence the model is correct, the measured data differ from the model only by the noise values

$$
y_{i}=f\left(s_{i} \mid \boldsymbol{\theta}\right)+\eta_{i} .
$$

The noise is supposed to be additive and i.n.d. with individual known standard deviation $\sigma_{i}$ for each data point. The error distribution and its parameter are part of our background knowledge and not to be questioned. ${ }^{1}$ If part or all of the model parameters are unknown, they have to be determined by the maximum likelihood estimator (see Section 15.2 [p. 237 ]). As a result, the number of degrees of freedom (dof) is reduced to $v=N-r$, with $r$ being the number of unknown parameters.

The chi-squared test for this problem is thus

$$
\begin{equation*}
x=\sum_{i=1}^{N} \frac{\left(y_{i}-f\left(s_{i} \mid \theta\right)\right)^{2}}{\sigma_{i}^{2}}, \tag{19.43}
\end{equation*}
$$

PDF: $p_{\chi^{2}}(x \mid \nu)=\frac{2^{-\frac{\nu}{2}}}{\Gamma\left(\frac{\nu}{2}\right)} \quad x^{\frac{\nu}{2}-1} e^{-\frac{x}{2}}$,
number of dof:

$$

By way of an example, we consider the ohmic resistor problem outlined before. Given a set of values for the current (control parameter) $\left\{I_{1}, \ldots, I_{N}\right\}$ the associated values for the voltage $\left\{U_{1}, \ldots, U_{N}\right\}$ are determined experimentally. The statistical errors of the voltage
measurement are additive, uncorrelated and Gaussian. The variance may depend on $I$ so we have a set of individual standard deviations $\left\{\sigma_{1}, \ldots, \sigma_{N}\right\}$. The theoretical function in this case is $f(U \mid R)=R \cdot I$. The nuisance parameter $R$ is fixed by its ML estimator. To this end we minimize the misfit

$$
x=\sum_{i=1}^{N}\left(\frac{U_{i}-R I_{i}}{\sigma_{i}}\right)^{2}=\sum_{i=1}^{N}\left(\frac{U_{i}}{\sigma_{i}}\right)^{2}-2 R \sum_{i=1}^{N}\left(\frac{U_{i} I_{i}}{\sigma_{i}^{2}}\right)+R^{2} \sum_{i=1}^{N}\left(\frac{I_{i}}{\sigma_{i}}\right)^{2} .
$$

In terms of weighted sample means, defined by

$$
\langle O\rangle_{\sigma}:=\frac{\sum_{i=1}^{N} O_{i} \sigma_{i}^{-2}}{\sum_{i=1}^{N} \sigma_{i}^{-2}},
$$

the misfit reads

$$
\begin{equation*}
x=\left(\sum_{i=1}^{N} \sigma_{i}^{-2}\right)\left(\left\langle U^{2}\right\rangle_{\sigma}-2 R\langle U \cdot I\rangle_{\sigma}+R^{2}\left\langle I^{2}\right\rangle_{\sigma}\right) . \tag{19.46}
\end{equation*}
$$

It is minimized by

$$
R^{\mathrm{ML}}=\frac{\langle U \cdot I\rangle_{\sigma}}{\left\langle I^{2}\right\rangle_{\sigma}}
$$

The number of degrees of freedom is reduced by one $(v=N-1)$. The value of the test statistic based on the data is obtained by inserting $R^{\mathrm{ML}}$ in equation (19.46). In Figure 19.5 mock data are presented for this problem. The sample size is $N=21$ so that $v=20$. The ML estimate for the resistance yields $R^{\mathrm{ML}}=25.31$. The corresponding $U-I$ curve is also depicted in the figure and fits the data reasonably well. The mean value of the chi-squared distribution with $\nu$ degrees of freedom is generally $\nu$. So in the present example on average we expect $\langle x\rangle=20$. The numerical example depicted in the figure yields $x_{0}=48.33$, which appears to be in the rightmost part of the chi-squared distribution. From Table 19.1 we read off that $x_{0}$ is much greater than $x_{r}^{\mathrm{cr}}$ for both $\alpha=0.05$ and $\alpha=0.01$. Consequently, the null hypothesis can safely be rejected. In the mock example we are in the privileged position that we know the true answer, which is usually not the case. The truth is that the data have been generated based on a different theoretical model $U=R \cdot I+20 \cdot I^{2}$ that also included an $I^{2}$ term. So, rejecting the null hypothesis was the correct decision.

the central limit theorem the sampling distribution of $x$ converges for large $N$ towards the chi-squared distribution with $L-1$ degrees of freedom. The reduction by one is due to the fact that the counts $n_{i}$ sum up to $N$.

## Example: Biased coin

The simplest example for this application of a chi-squared test is the coin experiment. The null hypothesis claims the coin is fair, i.e. $P_{\text {heads }}=P_{\text {tails }}=\frac{1}{2}$. The coin is tossed $N$ times and heads occurs $n_{1}$ and tails $n_{2}$ times. In total $N=n_{1}+n_{2}$. The chi-squared value is

$$
x_{0}=\frac{\left(n_{1}-\frac{N}{2}\right)^{2}}{\frac{N}{2}}+\frac{\left(n_{2}-\frac{N}{2}\right)^{2}}{\frac{N}{2}}=\frac{\left(2 n_{1}-N\right)^{2}}{N} .
$$

The number of degrees of freedom is $v=2-1=1$. In this example a right-tailed test in $x_{0}$ is presumably adequate; we would also accept small values of $x_{0}$ as it would be difficult to find a sensible alternative hypothesis that could explain small deviations from the theoretical value $n=\frac{N}{2}$ better than the null hypothesis. Hence the data will be rejected if $x_{0}>x^{\mathrm{cr}}$, or alternatively if

$$
n_{1}<\frac{1}{2}\left(N-\sqrt{x^{\mathrm{cr}} N}\right) \quad \text { or } \quad n_{1}>\frac{1}{2}\left(N+\sqrt{x^{\mathrm{cr}} N}\right) .
$$

According to Table 19.2 the corresponding critical value for e.g. $\alpha=0.1$ is $x_{1 \%}^{\mathrm{cr}}=6.63$. For a sample of size $N$ that means the hypothesis is rejected if

$$
n_{1}>62 \text { or } n_{1}<38
$$

Now we want to generalize the test on distributions if none or not all parameters are specified. As outlined before, the unspecified parameters are replaced by their ML estimates. We use the ML values to determine the theoretical mean occupation

$$

$$

and compute the chi-squared value as before. The number of degrees of freedom is reduced by the number $N_{p}$ of ML-estimated parameters

$$

One degree of freedom is lost by the sum rule $N=\sum_{i} n_{i}=\sum_{i}\left\langle n_{i}\right\rangle$.

## Contingency tables

Another scope of application of the chi-squared test are the so-called contingency tables. The issue covered is as follows. We measure two properties $x$ and $y$, which, for simplicity, shall only assume discrete values $x_{1}, x_{2}, \ldots, x_{l}$, or rather $y_{1}, y_{2}, \ldots, y_{k}$. The experiment is repeated $N$ times. The number of experiments that yield the data pair $\left(x_{i}, y_{j}\right)$ is $n_{i j}$.

We fill the contingency table with these numbers, as illustrated in Table 19.4. We have introduced the following abbreviations:

$$
\begin{aligned}
n_{i, .} & =\sum_{j} n_{i j}, \\
n_{., j} & =\sum_{i} n_{i j}, \\
N & =\sum_{i j} n_{i j} .
\end{aligned}
$$

We denote the probability for the occurrence of the value $x_{i}$ by $p_{i}$ and that for the value $y_{j}$ by $q_{j}$. The goal is to test whether there are correlations between the $x$ and $y$ values.

To this end the null hypothesis is
$H$ : The data are uncorrelated.

That means

$$
P\left(x_{i}, y_{j}\right)=p_{i} q_{j} .
$$

If the hypothesis is correct, the average entry in the contingency table for the pair ( $x_{i} y_{j}$ ) should be $N p_{i} q_{j}$. Unfortunately, we don't know the probabilities $p_{i}$ and $q_{j}$ and we have to determine them via maximum likelihood. The likelihood function is

$$
p(\boldsymbol{n} \mid \boldsymbol{p}, \boldsymbol{q}, N) \propto \prod_{i j}\left(p_{i} q_{j}\right)^{n_{i j}},
$$

where the bold symbols stand for the set of corresponding values. We obtain the ML solution by the zeros of the derivative of the log-likelihood subject to the normalization constraints

$$
\sum_{i=1}^{l} p_{i}=1 \quad \text { and } \quad \sum_{j=1}^{k} q_{j}=1 .
$$

The constraints are incorporated via the Lagrange method and the condition for the ML solution is

$$
\frac{\partial}{\partial p_{\nu}}\left(\sum_{i j} n_{i j} \ln \left(p_{i} \cdot q_{j}\right)-\lambda_{1} \sum_{i} p_{i}-\lambda_{2} \sum_{j} q_{j}\right)=\frac{\sum_{j} n_{\nu j}}{p_{\nu}}-\lambda_{1} \stackrel{!}{=} 0 .
$$

Repeating the calculation for $q_{\mu}$ and utilizing the normalization condition leads to the desired result

$$
\begin{aligned}
& p_{\nu}^{\mathrm{ML}}=\frac{1}{N} n_{\nu, .}, \\
& q_{\mu}^{\mathrm{ML}}=\frac{1}{N} n_{., \mu} .
\end{aligned}
$$

The chi-squared statistic therefore reads

$$
x=\sum_{i j} \frac{\left(n_{i j}-N p_{i}^{\mathrm{ML}} q_{j}^{\mathrm{ML}}\right)^{2}}{N\left(p_{i}^{\mathrm{ML}} q_{j}^{\mathrm{ML}}\right)} .
$$

The number of degrees of freedom is reduced from $L-1$, as we have determined the probabilities $p_{i}$ and $q_{j}$. In principle, there are in total $k+l$ unknown parameters, but since both sets of probabilities add up to one, the reduction is only $k+l-2$. In total we have

$$
v=l \cdot k-1-k-l+2=(l-1)(k-1) .
$$

In summary, the chi-squared test for contingency tables is

## Example: Are two histograms based on the same probability distribution?

Suppose we want to test whether two histograms originate from the same unknown distribution, however, without making any assumptions about the underlying distribution. In other words, any set of probabilities $\left\{P_{i}\right\}$ is acceptable. The situation is actually closely related to that described earlier in this section, where data are tested for a particular distribution. There the data are confronted with the ML prediction, as summarized in Table 19.3. As a matter of fact, this is indeed a contingency table, which has the special feature, however, that the row sums are equal. In the case of two arbitrary histograms, the sum of the two rows, $N_{\alpha}$ say, will be different. The test statistic for contingency tables, given in equation (19.48), simplifies if two histograms are compared:

$$
\begin{equation*}
x=\sum_{\alpha=1}^{2} \sum_{j=1}^{k} \frac{\left(n_{\alpha, j}-N_{\alpha} q_{j}^{\mathrm{ML}}\right)^{2}}{N_{\alpha} q_{j}^{\mathrm{ML}}}=\sum_{\alpha=1}^{2} N_{\alpha} \sum_{j=1}^{k} \frac{\left(f_{j}^{(\alpha)}-q_{j}^{\mathrm{ML}}\right)^{2}}{q_{j}^{\mathrm{ML}}} \tag{19.49a}
\end{equation*}
$$

with

$$
\begin{equation*}
q_{j}^{\mathrm{ML}}=\frac{\sum_{\alpha=1}^{2} n_{\alpha, j}}{N_{1}+N_{2}} \quad \text { and } \quad f_{j}^{(\alpha)}:=\frac{n_{\alpha, j}}{N_{\alpha}} . \tag{19.49b}

$$

In the previous test we assumed the data are Poissonian and, after ML determination of the unknown parameters, the null hypothesis implied that the second row in Table 19.3 contains the correct data and the first row is tested against it. Now, however, we no longer prefer one row over the other and we only assess the mutual proximity. Apparently, as expected, the test is much less stringent and the data are not significant (to reject the null hypothesis). So we cannot say that the two histograms are different. For that decision the data have to diverge on a different scale.

The basic assumptions underlying the $t$-test are the same as for the $z$-test, i.e. a sample of i.i.n.d. random numbers. The only difference is that the variance is unknown. Possible applications are the same as for the $z$-test.

One common application is to test whether a particular $\mu_{0}$ is the true mean. The Student $t$-test statistic for this case has been given in equation (19.31) [p. 301]:

$$
\begin{aligned}
t_{0} & =\frac{\bar{x}-\xi}{\mathrm{SE}^{*}} \\
\mathrm{SE}^{* 2} & =\frac{\sum_{i=1}^{N}\left(x_{i}-\bar{x}\right)^{2}}{N(N-1)}, \quad \bar{x}=\frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^{N} x_{i} .

$$

### 19.7.5 F-Test

As we have seen in Section 19.5 [p. 302], the F-statistic is invoked to test the null hypothesis
$H_{0}$ : Two samples have the same variance
based on two i.i.n.d. samples $\left\{d_{1}^{(\alpha)}, \ldots, d_{N_{\alpha}}^{(\alpha)}\right\}$ which have a common variance. The intrinsic variance is not given, nor are the individual means further specified. The test statistic of the $F$-test is the ratio of the unbiased sample variances

$$
\begin{align*}
f & =\frac{\left(\sigma_{1}^{*}\right)^{2}}{\left(\sigma_{2}^{*}\right)^{2}}=\frac{n_{2} v_{1}}{n_{1} v_{2}}  \tag{19.50}\\
v_{\alpha} & =\sum_{i=1}^{N_{\alpha}}\left(d_{i}^{(\alpha)}-\bar{d}_{\alpha}\right)^{2}
\end{align*}
$$

We have shown in Section 19.5 [p. 302] that $f$ is distributed as $p_{F}\left(f \mid n_{1}, n_{2}\right)$, where $n_{\alpha}= N_{\alpha}-1$ is the number of degrees of freedom in sample $\alpha$. One degree of freedom is lost in order to fix the unknown mean. Otherwise, the means do not enter the analysis.

## Example: Is an instrument still calibrated?

The $F$-test shall be applied to the question outlined in Section 17.2.4 [p. 275], of whether an instrument is still calibrated, evidenced by a variance ratio of 1 . We used two series of measurements of sizes $N_{1}=N_{2}=20$. The null hypothesis claimed
$H$ : The variance of the experimental noise is the same in both data sets.
The estimates for the standard deviations were $\sigma_{1}^{*}=2.5$ and $\sigma_{2}^{*}=4.4$, which implied $f_{0}=3.1$. In this context it is reasonable to use a two-tailed test as deviations in both directions from $f=1$ indicate likewise that the hypothesis is incorrect.

Since the $F$-distribution is invariant against the interchange of the sample variances, the $P$ value for the two-sided test is

$$
P=2 \cdot \int_{f_{0}}^{\infty} p_{F}\left(f \mid n_{1}, n_{2}\right) d f=0.018
$$

Hence, based on $\alpha=5 \%$ the data are significant and based on $\alpha=1 \%$ the data are not significant. So the result is almost balanced and a decision in either direction is risky. Consequently, in this example the significance of the $F$-test is marginal.

This is in stark contrast to the Bayesian result, derived in Section 17.2.4 [p. 275], which highly favours the hypothesis $(P(H \mid D, \mathcal{I})=0.99)$ leading to the conclusion that the measurement apparatus is still working perfectly well.

## Comparison of Bayesian and frequentist hypothesis tests

In previous chapters we have applied the Bayesian and the frequentist approach to some basic problems. The key differences of the two approaches are summarized in Table 20.1. The last two rows deserve additional remarks, which will be given in the following sections.

### 20.1 Prior knowledge is prior data

In scientific problems the present data are certainly not the only information that is known for the problem under consideration. Usually, there exists a wealth of knowledge in the form of previous experimental data and theoretical facts, such as positivity constraints, sum rules, asymptotic behaviour. A scientist is never in the situation that only the current data count. The Bayesian approach allows us to exploit all this information consistently. It has been criticized that priors are a subjective element of the theory. This is not really correct, as the Bayesian approach is internally consistent and deterministic. The only part that could be described as subjective is the knowledge that goes into the prior probabilities. But this degree of subjectivity is actually the foundation of all science. It is the expertise that exists in the respective discipline. The generation of experimental data is based on the same subjectivity, as it is generally motivated by prior knowledge in the form of previous data or theoretical models.

A great part of the prior knowledge is a summary of a conglomeration of previous measurements, with completely different meanings and sources of statistical errors. The Bayesian approach is proved to be consistent and it is no surprise that it allows a systematic and consistent treatment of these data in the form of prior probabilities. Suppose there is a new data set $D$ and a collection of old data sets $\left\{D_{i}\right\}$ collected over the years. It is just simply a matter of applying the product rule to determine the odds ratio for the null hypothesis:

$$
\begin{aligned}
o\left(H_{0} \mid D, D_{1}, \ldots, D_{N}, \mathcal{I}\right) & =o\left(D \mid H_{0}, D_{1}, \ldots, D_{N}, \mathcal{I}\right) o\left(H_{0} \mid D_{1}, \ldots, D_{N}, \mathcal{I}\right) \\
& =o\left(D \mid H_{0}, \mathcal{I}\right) o\left(H_{0} \mid D_{1}, \ldots, D_{N}, \mathcal{I}\right)
\end{aligned}
$$

The previous data sets are immaterial in the first factor as it is a crucial feature of the null hypothesis that it uniquely fixes the likelihood. The second factor is nothing but

If all data samples are properly accounted for they will predominate the result, unless there is a contradiction (e.g. if the data favour a negative value for a model parameter, while the theory tells us that it has to be positive).

### 20.2 Dependence on the stopping criterion

We have already addressed the impact of the stopping criterion on the likelihood estimator in Section 15.4 [p. 241]. Here we will deepen the discussion and study the impact of the stopping rule on hypothesis testing. The original idea, outlined here, traces back to L. J. Savage [60] and is discussed in more detail in [13]. We use a similar problem based on Bernoulli experiments. Let $H_{0}$ be the hypothesis that the probability for an individual trial is $p_{0}$. Based on the data pair ( $n, N$ ), which is the number of successes $n$ and total trials $N$, we want to scrutinize the validity of the null hypothesis $H_{0}$.

So far it sounds very familiar, but this time the data shall be generated based on different stopping conditions: the experiment was stopped (a) after $N$ trials, or (b) when the $n$th success was observed.

### 20.2.1 Bayesian analysis

The Bayesian approach starts in both cases with the odds ratio

$$
o=\frac{P\left(n, N \mid H_{0}, \mathcal{I}_{\alpha}\right)}{P\left(n, N \mid \bar{H}_{0}, \mathcal{I}_{\alpha}\right)} \frac{P\left(H_{0} \mid \mathcal{I}_{\alpha}\right)}{P\left(\overline{H_{0}} \mid \mathcal{I}_{\alpha}\right)},
$$

where the background information $\mathcal{I}_{\alpha}$ for $\alpha \in\{a, b\}$ encodes the information about the stopping criterion. First of all, the choice of the stopping condition definitely has no influence on our prior knowledge about $H_{0}$. In other words, the odds ratio is independent of $H_{0}$. For the Bayes factor we investigate the two cases separately.

## Predefined sample size

The likelihood conditional on $H_{0}$ is

$$
P\left(n, N \mid H_{0}, B_{a}\right)=P\left(n \mid N, H_{0}, B_{a}\right) \underbrace{P\left(N \mid H_{0}, B_{a}\right)}_{=1} .
$$

The second factor is equal to 1 , as for the predefined sample size $N$ it is the true proposition. The first factor is simply the binomial distribution, from which we merely need the $p_{0}$ dependence. We therefore write

$$
P\left(n, N \mid H_{0}, B_{a}\right)=C_{a} p_{0}^{n}\left(1-p_{0}\right)^{N-n} .
$$

For the marginal likelihood, conditioned on the complementary hypothesis $\bar{H}_{0}$, we employ the marginalization rule in order to introduce the unknown success probability $p$, resulting in

$$
P\left(n, N \mid \bar{H}_{0}, B_{a}\right)=C_{a} \underbrace{\int p^{n}(1-p)^{N-n} p(p \mid B) d p}_{:=\mathcal{I}} .
$$

The Bayes factor is therefore

$$
\begin{align*}
o_{\mathrm{BF}, a} & =\frac{p_{0}^{n}\left(1-p_{0}\right)^{N-n}}{\mathcal{I}}  \tag{20.1a}\\
\mathcal{I} & =\int p^{n}(1-p)^{N-n} p(p \mid B) d p \tag{20.1b}
\end{align*}
$$

## Predefined number of successes

In this case the likelihood is

$$
P\left(n, N \mid H_{0}, B_{a}\right)=P\left(N \mid n, H_{0}, B_{b}\right) \underbrace{P\left(n \mid H_{0}, B_{b}\right)}_{=1} .
$$

Here $n$ is the true proposition, as the number of successes is predefined. The first factor is the probability distribution for the stopping time, discussed in Section 15.4 [p. 241]. The probability is, according to equation (15.11a) [p. 243],

$$
P\left(N \mid n, H_{0}, B_{b}\right)=C_{b} p_{0}^{n}\left(1-p_{0}\right)^{N-n} .
$$

As a matter of fact, it has the same $p_{0}$ dependence. The marginal likelihood required in case of $\overline{H_{0}}$ therefore reads

$$
P\left(N \mid n, \overline{H_{0}}, B_{b}\right)=C_{b} \mathcal{I} .
$$

So the Bayes factor is the same as in the first case (equation (20.1)):

$$
o_{\mathrm{BF}, b}=\frac{C_{b} p_{0}^{n}\left(1-p_{0}\right)^{N-n}}{C_{b} \mathcal{I}}=o_{\mathrm{BF}, a} .
$$

We see that the Bayesian inference does not care about the stopping criterion used. What is the underlying deeper reason why the stopping criterion is irrelevant in the Bayesian approach? It is the fact that the parameter dependence of the likelihood is independent of the choice of the stopping condition. Generally speaking, the likelihood for some data $\boldsymbol{D}$, given all required parameters $\boldsymbol{a}$, has the form

$$
p\left(\boldsymbol{D} \mid \boldsymbol{a}, \mathcal{I}_{\alpha}\right)=f\left(\boldsymbol{D}, \mathcal{I}_{a}\right) g(\boldsymbol{a}, \boldsymbol{D})
$$

Consequently, the posterior probability for the parameter(s) is also not affected by the stopping condition, as the prefactor $f$ drops out by the normalization. Similarly, in hypothesis testing, $f$ drops out of the odds ratio, as it occurs both in $p\left(\boldsymbol{D} \mid H_{0}, \mathcal{I}_{\alpha}\right)$ and $p\left(\boldsymbol{D} \mid \overline{H_{0}}, \mathcal{I}_{\alpha}\right)$.

### 20.2.2 Frequentist hypothesis testing

Now let's see how the frequentist approach deals with the stopping criterion. Typically, hypothesis testing is based on the $P$ value, which we will determine for the two cases. The point null hypothesis is: the single-trial probability is $p_{0}$.

## $P$ value if the sample size is predefined

If the sample size $N$ is predefined and the null hypothesis specifies $p_{0}$, then the expected number of successes and their variance is known. A reasonable measure for the discrepancy between measurement and theory (test statistic) is therefore

$$
\begin{aligned}
\xi(n) & :=\frac{|n-\langle n\rangle|}{\sigma_{n}} \\
\langle n\rangle & =N p \\
\sigma_{n}^{2} & =N p_{0}\left(1-p_{0}\right)
\end{aligned}
$$

We use the modulus since a two-tailed test appears adequate. The PDF for $\xi$ (sampling distribution) is

$$
\begin{aligned}
p\left(\xi \mid p_{0}, N, \mathcal{I}\right) & =\sum_{n=0}^{N} p\left(\xi \mid n, p_{0}, N, \mathcal{I}\right) P(n \mid p, N, \mathcal{I}) \\
& =\sum_{n=0}^{N} \delta[\xi-\xi(n)] P\left(n \mid p_{0}, N, \mathcal{I}\right)
\end{aligned}
$$

Given the experimental count $n^{*}$, the value of the test statistic is $\xi^{*}=\xi\left(n^{*}\right)$ and the $P$ value becomes

$$
P=\int_{\xi^{*}}^{\infty} p(\xi \mid p, N, \mathcal{I}) d \xi=\sum_{n=0}^{N} P(n \mid p, N, \mathcal{I}) \int_{\xi^{*}}^{\infty} \delta(\xi-\xi(n)) d \xi
$$

The inequality $\xi \geq \xi^{*}$ corresponds to $|n-\langle n\rangle| \geq\left|n^{*}-\langle n\rangle\right|:=\Delta n^{*}$. The sum over $n$ is therefore restricted to either $n \geq\langle n\rangle+\Delta n^{*}$ or $n \geq\langle n\rangle-\Delta n^{*}$, resulting in

$$
P=\sum_{n=0}^{\langle n\rangle-\Delta n^{*}} P(n \mid p, N, n, \mathcal{I})+\sum_{n=\langle n\rangle+\Delta n^{*}}^{N} P(n \mid p, N, n, \mathcal{I})
$$

As a numerical example we use $N=100, n^{*}=5$ and $p_{0}=0.1$. Then $\langle n\rangle=10$ and $\sigma_{n}=3$. The resulting $P$ value is $P=0.025$. Based on a significance level of $5 \%$, say, we would reject the hypothesis. At first glance, the decision appears reasonable, as the measured value $n^{*}$ lies clearly outside the $1-\sigma$ interval.

## $P$ value if the success count is predefined

Now we repeat the analysis for the other stopping criterion in which $N$ is the random variable. It obeys the negative binomial distribution with $\langle N\rangle=n / p_{0}$ and $\sigma_{N}^{2}=n(1- \left.p_{0}\right) / p_{0}^{2}$, according to equation (4.35) [p. 70]. In this case, a reasonable test statistic is

$$
\eta(N):=\frac{|N-\langle N\rangle|}{\sigma_{N}^{2}}
$$

The corresponding sampling distribution is

$$
p(\eta \mid p, n, \mathcal{I})=\sum_{N=n}^{\infty} \delta(\eta-\eta(n)) P(N \mid p, n, \mathcal{I})
$$

For the experimental count $N^{*}$ the value of the test statistic is $\eta^{*}=\eta\left(N^{*}\right)$, and the $P$ value becomes

$$
p=\int_{\eta^{*}}^{\infty} p(\eta \mid p, n, \mathcal{I}) d \eta=\sum_{N=n}^{\infty} P(N \mid p, n, \mathcal{I}) \int_{\eta^{*}}^{\infty} \delta(\eta-\eta(N)) d \eta
$$

As before, the interval $\eta \geq \eta^{*}$ corresponds to either $N \geq\langle N\rangle+\Delta N^{*}$ or $N \leq\langle N\rangle-\Delta N^{*}$, with $\Delta N^{*}=\left|N^{*}-\langle N\rangle\right|$. The resulting $P$ value is given by

$$
P=\sum_{N=n}^{\langle N\rangle-\Delta N^{*}} P(N \mid p, n, \mathcal{I})+\sum_{n=\langle N\rangle+\Delta N^{*}}^{\infty} P(N \mid p, n, \mathcal{I}) .
$$

For the same set of parameters as before, i.e. $n=5, N^{*}=100$ and $p_{0}=0.1$, we obtain $\langle N\rangle=5 / 0.1=50$ and $\Delta N^{*}=50$. Consequently, the first sum does not contribute and the second sum yields $P=0.13$. Based on the $5 \%$ significance level, the hypothesis would clearly not be ruled out. Depending on the stopping criterion, we come to contradictory conclusions about the validity of the null hypothesis $H_{0}$, although one and the same set of data is used. The dependence on the stopping condition is rather disturbing, particularly as in most publications the stopping condition is not quoted and presumably the experimenter did not even pay attention to it and might have used different criteria for different experiments.

### 20.2.3 Yet another stopping criterion

Let us consider an extreme but still legitimate stopping criterion. Suppose somebody is performing quality control and wants to determine the percentage of intact parts in a production, or in case of pharmaceutical research the percentage of successful treatments with a new drug. Generically, we are speaking of a Bernoulli experiment of $N$ repetitions and the outcome consists of $n_{s}$ successes and $n_{f}$ failures. The goal is to assess the hypothesis that the failure rate $\frac{n_{f}}{N}$ is below a certain threshold.

The experimenter could, for whatever reason, decide to stop the experiment when the number of successes exceeds the number of failures (for the first time). However, if that does not happen within a reasonable number of trials, $N_{\text {max }}$ say, he stops in any case.

The reported output is given by $\left\{n_{s}, n_{f}\right\}$, i.e. the number of successes and failures. In case of the first stopping condition $n_{s}=n_{f}+1$, otherwise $n_{s}+N_{f}=N_{\max }$.
