In this chapter, we consider a few random walk problems that are of great importance in statistical physics and in different guises for data analysis. The simplest one has already been discussed before. These problems also allow us to get acquainted with the various combinatorial tools discussed so far, and with the concept of generating functions. A thorough analysis of a wide range of random walk problems, along with useful mathematical tools, can be found in [218].

5.1 First return

As a first example we consider a one-dimensional random walk and determine the probability that a walker returns to his initial position for the first time after \(n\) steps. This probability allows us to answer the question of how long a random walk takes to come back to the origin and whether this will happen at all. The number of steps \(n\) to return to the origin is apparently even, \(n=2 m\). In addition, we consider the probability that the walker returns to his initial position after \(n\) steps, no matter if it is the first return. We introduce the corresponding propositions > \(f_{n}\) : First return after \(n\) steps. > \(r_{n}\) : Return after \(n\) steps.

The sought-for probability is \(P\left(f_{n} \mid p, \mathcal{I}\right)\), while the probability for the arbitrary return is \(P\left(r_{n} \mid p, \mathcal{I}\right)\). The latter probability is simply a binomial distribution

\[ P\left(r_{2 m} \mid p, \mathcal{I}\right)=P\left(N_{r}=m \mid N=2 m, p, \mathcal{I}\right)=\binom{2 m}{m} p^{m} q^{m} \]

The value of this probability for \(m=0\) is \(P\left(r_{0} \mid p, \mathcal{I}\right)=1\), which is alright, we only have to stretch the meaning of ‘return’. For \(f_{n}\), however, return really means that the walker has to leave his initial position first, so \(P\left(f_{0} \mid p, \mathcal{I}\right) \stackrel{!}{=} 0\). The corresponding generating function of \(P\left(r_{2 m} \mid p, \mathcal{I}\right)\) is

\[ \phi_{r}(z)=\sum_{m=0}^{\infty} P\left(r_{2 m} \mid p, \mathcal{I}\right) z^{2 m}=\sum_{m=0}^{\infty}\binom{2 m}{m}\left(p q z^{2}\right)^{m} \]

In order to simplify the characteristic function, it is advantageous to express the binomial coefficient with the negative binomial as outlined in Appendix equation (A.23) [p. 605]:

\[ \binom{2 m}{m}=2^{2 m}(-1)^{m}\binom{-\frac{1}{2}}{m} . \]

Hence, we have

\[ \phi_{r}(z)=\sum_{m=0}^{\infty}\binom{-\frac{1}{2}}{m}\left(-4 p q z^{2}\right)^{m}=\left(1-4 p q z^{2}\right)^{-1 / 2} \]

The sum over all propositions yields

\[ \begin{equation*} \sum_{m=0}^{\infty} P\left(r_{2 m} \mid p, \mathcal{I}\right)=\phi(z=1)=\left.\left(1-4 p q z^{2}\right)^{-\frac{1}{2}}\right|_{z=1}=(1-4 p q)^{-\frac{1}{2}} . \tag{5.1} \end{equation*} \]

The result can exceed 1 , since the propositions are not mutually exclusive. Next, we decompose the proposition \(r_{2 m}\) such that we are able to compute the probability for \(f_{2 n}\). An arbitrary return at time \(2 m\), for \(m \geq 1\), implies that the walker returns for the first time after \(2 n\) steps and in the remaining time \(2(m-n)\) returns again (not necessarily for the first time). A special case is given if \(n=m\), i.e. the walker returns only once. In mathematical terms, we introduce the mutually exclusive propositions \(f_{2 n}\) via the marginalization rule, and obtain for \(m \geq 1\)

\[ \begin{aligned} P\left(r_{2 m} \mid p, \mathcal{I}\right) & =\sum_{l=1}^{m} P\left(r_{2 m} \mid f_{2 l}, p, \mathcal{I}\right) P\left(f_{2 l} \mid p, \mathcal{I}\right) \\ & =\sum_{l=1}^{m} P\left(r_{2(m-l)} \mid p, \mathcal{I}\right) P\left(f_{2 l} \mid p, \mathcal{I}\right) \end{aligned} \]

The last term of the sum, i.e. the one for \(l=m\), yields the correct contribution, since \(P\left(r_{0} \mid p, \mathcal{I}\right)=1\). As we have defined \(P\left(f_{0} \mid p, \mathcal{I}\right)=0\), we can extend the sum on the righthand side and include \(l=0\) as well, resulting in a complete formula for the convolution. For the case \(m=0\), the equation does not hold. In order to cover also this limiting case we add a Kronecker \(\delta_{m, 0}\) on the right-hand side and end up with the expression, which is now valid for \(m \geq 0\)

\[ P\left(r_{2 m} \mid p, \mathcal{I}\right)=\sum_{l=0}^{m} P\left(r_{2(m-l)} \mid p, \mathcal{I}\right) P\left(f_{2 l} \mid p, \mathcal{I}\right)+\delta_{m, 0} \]

The generating function on the left-hand side is \(\phi_{r}(z)\). The generating function on the right-hand side is the sum of the characteristic function of the convolution and that of the Kronecker \(\delta\). The latter is 1 and we obtain

\[ \phi_{r}(z)=\phi_{r}(z) \cdot \phi_{f}(z)+1 \]

or rather

\[ \begin{align*} & \phi_{f}(z)=1-\phi_{r}(z)^{-1} \tag{5.2a}\\ & \phi_{f}(z) \stackrel{(5.1)}{=} 1-\sqrt{1-4 p q z^{2}} \tag{5.2b} \end{align*} \]

From the characteristic function we conclude a couple of important results. First of all, the probability that the walker returns at all, either after 2,4 , or any other even number of steps, is equal to the normalization, i.e. \(\phi_{f}(z=1)=1-\sqrt{1-4 p q}\), and hence the probability that the walker never returns to his initial position is \(\sqrt{1-4 p q}\). For \(p \neq q\) this probability is greater than zero, which is plausible since in that case there is a drift/bias in the random walk. For \(p=q=\frac{1}{2}\), the return probability is 1 . Hence, the walker will definitely return to his initial position. An interesting quantity then is the mean waiting time for the first return, or rather the mean number of steps up to the first return:

\[ \langle m\rangle=\left.\frac{\partial}{\partial z} \phi_{f}(z)\right|_{z=1}=\left.2 p q(1-4 p q)^{-1 / 2}\right|_{p=q=\frac{1}{2}}=\infty . \]

Although the walker will definitely return, on average it takes him an eternity. Next we will analyse the result in more detail. To this end we invoke Newton’s binomial theorem:

\[ \Phi_{f}(z)=1-\sqrt{1-4 p q z^{2}}=1-\sum_{n=0}^{\infty}\binom{\frac{1}{2}}{n}\left(-4 p q z^{2}\right)^{n} . \]

Comparison with the definition of the generating function

\[ \Phi_{f}(z)=\sum_{m=0}^{\infty} p\left(f_{2 m} \mid p, \mathcal{I}\right) z^{2 m} \]

reveals

\[ \text { for } m=0: \quad \begin{aligned} P\left(f_{0} \mid p, \mathcal{I}\right) & =0 \\ \text { for } m>0: \quad P\left(f_{2 m} \mid p, \mathcal{I}\right) & =-\binom{\frac{1}{2}}{m}(-4 p q)^{m} \\ & \stackrel{\text { (A.24a) }}{=}\binom{2 m}{m}(-4)^{-m} \frac{1}{2 m-1}(-4 p q)^{m} \\ & =\frac{1}{2 m-1} P_{\mathrm{B}}(m \mid N=2 m, p, \mathcal{I}) \\ & =\frac{1}{m \gg 1} \frac{(4 p q)^{m}}{2 m-1} \frac{\text { see (6.8) }}{\sqrt{4 \pi p q m}} \end{aligned} \]

We are merely interested in the case \(p=q=1 / 2\). In this case we have, for \(m \gg 1\) :

\[ P\left(f_{2 m} \mid p, \mathcal{I}\right)=\frac{1}{2 m} \frac{1}{\sqrt{\pi m}} \quad \text { see (6.8). } \]

Apparently, in this case the median is \(\breve{m}=1\) and the third quartile, which can be obtained by numerical inspection, is at \(m \approx 5\). Nonetheless, the mean value \(\langle m\rangle\) diverges, since for large \(m\) the probability decays only as \(m^{-\frac{3}{2}}\).

5.1.1 First return in 2-D and 3-D

Interestingly, equation (5.2a) is independent of the spatial dimension and we will use it for the computation of the first-return probability for two and three dimensions. We will only consider the isotropic case, with equal hopping probability in each direction. The explicit mention of this probability in the conditional part of the probabilities will be omitted. The key quantity that we need to compute is the generating function \(\phi_{r}(z=1)\), which could be determined similarly as in the 1-D case. However, there is actually a more elegant approach, which is particularly preferable for higher dimensions. To begin with, we consider the probability \(P_{n}(\boldsymbol{x})\) that the walker will cover a distance \(\boldsymbol{x}\) in \(n\) steps, no matter how often the point \(\boldsymbol{x}\) occurs throughout this journey. Based on the marginalization rule we can derive a simple recursion relation

\[ \begin{equation*} P_{n}(x)=\sum_{\Delta x} P_{n-1}(x-\Delta x) P_{1}(\Delta x) . \tag{5.3} \end{equation*} \]

Next we introduce the Fourier transform of these terms, i.e.

\[ \begin{equation*} \tilde{P}_{n}(\boldsymbol{k}):=\sum_{\boldsymbol{x}} e^{i \boldsymbol{k} \boldsymbol{x}} P_{n}(\boldsymbol{x}), \quad \varepsilon(\boldsymbol{k}):=\sum_{\boldsymbol{x}} e^{i \boldsymbol{k} \boldsymbol{x}} P_{1}(\boldsymbol{x}), \tag{5.4} \end{equation*} \]

where the sum runs over all points of the simple cubic lattice of size \(N=L^{D}\), with \(D\) being the spatial dimension of the lattice. Throughout the calculation we assume a finite lattice and at the end we will consider \(N \rightarrow \infty\). Moreover, we assume periodic boundary conditions, which have no influence on the result for the infinite lattice but simplify the calculations. In the statistical context the Fourier transform is also called the ‘characteristic function’. It will be discussed in more detail in Section 7.8 [p. 124], where we will also prove that the characteristic function of a convolution, i.e. an expression like equation (5.3), is simply the product of the characteristic functions of the individual factors. Then

\[ \tilde{P}_{n}(\boldsymbol{k})=\tilde{P}_{n-1}(\boldsymbol{k}) \tilde{P}_{1}(\boldsymbol{k})=[\varepsilon(\boldsymbol{k})]^{n} . \]

The inverse Fourier transform immediately yields the aspired probability

\[ P_{n}(x)=\frac{1}{N} \sum_{k}^{1 . \text { b.z. }} e^{-i k x}[\varepsilon(k)]^{n}, \]

where the sum covers the first Brillouin zone and the wavevectors are \(k_{\alpha}=\frac{2 \pi}{L} n_{\alpha}\). Since we are interested in an infinite lattice, we can now replace the sum by an integral and we also restrict the further discussion to \(\boldsymbol{x}=\mathbf{0}\), relevant for the first-return probability

\[ P_{n}(\boldsymbol{x})=\frac{1}{(2 \pi)^{D}} \int_{-\pi}^{\pi} d^{D} k[\varepsilon(\boldsymbol{k})]^{n} \]

We employ this expression for the evaluation of the generating function \(\phi_{r}(z=1)\) :

\[ \begin{align*} \phi_{r}(z=1) & =\sum_{n=0}^{\infty} P_{n}(\mathbf{0})=\frac{1}{(2 \pi)^{D}} \int_{-\pi}^{\pi} d^{D} k \sum_{n=0}^{\infty}[\varepsilon(\boldsymbol{k})]^{n} \\ & =\frac{1}{(2 \pi)^{D}} \int_{-\pi}^{\pi} d^{D} k \frac{1}{1-\varepsilon(\boldsymbol{k})} . \tag{5.5} \end{align*} \]

Finally, we compute \(\varepsilon(\boldsymbol{k})\) :

\[ \varepsilon(\boldsymbol{k})=\sum_{\boldsymbol{x}}^{i \boldsymbol{k} \boldsymbol{x}} P_{1}(\boldsymbol{x})=\frac{1}{2 D} \sum_{\alpha=1}^{D}\left(e^{i k_{\alpha}}+e^{-i k_{\alpha}}\right)=\frac{1}{D} \sum_{\alpha=1}^{D} \cos \left(k_{\alpha}\right) . \]

Apart from the prefactor this is the tight-binding dispersion, for which the density of states (dos) can be found in the literature. Now the numerical evaluation of the integral in equation (5.5) is straightforward and not really demanding, but it is worth mentioning that there is an even more elegant way to proceed. As standard in solid-state physics, we introduce the density of states (dos) \(\rho(\varepsilon)\), which reduces the integral in equation (5.5) to a onedimensional integral

\[ \begin{equation*} \phi_{r}(z=1)=\int_{-1}^{1} d \varepsilon \frac{\rho(\varepsilon)}{1-\varepsilon} \xi, \quad \rho(\varepsilon):=\frac{1}{(2 \pi)^{D}} \int_{-\pi}^{\pi} d^{D} k \delta(\varepsilon-\varepsilon(\boldsymbol{k})) \tag{5.6} \end{equation*} \]

The dos for the 2-D lattice can be found in [59]. For our purposes, we merely need that \(\rho(\varepsilon)\) is finite for \(\varepsilon \rightarrow D\), resulting in a logarithmic divergence and \(\phi_{r}^{(2-D)}(z=1)=\infty\). According to equation (5.2a), we find \(\phi_{f}^{(2-D)}(z=1)=1\). In other words, the walker will definitely return to his starting position.

The situation is different in three spatial dimensions, because the dos now vanishes at the band edges \(\varepsilon \rightarrow \pm 1\), and the integral in equation (5.6) yields a finite value. For the detailed calculation we employed the dos expressed in terms of complete elliptic integrals, as reported in [99]. Straightforward numerical integration of the integral yields \(\phi_{r}(z=1)=1.516\), resulting in \(\phi_{f}(z=1)=0.341\). Apparently, it is now very likely ( \(P=\) 0.659 ) that the walker will never return to the origin of his journey. Hence a well-meant piece of advice in [91] reads: ‘one should not get drunk in more than two dimensions’.

5.2 First lead

Next we will study a related question, which will become important in the context of hypothesis testing. Given a Bernoulli experiment with two alternative outcomes called success and failure, what is the probability that it takes \(N\) trials before the total number of successes exceeds for the first time the total number of the failures. We call this event first lead. It is expedient to translate the problem into the random walk (Brownian motion) language. The problem of first lead is equivalent to a random walk with up (success) and down (failure) moves, and we seek the probability that the random walk occurs above the abscissa for the first time at step \(N\). For the analysis we introduce the number \(n_{u}\) of up steps and the number \(n_{d}\) of down steps. The total number is \(N=n_{u}+n_{d}\). Clearly, the ‘first lead’ can only occur for an odd number \(N=2 L+1\) of trials and, right before the first lead, the random walk has to be balanced, i.e. \(n_{u}=n_{d}\). This return event need not be the first, but the walker shall never cross the abscissa before. We call this return from below in \(2 L\) steps. Therefore, the probability for the first lead in \(N=2 L+1\) steps is equal to the probability ( \(p\) ) for the final step upwards times the probability for a return from below in \(2 L\) steps.

Now we define the relevant propositions:

\[ \begin{aligned} L_{2 L+1} & : \text { First lead at step } 2 L+1 . \\ F_{2 L}^{b} & : \text { First return from below at step } 2 L . \end{aligned} \]

The probability for the ‘first return from below’ in \(2 L\) steps is \((p q)^{L}\) times the number of paths from \((0,0)\) to \((2 L, 0)\) that are never above the abscissa. This number is equivalent to the number \(\mathcal{N}\left(y_{0}, y_{N} \mid y<0, N=2 L, \mathcal{I}\right)\) of paths from \((0,-1)\) to \((2 L,-1)\) that never touch the abscissa. The sought-for probability is therefore

\[ \begin{equation*} P\left(L_{2 L+1} \mid p, \mathcal{I}\right)=p^{L+1} q^{L} \mathcal{N}\left(y_{0}, y_{N} \mid y<0, N=2 L, \mathcal{I}\right) . \tag{5.7} \end{equation*} \]

Such restricted random walks can best be analysed based on the ‘reflection principle’ [170] that we will briefly outline in the next section.

5.2.1 Reflection principle

Here we consider the more general case of a random walk that starts at time \(t=0\) at a negative position \(y_{0}=-\left|y_{0}^{*}\right|\) and ends again after \(N\) time steps at a negative position \(y_{N}=-\left|y_{N}^{*}\right|\). We are interested in the number of paths between these points, that never reach \(y=0\). A representative path is depicted in Figure 5.1 as a dashed curve. This number is apparently equal to the number of unrestricted paths minus the number of paths that reach \(y=0\) at least once. The number of unrestricted paths is homogeneous in \(y\), i.e.

\[ \begin{aligned} & \mathcal{N}\left(y_{0}, y_{N} \mid N, \mathcal{I}\right)=\mathcal{N}(0, \Delta y \mid N, \mathcal{I}) \\ & \Delta y:=y_{N}-y_{0} \end{aligned} \]

In order to cover a distance of \(\Delta y\) in \(N\) steps, the number \(L\) of downward steps is given by

\[ \begin{aligned} \Delta y & =(N-L)-L=N-2 L, \\ L & =\frac{N-\Delta y}{2} . \end{aligned} \]

Apparently, we have to take into account that in an even (odd) number \(N\) only an even (odd) distance can be covered. In other words, \(\Delta y\) and \(N\) need to have the same parity. So we have

Number of unrestricted paths ( \(\Delta \boldsymbol{y}\) and \(\boldsymbol{N}\) HAVE THE SAME PARITY)

\[ \begin{align*} \mathcal{N}(0, \Delta y \mid N, \mathcal{I}) & =\binom{N}{L(\Delta y)} \tag{5.8a}\\ L(\Delta y) & :=\frac{N-\Delta y}{2} \tag{5.8b} \end{align*} \]

In the present case, we have

\[ \begin{equation*} \Delta y=\Delta y^{*}:=y_{N}-y_{0}=-\left(\left|y_{N}^{*}\right|-\left|y_{0}^{*}\right|\right) . \tag{5.9} \end{equation*} \]

Next we determine the number of those paths that start at \(y_{0}=-\left|y_{0}^{*}\right|\), end at \(y_{N}=-\left|y_{N}^{*}\right|\), and which reach \(y=0\) at least once. A representative path is depicted in Figure 5.1, marked by circles. This number is easily determined based on the reflection principle [170]. To this end, we identify the initial part of the path up to the point where it first reaches the abscissa. Then we reflect this part of the path on the abscissa, as represented in Figure 5.1 with the curve marked with stars. All such reflected paths start at \(y_{0}=+\left|y_{0}^{*}\right|\) and end at \(y_{N}=-\left|y_{N}^{*}\right|\). One can easily convince oneself that there is a one-to-one correspondence between the set of restricted paths we are interested in and the set \(\mathcal{R}\) of unrestricted paths from \(y_{0}=\left|y_{0}^{*}\right|\) to \(y_{N}=-\left|y_{N}^{*}\right|\). In other words, for each restricted path there is by construction a reflected path. And for each path of the set \(\mathcal{R}\) there is a path of the original set of restricted paths that we can construct by simply applying the reflection described before. Therefore, the number \(N_{r}\) of restricted paths, starting at \(y_{0}=-\left|y_{0}^{*}\right|\) and reaching at least once \(y=0\), is equal to the number of unrestricted paths starting at \(y_{0}=\left|y_{0}^{*}\right|\) :

\[ N_{r}=\mathcal{N}\left(0,-\left|y_{N}^{*}\right|-\left|y_{0}^{*}\right| \mid N, \mathcal{I}\right)=\binom{N}{\frac{N+\left|y_{N}^{*}\right|+\left|y_{0}^{*}\right|}{2}}=\binom{N}{L\left(\Delta y^{*}\right)+\left|y_{0}^{*}\right|} . \]

Finally, the sought-for number of paths starting at \(y=-\left|y_{0}^{*}\right|\) and reaching \(y=-\left|y_{N}^{*}\right|\) in \(N\) steps avoiding the abscissa is given by

Number of abscissa avoiding paths from \(-\left|y_{0}^{*}\right|\) to \(-\left|y_{N}^{*}\right|\) IN \(N\) steps

\[ \begin{align*} \mathcal{N}\left(-\left|y_{0}^{*}\right|,-\left|y_{N}^{*}\right| \mid y<0, N, \mathcal{I}\right) & =\binom{N}{L\left(\Delta y^{*}\right)}-\binom{N}{L\left(\Delta y^{*}\right)+\left|y_{0}^{*}\right|} \tag{5.10}\\ L\left(\Delta y^{*}\right) & :=\frac{N+\left(\left|y_{N}^{*}\right|-\left|y_{0}^{*}\right|\right)}{2} . \end{align*} \]

5.2.2 First lead problem continued

Now we are prepared to compute the probability for the first lead. The key quantity in equation (5.2) is

\[ \mathcal{N}\left(y_{0}=-1, y_{N}=-1 \mid N=2 L, \mathcal{I}\right) . \]

Hence, \(\left|y_{N}^{*}\right|=\left|y_{0}^{*}\right|=1\) and we get, according to equation (5.10),

\[ \mathcal{N}\left(y_{0}=-1, y_{N}=-1 \mid N=2 L, \mathcal{I}\right)=\binom{2 L}{L}-\binom{2 L}{L+1}=\binom{2 L}{L} \frac{1}{L+1} . \]

Eventually we find

Probability for being in lead for the first time AT STEP \(N=2 L+1\)

\[ \begin{equation*} P\left(L_{2 L+1} \mid p, \mathcal{I}\right)=\binom{2 L}{L} \frac{1}{L+1} p^{L+1} q^{L} \tag{5.11} \end{equation*} \]

Next we will determine the corresponding generating function

\[ \begin{aligned} \Phi_{L}(z) & =\sum_{L=0}^{\infty}\binom{2 L}{L} \frac{1}{L+1} p^{L+1} q^{L} z^{L} \\ & =\int_{0}^{p} d x \sum_{L=0}^{\infty}\binom{2 L}{L}(x q z)^{L} \end{aligned} \]

We use equation (A.23) [p. 605] to transform the binomial coefficient

\[ \begin{aligned} \Phi_{L}(z) & =\int_{0}^{p} d x \sum_{L=0}^{\infty}\binom{-\frac{1}{2}}{L}(-4 x q z)^{L} \\ & =\int_{0}^{p} d x(1-4 x q z)^{-1 / 2} \end{aligned} \]

resulting in

Generating function FOR FIRST LEAD

\[ \begin{equation*} \Phi_{L}(z)=\frac{1}{2 q z}(1-\sqrt{1-4 p q z}) . \tag{5.12} \end{equation*} \]

The probability \(P(L \mid P, \mathcal{I})\) that a lead occurs at all is, according to equation (5.2), given by

\[ \begin{aligned} P(L \mid P, \mathcal{I}) & =\Phi_{L}(z=1)=\frac{1}{2 q}(1-\sqrt{1-4 p(1-p)}) \\ & =\frac{1}{2 q}\left(1-\sqrt{(1-2 p)^{2}}\right) \\ & =\frac{1}{2 q}(1-|1-2 p|) \end{aligned} \]

Probability for ever being in the lead

\[ p_{L}=\left\{\begin{array}{ll} 1 & \text { for } p \geq \frac{1}{2} \\ \frac{p}{1-p} & \text { for } p<\frac{1}{2} \end{array}\right. \text {. } \]

The probability is one for \(p \geq 1 / 2\) and it decreases with decreasing \(p<1 / 2\) and is zero for \(p=0\). It should be noted that the presented problem is closely related to Bertrand’s ballot

5.3 Random walk with absorbing wall

Next we consider a similar random walk setup, depicted in Figure 5.2. In addition to the absorbing wall \({ }^{1}\) at \(y=0\), that we used before, we also introduce an absorbing wall at \(x=N^{*}\). In other words, the random walk introduced in the first lead problem will end after \(N^{*}\) steps, at the latest. Here we will denote the number of upward/downward steps by \(u / d\). The pairs ( \(u, d\) ) of steps, required to reach specific points on the boundary, are given in Figure 5.2 as well.

We have to treat the two stopping events separately. The absorption condition at the upper boundary is identical to the stopping in the first lead case. In this case, \((u, d)= (L+1, L)\), with \(L \in\left\{0,1, \ldots, L^{*}-1\right\}\), where

\[ \begin{equation*} L^{*}=\left\lceil\frac{N^{*}}{2}\right\rceil . \tag{5.13} \end{equation*} \]

[^1]The probability that the walk ends with \((L+1, L)\) at the top boundary is identical to \(P\left(L_{2 L+1} \mid p, \mathcal{I}\right)\) given in equation (5.11):

\[ P((L+1, L) \mid p, \mathcal{I})=\boldsymbol{I}\left(0 \leq L<L^{*}\right)\binom{2 L}{L} \frac{1}{L+1} p^{L+1} q^{L} . \]

If the walk reaches the right boundary, then \((u, d)=\left(N^{*}-L, L\right)\), with \(L \in\left\{L^{*}, \ldots, N^{*}\right\}\). This situation is also covered by equation (5.10), with \(N=N^{*}\) and \(L\left(\Delta y^{*}\right)=L\) :

\[ \begin{aligned} P\left(\left(N^{*}-L, L\right) \mid p, N^{*}, \mathcal{I}\right) & =\boldsymbol{1}\left(L^{*} \leq L \leq N^{*}\right)\left[\binom{N^{*}}{L}-\binom{N^{*}}{L+1}\right] p^{N^{*}-L} q^{L} \\ & =\boldsymbol{1}\left(L^{*} \leq L \leq N^{*}\right)\binom{N^{*}}{L}\left(1-\frac{N^{*}-L}{L+1}\right) p^{N^{*}-L} q^{L} \end{aligned} \]

Probability for ( \(\boldsymbol{u}, \boldsymbol{d}\) ) IN RANDOM WALK WITH TWO ABSORBING WALLS

## Random walks

In this chapter, we consider a few random walk problems that are of great importance in statistical physics and in different guises for data analysis. The simplest one has already been discussed before. These problems also allow us to get acquainted with the various combinatorial tools discussed so far, and with the concept of generating functions. A thorough analysis of a wide range of random walk problems, along with useful mathematical tools, can be found in [218].

### 5.1 First return

As a first example we consider a one-dimensional random walk and determine the probability that a walker returns to his initial position for the first time after $n$ steps. This probability allows us to answer the question of how long a random walk takes to come back to the origin and whether this will happen at all. The number of steps $n$ to return to the origin is apparently even, $n=2 m$. In addition, we consider the probability that the walker returns to his initial position after $n$ steps, no matter if it is the first return. We introduce the corresponding propositions
> $f_{n}$ : First return after $n$ steps.
> $r_{n}$ : Return after $n$ steps.

The sought-for probability is $P\left(f_{n} \mid p, \mathcal{I}\right)$, while the probability for the arbitrary return is $P\left(r_{n} \mid p, \mathcal{I}\right)$. The latter probability is simply a binomial distribution

$$
P\left(r_{2 m} \mid p, \mathcal{I}\right)=P\left(N_{r}=m \mid N=2 m, p, \mathcal{I}\right)=\binom{2 m}{m} p^{m} q^{m}
$$

The value of this probability for $m=0$ is $P\left(r_{0} \mid p, \mathcal{I}\right)=1$, which is alright, we only have to stretch the meaning of 'return'. For $f_{n}$, however, return really means that the walker has to leave his initial position first, so $P\left(f_{0} \mid p, \mathcal{I}\right) \stackrel{!}{=} 0$. The corresponding generating function of $P\left(r_{2 m} \mid p, \mathcal{I}\right)$ is

$$
\phi_{r}(z)=\sum_{m=0}^{\infty} P\left(r_{2 m} \mid p, \mathcal{I}\right) z^{2 m}=\sum_{m=0}^{\infty}\binom{2 m}{m}\left(p q z^{2}\right)^{m}
$$

In order to simplify the characteristic function, it is advantageous to express the binomial coefficient with the negative binomial as outlined in Appendix equation (A.23) [p. 605]:

$$
\binom{2 m}{m}=2^{2 m}(-1)^{m}\binom{-\frac{1}{2}}{m} .
$$

Hence, we have

$$
\phi_{r}(z)=\sum_{m=0}^{\infty}\binom{-\frac{1}{2}}{m}\left(-4 p q z^{2}\right)^{m}=\left(1-4 p q z^{2}\right)^{-1 / 2}
$$

The sum over all propositions yields

$$
\begin{equation*}
\sum_{m=0}^{\infty} P\left(r_{2 m} \mid p, \mathcal{I}\right)=\phi(z=1)=\left.\left(1-4 p q z^{2}\right)^{-\frac{1}{2}}\right|_{z=1}=(1-4 p q)^{-\frac{1}{2}} . \tag{5.1}
\end{equation*}
$$

The result can exceed 1 , since the propositions are not mutually exclusive. Next, we decompose the proposition $r_{2 m}$ such that we are able to compute the probability for $f_{2 n}$. An arbitrary return at time $2 m$, for $m \geq 1$, implies that the walker returns for the first time after $2 n$ steps and in the remaining time $2(m-n)$ returns again (not necessarily for the first time). A special case is given if $n=m$, i.e. the walker returns only once. In mathematical terms, we introduce the mutually exclusive propositions $f_{2 n}$ via the marginalization rule, and obtain for $m \geq 1$

$$
\begin{aligned}
P\left(r_{2 m} \mid p, \mathcal{I}\right) & =\sum_{l=1}^{m} P\left(r_{2 m} \mid f_{2 l}, p, \mathcal{I}\right) P\left(f_{2 l} \mid p, \mathcal{I}\right) \\
& =\sum_{l=1}^{m} P\left(r_{2(m-l)} \mid p, \mathcal{I}\right) P\left(f_{2 l} \mid p, \mathcal{I}\right)
\end{aligned}
$$

The last term of the sum, i.e. the one for $l=m$, yields the correct contribution, since $P\left(r_{0} \mid p, \mathcal{I}\right)=1$. As we have defined $P\left(f_{0} \mid p, \mathcal{I}\right)=0$, we can extend the sum on the righthand side and include $l=0$ as well, resulting in a complete formula for the convolution. For the case $m=0$, the equation does not hold. In order to cover also this limiting case we add a Kronecker $\delta_{m, 0}$ on the right-hand side and end up with the expression, which is now valid for $m \geq 0$

$$
P\left(r_{2 m} \mid p, \mathcal{I}\right)=\sum_{l=0}^{m} P\left(r_{2(m-l)} \mid p, \mathcal{I}\right) P\left(f_{2 l} \mid p, \mathcal{I}\right)+\delta_{m, 0}
$$

The generating function on the left-hand side is $\phi_{r}(z)$. The generating function on the right-hand side is the sum of the characteristic function of the convolution and that of the Kronecker $\delta$. The latter is 1 and we obtain

$$
\phi_{r}(z)=\phi_{r}(z) \cdot \phi_{f}(z)+1
$$

or rather

$$
\begin{align*}
& \phi_{f}(z)=1-\phi_{r}(z)^{-1}  \tag{5.2a}\\
& \phi_{f}(z) \stackrel{(5.1)}{=} 1-\sqrt{1-4 p q z^{2}} \tag{5.2b}
\end{align*}
$$

From the characteristic function we conclude a couple of important results. First of all, the probability that the walker returns at all, either after 2,4 , or any other even number of steps, is equal to the normalization, i.e. $\phi_{f}(z=1)=1-\sqrt{1-4 p q}$, and hence the probability that the walker never returns to his initial position is $\sqrt{1-4 p q}$. For $p \neq q$ this probability is greater than zero, which is plausible since in that case there is a drift/bias in the random walk. For $p=q=\frac{1}{2}$, the return probability is 1 . Hence, the walker will definitely return to his initial position. An interesting quantity then is the mean waiting time for the first return, or rather the mean number of steps up to the first return:

$$
\langle m\rangle=\left.\frac{\partial}{\partial z} \phi_{f}(z)\right|_{z=1}=\left.2 p q(1-4 p q)^{-1 / 2}\right|_{p=q=\frac{1}{2}}=\infty .
$$

Although the walker will definitely return, on average it takes him an eternity. Next we will analyse the result in more detail. To this end we invoke Newton's binomial theorem:

$$
\Phi_{f}(z)=1-\sqrt{1-4 p q z^{2}}=1-\sum_{n=0}^{\infty}\binom{\frac{1}{2}}{n}\left(-4 p q z^{2}\right)^{n} .
$$

Comparison with the definition of the generating function

$$
\Phi_{f}(z)=\sum_{m=0}^{\infty} p\left(f_{2 m} \mid p, \mathcal{I}\right) z^{2 m}
$$

reveals

$$
\text { for } m=0: \quad \begin{aligned}
P\left(f_{0} \mid p, \mathcal{I}\right) & =0 \\
\text { for } m>0: \quad P\left(f_{2 m} \mid p, \mathcal{I}\right) & =-\binom{\frac{1}{2}}{m}(-4 p q)^{m} \\
& \stackrel{\text { (A.24a) }}{=}\binom{2 m}{m}(-4)^{-m} \frac{1}{2 m-1}(-4 p q)^{m} \\
& =\frac{1}{2 m-1} P_{\mathrm{B}}(m \mid N=2 m, p, \mathcal{I}) \\
& =\frac{1}{m \gg 1} \frac{(4 p q)^{m}}{2 m-1} \frac{\text { see (6.8) }}{\sqrt{4 \pi p q m}}
\end{aligned}
$$

We are merely interested in the case $p=q=1 / 2$. In this case we have, for $m \gg 1$ :

$$
P\left(f_{2 m} \mid p, \mathcal{I}\right)=\frac{1}{2 m} \frac{1}{\sqrt{\pi m}} \quad \text { see (6.8). }
$$

Apparently, in this case the median is $\breve{m}=1$ and the third quartile, which can be obtained by numerical inspection, is at $m \approx 5$. Nonetheless, the mean value $\langle m\rangle$ diverges, since for large $m$ the probability decays only as $m^{-\frac{3}{2}}$.

### 5.1.1 First return in 2-D and 3-D

Interestingly, equation (5.2a) is independent of the spatial dimension and we will use it for the computation of the first-return probability for two and three dimensions. We will only consider the isotropic case, with equal hopping probability in each direction. The explicit mention of this probability in the conditional part of the probabilities will be omitted. The key quantity that we need to compute is the generating function $\phi_{r}(z=1)$, which could be determined similarly as in the 1-D case. However, there is actually a more elegant approach, which is particularly preferable for higher dimensions. To begin with, we consider the probability $P_{n}(\boldsymbol{x})$ that the walker will cover a distance $\boldsymbol{x}$ in $n$ steps, no matter how often the point $\boldsymbol{x}$ occurs throughout this journey. Based on the marginalization rule we can derive a simple recursion relation

$$
\begin{equation*}
P_{n}(x)=\sum_{\Delta x} P_{n-1}(x-\Delta x) P_{1}(\Delta x) . \tag{5.3}
\end{equation*}
$$

Next we introduce the Fourier transform of these terms, i.e.

$$
\begin{equation*}
\tilde{P}_{n}(\boldsymbol{k}):=\sum_{\boldsymbol{x}} e^{i \boldsymbol{k} \boldsymbol{x}} P_{n}(\boldsymbol{x}), \quad \varepsilon(\boldsymbol{k}):=\sum_{\boldsymbol{x}} e^{i \boldsymbol{k} \boldsymbol{x}} P_{1}(\boldsymbol{x}), \tag{5.4}
\end{equation*}
$$

where the sum runs over all points of the simple cubic lattice of size $N=L^{D}$, with $D$ being the spatial dimension of the lattice. Throughout the calculation we assume a finite lattice and at the end we will consider $N \rightarrow \infty$. Moreover, we assume periodic boundary conditions, which have no influence on the result for the infinite lattice but simplify the calculations. In the statistical context the Fourier transform is also called the 'characteristic function'. It will be discussed in more detail in Section 7.8 [p. 124], where we will also prove that the characteristic function of a convolution, i.e. an expression like equation (5.3), is simply the product of the characteristic functions of the individual factors. Then

$$
\tilde{P}_{n}(\boldsymbol{k})=\tilde{P}_{n-1}(\boldsymbol{k}) \tilde{P}_{1}(\boldsymbol{k})=[\varepsilon(\boldsymbol{k})]^{n} .
$$

The inverse Fourier transform immediately yields the aspired probability

$$
P_{n}(x)=\frac{1}{N} \sum_{k}^{1 . \text { b.z. }} e^{-i k x}[\varepsilon(k)]^{n},
$$

where the sum covers the first Brillouin zone and the wavevectors are $k_{\alpha}=\frac{2 \pi}{L} n_{\alpha}$. Since we are interested in an infinite lattice, we can now replace the sum by an integral and we also restrict the further discussion to $\boldsymbol{x}=\mathbf{0}$, relevant for the first-return probability

$$
P_{n}(\boldsymbol{x})=\frac{1}{(2 \pi)^{D}} \int_{-\pi}^{\pi} d^{D} k[\varepsilon(\boldsymbol{k})]^{n}
$$

We employ this expression for the evaluation of the generating function $\phi_{r}(z=1)$ :

$$
\begin{align*}
\phi_{r}(z=1) & =\sum_{n=0}^{\infty} P_{n}(\mathbf{0})=\frac{1}{(2 \pi)^{D}} \int_{-\pi}^{\pi} d^{D} k \sum_{n=0}^{\infty}[\varepsilon(\boldsymbol{k})]^{n} \\
& =\frac{1}{(2 \pi)^{D}} \int_{-\pi}^{\pi} d^{D} k \frac{1}{1-\varepsilon(\boldsymbol{k})} . \tag{5.5}
\end{align*}
$$

Finally, we compute $\varepsilon(\boldsymbol{k})$ :

$$
\varepsilon(\boldsymbol{k})=\sum_{\boldsymbol{x}}^{i \boldsymbol{k} \boldsymbol{x}} P_{1}(\boldsymbol{x})=\frac{1}{2 D} \sum_{\alpha=1}^{D}\left(e^{i k_{\alpha}}+e^{-i k_{\alpha}}\right)=\frac{1}{D} \sum_{\alpha=1}^{D} \cos \left(k_{\alpha}\right) .
$$

Apart from the prefactor this is the tight-binding dispersion, for which the density of states (dos) can be found in the literature. Now the numerical evaluation of the integral in equation (5.5) is straightforward and not really demanding, but it is worth mentioning that there is an even more elegant way to proceed. As standard in solid-state physics, we introduce the density of states (dos) $\rho(\varepsilon)$, which reduces the integral in equation (5.5) to a onedimensional integral

$$
\begin{equation*}
\phi_{r}(z=1)=\int_{-1}^{1} d \varepsilon \frac{\rho(\varepsilon)}{1-\varepsilon} \xi, \quad \rho(\varepsilon):=\frac{1}{(2 \pi)^{D}} \int_{-\pi}^{\pi} d^{D} k \delta(\varepsilon-\varepsilon(\boldsymbol{k})) \tag{5.6}
\end{equation*}
$$

The dos for the 2-D lattice can be found in [59]. For our purposes, we merely need that $\rho(\varepsilon)$ is finite for $\varepsilon \rightarrow D$, resulting in a logarithmic divergence and $\phi_{r}^{(2-D)}(z=1)=\infty$. According to equation (5.2a), we find $\phi_{f}^{(2-D)}(z=1)=1$. In other words, the walker will definitely return to his starting position.

The situation is different in three spatial dimensions, because the dos now vanishes at the band edges $\varepsilon \rightarrow \pm 1$, and the integral in equation (5.6) yields a finite value. For the detailed calculation we employed the dos expressed in terms of complete elliptic integrals, as reported in [99]. Straightforward numerical integration of the integral yields $\phi_{r}(z=1)=1.516$, resulting in $\phi_{f}(z=1)=0.341$. Apparently, it is now very likely ( $P=$ 0.659 ) that the walker will never return to the origin of his journey. Hence a well-meant piece of advice in [91] reads: 'one should not get drunk in more than two dimensions'.

### 5.2 First lead

Next we will study a related question, which will become important in the context of hypothesis testing. Given a Bernoulli experiment with two alternative outcomes called
success and failure, what is the probability that it takes $N$ trials before the total number of successes exceeds for the first time the total number of the failures. We call this event first lead. It is expedient to translate the problem into the random walk (Brownian motion) language. The problem of first lead is equivalent to a random walk with up (success) and down (failure) moves, and we seek the probability that the random walk occurs above the abscissa for the first time at step $N$. For the analysis we introduce the number $n_{u}$ of up steps and the number $n_{d}$ of down steps. The total number is $N=n_{u}+n_{d}$. Clearly, the 'first lead' can only occur for an odd number $N=2 L+1$ of trials and, right before the first lead, the random walk has to be balanced, i.e. $n_{u}=n_{d}$. This return event need not be the first, but the walker shall never cross the abscissa before. We call this return from below in $2 L$ steps. Therefore, the probability for the first lead in $N=2 L+1$ steps is equal to the probability ( $p$ ) for the final step upwards times the probability for a return from below in $2 L$ steps.

Now we define the relevant propositions:

$$
\begin{aligned}
L_{2 L+1} & : \text { First lead at step } 2 L+1 . \\
F_{2 L}^{b} & : \text { First return from below at step } 2 L .
\end{aligned}
$$

The probability for the 'first return from below' in $2 L$ steps is $(p q)^{L}$ times the number of paths from $(0,0)$ to $(2 L, 0)$ that are never above the abscissa. This number is equivalent to the number $\mathcal{N}\left(y_{0}, y_{N} \mid y<0, N=2 L, \mathcal{I}\right)$ of paths from $(0,-1)$ to $(2 L,-1)$ that never touch the abscissa. The sought-for probability is therefore

$$
\begin{equation*}
P\left(L_{2 L+1} \mid p, \mathcal{I}\right)=p^{L+1} q^{L} \mathcal{N}\left(y_{0}, y_{N} \mid y<0, N=2 L, \mathcal{I}\right) . \tag{5.7}
\end{equation*}
$$

Such restricted random walks can best be analysed based on the 'reflection principle' [170] that we will briefly outline in the next section.

### 5.2.1 Reflection principle

Here we consider the more general case of a random walk that starts at time $t=0$ at a negative position $y_{0}=-\left|y_{0}^{*}\right|$ and ends again after $N$ time steps at a negative position $y_{N}=-\left|y_{N}^{*}\right|$. We are interested in the number of paths between these points, that never reach $y=0$. A representative path is depicted in Figure 5.1 as a dashed curve. This number is apparently equal to the number of unrestricted paths minus the number of paths that reach $y=0$ at least once. The number of unrestricted paths is homogeneous in $y$, i.e.

$$
\begin{aligned}
& \mathcal{N}\left(y_{0}, y_{N} \mid N, \mathcal{I}\right)=\mathcal{N}(0, \Delta y \mid N, \mathcal{I}) \\
& \Delta y:=y_{N}-y_{0}
\end{aligned}
$$

In order to cover a distance of $\Delta y$ in $N$ steps, the number $L$ of downward steps is given by

$$
\begin{aligned}
\Delta y & =(N-L)-L=N-2 L, \\
L & =\frac{N-\Delta y}{2} .
\end{aligned}
$$

Apparently, we have to take into account that in an even (odd) number $N$ only an even (odd) distance can be covered. In other words, $\Delta y$ and $N$ need to have the same parity. So we have

## Number of unrestricted paths ( $\Delta \boldsymbol{y}$ and $\boldsymbol{N}$ HAVE THE SAME PARITY)

$$
\begin{align*}
\mathcal{N}(0, \Delta y \mid N, \mathcal{I}) & =\binom{N}{L(\Delta y)}  \tag{5.8a}\\
L(\Delta y) & :=\frac{N-\Delta y}{2} \tag{5.8b}
\end{align*}
$$

In the present case, we have

$$
\begin{equation*}
\Delta y=\Delta y^{*}:=y_{N}-y_{0}=-\left(\left|y_{N}^{*}\right|-\left|y_{0}^{*}\right|\right) . \tag{5.9}
\end{equation*}
$$

Next we determine the number of those paths that start at $y_{0}=-\left|y_{0}^{*}\right|$, end at $y_{N}=-\left|y_{N}^{*}\right|$, and which reach $y=0$ at least once. A representative path is depicted in Figure 5.1, marked by circles. This number is easily determined based on the reflection principle [170]. To this end, we identify the initial part of the path up to the point where it first reaches the abscissa. Then we reflect this part of the path on the abscissa, as represented in Figure 5.1 with the curve marked with stars. All such reflected paths start at $y_{0}=+\left|y_{0}^{*}\right|$ and end at $y_{N}=-\left|y_{N}^{*}\right|$. One can easily convince oneself that there is a one-to-one correspondence
between the set of restricted paths we are interested in and the set $\mathcal{R}$ of unrestricted paths from $y_{0}=\left|y_{0}^{*}\right|$ to $y_{N}=-\left|y_{N}^{*}\right|$. In other words, for each restricted path there is by construction a reflected path. And for each path of the set $\mathcal{R}$ there is a path of the original set of restricted paths that we can construct by simply applying the reflection described before. Therefore, the number $N_{r}$ of restricted paths, starting at $y_{0}=-\left|y_{0}^{*}\right|$ and reaching at least once $y=0$, is equal to the number of unrestricted paths starting at $y_{0}=\left|y_{0}^{*}\right|$ :

$$
N_{r}=\mathcal{N}\left(0,-\left|y_{N}^{*}\right|-\left|y_{0}^{*}\right| \mid N, \mathcal{I}\right)=\binom{N}{\frac{N+\left|y_{N}^{*}\right|+\left|y_{0}^{*}\right|}{2}}=\binom{N}{L\left(\Delta y^{*}\right)+\left|y_{0}^{*}\right|} .
$$

Finally, the sought-for number of paths starting at $y=-\left|y_{0}^{*}\right|$ and reaching $y=-\left|y_{N}^{*}\right|$ in $N$ steps avoiding the abscissa is given by

## Number of abscissa avoiding paths from $-\left|y_{0}^{*}\right|$ to $-\left|y_{N}^{*}\right|$ IN $N$ steps

$$
\begin{align*}
\mathcal{N}\left(-\left|y_{0}^{*}\right|,-\left|y_{N}^{*}\right| \mid y<0, N, \mathcal{I}\right) & =\binom{N}{L\left(\Delta y^{*}\right)}-\binom{N}{L\left(\Delta y^{*}\right)+\left|y_{0}^{*}\right|}  \tag{5.10}\\
L\left(\Delta y^{*}\right) & :=\frac{N+\left(\left|y_{N}^{*}\right|-\left|y_{0}^{*}\right|\right)}{2} .
\end{align*}
$$

resulting in

Generating function FOR FIRST LEAD

### 5.3 Random walk with absorbing wall

Next we consider a similar random walk setup, depicted in Figure 5.2. In addition to the absorbing wall ${ }^{1}$ at $y=0$, that we used before, we also introduce an absorbing wall at $x=N^{*}$. In other words, the random walk introduced in the first lead problem will end after $N^{*}$ steps, at the latest. Here we will denote the number of upward/downward steps by $u / d$. The pairs ( $u, d$ ) of steps, required to reach specific points on the boundary, are given in Figure 5.2 as well.

We have to treat the two stopping events separately. The absorption condition at the upper boundary is identical to the stopping in the first lead case. In this case, $(u, d)= (L+1, L)$, with $L \in\left\{0,1, \ldots, L^{*}-1\right\}$, where

$$
\begin{equation*}
L^{*}=\left\lceil\frac{N^{*}}{2}\right\rceil . \tag{5.13}
\end{equation*}
$$

[^1]The probability that the walk ends with $(L+1, L)$ at the top boundary is identical to $P\left(L_{2 L+1} \mid p, \mathcal{I}\right)$ given in equation (5.11):

$$
P((L+1, L) \mid p, \mathcal{I})=\boldsymbol{I}\left(0 \leq L<L^{*}\right)\binom{2 L}{L} \frac{1}{L+1} p^{L+1} q^{L} .
$$

If the walk reaches the right boundary, then $(u, d)=\left(N^{*}-L, L\right)$, with $L \in\left\{L^{*}, \ldots, N^{*}\right\}$. This situation is also covered by equation (5.10), with $N=N^{*}$ and $L\left(\Delta y^{*}\right)=L$ :

$$
\begin{aligned}
P\left(\left(N^{*}-L, L\right) \mid p, N^{*}, \mathcal{I}\right) & =\boldsymbol{1}\left(L^{*} \leq L \leq N^{*}\right)\left[\binom{N^{*}}{L}-\binom{N^{*}}{L+1}\right] p^{N^{*}-L} q^{L} \\
& =\boldsymbol{1}\left(L^{*} \leq L \leq N^{*}\right)\binom{N^{*}}{L}\left(1-\frac{N^{*}-L}{L+1}\right) p^{N^{*}-L} q^{L}
\end{aligned}
$$

## Probability for ( $\boldsymbol{u}, \boldsymbol{d}$ ) IN RANDOM WALK WITH TWO ABSORBING WALLS

$$
\begin{align*}
P\left(u, d \mid p, N^{*}, \mathcal{I}\right)= & p^{u} q^{d}\left\{\delta_{d, u-1} \mathbb{I}\left(0 \leq d<L^{*}\right)\binom{2 d}{d} \frac{1}{d+1} p^{u} q^{d}\right.  \tag{5.14}\\
& \left.+\delta_{d, N^{*}-u} \mathbb{I}\left(L^{*} \leq d \leq N^{*}\right)\binom{N^{*}}{d}\left(1-\frac{N^{*}-d}{d+1}\right)\right\} \\
L^{*}:= & \left\lceil\frac{N^{*}}{2}\right\rceil
\end{align*}
$$

Based on Figure 5.2 we see that the points where the boundary is hit are uniquely specified by the number $d$ of downward moves. The probability to find a walker at such a point is given by

$$
\begin{aligned}
P\left(d \mid p, N^{*}, \mathcal{I}\right)= & \left\{\boldsymbol{I}\left(0 \leq d<L^{*}\right)\binom{2 d}{d} \frac{1}{d+1} p^{u} q^{d}\right. \\
& \left.+\boldsymbol{I}\left(L^{*} \leq d \leq 2 L^{*}\right)\binom{2 L^{*}}{d}\left(1-\frac{2 L^{*}-d}{d+1}\right)\right\} \times p^{u} q^{d}
\end{aligned}
$$
