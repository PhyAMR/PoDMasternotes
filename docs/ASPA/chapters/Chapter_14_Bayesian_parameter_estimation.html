14.1 The estimation problem

Bayesian probability theory provides a straightforward approach to estimate a (vectorvalued) parameter \(\boldsymbol{a}\) from measured data \(\boldsymbol{y}\). The probability density for \(\boldsymbol{a}\), given the data \(\boldsymbol{y}\), can be expressed using Bayes’ theorem as

\[ p(a \mid \boldsymbol{y}, \mathcal{I})=\frac{p(\boldsymbol{y} \mid a, \mathcal{I}) p(a \mid \mathcal{I})}{p(\boldsymbol{y} \mid \mathcal{I})} \propto p(\boldsymbol{y} \mid a, \mathcal{I}) p(a \mid \mathcal{I}) . \]

In many cases one wishes to summarize the full probability distribution of \(\boldsymbol{a}\) in just a few characteristic values for \(\hat{\boldsymbol{a}}\). Most frequently, the characterization is given by a ‘location parameter’ and a ‘confidence interval’. It depends on the application at hand which characterization is the most appropriate one. In many situations the location parameter is represented by the mode, which is the maximum of the posterior distribution \(p(a \mid \boldsymbol{y}, \mathcal{I})\), often denoted as the MAP solution. If the prior influence is negligible, for example in the case of a large number of informative data, then the MAP solution is identical to the ‘maximum-likelihood’ (ML) estimate. However, besides the mode there are other ways to summarize a probability distribution, which will be discussed in this chapter.

14.2 Loss and risk function

A systematic approach to find the appropriate estimate is provided by a decision-theoretic treatment [12] based on the concept of a ‘utility function’ or ‘cost function’. First we need to define a cost (or loss) function \(C(\boldsymbol{a}, \hat{\boldsymbol{a}})\), which will depend on the true value of the (vector-valued) parameter \(\boldsymbol{a}\) and its estimate \(\hat{\boldsymbol{a}}\). The precise form of the cost function depends on the problem and specifies how deviations are penalized. The mean (expected) loss is then given by

\[ \langle C\rangle=\int d^{N_{p}} a C(\boldsymbol{a}, \hat{\boldsymbol{a}}) p(a \mid \boldsymbol{y}, \mathcal{I}) \]

Most cost functions \(C\) depend only on the difference \(C(\boldsymbol{a}, \hat{\boldsymbol{a}})=C(\boldsymbol{a}-\hat{\boldsymbol{a}})\). The most common cost functions are:

  • Squared error loss

\[ \begin{equation*} C(\boldsymbol{a}-\hat{\boldsymbol{a}})=\sum_{i}\left(a_{i}-\hat{a}_{i}\right)^{2} . \tag{14.1} \end{equation*} \]

The ‘squared error loss’ penalizes large deviations more than small ones. The resulting estimate will minimize the mean square deviation, thus being influenced most by the largest deviations.

  • Absolute error loss

\[ \begin{equation*} C(\boldsymbol{a}-\hat{\boldsymbol{a}})=\sum_{i}\left|a_{i}-\hat{a}_{i}\right| . \tag{14.2} \end{equation*} \]

In many situations the contribution of a deviation shall be proportional to its magnitude. In that case the absolute error loss is appropriate. It is also less susceptible to the influence of ‘outliers’ in the data.

  • Box error loss (1-0 loss)

If deviations are only acceptable within a certain tolerance region then the ‘box error loss’ function is appropriate. A typical situation is the production of products. If certain specifications of a product are within tolerance range it is acceptable and can be sold, otherwise it has to be replaced, resulting in a constant loss. The corresponding loss function reads

\[ \begin{equation*} C(\boldsymbol{a}-\hat{\boldsymbol{a}})=-\boldsymbol{I}(|\boldsymbol{a}-\hat{\boldsymbol{a}}| \leq \varepsilon):=-\prod_{i=1}^{N_{p}} \boldsymbol{I}\left(\left|a_{i}-\hat{a}_{i}\right| \leq \varepsilon\right) . \tag{14.3} \end{equation*} \]

This cost function is also referred to as ’ \(1-0\) loss’. The best estimator for a given loss function minimizes the expected loss. Therefore, the derivative of the loss function with respect to \(\hat{a}_{i}\) has to be zero.

14.2.1 Squared error loss

In the case of the squared error loss in equation (14.1), we get

\[ \begin{aligned} \frac{\partial}{\partial \hat{a}_{i}}\langle C\rangle & =2 \int\left(a_{i}-\hat{a}_{i}\right) p(\boldsymbol{a} \mid \boldsymbol{y}, \mathcal{I}) d^{N_{p}} a \\ & =2\left(\int a_{i} p(\boldsymbol{a} \mid \boldsymbol{y}, \mathcal{I}) d^{N_{p}} a-\hat{a}_{i}\right) \stackrel{!}{=} 0 \\ & \Rightarrow \\ \hat{a}_{i} & =\int a_{i} p(a \mid \boldsymbol{y}, \mathcal{I}) d^{N_{p}} a=\left\langle a_{i}\right\rangle_{\boldsymbol{y}} \\ \hat{\boldsymbol{a}} & =\hat{\boldsymbol{a}}_{\mathrm{PM}} . \end{aligned} \]

For the squared error loss the best estimator \(\hat{a}\) is given by the ‘posterior mean’, which is the mean of the posterior distribution \(p(a \mid y, \mathcal{I})\).

14.2.2 Absolute error loss

Now we compute the best estimator for the absolute error loss given in equation (14.2):

\[ \begin{aligned} \frac{\partial}{\partial \hat{a}_{i}}\langle C\rangle & =\int \sum_{j} \frac{\partial}{\partial \hat{a}_{i}}\left|\hat{a}_{j}-a_{j}\right| p(\boldsymbol{a} \mid \boldsymbol{y}, \mathcal{I}) d^{N_{p}} a \\ & =\int \operatorname{sgn}\left(\hat{a}_{i}-a_{i}\right) p(\boldsymbol{a} \mid \boldsymbol{y}, \mathcal{I}) d^{N_{p}} a \\ & =\int d a_{i} \operatorname{sgn}\left(\hat{a}_{i}-a_{i}\right) \underbrace{\int \prod_{j \neq i} d a_{j} p(\boldsymbol{a} \mid \boldsymbol{y}, \mathcal{I})}_{:=p\left(a_{i} \mid \boldsymbol{y}, \mathcal{I}\right)} . \end{aligned} \]

This implies

\[ \begin{aligned} \int_{-\infty}^{\hat{a}_{i}} p\left(a_{i} \mid \boldsymbol{y}, \mathcal{I}\right) d a_{i} & =\int_{\hat{a}_{i}}^{\infty} p\left(a_{i} \mid \boldsymbol{y}, \mathcal{I}\right) d a_{i} \\ \hat{\boldsymbol{a}} & =\hat{\boldsymbol{a}}_{\mathrm{MED}} . \end{aligned} \]

Consequently, the ‘median’ is the best estimator under absolute error loss.

14.2.3 Box error loss

Finally let us assume a small tolerance region of size \(\varepsilon\) in all parameters. Then we derive

\[ \begin{aligned} \frac{\partial}{\partial \hat{a}_{i}}\langle C\rangle & =-\frac{\partial}{\partial \hat{a}_{i}} \int\left(\prod_{i} \boldsymbol{I}(|\hat{a}-a| \geq \varepsilon)\right) p(\boldsymbol{a} \mid \boldsymbol{y}, \mathcal{I}) d^{N_{p}} a \\ & =-\frac{\partial}{\partial \hat{a}_{i}} \int_{\hat{a}_{i}-\varepsilon}^{\hat{a}_{i}+\varepsilon} d a_{i} \underbrace{\left(\prod_{j \neq i_{\hat{a}_{j}-\varepsilon}} \int_{j \neq i}^{\hat{a}_{j}+\varepsilon} \prod_{j} d a_{j} p(\boldsymbol{a} \mid \boldsymbol{y}, \mathcal{I})\right)}_{:=X_{i}} . \end{aligned} \]

Let us analyse the integral in the bracket. Since \(\varepsilon\) is small, we can Taylor expand the PDF about \(a_{j}=\hat{a}_{j}\) for all \(j \neq i\). In order to simplify notation, we define \(\hat{\boldsymbol{a}}_{i}=\left(\hat{a}_{1}, \ldots, \hat{a}_{i-1}\right.\), \(\left.a_{i}, \hat{a}_{i+1}, \ldots, \hat{a}_{N}\right)\) and \(\Delta a_{i}=a_{i}-\hat{a}_{i}\). Then

\[ \begin{aligned} p(\boldsymbol{a} \mid \boldsymbol{y}, \mathcal{I})= & p\left(\hat{\boldsymbol{a}}_{i} \mid \boldsymbol{y}, \mathcal{I}\right)+\left.\sum_{l}^{l \neq i} \frac{\partial}{\partial a_{l}} p(\hat{a} \mid \boldsymbol{y}, \mathcal{I})\right|_{\boldsymbol{a}=\hat{\boldsymbol{a}}_{i}} \Delta a_{l} \\ & +\sum_{l, l^{\prime}}^{l \neq i} \underbrace{\left.\frac{1}{2} \frac{\partial^{2}}{\partial a_{l} \partial a_{l^{\prime}}} p(\hat{a} \mid \boldsymbol{y}, \mathcal{I})\right|_{\boldsymbol{a}=\hat{\boldsymbol{a}}_{i}}}_{:=c_{l l^{\prime}}} \Delta a_{l} \Delta a_{l^{\prime}}+\cdots \end{aligned} \]

Since we integrate \(\Delta a_{l}\) over \((-\varepsilon, \varepsilon)\), linear terms in \(\Delta a_{l}\) vanish and we obtain

\[ \begin{aligned} X_{i} & =p\left(\hat{\boldsymbol{a}}_{i} \mid \boldsymbol{y}, \mathcal{I}\right)(2 \varepsilon)^{N_{p}-1}+(2 \varepsilon)^{N_{p}+1} \frac{1}{3} \sum_{l}^{l \neq j} c_{l l} \\ & =p\left(\hat{\boldsymbol{a}}_{i} \mid \boldsymbol{y}, \mathcal{I}\right)(2 \varepsilon)^{N_{p}-1}\left(1+O\left(\varepsilon^{2}\right)\right) \end{aligned} \]

The remaining integral has the form

\[ 0=\frac{\partial}{\partial a_{i}} \int_{a_{i}-\varepsilon}^{a_{i}+\varepsilon} p(a) d a=\frac{\partial}{\partial a_{i}}\left(F\left(a_{i}+\varepsilon\right)-F\left(a_{i}-\varepsilon\right)\right)=p\left(a_{i}+\varepsilon\right)-p\left(a_{i}-\varepsilon\right) . \]

Consequently, \(\frac{\partial}{\partial a_{i}} p(a)=0\). For our problem that implies

\[ \left.\frac{\partial}{\partial a_{i}} p(\boldsymbol{a} \mid \boldsymbol{y}, \mathcal{I})\right|_{\boldsymbol{a}=\hat{\boldsymbol{a}}}=0 . \]

Here the best estimator is given by the maximum posterior solution. It is also referred to as the ‘posterior mode’. As we have already seen, the MAP solution and the ML solution coincide for uniform priors. In this case the ML solution can be considered as the best solution under a box error loss.

The loss function, averaged over all data and parameter values with the corresponding joint PDF \(p(a, y \mid \mathcal{I})\)

\[ R:=\iint K(\boldsymbol{a}-\hat{\boldsymbol{a}}(\boldsymbol{y})) p(\boldsymbol{a}, \boldsymbol{y} \mid \mathcal{I}) d^{N} y d^{N_{p}} a \]

is also termed ‘risk’. The product rule yields

\[ R=\int p(y \mid \mathcal{I})\left(\int K(a-\hat{\boldsymbol{a}}(y)) p(a \mid y, \mathcal{I}) d^{N_{p}} a\right) d^{N} y \]

The risk is therefore equivalent to the mean loss \(\langle C\rangle\) averaged over all data weighted by \(p(y \mid \mathcal{I})\). We are interested in the functional \(\hat{a}(y)\), which minimizes the risk. The root of the functional derivative is given by

\[ 0=\frac{\delta}{\delta \hat{a}(\boldsymbol{y})} R=\int p(y \mid \mathcal{I})\left(\frac{\delta}{\delta \hat{a}(\boldsymbol{y})} \int K(a-\hat{a}(\boldsymbol{y})) p(a \mid \boldsymbol{y}, \mathcal{I}) d^{N_{p}} a\right) d^{N} y . \]

Therefore, the inner bracket has to be zero since \(p(y \mid \mathcal{I}) \geq 0\). This implies that the costminimizing estimators minimize the risk also at the same time.

14.3 Confidence intervals

In Bayesian probability theory the confidence interval of a parameter \(a_{i}\) is given by the standard deviation of the posterior probability

\[ \left\langle\left(\Delta a_{i}\right)^{2}\right\rangle=\int\left(\Delta a_{i}\right)^{2} p(a \mid d, \mathcal{I}) d^{N_{p}} a \]

Sometimes, to emphasize the different interpretation of confidence intervals in frequentist and Bayesian probability theory, a Bayesian confidence interval is also termed a ‘credibility interval’. In order to assess correlated uncertainties it is sometimes useful to consider the posterior covariance

\[ C_{i j}:=\int \Delta a_{i} \Delta a_{j} p(a \mid d, \mathcal{I}) d^{N_{p}} a \]

14.4 Examples

14.4.1 The Bernoulli problem

The binary Bernoulli problem (which we will consider again in Section 15.3.5 [p. 240]) will serve as an example for a typical parameter estimation problem. We consider an urn with red and green balls. Let the relative frequency of green balls be \(q\). Given a sample of size \(N, n_{g}\) green balls are found. The probability density \(p\left(q \mid N, n_{g}, \mathcal{I}\right)\) based on Bayes’ theorem is:

\[ p\left(q \mid N, n_{g}, \mathcal{I}\right)=\frac{1}{Z} q^{n_{g}}(1-q)^{N-n_{g}} p(q \mid \mathcal{I}) . \]

Using a flat (uniform) prior for \(q\), i.e. \(p(q \mid \mathcal{I})=\mathbb{I}(0 \leq q \leq 1)\), we end up with a \(\beta\) distribution (see equation (7.12a) [p. 100]):

\[ p\left(q \mid N, n_{g}, \mathcal{I}\right)=\frac{(N+1)!}{n_{g}!\left(N-n_{g}\right)!} q^{n_{g}}(1-q)^{N-n_{g}} . \]

Owing to the uniform prior, the MAP solution is identical to the maximum likelihood solution

\[ q_{\mathrm{MAP}}=q_{\mathrm{ML}}=\frac{n_{g}}{N} \]

The mean of the posterior and the confidence interval are provided by the mean and standard deviation of the \(\beta\) distribution (equations (7.12c) and (7.12e) [p.100]):

\[ \begin{aligned} q_{\mathrm{PM}} & =\langle q\rangle=\frac{n_{g}+1}{N+2} \\ \sqrt{\operatorname{var}(q)} & =\sqrt{\frac{\langle q\rangle(1-\langle q\rangle)}{N+3}} \end{aligned} \]

In summary, the posterior of a Bernoulli experiment is

Posterior PDF of a Bernoulli experiment WITH UNIFORM PRIOR

\[ \begin{array}{rlrl} \text { sample: } & N, n_{g} & \\ \text { posterior PDF: } & \quad p\left(q \mid N, n_{g}, \mathcal{I}\right) & =p_{\beta}(q \mid \alpha, \rho), \\ \alpha & =n_{g}+1, \quad \rho=N-n_{g}+1, \\ \langle q\rangle & =\frac{n_{g}+1}{N+2}, \\ \operatorname{var}(q) & =\frac{\langle q\rangle(1-\langle q\rangle)}{N+3} . \tag{14.4c} \end{array} \]

The difference between the MAP solution and the posterior mean is of order \(O(1 / N)\) (the posterior distribution is almost symmetric). The confidence interval, however, is much larger than the difference between the MAP and the MP solution. It is of order \(O(1 / \sqrt{N})\). 14.4.2 Mean of a Poisson distribution

Given a Poissonian sample of counts \(\boldsymbol{l}=\left\{l_{1}, \ldots, l_{N}\right\}\) that occur in a fixed time window \(\Delta t\), as the individual counts are uncorrelated the likelihood for \(l\) is a product

\[ \begin{aligned} P(l \mid \mu, \mathcal{I}) & =\prod_{i=1}^{N} \ln \left[P\left(l_{i} \mid \mu, \mathcal{I}\right)\right]=\prod_{i=1}^{N}\left\{e^{-\mu} \frac{\mu^{l_{i}}}{l_{i}!}\right\} \\ & =\frac{1}{Z} e^{-N \mu}+\mu^{\sum_{i} l_{i}}=\frac{1}{Z} e^{-N \mu} \mu^{N \bar{l}} \end{aligned} \]

where \(Z\) contains \(\mu\)-independent factors. For the posterior we also need the prior \(p(\mu \mid \mathcal{I})\). Since \(\mu\) is the mean count in the time window \(\Delta t\), it is a scale parameter and we use the corresponding Jeffreys’ prior \(p(\mu \mid \mathcal{I}) \propto 1 / \mu\). Then the posterior reads

\[ p(\mu \mid l, \mathcal{I}) \propto \mu^{N \bar{l}-1} e^{-N \mu} . \]

It is a Gamma distribution with \(\alpha=\bar{l}\) and \(\beta=N\). The posterior mean and variance follow from equation (7.14) [p.102]. We summarize the result:

Posterior PDF for the mean of a Poisson distribution WITH Jeffreys’ ScALE PRIOR

sample of counts:

\[ \begin{align*} l & =\left\{l_{1}, \ldots, l_{N}\right\}, \\ p(\mu \mid l, N, \mathcal{I}) & =p_{\Gamma}(\mu \mid \alpha, \beta), \tag{14.5a}\\ \alpha & =N \bar{l}, \quad \beta=N, \\ \langle\mu\rangle & =\bar{l}, \tag{14.5b}\\ \operatorname{var}(\mu) & =\frac{\bar{l}}{N} . \tag{14.5c} \end{align*} \]

14.4.3 Estimation of half-lives

The estimation of parameters of systems with exponential behaviour is a common task in many areas of engineering and physics. Here we illustrate the approach with the estimation of the decay parameter of a radioactive source. The data set \(=\left\{t_{1}, \ldots, t_{N}\right\}\) is a sample of recorded times of decay events, e.g. measured by a Geiger-Müller counter. The probability for a decay event is given by

\[ p(t \mid \tau)=\frac{1}{\tau} e^{-t / \tau} . \]

We are interested in the probability density \(p(\tau \mid t, \mathcal{I})\) of \(\tau\). Bayes’ theorem relates this density to the likelihood

\[ p(\tau \mid t, \mathcal{I}) \propto p(t \mid \tau, \mathcal{I}) p(\tau \mid \mathcal{I}) . \]

Since all the decay events are uncorrelated, the likelihood is given by the product of the individual PDFs:

\[ p(t \mid \tau, \mathcal{I})=\prod_{i=1}^{N}\left(\frac{1}{\tau} e^{-t_{i} / \tau}\right)=\tau^{-N} e^{-N \bar{t} / \tau} \]

The symbol \(\bar{t}\) stands for the sample mean of the recorded decay times. Next we need to assign the prior for \(\tau\). Without further prior knowledge, \(\tau\) is a scale parameter for which Jeffreys’ prior is in most cases appropriate. This finally results in the posterior distribution

\[ p(\tau \mid t, \mathcal{I})=\frac{1}{Z} \tau^{-N-1} e^{-N \bar{t} / \tau} \]

The computation of the normalization constant and the moments of \(\tau\) requires the integral

\[ \begin{aligned} I_{v} & :=\int_{0}^{\infty} \tau^{-N+v} e^{-N \bar{t} / \tau} \frac{d \tau}{\tau} \\ & =\int_{0}^{\infty}\left(\frac{x}{N \bar{t}}\right)^{N-v} e^{-x} \frac{d x}{x} \\ & =(N \bar{t})^{-N+v} \Gamma(N-v) \end{aligned} \]

Hence, the norm and mean are given by

\[ \begin{aligned} Z & =I_{0}=(N \bar{t})^{-N} \Gamma(N) \\ \langle\tau\rangle & =\frac{I_{1}}{I_{0}}=\frac{N}{N-1} \bar{t} \end{aligned} \]

The variance can be computed analogously:

\[ \begin{aligned} \operatorname{var}(\tau) & =\frac{I_{2}}{I_{0}}-\langle\tau\rangle^{2}=\frac{N^{2}}{(N-1)(N-2)} \bar{t}^{2}-\frac{N^{2}}{(N-1)(N-1)} \bar{t}^{2} \\ & =\langle\tau\rangle^{2} \frac{1}{N-2} . \end{aligned} \]

The confidence interval is therefore \(\mathrm{CI}=\bar{t} / \sqrt{N-2}\). The estimation could be summarized as

\[ \tau=\bar{t} \frac{N}{N-1}\left(1 \pm \frac{1}{\sqrt{N-2}}\right) \]

Again we find that the difference in the estimators for the location parameter (MAP, PM) is of order \(1 / N\), while the confidence interval (uncertainty) is of order \(1 / \sqrt{N}\). The key result is:

Posterior PDF for the half-life WITH JEFFREYS’ SCALE PRIOR

sample of counts:

\[ \begin{align*} t & =\left\{t_{1}, \ldots, t_{N}\right\}, \\ p(\tau \mid t, N, \mathcal{I}) & =\frac{(N \bar{t})^{N}}{\Gamma(N)} e^{-N \bar{t} / \tau}, \tag{14.6a}\\ \langle\tau\rangle & =\bar{t} \frac{N}{N-1}, \tag{14.6b}\\ \operatorname{var}(\tau) & =\bar{t}^{2} \frac{1}{N-2} . \tag{14.6c} \end{align*} \]

## Bayesian parameter estimation

### 14.1 The estimation problem

Bayesian probability theory provides a straightforward approach to estimate a (vectorvalued) parameter $\boldsymbol{a}$ from measured data $\boldsymbol{y}$. The probability density for $\boldsymbol{a}$, given the data $\boldsymbol{y}$, can be expressed using Bayes' theorem as

$$
p(a \mid \boldsymbol{y}, \mathcal{I})=\frac{p(\boldsymbol{y} \mid a, \mathcal{I}) p(a \mid \mathcal{I})}{p(\boldsymbol{y} \mid \mathcal{I})} \propto p(\boldsymbol{y} \mid a, \mathcal{I}) p(a \mid \mathcal{I}) .
$$

In many cases one wishes to summarize the full probability distribution of $\boldsymbol{a}$ in just a few characteristic values for $\hat{\boldsymbol{a}}$. Most frequently, the characterization is given by a 'location parameter' and a 'confidence interval'. It depends on the application at hand which characterization is the most appropriate one. In many situations the location parameter is represented by the mode, which is the maximum of the posterior distribution $p(a \mid \boldsymbol{y}, \mathcal{I})$, often denoted as the MAP solution. If the prior influence is negligible, for example in the case of a large number of informative data, then the MAP solution is identical to the 'maximum-likelihood' (ML) estimate. However, besides the mode there are other ways to summarize a probability distribution, which will be discussed in this chapter.

### 14.2 Loss and risk function

A systematic approach to find the appropriate estimate is provided by a decision-theoretic treatment [12] based on the concept of a 'utility function' or 'cost function'. First we need to define a cost (or loss) function $C(\boldsymbol{a}, \hat{\boldsymbol{a}})$, which will depend on the true value of the (vector-valued) parameter $\boldsymbol{a}$ and its estimate $\hat{\boldsymbol{a}}$. The precise form of the cost function depends on the problem and specifies how deviations are penalized. The mean (expected) loss is then given by

$$
\langle C\rangle=\int d^{N_{p}} a C(\boldsymbol{a}, \hat{\boldsymbol{a}}) p(a \mid \boldsymbol{y}, \mathcal{I})
$$

Most cost functions $C$ depend only on the difference $C(\boldsymbol{a}, \hat{\boldsymbol{a}})=C(\boldsymbol{a}-\hat{\boldsymbol{a}})$. The most common cost functions are:

- Squared error loss

$$
\begin{equation*}
C(\boldsymbol{a}-\hat{\boldsymbol{a}})=\sum_{i}\left(a_{i}-\hat{a}_{i}\right)^{2} . \tag{14.1}
\end{equation*}
$$

The 'squared error loss' penalizes large deviations more than small ones. The resulting estimate will minimize the mean square deviation, thus being influenced most by the largest deviations.

- Absolute error loss

$$
\begin{equation*}
C(\boldsymbol{a}-\hat{\boldsymbol{a}})=\sum_{i}\left|a_{i}-\hat{a}_{i}\right| . \tag{14.2}
\end{equation*}
$$

In many situations the contribution of a deviation shall be proportional to its magnitude. In that case the absolute error loss is appropriate. It is also less susceptible to the influence of 'outliers' in the data.

- Box error loss (1-0 loss)

If deviations are only acceptable within a certain tolerance region then the 'box error loss' function is appropriate. A typical situation is the production of products. If certain specifications of a product are within tolerance range it is acceptable and can be sold, otherwise it has to be replaced, resulting in a constant loss. The corresponding loss function reads

$$
\begin{equation*}
C(\boldsymbol{a}-\hat{\boldsymbol{a}})=-\boldsymbol{I}(|\boldsymbol{a}-\hat{\boldsymbol{a}}| \leq \varepsilon):=-\prod_{i=1}^{N_{p}} \boldsymbol{I}\left(\left|a_{i}-\hat{a}_{i}\right| \leq \varepsilon\right) . \tag{14.3}
\end{equation*}
$$

This cost function is also referred to as ' $1-0$ loss'.
The best estimator for a given loss function minimizes the expected loss. Therefore, the derivative of the loss function with respect to $\hat{a}_{i}$ has to be zero.

### 14.2.1 Squared error loss

In the case of the squared error loss in equation (14.1), we get

$$
\begin{aligned}
\frac{\partial}{\partial \hat{a}_{i}}\langle C\rangle & =2 \int\left(a_{i}-\hat{a}_{i}\right) p(\boldsymbol{a} \mid \boldsymbol{y}, \mathcal{I}) d^{N_{p}} a \\
& =2\left(\int a_{i} p(\boldsymbol{a} \mid \boldsymbol{y}, \mathcal{I}) d^{N_{p}} a-\hat{a}_{i}\right) \stackrel{!}{=} 0 \\
& \Rightarrow \\
\hat{a}_{i} & =\int a_{i} p(a \mid \boldsymbol{y}, \mathcal{I}) d^{N_{p}} a=\left\langle a_{i}\right\rangle_{\boldsymbol{y}} \\
\hat{\boldsymbol{a}} & =\hat{\boldsymbol{a}}_{\mathrm{PM}} .
\end{aligned}
$$

For the squared error loss the best estimator $\hat{a}$ is given by the 'posterior mean', which is the mean of the posterior distribution $p(a \mid y, \mathcal{I})$.

### 14.2.2 Absolute error loss

Now we compute the best estimator for the absolute error loss given in equation (14.2):

$$
\begin{aligned}
\frac{\partial}{\partial \hat{a}_{i}}\langle C\rangle & =\int \sum_{j} \frac{\partial}{\partial \hat{a}_{i}}\left|\hat{a}_{j}-a_{j}\right| p(\boldsymbol{a} \mid \boldsymbol{y}, \mathcal{I}) d^{N_{p}} a \\
& =\int \operatorname{sgn}\left(\hat{a}_{i}-a_{i}\right) p(\boldsymbol{a} \mid \boldsymbol{y}, \mathcal{I}) d^{N_{p}} a \\
& =\int d a_{i} \operatorname{sgn}\left(\hat{a}_{i}-a_{i}\right) \underbrace{\int \prod_{j \neq i} d a_{j} p(\boldsymbol{a} \mid \boldsymbol{y}, \mathcal{I})}_{:=p\left(a_{i} \mid \boldsymbol{y}, \mathcal{I}\right)} .
\end{aligned}
$$

This implies

$$
\begin{aligned}
\int_{-\infty}^{\hat{a}_{i}} p\left(a_{i} \mid \boldsymbol{y}, \mathcal{I}\right) d a_{i} & =\int_{\hat{a}_{i}}^{\infty} p\left(a_{i} \mid \boldsymbol{y}, \mathcal{I}\right) d a_{i} \\
\hat{\boldsymbol{a}} & =\hat{\boldsymbol{a}}_{\mathrm{MED}} .
\end{aligned}
$$

Consequently, the 'median' is the best estimator under absolute error loss.

### 14.2.3 Box error loss

Finally let us assume a small tolerance region of size $\varepsilon$ in all parameters. Then we derive

$$
\begin{aligned}
\frac{\partial}{\partial \hat{a}_{i}}\langle C\rangle & =-\frac{\partial}{\partial \hat{a}_{i}} \int\left(\prod_{i} \boldsymbol{I}(|\hat{a}-a| \geq \varepsilon)\right) p(\boldsymbol{a} \mid \boldsymbol{y}, \mathcal{I}) d^{N_{p}} a \\
& =-\frac{\partial}{\partial \hat{a}_{i}} \int_{\hat{a}_{i}-\varepsilon}^{\hat{a}_{i}+\varepsilon} d a_{i} \underbrace{\left(\prod_{j \neq i_{\hat{a}_{j}-\varepsilon}} \int_{j \neq i}^{\hat{a}_{j}+\varepsilon} \prod_{j} d a_{j} p(\boldsymbol{a} \mid \boldsymbol{y}, \mathcal{I})\right)}_{:=X_{i}} .
\end{aligned}
$$

Let us analyse the integral in the bracket. Since $\varepsilon$ is small, we can Taylor expand the PDF about $a_{j}=\hat{a}_{j}$ for all $j \neq i$. In order to simplify notation, we define $\hat{\boldsymbol{a}}_{i}=\left(\hat{a}_{1}, \ldots, \hat{a}_{i-1}\right.$, $\left.a_{i}, \hat{a}_{i+1}, \ldots, \hat{a}_{N}\right)$ and $\Delta a_{i}=a_{i}-\hat{a}_{i}$. Then

$$
\begin{aligned}
p(\boldsymbol{a} \mid \boldsymbol{y}, \mathcal{I})= & p\left(\hat{\boldsymbol{a}}_{i} \mid \boldsymbol{y}, \mathcal{I}\right)+\left.\sum_{l}^{l \neq i} \frac{\partial}{\partial a_{l}} p(\hat{a} \mid \boldsymbol{y}, \mathcal{I})\right|_{\boldsymbol{a}=\hat{\boldsymbol{a}}_{i}} \Delta a_{l} \\
& +\sum_{l, l^{\prime}}^{l \neq i} \underbrace{\left.\frac{1}{2} \frac{\partial^{2}}{\partial a_{l} \partial a_{l^{\prime}}} p(\hat{a} \mid \boldsymbol{y}, \mathcal{I})\right|_{\boldsymbol{a}=\hat{\boldsymbol{a}}_{i}}}_{:=c_{l l^{\prime}}} \Delta a_{l} \Delta a_{l^{\prime}}+\cdots
\end{aligned}
$$

Since we integrate $\Delta a_{l}$ over $(-\varepsilon, \varepsilon)$, linear terms in $\Delta a_{l}$ vanish and we obtain

$$
\begin{aligned}
X_{i} & =p\left(\hat{\boldsymbol{a}}_{i} \mid \boldsymbol{y}, \mathcal{I}\right)(2 \varepsilon)^{N_{p}-1}+(2 \varepsilon)^{N_{p}+1} \frac{1}{3} \sum_{l}^{l \neq j} c_{l l} \\
& =p\left(\hat{\boldsymbol{a}}_{i} \mid \boldsymbol{y}, \mathcal{I}\right)(2 \varepsilon)^{N_{p}-1}\left(1+O\left(\varepsilon^{2}\right)\right)
\end{aligned}
$$

The remaining integral has the form

$$
0=\frac{\partial}{\partial a_{i}} \int_{a_{i}-\varepsilon}^{a_{i}+\varepsilon} p(a) d a=\frac{\partial}{\partial a_{i}}\left(F\left(a_{i}+\varepsilon\right)-F\left(a_{i}-\varepsilon\right)\right)=p\left(a_{i}+\varepsilon\right)-p\left(a_{i}-\varepsilon\right) .
$$

Consequently, $\frac{\partial}{\partial a_{i}} p(a)=0$. For our problem that implies

$$
\left.\frac{\partial}{\partial a_{i}} p(\boldsymbol{a} \mid \boldsymbol{y}, \mathcal{I})\right|_{\boldsymbol{a}=\hat{\boldsymbol{a}}}=0 .
$$

Here the best estimator is given by the maximum posterior solution. It is also referred to as the 'posterior mode'. As we have already seen, the MAP solution and the ML solution coincide for uniform priors. In this case the ML solution can be considered as the best solution under a box error loss.

The loss function, averaged over all data and parameter values with the corresponding joint PDF $p(a, y \mid \mathcal{I})$

$$
R:=\iint K(\boldsymbol{a}-\hat{\boldsymbol{a}}(\boldsymbol{y})) p(\boldsymbol{a}, \boldsymbol{y} \mid \mathcal{I}) d^{N} y d^{N_{p}} a
$$

is also termed 'risk'. The product rule yields

$$
R=\int p(y \mid \mathcal{I})\left(\int K(a-\hat{\boldsymbol{a}}(y)) p(a \mid y, \mathcal{I}) d^{N_{p}} a\right) d^{N} y
$$

The risk is therefore equivalent to the mean loss $\langle C\rangle$ averaged over all data weighted by $p(y \mid \mathcal{I})$. We are interested in the functional $\hat{a}(y)$, which minimizes the risk. The root of the functional derivative is given by

$$
0=\frac{\delta}{\delta \hat{a}(\boldsymbol{y})} R=\int p(y \mid \mathcal{I})\left(\frac{\delta}{\delta \hat{a}(\boldsymbol{y})} \int K(a-\hat{a}(\boldsymbol{y})) p(a \mid \boldsymbol{y}, \mathcal{I}) d^{N_{p}} a\right) d^{N} y .
$$

Therefore, the inner bracket has to be zero since $p(y \mid \mathcal{I}) \geq 0$. This implies that the costminimizing estimators minimize the risk also at the same time.

### 14.3 Confidence intervals

In Bayesian probability theory the confidence interval of a parameter $a_{i}$ is given by the standard deviation of the posterior probability

$$
\left\langle\left(\Delta a_{i}\right)^{2}\right\rangle=\int\left(\Delta a_{i}\right)^{2} p(a \mid d, \mathcal{I}) d^{N_{p}} a
$$

Sometimes, to emphasize the different interpretation of confidence intervals in frequentist and Bayesian probability theory, a Bayesian confidence interval is also termed a 'credibility interval'. In order to assess correlated uncertainties it is sometimes useful to consider the posterior covariance

$$
C_{i j}:=\int \Delta a_{i} \Delta a_{j} p(a \mid d, \mathcal{I}) d^{N_{p}} a
$$

### 14.4 Examples

### 14.4.1 The Bernoulli problem

The binary Bernoulli problem (which we will consider again in Section 15.3.5 [p. 240]) will serve as an example for a typical parameter estimation problem. We consider an urn with red and green balls. Let the relative frequency of green balls be $q$. Given a sample of size $N, n_{g}$ green balls are found. The probability density $p\left(q \mid N, n_{g}, \mathcal{I}\right)$ based on Bayes' theorem is:

$$
p\left(q \mid N, n_{g}, \mathcal{I}\right)=\frac{1}{Z} q^{n_{g}}(1-q)^{N-n_{g}} p(q \mid \mathcal{I}) .
$$

Using a flat (uniform) prior for $q$, i.e. $p(q \mid \mathcal{I})=\mathbb{I}(0 \leq q \leq 1)$, we end up with a $\beta$ distribution (see equation (7.12a) [p. 100]):

$$
p\left(q \mid N, n_{g}, \mathcal{I}\right)=\frac{(N+1)!}{n_{g}!\left(N-n_{g}\right)!} q^{n_{g}}(1-q)^{N-n_{g}} .
$$

Owing to the uniform prior, the MAP solution is identical to the maximum likelihood solution

$$
q_{\mathrm{MAP}}=q_{\mathrm{ML}}=\frac{n_{g}}{N}
$$

The mean of the posterior and the confidence interval are provided by the mean and standard deviation of the $\beta$ distribution (equations (7.12c) and (7.12e) [p.100]):

$$
\begin{aligned}
q_{\mathrm{PM}} & =\langle q\rangle=\frac{n_{g}+1}{N+2} \\
\sqrt{\operatorname{var}(q)} & =\sqrt{\frac{\langle q\rangle(1-\langle q\rangle)}{N+3}}
\end{aligned}
$$

In summary, the posterior of a Bernoulli experiment is

## Posterior PDF of a Bernoulli experiment WITH UNIFORM PRIOR

$$
\begin{array}{rlrl}
\text { sample: } & N, n_{g} & \\
\text { posterior PDF: } & \quad p\left(q \mid N, n_{g}, \mathcal{I}\right) & =p_{\beta}(q \mid \alpha, \rho), \\
\alpha & =n_{g}+1, \quad \rho=N-n_{g}+1, \\
\langle q\rangle & =\frac{n_{g}+1}{N+2}, \\
\operatorname{var}(q) & =\frac{\langle q\rangle(1-\langle q\rangle)}{N+3} . \tag{14.4c}
\end{array}
$$

The difference between the MAP solution and the posterior mean is of order $O(1 / N)$ (the posterior distribution is almost symmetric). The confidence interval, however, is much larger than the difference between the MAP and the MP solution. It is of order $O(1 / \sqrt{N})$. Figure 14.1 displays the probability density for $n_{g}=6$ and $N=20$.

### 14.4.2 Mean of a Poisson distribution

Given a Poissonian sample of counts $\boldsymbol{l}=\left\{l_{1}, \ldots, l_{N}\right\}$ that occur in a fixed time window $\Delta t$, as the individual counts are uncorrelated the likelihood for $l$ is a product

$$
\begin{aligned}
P(l \mid \mu, \mathcal{I}) & =\prod_{i=1}^{N} \ln \left[P\left(l_{i} \mid \mu, \mathcal{I}\right)\right]=\prod_{i=1}^{N}\left\{e^{-\mu} \frac{\mu^{l_{i}}}{l_{i}!}\right\} \\
& =\frac{1}{Z} e^{-N \mu}+\mu^{\sum_{i} l_{i}}=\frac{1}{Z} e^{-N \mu} \mu^{N \bar{l}}
\end{aligned}
$$

where $Z$ contains $\mu$-independent factors. For the posterior we also need the prior $p(\mu \mid \mathcal{I})$. Since $\mu$ is the mean count in the time window $\Delta t$, it is a scale parameter and we use the corresponding Jeffreys' prior $p(\mu \mid \mathcal{I}) \propto 1 / \mu$. Then the posterior reads

$$
p(\mu \mid l, \mathcal{I}) \propto \mu^{N \bar{l}-1} e^{-N \mu} .
$$

It is a Gamma distribution with $\alpha=\bar{l}$ and $\beta=N$. The posterior mean and variance follow from equation (7.14) [p.102]. We summarize the result:

## Posterior PDF for the mean of a Poisson distribution WITH Jeffreys' ScALE PRIOR

sample of counts:

$$
\begin{align*}
l & =\left\{l_{1}, \ldots, l_{N}\right\}, \\
p(\mu \mid l, N, \mathcal{I}) & =p_{\Gamma}(\mu \mid \alpha, \beta),  \tag{14.5a}\\
\alpha & =N \bar{l}, \quad \beta=N, \\
\langle\mu\rangle & =\bar{l},  \tag{14.5b}\\
\operatorname{var}(\mu) & =\frac{\bar{l}}{N} . \tag{14.5c}
\end{align*}
$$

### 14.4.3 Estimation of half-lives

The estimation of parameters of systems with exponential behaviour is a common task in many areas of engineering and physics. Here we illustrate the approach with the estimation of the decay parameter of a radioactive source. The data set $=\left\{t_{1}, \ldots, t_{N}\right\}$ is a sample of recorded times of decay events, e.g. measured by a Geiger-Müller counter. The probability for a decay event is given by

$$
p(t \mid \tau)=\frac{1}{\tau} e^{-t / \tau} .
$$

We are interested in the probability density $p(\tau \mid t, \mathcal{I})$ of $\tau$. Bayes' theorem relates this density to the likelihood

$$
p(\tau \mid t, \mathcal{I}) \propto p(t \mid \tau, \mathcal{I}) p(\tau \mid \mathcal{I}) .
$$

Since all the decay events are uncorrelated, the likelihood is given by the product of the individual PDFs:

$$
p(t \mid \tau, \mathcal{I})=\prod_{i=1}^{N}\left(\frac{1}{\tau} e^{-t_{i} / \tau}\right)=\tau^{-N} e^{-N \bar{t} / \tau}
$$

The symbol $\bar{t}$ stands for the sample mean of the recorded decay times. Next we need to assign the prior for $\tau$. Without further prior knowledge, $\tau$ is a scale parameter for which Jeffreys' prior is in most cases appropriate. This finally results in the posterior distribution

$$
p(\tau \mid t, \mathcal{I})=\frac{1}{Z} \tau^{-N-1} e^{-N \bar{t} / \tau}
$$

The computation of the normalization constant and the moments of $\tau$ requires the integral

$$
\begin{aligned}
I_{v} & :=\int_{0}^{\infty} \tau^{-N+v} e^{-N \bar{t} / \tau} \frac{d \tau}{\tau} \\
& =\int_{0}^{\infty}\left(\frac{x}{N \bar{t}}\right)^{N-v} e^{-x} \frac{d x}{x} \\
& =(N \bar{t})^{-N+v} \Gamma(N-v)
\end{aligned}
$$

Hence, the norm and mean are given by

$$
\begin{aligned}
Z & =I_{0}=(N \bar{t})^{-N} \Gamma(N) \\
\langle\tau\rangle & =\frac{I_{1}}{I_{0}}=\frac{N}{N-1} \bar{t}
\end{aligned}
$$

The variance can be computed analogously:

$$
\begin{aligned}
\operatorname{var}(\tau) & =\frac{I_{2}}{I_{0}}-\langle\tau\rangle^{2}=\frac{N^{2}}{(N-1)(N-2)} \bar{t}^{2}-\frac{N^{2}}{(N-1)(N-1)} \bar{t}^{2} \\
& =\langle\tau\rangle^{2} \frac{1}{N-2} .
\end{aligned}
$$

The confidence interval is therefore $\mathrm{CI}=\bar{t} / \sqrt{N-2}$. The estimation could be summarized as

$$
\tau=\bar{t} \frac{N}{N-1}\left(1 \pm \frac{1}{\sqrt{N-2}}\right)
$$

Again we find that the difference in the estimators for the location parameter (MAP, PM) is of order $1 / N$, while the confidence interval (uncertainty) is of order $1 / \sqrt{N}$. The key result is:

## Posterior PDF for the half-life WITH JEFFREYS' SCALE PRIOR

sample of counts:

$$
\begin{align*}
t & =\left\{t_{1}, \ldots, t_{N}\right\}, \\
p(\tau \mid t, N, \mathcal{I}) & =\frac{(N \bar{t})^{N}}{\Gamma(N)} e^{-N \bar{t} / \tau},  \tag{14.6a}\\
\langle\tau\rangle & =\bar{t} \frac{N}{N-1},  \tag{14.6b}\\
\operatorname{var}(\tau) & =\bar{t}^{2} \frac{1}{N-2} . \tag{14.6c}
\end{align*}
$$
