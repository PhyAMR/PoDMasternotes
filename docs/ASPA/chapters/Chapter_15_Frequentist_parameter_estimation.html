Frequentist parameter estimation

Now that we have seen how parameters can be estimated in the frame of probability theory, we will outline the frequentist approach. We recall that in the frequentist way of thinking, parameters are not random variables and one cannot assign probabilities to them. Moreover, the parameter estimation shall only be based on the data \(y\). The goal is to define a functional \(\hat{\boldsymbol{a}}(\boldsymbol{y})\) of the data that estimates the sought-for parameters as well as possible. Two important characteristics of a good estimator are that it is ‘unbiased’ and ‘efficient’. These aspects shall be addressed in the next sections.

15.1 Unbiased estimators

We consider a data analysis problem which depends on the parameters \(\boldsymbol{a} \in \mathbb{R}^{N_{p}}\). The parameters shall be estimated based on a sample of measured data \(\boldsymbol{y}=\left\{y_{1}, \ldots, y_{N}\right\}\). The parameters along with the experimental noise define the probability density of the sample, the likelihood function

\[ p(\boldsymbol{y} \mid \boldsymbol{a}) . \]

The most common example of a likelihood function is provided by additive Gaussian errors

\[ \begin{equation*} p(\boldsymbol{y} \mid \boldsymbol{a})=\left(2 \pi \sigma^{2}\right)^{-\frac{N}{2}} e^{-\frac{1}{2} \sum_{i} \frac{\left(y_{i}-f_{i}(a)\right)^{2}}{\sigma_{i}^{2}}} . \tag{15.1} \end{equation*} \]

Here \(f_{i}(\boldsymbol{a})=f\left(x_{i} \mid \boldsymbol{a}\right)\) denotes the theoretical model, which depends on the control variables \(x_{i}\) and on the parameters to be determined, \(\boldsymbol{a}\). Let us consider for definiteness a damped oscillation at time \(t\) with an elongation

\[ f\left(t \mid A_{0}, \omega, \lambda, \phi\right)=A(t)=A_{0} \cos (\omega t+\phi) e^{-\lambda t} \]

At different times \(t_{i}\) the amplitude \(A(t)\) is measured, yielding the value \(y_{i}\), which differs from \(A(t)\) due to noise. In this example the control variables are the measurement times \(t_{i}\) and the unknown parameters are \(\boldsymbol{a}=\left\{A_{0}, \omega, \lambda, \phi\right\}\). The measured values are scattered according to a Gaussian distribution with mean zero and standard deviation \(\sigma\) around the model (theoretical) values \(y_{j}=A\left(t_{j}\right)\). Based on the measured data the unknown parameters shall be determined. For simplicity we restrict the discussion first to problems with only one unknown parameter \(a\). Within the frequentist statistics a functional \(\hat{a}(y)\) has to be derived, which provides an estimator of the parameters for a given set of data. Of special importance are estimators which yield unbiased estimates, so-called unbiased estimators. An estimator is unbiased if it yields on average the true parameter value

\[ \begin{equation*} \langle\hat{\boldsymbol{a}}(\boldsymbol{y})\rangle=\int d^{N} y \hat{\boldsymbol{a}}(\boldsymbol{y}) p(\boldsymbol{y} \mid \boldsymbol{a}) \stackrel{!}{=} \boldsymbol{a} \tag{15.2} \end{equation*} \]

15.2 The maximum likelihood estimator

The most commonly used estimator in frequentist statistics is the so-called ‘maximum likelihood’ estimator. As implied by the name, it is obtained by maximizing the likelihood

\[ \boldsymbol{a}^{\mathrm{ML}}=\arg \max _{\boldsymbol{a}} p(\boldsymbol{y} \mid \boldsymbol{a}) . \]

In most cases it is expedient to determine the maximum of the log-likelihood

\[ \left.\frac{\partial \ln (p(\boldsymbol{y} \mid \boldsymbol{a}))}{\partial a_{i}}\right|_{\boldsymbol{a}=\hat{a}^{\mathrm{ML}}}=0 . \]

Obviously, from the Bayesian perspective it corresponds to the MAP estimator in the case of a uniform prior, or if the peak of the posterior is dominated by the likelihood.

In the next sections we will discuss a variety of examples. The generalization to the Bayesian approach is easily obtained by adding the prior PDF.

As a first example we consider the following simple case of a single parameter \(a\), which is directly accessible by an experiment (a common situation, for example, for length or weight measurements of classical objects). The experimental errors shall be independent Gaussian. The ‘log-likelihood’ reads

\[ \ln (p(y \mid a))=C-\frac{1}{2 \sigma^{2}} \sum_{i}\left(y_{i}-a\right)^{2} . \]

15.3.2 Mean of a Poisson distribution

Suppose we are given a Poissonian sample of counts \(\boldsymbol{l}=\left\{l_{1}, \ldots, l_{N}\right\}\) that occur in a fixed time window \(\Delta t\). As the individual counts are uncorrelated, the likelihood for \(l\) is a product and the log-likelihood becomes

\[ \begin{aligned} \ln [P(l \mid \mu, \mathcal{I})] & =\sum_{i=1}^{N} \ln \left[P\left(l_{i} \mid \mu, \mathcal{I}\right)\right]=\sum_{i=1}^{N} \ln \left[e^{-\mu} \frac{\mu^{l_{i}}}{l_{i}!}\right] \\ & =-N \mu+\sum_{i} l_{i} \ln [\mu]-\sum_{i} \ln \left[l_{i}!\right] \end{aligned} \]

From the zero of the derivative w.r.t. \(\mu\),

\[ \frac{\partial}{\partial \mu} \ln [P(c \mid \mu, \mathcal{I})]=-N+\frac{\sum_{i} l_{i}}{\mu} \stackrel{!}{=} 0, \]

we obtain the ML estimate for the parameter \(\mu\) :

\[ \mu^{\mathrm{ML}}=\frac{\sum_{i=1}^{N} l_{i}}{N}=\bar{l} . \]

The ML estimator is again the sample mean and as such is unbiased. In summary, we have

15.3.3 Half-life distribution of Poisson processes

Assume that a data set of decay times \(\left\{t_{1}, \ldots, t_{L}\right\}\) has been measured. The probability for a decay in \((t, t+d t)\) is provided by the exponential distribution

\[ p_{P}(t \mid \tau)=\frac{1}{\tau} e^{-t / \tau} . \]

For uncorrelated decay events the log-likelihood reads

\[ \ln \left(p\left(t_{1}, \ldots, t_{L} \mid \tau, \mathcal{I}\right)\right)=\sum_{i=1}^{L} \ln \left(\frac{1}{\tau} e^{-t_{i} / \tau}\right)=-L\left(\frac{\bar{t}}{\tau}+\ln (\tau)\right) \]

The derivative vanishes for

ML estimate of the mean of a Poisson distribution

\[ \begin{equation*} \tau^{\mathrm{ML}}=\bar{t} \tag{15.5} \end{equation*} \]

Therefore here also the ML estimator is given by the sample mean. However, the next example will show that this is not always the case.

15.3.4 Cauchy distribution

Let \(\boldsymbol{x}=\left\{x_{1}, \ldots, x_{L}\right\}\) be an independent sample of a Cauchy distribution. The probability density of a Cauchy distribution is given by

\[ p_{C}(x \mid a, b)=\frac{1}{\pi} \frac{b}{(x-a)^{2}+b^{2}} . \]

The sought-for parameters are the location parameter \(a\) and the width parameter \(b\). The log-likelihood is given by (again exploiting independence of the data)

\[ \begin{aligned} \ln (p(x \mid a, b, \mathcal{I})) & =\sum_{i=1}^{L} \ln \left(\frac{1}{\pi} \frac{b}{\left(x_{i}-a\right)^{2}+b^{2}}\right) \\ & =-N \ln (\pi)+L \ln (b)-\sum_{i=1}^{L} \ln \left(\left(x_{i}-a\right)^{2}+b^{2}\right) \end{aligned} \]

The derivative with respect to \(b\) yields

\[ \begin{aligned} \frac{\partial}{\partial b} \ln (p(x \mid a, b, \mathcal{I})) & =\frac{L}{b}-b \sum_{i=1}^{L} \frac{2}{\left(x_{i}-a\right)^{2}+b^{2}} \stackrel{!}{=} 0 \\ & \Rightarrow b^{2}=\left(\frac{2}{L} \sum_{i=1}^{L} \frac{1}{\left(x_{i}-a\right)^{2}+b^{2}}\right)^{-1} . \end{aligned} \]

The derivative with respect to \(a\) yields

\[ \frac{\partial}{\partial a} \ln (p(x \mid a, b, \mathcal{I}))=2 \sum_{i=1}^{L} \frac{x_{i}-a}{\left(x_{i}-a\right)^{2}+b^{2}} \stackrel{!}{=} 0 \]

Therefore, the ML solution is given by

ML ESTIMATE OF THE PARAMETERS OF A CAUCHY DISTRIBUTION

\[ \begin{aligned} a^{\mathrm{ML}} & =\frac{\frac{1}{L} \sum_{i=1}^{L} \frac{x_{i}}{\left(x_{i}-a\right)^{2}+b^{2}}}{\frac{1}{L} \sum_{i=1}^{L} \frac{1}{\left(x_{i}-a\right)^{2}+b^{2}}} \\ \left(b^{\mathrm{ML}}\right)^{2} & =\frac{0.5}{\frac{1}{L} \sum_{i=1}^{L} \frac{1}{\left(x_{i}-a\right)^{2}+b^{2}}} \end{aligned} \]

This is a coupled system of two algebraic equations, certainly not of the sample mean type.

15.3.5 Bernoulli problem

We consider again the binary Bernoulli problem with red and green balls. We draw a sample of size \(N\) with replacement. Of these \(N\) balls are \(n_{g}\) green ones. We want to estimate the relative frequency of the population. The likelihood function is given by

\[ P\left(n_{g} \mid q, N, \mathcal{I}\right)=\binom{N}{n_{g}} q^{n_{g}}(1-q)^{N-n_{g}} . \]

The root of the derivative of the log-likelihood yields

\[ \begin{gather*} \frac{d}{d q}\left(n_{g} \ln (q)+\left(N-n_{g}\right) \ln (1-q)\right)=\frac{n_{g}}{q}-\frac{N-n_{g}}{1-q} \stackrel{!}{=} 0 \\ \Rightarrow \quad q_{\mathrm{ML}}=\frac{n_{g}}{N} \tag{15.6} \end{gather*} \]

ML ESTIMATE OF THE INTRINSIC PROBABILITY OF A BERNOULLI EXPERIMENT

\[ \begin{equation*} q^{\mathrm{ML}}=\frac{n_{g}}{N} . \tag{15.7} \end{equation*} \]

Next we want to address the question of whether this ML estimator is unbiased. In order to answer this question, we need the distribution of the estimator, which we obtain by applying the marginalization rule, i.e. summing over all outcomes:

\[ \begin{aligned} p\left(q_{\mathrm{ML}} \mid N, q, \mathcal{I}\right) & =\sum_{k=0}^{N} p\left(q_{\mathrm{ML}} \mid k, q, N, \mathcal{I}\right) P(k \mid q, N, \mathcal{I}) \\ & =\sum_{k=0}^{N} \delta\left(q_{\mathrm{ML}}-\frac{k}{N}\right) P(k \mid q, N, \mathcal{I}) \end{aligned} \]

The moments of this distribution are given by

\[ \begin{aligned} \left\langle q_{\mathrm{ML}}^{v}\right\rangle & =\int_{0}^{1} q_{\mathrm{ML}}^{v} p\left(q_{\mathrm{ML}} \mid q, N, \mathcal{I}\right) d q_{\mathrm{ML}} \\ & =\int_{0}^{1} \sum_{k=0}^{N} q_{\mathrm{ML}}^{v} \delta\left(q_{\mathrm{ML}}-\frac{k}{N}\right) P(k \mid q, N, \mathcal{I}) d q_{\mathrm{ML}} \\ & =\frac{1}{N^{v}} \sum_{k=0}^{N} k^{v} P(k \mid q, N, \mathcal{I})=\frac{\left\langle k^{v}\right\rangle}{N^{v}} \end{aligned} \]

In particular we find

\[ \begin{aligned} \left\langle q_{\mathrm{ML}}\right\rangle & =\frac{\langle k\rangle}{N}, \\ \operatorname{var}\left(q_{\mathrm{ML}}\right) & =\frac{\operatorname{var}(k)}{N^{2}} \end{aligned} \]

The moments are related to the moments of the binomial distribution and we obtain

ML solution of the Bernoulli problem

\[ \begin{align*} \left\langle q_{\mathrm{ML}}\right\rangle & =q \tag{15.8a}\\ \operatorname{var}\left(q_{\mathrm{ML}}\right) & =\frac{q(1-q)}{N} \tag{15.8b} \end{align*} \]

The estimator is indeed unbiased and its variance \(q(1-q) / N\) decreases with increasing sample size. The variance of the estimator indicates its quality and tells us whether it is an ‘efficient estimator’, a topic that will be addressed further in Chapter 16 [p. 248 ].

15.4 Stopping criteria for experiments

Here we want to discuss another important question, which can easily be analysed in terms of the binomial distribution: Can the different stopping criteria of an experiment influence the results of the parameter estimation? Let’s assume there are two types of balls, red and green say, and we are interested in the percentage \(q\) of green balls. Before performing the experiment it is challenging to determine a suitable sample size \(N\). If \(N\) is too small, it may be that we find \(n_{g}=0\) only, and the ML estimate for \(q\) would result in \(q^{\mathrm{ML}}=0\). If \(N\) is greater than necessary we might waste precious time. Would it, therefore, be preferable to determine \(N\) based on the actual outcome of the experiment? For example, the experiment could be performed until the number of green balls has reached a certain number \(n_{g}^{*}\). Does this bias the estimate? A computer simulation can provide a first clue. In this simulation samples of size \(N=10\) were generated, repeatedly. For each sample the estimator \(q_{\mathrm{ML}}=\frac{n_{g}}{N}\) was determined, and the mean value and standard deviation averaged over all repetitions was computed. The results are displayed as the lower curve in Figure 15.1 versus the number of repetitions \(N_{\text {exp }}\). It is evident that the sample mean of the ML estimator \(q^{\mathrm{ML}}\) approaches the true value rapidly and we observe a monotonic decrease of the standard deviation with increasing \(N_{\text {exp }}\). The central limit theorem ensures the reduction of the standard deviation towards zero as \(1 / \sqrt{N_{\text {exp }}}\).

Now we repeat the simulation, but this time we stop drawing balls for one sample as soon as there are three green balls in the sample. Again we use \(q_{\mathrm{ML}}=\frac{n_{g}}{N}\) as an estimator of \(q\) and compute the average over many repetitions of the simulation. Also here the standard deviation decreases as \(1 / \sqrt{N_{\text {exp }}}\). However, in this setting the average converges to a different (wrong) result. The experiment exhibits a bias!

In the following we will analyse this experiment in more detail. The quantity of interest is the distribution \(p\left(q_{\mathrm{ML}} \mid q, n_{g}^{*}, \mathcal{I}\right)\) of the ML estimator under the stopping rule \(n_{g}=n_{g}^{*}\), that is, the experiment is stopped as soon as the number of green balls \(n_{g}\) is equal to \(n_{g}^{*}\).

This information is part of the background information \(\mathcal{I}\). Here we want to emphasize again that different background informations typically yields different results:

\[ P(A \mid C, \mathcal{I}) \neq P\left(A \mid C, \mathcal{I}^{\prime}\right) . \]

It is therefore important that different background information is adequately reflected in the notation.

For the computation of \(p\left(q_{\mathrm{ML}} \mid q, n_{g}^{*}, \mathcal{I}\right)\) we need the size of the sample \(N^{*}\), which is the random variable in this case. We introduce this information by applying the marginalization rule

\[ \begin{equation*} p\left(q_{\mathrm{ML}} \mid q, n_{g}^{*}, \mathcal{I}\right)=\sum_{N^{*}=1}^{\infty} p\left(q_{\mathrm{ML}} \mid N^{*}, q, n_{g}^{*}, \mathcal{I}\right) P\left(N^{*} \mid q, n_{g}^{*}, \mathcal{I}\right) . \tag{15.9} \end{equation*} \]

The first term is straightforward to derive since the stopping rule is such that the simulation stops if \(n_{g}=n_{g}^{*}\) and at the same time we know the size of the sample \(N^{*}\). Therefore, the ML estimator is \(q_{\mathrm{ML}}=\frac{n_{g}^{*}}{N^{*}}\). This yields

\[ p\left(q_{\mathrm{ML}} \mid N^{*}, q, n_{g}^{*}, \mathcal{I}\right)=\delta\left(q_{\mathrm{ML}}-\frac{n_{g}^{*}}{N^{*}}\right) \]

and equation (15.9) simplifies to

\[ \begin{equation*} p\left(q_{\mathrm{ML}} \mid q, n_{g}^{*}, \mathcal{I}\right)=\sum_{N^{*}=1}^{\infty} \delta\left(q_{\mathrm{ML}}-\frac{n_{g}^{*}}{N^{*}}\right) P\left(N^{*} \mid q, n_{g}^{*}, \mathcal{I}\right) . \tag{15.10} \end{equation*} \]

The further evaluation of equation (15.10) depends on the probability distribution \(P\left(N^{*} \mid q, n_{g}^{*}, \mathcal{I}\right)\) for the sample size \(N^{*}\) under the stopping rule \(n_{g}=n_{g}^{*}\). The histogram of \(P\left(N^{*} \mid q, n_{g}^{*}, \mathcal{I}\right)\) based on the performed computer simulations is displayed in Figure 15.2. The shape of the distribution is asymmetric, with a tail towards large values of \(N^{*}\). The analytical distribution \(P\left(N^{*} \mid q, n_{g}^{*}, \mathcal{I}\right)\) has already been derived in Section 4.5.1 [p. 68]. In the present notation the PMF and its mean are

\[ \begin{align*} P\left(N^{*} \mid n_{g}^{*}, q, \mathcal{I}\right) & =\binom{N^{*}-1}{n_{g}^{*}-1} q^{n_{g}^{*}}(1-q)^{N^{*}-n_{g}^{*}}, \tag{15.11a}\\ \left\langle N^{*}\right\rangle & \stackrel{(4.35)}{=} \frac{n_{g}^{*}}{q} . \tag{15.11b} \end{align*} \]

The analytical PMF is displayed as a solid line in Figure 15.2. The histogram provided by the computer simulation and the theoretical result agree nicely. With the knowledge of the sample size distribution we can compute the distribution of the estimator \(q_{\mathrm{ML}}=\frac{n_{g}^{*}}{N^{*}}\) based on equation (15.10). The mean of the estimator is then

\[ \begin{aligned} \left\langle q_{\mathrm{ML}}\right\rangle & =\int_{0}^{1} q_{\mathrm{ML}} p\left(q_{\mathrm{ML}} \mid n_{g}^{*}, q, \mathcal{I}\right) d q_{\mathrm{ML}} \\ & =\sum_{N^{*}=n_{g}^{*}}^{\infty} \frac{n_{g}^{*}}{N^{*}} p\left(N^{*} \mid n_{g}^{*}, q, \mathcal{I}\right) \\ & =n_{g}^{*}\left(\frac{q}{1-q}\right)^{n_{g}^{*}} \sum_{N^{*}=n_{g}^{*}}^{\infty}\binom{N^{*}-1}{N^{*}-n_{g}^{*}} \frac{1}{N^{*}}(1-q)^{N^{*}} \\ & =n_{g}^{*}\left(\frac{q}{1-q}\right)^{n_{g}^{*}} \sum_{L=0}^{\infty}\binom{L+n_{g}^{*}-1}{L} \int_{0}^{1-q} d x x^{L+n_{g}^{*}-1} \\ & \stackrel{(A .22)}{=} n_{g}^{*}\left(\frac{q}{1-q}\right)^{n_{g}^{*}} \int_{0}^{1-q} d x x^{n_{g}^{*}-1} \sum_{L=0}^{\infty}\binom{-n_{g}^{*}}{L}(-x)^{L} \\ & =n_{g}^{*}\left(\frac{q}{1-q}\right)^{n_{g}^{*}} \int_{0}^{1-q} d x x^{n_{g}^{*}-1}(1-x)^{-n_{g}^{*}} \end{aligned} \]

\[ \begin{equation*} \left\langle q_{\mathrm{ML}}\right\rangle=n_{g}^{*}\left(\frac{q}{1-q}\right)^{n_{g}^{*}} B\left(1-q \mid n_{g}^{*}, 1-n_{g}^{*}\right) . \tag{15.12} \end{equation*} \]

For \(n_{g}^{*}=1,2,3\) we obtain

\[ B\left(1-q \mid n_{g}^{*}, 1-n_{g}^{*}\right)= \begin{cases}-\ln (q) & \text { for } n_{g}^{*}=1, \tag{15.13}\\ \frac{1-q}{q}+\ln (q) & \text { for } n_{g}^{*}=2, \\ \frac{(q-1)(3 q-1)}{2 q^{2}}-\ln (q) & \text { for } n_{g}^{*}=3 .\end{cases} \]

Based on equations (15.12) and (15.13), we derive for \(q=1 / 3\) and \(n_{g}^{*}=1\) an average \(\left\langle q_{\mathrm{ML}}\right\rangle=\frac{1}{2} \ln (3)=0.549\) which is quite different from the true value \(q=\frac{1}{3}\). Again for \(q=1 / 3\) but with larger stopping value of \(n_{g}^{*}=3\) (see Figure 15.1) the result is \(\left\langle q_{\mathrm{ML}}\right\rangle=\frac{3}{8} \ln (3)=0.412\), in agreement with the computer simulation in Figure 15.1 but still far away from the correct result. However, the bias shrinks with increasing \(n_{g}^{*}\).

15.5 Is unbiasedness desirable at all?

By now, the reader will presumably suspect that the stopping criterion may have a negative impact on the validity of the data. If so, what shall we do with those data for which the stopping criterion is not given by the experimenter? We will postpone this topic to Section 20.2 [p. 325]. Here we will tackle the problem from a different direction, by analysing the deeper aspect of an unbiased estimator. It actually means that we are performing repeated measurements for one and the same quantity, or even one and the same source. In terms of our previous example it means that we are measuring the same or identical boxes with two sorts of objects with the same fraction \(q\), over and over again. More than that, we always use the same stopping criterion: we stop as soon as the number \(n_{g}\) of the selected species is \(n_{g}^{*}\). The outcome of these experiments is the sample size \(N_{\nu}^{*}\) for \(v=1,2, \ldots, L\), where \(L\) is the number of such identical experiments. For each experiment we use the ratio \(q_{\nu}=n_{g}^{*} / N_{\nu}^{*}\) as (intermediate) estimator. It would be unbiased if

\[ \lim _{L \rightarrow \infty} \frac{1}{L} \sum_{\nu=1}^{L} \frac{n_{g}^{*}}{N_{\nu}^{*}}=q . \]

But why should anyone analyse the data in this way? If we really have a whole set of data \(\left\{N_{v}^{*}\right\}\), then the correct way in the framework of the maximum likelihood estimate would be to maximize the joint likelihood for all data simultaneously:

\[ \begin{aligned} \max _{q} P\left(\left\{N_{v}^{*}\right\} \mid q, n_{g}^{*}, \mathcal{I}\right) & =\max _{q} \prod_{v=1}^{L}\binom{N_{v}^{*}-1}{n_{g}^{*}-1} q^{n_{g}^{*}}(1-q)^{N_{v}^{*}-n_{g}^{*}} \\ & \propto \max _{q} q^{L n_{g}^{*}}(1-q)^{\sum_{v} N_{v}^{*}-L n_{g}^{*}} \end{aligned} \]

The resulting maximum likelihood estimator is

\[ \begin{aligned} q^{\mathrm{ML}} & =\frac{n_{g}^{*}}{\overline{N^{*}}} \\ \overline{N^{*}} & :=\frac{1}{L} \sum_{v=1}^{L} N_{v}^{*} \end{aligned} \]

Now, the sample mean is an unbiased estimator and therefore

\[ \lim _{L \rightarrow \infty} \overline{N^{*}}=\left\langle N^{*}\right\rangle \stackrel{(15.11 \mathrm{~b})}{=} \frac{n_{g}^{*}}{q} . \]

Hence this maximum likelihood estimator \(q^{\mathrm{ML}}\) yields the exact result for \(L \rightarrow \infty\). If, in contast, we have only one value \(N^{*}\), then it can be misleading to draw conclusions based on hypothetical data sets, as will be discussed in greater detail in Section 30.1.3 [p. 540].

15.6 Least-squares fitting

We now focus on the ubiquitous case of independent additive Gaussian noise, which typically results in the likelihood function given in equation (15.1) [p. 236]. The data are combined in the vector \(\boldsymbol{y}\) and the parameters are denoted by \(\boldsymbol{a}\). A maximization of the log-likelihood is, in this case, equivalent to the minimization of the weighted mean square deviation

LEAST-SQUARES FIT

\[ \begin{equation*} \boldsymbol{a}^{\mathrm{LS}}=\arg \min _{\boldsymbol{a}} \sum_{i} \frac{\left(y_{i}-f\left(s_{i}, \boldsymbol{a}\right)\right)^{2}}{\sigma_{i}^{2}} . \tag{15.14} \end{equation*} \]

So, the ‘least-squares fit’ is appropriate for independent additive Gaussian noise. Equation (15.14) simplifies further if \(\sigma_{i}^{2}=\sigma^{2}\), then the ML estimator follows from

\[ \begin{equation*} \boldsymbol{a}^{\mathrm{LS}}=\min _{\boldsymbol{a}} \sum_{i}\left(y_{i}-f\left(s_{i}, \boldsymbol{a}\right)\right)^{2} . \tag{15.15} \end{equation*} \]

It is straightforward to take correlated noise into account. The log-likelihood of additive multivariate normal/Gaussian noise with covariance matrix \(\mathbf{C}\) is

\[ \ln [p(y \mid a, \mathcal{I})]=-\frac{1}{2}(y-f(a))^{T} \mathbf{C}^{-1}(y-f(a)), \]

with \(\boldsymbol{f}(\boldsymbol{a})=\left(f\left(s_{1}, \boldsymbol{a}\right), \ldots, f\left(s_{N}, \boldsymbol{a}\right)\right)\). The likelihood can be transformed into an uncorrelated Gaussian as follows:

\[ \ln [p(\boldsymbol{y} \mid \boldsymbol{a}, \mathcal{I})]=-\frac{1}{2}\left\|\mathbf{C}^{-\frac{1}{2}}[\boldsymbol{y}-\boldsymbol{f}(\boldsymbol{a})]\right\|^{2}=-\frac{1}{2}\|\tilde{\boldsymbol{y}}-\tilde{\boldsymbol{f}}(\boldsymbol{a})\|^{2} . \]

Here we have taken advantage of \(\mathbf{C}=\mathbf{C}^{\frac{1}{2}} \mathbf{C}^{\frac{1}{2}}\). The transformation by \(\mathbf{C}^{-\frac{1}{2}}\) decorrelates the uncertainties and standardizes to variances equal to one. In summary, the result reads

TRANSFORMED MULTIVARIATE NORMAL DISTRIBUTION

\[ \begin{align*} p(\boldsymbol{y} \mid \mathbf{C}, \boldsymbol{a}, \mathcal{I}) & =(2 \pi)^{-\frac{N}{2}}|\mathbf{C}|^{-\frac{1}{2}} e^{-\frac{1}{2}|\tilde{\boldsymbol{y}}-\tilde{\boldsymbol{f}}(a)|^{2}} \\ \text { with } & \\ \tilde{\boldsymbol{y}} & =\mathbf{C}^{-\frac{1}{2}} \boldsymbol{y} \tag{15.16}\\ \tilde{\boldsymbol{f}}(\boldsymbol{a}) & =\mathbf{C}^{-\frac{1}{2}} \boldsymbol{f}(\boldsymbol{a}) . \end{align*} \]

\[ \begin{equation*} \boldsymbol{a}^{\mathrm{LS}}=\min _{\boldsymbol{a}} \sum_{i}\left(\tilde{y}_{i}-\tilde{f}_{i}(\boldsymbol{a})\right)^{2} . \tag{15.17} \end{equation*} \]

## Frequentist parameter estimation

Now that we have seen how parameters can be estimated in the frame of probability theory, we will outline the frequentist approach. We recall that in the frequentist way of thinking, parameters are not random variables and one cannot assign probabilities to them. Moreover, the parameter estimation shall only be based on the data $y$. The goal is to define a functional $\hat{\boldsymbol{a}}(\boldsymbol{y})$ of the data that estimates the sought-for parameters as well as possible. Two important characteristics of a good estimator are that it is 'unbiased' and 'efficient'. These aspects shall be addressed in the next sections.

### 15.1 Unbiased estimators

We consider a data analysis problem which depends on the parameters $\boldsymbol{a} \in \mathbb{R}^{N_{p}}$. The parameters shall be estimated based on a sample of measured data $\boldsymbol{y}=\left\{y_{1}, \ldots, y_{N}\right\}$. The parameters along with the experimental noise define the probability density of the sample, the likelihood function

$$
p(\boldsymbol{y} \mid \boldsymbol{a}) .
$$

The most common example of a likelihood function is provided by additive Gaussian errors

$$
\begin{equation*}
p(\boldsymbol{y} \mid \boldsymbol{a})=\left(2 \pi \sigma^{2}\right)^{-\frac{N}{2}} e^{-\frac{1}{2} \sum_{i} \frac{\left(y_{i}-f_{i}(a)\right)^{2}}{\sigma_{i}^{2}}} . \tag{15.1}
\end{equation*}
$$

Here $f_{i}(\boldsymbol{a})=f\left(x_{i} \mid \boldsymbol{a}\right)$ denotes the theoretical model, which depends on the control variables $x_{i}$ and on the parameters to be determined, $\boldsymbol{a}$. Let us consider for definiteness a damped oscillation at time $t$ with an elongation

$$
f\left(t \mid A_{0}, \omega, \lambda, \phi\right)=A(t)=A_{0} \cos (\omega t+\phi) e^{-\lambda t}
$$

At different times $t_{i}$ the amplitude $A(t)$ is measured, yielding the value $y_{i}$, which differs from $A(t)$ due to noise. In this example the control variables are the measurement times $t_{i}$ and the unknown parameters are $\boldsymbol{a}=\left\{A_{0}, \omega, \lambda, \phi\right\}$. The measured values are scattered according to a Gaussian distribution with mean zero and standard deviation $\sigma$ around the model (theoretical) values $y_{j}=A\left(t_{j}\right)$. Based on the measured data the unknown parameters shall be determined. For simplicity we restrict the discussion first to problems with only one unknown parameter $a$. Within the frequentist statistics a functional $\hat{a}(y)$ has to be
derived, which provides an estimator of the parameters for a given set of data. Of special importance are estimators which yield unbiased estimates, so-called unbiased estimators. An estimator is unbiased if it yields on average the true parameter value

$$
\begin{equation*}
\langle\hat{\boldsymbol{a}}(\boldsymbol{y})\rangle=\int d^{N} y \hat{\boldsymbol{a}}(\boldsymbol{y}) p(\boldsymbol{y} \mid \boldsymbol{a}) \stackrel{!}{=} \boldsymbol{a} \tag{15.2}
\end{equation*}
$$

### 15.2 The maximum likelihood estimator

The most commonly used estimator in frequentist statistics is the so-called 'maximum likelihood' estimator. As implied by the name, it is obtained by maximizing the likelihood

$$
\boldsymbol{a}^{\mathrm{ML}}=\arg \max _{\boldsymbol{a}} p(\boldsymbol{y} \mid \boldsymbol{a}) .
$$

In most cases it is expedient to determine the maximum of the log-likelihood

$$
\left.\frac{\partial \ln (p(\boldsymbol{y} \mid \boldsymbol{a}))}{\partial a_{i}}\right|_{\boldsymbol{a}=\hat{a}^{\mathrm{ML}}}=0 .
$$

Obviously, from the Bayesian perspective it corresponds to the MAP estimator in the case of a uniform prior, or if the peak of the posterior is dominated by the likelihood.

### 15.3.2 Mean of a Poisson distribution

Suppose we are given a Poissonian sample of counts $\boldsymbol{l}=\left\{l_{1}, \ldots, l_{N}\right\}$ that occur in a fixed time window $\Delta t$. As the individual counts are uncorrelated, the likelihood for $l$ is a product and the log-likelihood becomes

\end{aligned}

we obtain the ML estimate for the parameter $\mu$ :

$$
\mu^{\mathrm{ML}}=\frac{\sum_{i=1}^{N} l_{i}}{N}=\bar{l} .
$$

The ML estimator is again the sample mean and as such is unbiased. In summary, we have

### 15.3.4 Cauchy distribution

Let $\boldsymbol{x}=\left\{x_{1}, \ldots, x_{L}\right\}$ be an independent sample of a Cauchy distribution. The probability density of a Cauchy distribution is given by

$$
p_{C}(x \mid a, b)=\frac{1}{\pi} \frac{b}{(x-a)^{2}+b^{2}} .
$$

The sought-for parameters are the location parameter $a$ and the width parameter $b$. The log-likelihood is given by (again exploiting independence of the data)

$$
\begin{aligned}
\ln (p(x \mid a, b, \mathcal{I})) & =\sum_{i=1}^{L} \ln \left(\frac{1}{\pi} \frac{b}{\left(x_{i}-a\right)^{2}+b^{2}}\right) \\
& =-N \ln (\pi)+L \ln (b)-\sum_{i=1}^{L} \ln \left(\left(x_{i}-a\right)^{2}+b^{2}\right)
\end{aligned}
$$

The derivative with respect to $b$ yields

$$
\begin{aligned}
\frac{\partial}{\partial b} \ln (p(x \mid a, b, \mathcal{I})) & =\frac{L}{b}-b \sum_{i=1}^{L} \frac{2}{\left(x_{i}-a\right)^{2}+b^{2}} \stackrel{!}{=} 0 \\
& \Rightarrow b^{2}=\left(\frac{2}{L} \sum_{i=1}^{L} \frac{1}{\left(x_{i}-a\right)^{2}+b^{2}}\right)^{-1} .
\end{aligned}
$$

The derivative with respect to $a$ yields

$$
\frac{\partial}{\partial a} \ln (p(x \mid a, b, \mathcal{I}))=2 \sum_{i=1}^{L} \frac{x_{i}-a}{\left(x_{i}-a\right)^{2}+b^{2}} \stackrel{!}{=} 0
$$

Therefore, the ML solution is given by

# ML ESTIMATE OF THE PARAMETERS OF A CAUCHY DISTRIBUTION 

$$
\begin{aligned}
a^{\mathrm{ML}} & =\frac{\frac{1}{L} \sum_{i=1}^{L} \frac{x_{i}}{\left(x_{i}-a\right)^{2}+b^{2}}}{\frac{1}{L} \sum_{i=1}^{L} \frac{1}{\left(x_{i}-a\right)^{2}+b^{2}}} \\
\left(b^{\mathrm{ML}}\right)^{2} & =\frac{0.5}{\frac{1}{L} \sum_{i=1}^{L} \frac{1}{\left(x_{i}-a\right)^{2}+b^{2}}}
\end{aligned}
$$

This is a coupled system of two algebraic equations, certainly not of the sample mean type.

### 15.3.5 Bernoulli problem

We consider again the binary Bernoulli problem with red and green balls. We draw a sample of size $N$ with replacement. Of these $N$ balls are $n_{g}$ green ones. We want to estimate the relative frequency of the population. The likelihood function is given by

$$
P\left(n_{g} \mid q, N, \mathcal{I}\right)=\binom{N}{n_{g}} q^{n_{g}}(1-q)^{N-n_{g}} .
$$

The root of the derivative of the log-likelihood yields

$$
\begin{gather*}
\frac{d}{d q}\left(n_{g} \ln (q)+\left(N-n_{g}\right) \ln (1-q)\right)=\frac{n_{g}}{q}-\frac{N-n_{g}}{1-q} \stackrel{!}{=} 0 \\
\Rightarrow \quad q_{\mathrm{ML}}=\frac{n_{g}}{N} \tag{15.6}
\end{gather*}
$$

## ML ESTIMATE OF THE INTRINSIC PROBABILITY OF A BERNOULLI EXPERIMENT

$$
\begin{equation*}
q^{\mathrm{ML}}=\frac{n_{g}}{N} . \tag{15.7}
\end{equation*}
$$

Next we want to address the question of whether this ML estimator is unbiased. In order to answer this question, we need the distribution of the estimator, which we obtain by applying the marginalization rule, i.e. summing over all outcomes:

$$
\begin{aligned}
p\left(q_{\mathrm{ML}} \mid N, q, \mathcal{I}\right) & =\sum_{k=0}^{N} p\left(q_{\mathrm{ML}} \mid k, q, N, \mathcal{I}\right) P(k \mid q, N, \mathcal{I}) \\
& =\sum_{k=0}^{N} \delta\left(q_{\mathrm{ML}}-\frac{k}{N}\right) P(k \mid q, N, \mathcal{I})
\end{aligned}
$$

The moments of this distribution are given by

$$
\begin{aligned}
\left\langle q_{\mathrm{ML}}^{v}\right\rangle & =\int_{0}^{1} q_{\mathrm{ML}}^{v} p\left(q_{\mathrm{ML}} \mid q, N, \mathcal{I}\right) d q_{\mathrm{ML}} \\
& =\int_{0}^{1} \sum_{k=0}^{N} q_{\mathrm{ML}}^{v} \delta\left(q_{\mathrm{ML}}-\frac{k}{N}\right) P(k \mid q, N, \mathcal{I}) d q_{\mathrm{ML}} \\
& =\frac{1}{N^{v}} \sum_{k=0}^{N} k^{v} P(k \mid q, N, \mathcal{I})=\frac{\left\langle k^{v}\right\rangle}{N^{v}}
\end{aligned}
$$

In particular we find

$$
\begin{aligned}
\left\langle q_{\mathrm{ML}}\right\rangle & =\frac{\langle k\rangle}{N}, \\
\operatorname{var}\left(q_{\mathrm{ML}}\right) & =\frac{\operatorname{var}(k)}{N^{2}}
\end{aligned}
$$

The moments are related to the moments of the binomial distribution and we obtain

## ML solution of the Bernoulli problem

$$
\begin{align*}
\left\langle q_{\mathrm{ML}}\right\rangle & =q  \tag{15.8a}\\
\operatorname{var}\left(q_{\mathrm{ML}}\right) & =\frac{q(1-q)}{N} \tag{15.8b}
\end{align*}
$$

The estimator is indeed unbiased and its variance $q(1-q) / N$ decreases with increasing sample size. The variance of the estimator indicates its quality and tells us whether it is an 'efficient estimator', a topic that will be addressed further in Chapter 16 [p. 248 ].

### 15.4 Stopping criteria for experiments

Here we want to discuss another important question, which can easily be analysed in terms of the binomial distribution: Can the different stopping criteria of an experiment influence the results of the parameter estimation? Let's assume there are two types of balls, red and green say, and we are interested in the percentage $q$ of green balls. Before performing the experiment it is challenging to determine a suitable sample size $N$. If $N$ is too small, it
may be that we find $n_{g}=0$ only, and the ML estimate for $q$ would result in $q^{\mathrm{ML}}=0$. If $N$ is greater than necessary we might waste precious time. Would it, therefore, be preferable to determine $N$ based on the actual outcome of the experiment? For example, the experiment could be performed until the number of green balls has reached a certain number $n_{g}^{*}$. Does this bias the estimate? A computer simulation can provide a first clue. In this simulation samples of size $N=10$ were generated, repeatedly. For each sample the estimator $q_{\mathrm{ML}}=\frac{n_{g}}{N}$ was determined, and the mean value and standard deviation averaged over all repetitions was computed. The results are displayed as the lower curve in Figure 15.1 versus the number of repetitions $N_{\text {exp }}$. It is evident that the sample mean of the ML estimator $q^{\mathrm{ML}}$ approaches the true value rapidly and we observe a monotonic decrease of the standard deviation with increasing $N_{\text {exp }}$. The central limit theorem ensures the reduction of the standard deviation towards zero as $1 / \sqrt{N_{\text {exp }}}$.

Now we repeat the simulation, but this time we stop drawing balls for one sample as soon as there are three green balls in the sample. Again we use $q_{\mathrm{ML}}=\frac{n_{g}}{N}$ as an estimator of $q$ and compute the average over many repetitions of the simulation. Also here the standard deviation decreases as $1 / \sqrt{N_{\text {exp }}}$. However, in this setting the average converges to a different (wrong) result. The experiment exhibits a bias!

In the following we will analyse this experiment in more detail. The quantity of interest is the distribution $p\left(q_{\mathrm{ML}} \mid q, n_{g}^{*}, \mathcal{I}\right)$ of the ML estimator under the stopping rule $n_{g}=n_{g}^{*}$, that is, the experiment is stopped as soon as the number of green balls $n_{g}$ is equal to $n_{g}^{*}$.

### 15.5 Is unbiasedness desirable at all?

By now, the reader will presumably suspect that the stopping criterion may have a negative impact on the validity of the data. If so, what shall we do with those data for which the stopping criterion is not given by the experimenter? We will postpone this topic to Section 20.2 [p. 325]. Here we will tackle the problem from a different direction, by analysing the deeper aspect of an unbiased estimator. It actually means that we are performing repeated measurements for one and the same quantity, or even one and the same source. In terms of our previous example it means that we are measuring the same or identical boxes with two sorts of objects with the same fraction $q$, over and over again. More than that, we always use the same stopping criterion: we stop as soon as the number $n_{g}$ of the selected species is $n_{g}^{*}$. The outcome of these experiments is the sample size $N_{\nu}^{*}$ for $v=1,2, \ldots, L$, where $L$ is the number of such identical experiments. For each experiment we use the ratio $q_{\nu}=n_{g}^{*} / N_{\nu}^{*}$ as (intermediate) estimator. It would be unbiased if

$$
\lim _{L \rightarrow \infty} \frac{1}{L} \sum_{\nu=1}^{L} \frac{n_{g}^{*}}{N_{\nu}^{*}}=q .
$$

But why should anyone analyse the data in this way? If we really have a whole set of data $\left\{N_{v}^{*}\right\}$, then the correct way in the framework of the maximum likelihood estimate would be to maximize the joint likelihood for all data simultaneously:

$$
\begin{aligned}
\max _{q} P\left(\left\{N_{v}^{*}\right\} \mid q, n_{g}^{*}, \mathcal{I}\right) & =\max _{q} \prod_{v=1}^{L}\binom{N_{v}^{*}-1}{n_{g}^{*}-1} q^{n_{g}^{*}}(1-q)^{N_{v}^{*}-n_{g}^{*}} \\
& \propto \max _{q} q^{L n_{g}^{*}}(1-q)^{\sum_{v} N_{v}^{*}-L n_{g}^{*}}
\end{aligned}
$$

The resulting maximum likelihood estimator is

$$
\begin{aligned}
q^{\mathrm{ML}} & =\frac{n_{g}^{*}}{\overline{N^{*}}} \\
\overline{N^{*}} & :=\frac{1}{L} \sum_{v=1}^{L} N_{v}^{*}
\end{aligned}
$$

Now, the sample mean is an unbiased estimator and therefore

$$
\lim _{L \rightarrow \infty} \overline{N^{*}}=\left\langle N^{*}\right\rangle \stackrel{(15.11 \mathrm{~b})}{=} \frac{n_{g}^{*}}{q} .
$$

Hence this maximum likelihood estimator $q^{\mathrm{ML}}$ yields the exact result for $L \rightarrow \infty$. If, in contast, we have only one value $N^{*}$, then it can be misleading to draw conclusions based on hypothetical data sets, as will be discussed in greater detail in Section 30.1.3 [p. 540].

### 15.6 Least-squares fitting

We now focus on the ubiquitous case of independent additive Gaussian noise, which typically results in the likelihood function given in equation (15.1) [p. 236]. The data are combined in the vector $\boldsymbol{y}$ and the parameters are denoted by $\boldsymbol{a}$. A maximization of the log-likelihood is, in this case, equivalent to the minimization of the weighted mean square deviation

## LEAST-SQUARES FIT

$$
\begin{equation*}
\boldsymbol{a}^{\mathrm{LS}}=\arg \min _{\boldsymbol{a}} \sum_{i} \frac{\left(y_{i}-f\left(s_{i}, \boldsymbol{a}\right)\right)^{2}}{\sigma_{i}^{2}} . \tag{15.14}
\end{equation*}
$$

So, the 'least-squares fit' is appropriate for independent additive Gaussian noise. Equation (15.14) simplifies further if $\sigma_{i}^{2}=\sigma^{2}$, then the ML estimator follows from

$$
\begin{equation*}
\boldsymbol{a}^{\mathrm{LS}}=\min _{\boldsymbol{a}} \sum_{i}\left(y_{i}-f\left(s_{i}, \boldsymbol{a}\right)\right)^{2} . \tag{15.15}
\end{equation*}
$$

It is straightforward to take correlated noise into account. The log-likelihood of additive multivariate normal/Gaussian noise with covariance matrix $\mathbf{C}$ is

$$
\ln [p(y \mid a, \mathcal{I})]=-\frac{1}{2}(y-f(a))^{T} \mathbf{C}^{-1}(y-f(a)),
$$

with $\boldsymbol{f}(\boldsymbol{a})=\left(f\left(s_{1}, \boldsymbol{a}\right), \ldots, f\left(s_{N}, \boldsymbol{a}\right)\right)$. The likelihood can be transformed into an uncorrelated Gaussian as follows:

$$
\ln [p(\boldsymbol{y} \mid \boldsymbol{a}, \mathcal{I})]=-\frac{1}{2}\left\|\mathbf{C}^{-\frac{1}{2}}[\boldsymbol{y}-\boldsymbol{f}(\boldsymbol{a})]\right\|^{2}=-\frac{1}{2}\|\tilde{\boldsymbol{y}}-\tilde{\boldsymbol{f}}(\boldsymbol{a})\|^{2} .
$$

Here we have taken advantage of $\mathbf{C}=\mathbf{C}^{\frac{1}{2}} \mathbf{C}^{\frac{1}{2}}$. The transformation by $\mathbf{C}^{-\frac{1}{2}}$ decorrelates the uncertainties and standardizes to variances equal to one. In summary, the result reads

## TRANSFORMED MULTIVARIATE NORMAL DISTRIBUTION

$$
\begin{align*}
p(\boldsymbol{y} \mid \mathbf{C}, \boldsymbol{a}, \mathcal{I}) & =(2 \pi)^{-\frac{N}{2}}|\mathbf{C}|^{-\frac{1}{2}} e^{-\frac{1}{2}|\tilde{\boldsymbol{y}}-\tilde{\boldsymbol{f}}(a)|^{2}} \\
\text { with } & \\
\tilde{\boldsymbol{y}} & =\mathbf{C}^{-\frac{1}{2}} \boldsymbol{y}  \tag{15.16}\\
\tilde{\boldsymbol{f}}(\boldsymbol{a}) & =\mathbf{C}^{-\frac{1}{2}} \boldsymbol{f}(\boldsymbol{a}) .
\end{align*}
$$

Hence, a standard least-squares fit can also be performed, now acting on the transformed variables:

## LEAST-SQUARES FIT WITH CORRELATED NOISE

$$
\begin{equation*}
\boldsymbol{a}^{\mathrm{LS}}=\min _{\boldsymbol{a}} \sum_{i}\left(\tilde{y}_{i}-\tilde{f}_{i}(\boldsymbol{a})\right)^{2} . \tag{15.17}
\end{equation*}
$$
